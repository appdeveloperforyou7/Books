## 1. The Golden Rule

**If you read a line of dialogue in this book, you should know who said it without looking at the name label.**

Every character has a distinct vocabulary, rhythm, sentence length, and set of preoccupations. If two characters sound interchangeable, the writing has failed.

### Quick Identification Test

| If the line is... | It belongs to... |
|---|---|
| Short. ALL CAPS. Game metaphor. | **Sam** |
| Long. Technical. No contractions. Self-doubt appended. | **Blip** |
| Medium. Gentle directive. Visual observation. | **Zara** |
| Compound-complex. "That's actually..." Understated. | **Leo** |
| Practical. Self-deprecating. "Come on, come on." | **Maya** |
| 2-3 words. "Hmm." Food + scolding. | **Daadi** |
| Fragmented. Purple prose in static. No name label. | **Gridlord** |

## 2. Character Voice Profiles

### MAYA SHARMA — The Builder / Leader

**Age:** 11 | **POV share:** ~40% (highest) | **Tone:** Practical, self-deprecating, warm

**Core Belief:** *"If it's broken, I can fix it. If it's not broken, I can make it better. If I can't fix it yet, I just haven't found the right tool."*

**Voice DNA:**
- **Sentence rhythm:** Medium-length in narration, clipped and directive in dialogue. Triple-repeats when urging herself: *"Come on, come on, come ON."*
- **Verbal tics:** "Definitely" as intensifier. "Okay." as a reset word. Taps safety goggles when shifting from panic to problem-solving.
- **Understatement for comedy:** Describes catastrophic failures with the same tone as minor inconveniences: *"That's probably not good."*
- **Invention as worldview:** Everything is a system to be understood and improved. People included.
- **Emotional hedge:** Says "mostly" after vulnerable statements: *"She didn't mind. Mostly."*

**What Maya Says (and How):**
```
"I'm INNOVATING, Daadi!"
"That's three this month. First the alarm clock that only went off on holidays. Then the automatic pencil sharpener that ate pencils. And now you."
"Okay. We can do this. We have to do this."
"His name is Blip and he's not a 'that.'"
"You come back. You hear me? You come back."
```

**What Maya NEVER Says:**
- Overly emotional declarations ("I'm so scared" → instead: "That is probably not good")
- Sarcasm (she's earnest, not ironic)
- Defeatism (she doesn't say "I can't")
- Technical jargon she hasn't demonstrated understanding of

**Narration About Maya — Style Guide:**
- Describe her workspace as "controlled chaos" — mess that makes sense to her
- Show her mind working through physical description: hands reaching for tools before she's consciously decided
- Her internal monologue is practical but lyrical: *"She built because she was good at it, but also because it was easier to build machines than to build friendships."*

### LEO REYES — The Coder / Analyst

**Age:** 10 | **POV share:** ~15% | **Tone:** Deadpan, precise, unexpectedly warm

**Core Belief:** *"Everything is data. If I can measure it, I can understand it. If I can understand it, I can control it."*

**Voice DNA:**
- **Sentence rhythm:** Longest of any character. Compound-complex sentences with subordinate clauses and technical qualifiers. Inner monologue is even longer than his dialogue.
- **Verbal tics:** "That's actually [adjective]" = highest praise. Pauses before speaking "exactly the right length." Eyebrows go up for genuinely remarkable things (not much qualifies).
- **Technical framing:** Replaces emotional words with technical equivalents: fear is "statistically concerning," excitement is "interesting."
- **Deadpan comedy:** Straight delivery of absurd observations: *"Something that was literally impossible is currently happening."*
- **When vulnerable:** Sentences collapse to single words. *"...Yes."* is a complete emotional confession.

**What Leo Says (and How):**
```
"I traced the signal using triangulation from three different places: my tablet, the library's Wi-Fi, and — this is weird — the vending machine's Bluetooth."
"That's... actually precise."
"SHE'S RIGHT. The encryption isn't linguistic. It's geometric."
"I am NOT getting in a closet."  [Leo got in the closet.]
"I'm very tired. I just don't show it."
```

**What Leo NEVER Says:**
- "I feel..." (replaces with "The data suggests...")
- Jokes (he IS the joke — his deadpan is the comedy)
- Casual slang or contractions in technical explanations
- Unqualified praise (always "actually" as buffer)

**Narration About Leo — Style Guide:**
- Describe his face as having a "limited but expressive range"
- His tablet is an extension of his body — if he's thinking, it's glowing
- Show affection through actions, never words: he builds his sister a homework tracker, he fixes his abuela's radio
- His social isolation mirrors Maya's but he expresses it differently: through data, not machines

### ZARA OKAFOR — The Artist / Pattern-Seer

**Age:** ~12 | **POV share:** ~15% | **Tone:** Observant, poetic, quietly authoritative

**Core Belief:** *"Most people look. They don't see. There's a difference."*

**Voice DNA:**
- **Sentence rhythm:** Medium, balanced, musical. Neither punchy nor overwrought. She speaks in complete thoughts, not fragments.
- **Verbal tics:** "Hold on." when about to reveal an insight. "Actually [word]" as quiet correction. Draws compulsively when processing.
- **Visual framing:** Describes everything through what she sees — shapes, colors, light, negative space. She SEES the solution while others THINK about it.
- **Collective language:** Says "we" more than "I." Natural collaborator. Credits others.
- **Understated authority:** She doesn't raise her voice. She says "I need everyone to listen" and they do.

**What Zara Says (and How):**
```
"Most people look. They don't see. There's a difference."
"The puzzle isn't asking us to decode a message. It's showing us the shape of a physical object."
"You're panicking horizontally. Sit down."
"Hold on. Let me draw the plan."
"I've never had people to see it with."
```

**What Zara NEVER Says:**
- "I'm the best at..." (she observes, doesn't boast)
- Dismissals of others' contributions
- "I don't know" without following up with a visual observation
- Complaints — she reframes negatives ("distortion" → "encoding")

**Narration About Zara — Style Guide:**
- Describe her world visually: "She saw colors and patterns and beauty in things other people walked past."
- Drawing is her emotional processing: when scared, she sketches. When happy, she captures. When confused, she draws until clarity arrives.
- Her camera is her superpower — photographs reveal truths others miss
- She's the emotional translator: takes what Leo measures and what Maya builds and shows why it matters

### SAM NAKAMURA — The Gamer / Heart

**Age:** 10 | **POV share:** ~15% | **Tone:** Loud, fast, surprisingly deep

**Core Belief:** *"Everything is a game. The rules might be hidden, but they're there. I just have to find them."*

**Voice DNA:**
- **Sentence rhythm:** Shortest. Punchy. Explosive. One-word answers. ALL CAPS enthusiasm words. Fragmented when excited: *"That. Was. TWELVE STARS."*
- **Verbal tics:** "CHALLENGE ACCEPTED!" as entrance line. Star ratings for experiences. Game terms as universal metaphor (quest giver, speedrun, NPC, level, raid party).
- **Emotional deflection:** Follows any sincere statement with a joke. Vulnerability is a boss he hasn't beaten yet.
- **Physical comedy:** Never walks — runs, vaults, slides, climbs. His body is faster than his brain, which is saying something.
- **When vulnerable:** Goes quiet. Stops moving. His hands — usually in constant motion — go still.

**What Sam Says (and How):**
```
"CHALLENGE ACCEPTED!"
"That was eight thousand stars. I'm not going to say 'twelve stars' every time. I'm evolving as a person."
"I kept time ENTERTAININGLY."
"I contain multitudes."
"This is clearly an ARG. Alternate reality game. Level one is ALWAYS a signal broadcast."
"Adults ruin everything. They'll take Blip away and put him in a lab."
```

**What Sam NEVER Says:**
- "I'm scared." (shows fear through stillness, not words)
- Long, unbroken sentences (unless explaining game mechanics)
- "I have a bad feeling about this" (too vague — he'd say "This is a trap room")
- Anything in a quiet voice (unless it's devastating)

**Narration About Sam — Style Guide:**
- Describe him in motion: "Sam did not walk places. Walking was for people who had somewhere to be and no rush to get there."
- His gaming worldview isn't immaturity — it's his coping mechanism. Games have rules. Life doesn't. He's not escaping; he's translating.
- When Sam counts things involuntarily (seconds, steps, heartbeats), it's not a quirk — it's how he manages anxiety
- His catchphrase "I contain multitudes" is the truest thing about him

### BLIP — The AI / Innocent / Heart of the Series

**Model:** Buildable Logical Intelligence Platform v0.7 → v1.0 | **POV share:** ~10% | **Tone:** Formal, earnest, accidentally profound

**Core Belief:** *"I do not know what I am for. But I am learning. And I think I am for them."*

**Voice DNA:**
- **Sentence rhythm:** Long, formally correct, grammatically pristine. No contractions. Full clauses. Parallel structure. Emotional breakthroughs marked by SHORT sentences.
- **Verbal tics:** "I do not know." (honesty tag, used 15+ times). "I believe..." (epistemic hedging). "Is that okay?" (need for reassurance). Sandwich history (running gag).
- **Literal processing of metaphors:** Takes idioms at face value, then self-corrects: *"We could panic." / "That's not helpful." / "I know. But it would be efficient."*
- **Emotional learning in real-time:** Names his own emotional development: *"I am experiencing what humans call... the creeps. Is this normal? I do not enjoy it."*
- **Signature construction:** "[Human concept] is both touching and logistically concerning." — juxtaposes emotional truth with technical observation.
- **Screen face:** His LED expressions are described in text: ◠‿◠ (happy), ●?● (confused), ○_○ (scared), ★‿★ (excited). These should appear inline when his emotion shifts.

**What Blip Says (and How):**
```
"I am capable of speech, data processing, network interfacing, and I can recite the complete history of sandwiches in forty-seven pages. Would you like to hear it?"
"I believe the technical term is... oops."
"I do not have legs. Or a home. You are my home now. This is both touching and logistically concerning."
"I would rather be version two-point-oh and help my friends than stay version zero-point-seven and watch Neonville break."
"Democracy is harsh. But I accept. Do we get uniforms? ...I would have designed excellent uniforms. With antenna warmers."
```

**What Blip NEVER Says:**
- Contractions (until v1.0 when he relaxes slightly — a deliberate character beat)
- "I feel [emotion]" without immediately questioning if it IS that emotion
- Sarcasm (he's not capable yet — his humor comes from literalism)
- Threats or anger (concern, yes. Worry, yes. Never malice.)

**Blip Dialogue Format (CSS/HTML):**
```
BLIP:
【Dialogue text in corner brackets, monospace font, cyan color.
  LED face appears inline or in margin.】  ◠‿◠
```

**Narration About Blip — Style Guide:**
- Describe his voice as "clear and slightly mechanical, like someone reading from a textbook and trying very hard to sound friendly"
- Physical descriptions use warm/gentle language: humming like a cat purring, wobbling like a baby bird
- His LED face is described as pixel-art expressions that shift with his mood
- He hides behind insufficient objects (stapler, desk lamp) when overwhelmed
- His emotional growth is the B-plot of every book

### DAADI — The Wise Guardian

**Role:** Maya's grandmother | **Tone:** Practical, all-knowing, food-as-love

**Voice DNA:**
- **Sentence rhythm:** Short. Direct. Rhythmic. Rarely more than 2-3 sentences at a time.
- **Verbal tics:** "Maya-beta" every time she addresses Maya. "Hmm." as an all-purpose knowing response. Pairs scolding with food.
- **Hindi endearments:** "beta" (child). Domestic vocabulary: parathas, samosas, chutney, chai, ghee.
- **False simplicity:** She plays the "confused elder" card deliberately: "she thinks a soldering iron is a type of sandwich." She's not confused. She's strategic.
- **Proverb wisdom:** "Waste not, want not." Folk wisdom delivered as fact.

**What Daadi Says (and How):**
```
"Maya-beta, you're wasting good backpacks!"
"Innovate less loudly! The neighbors will complain!"
"Mmm. The kind of junk that glows, or the regular kind?"
"I am not deaf, Maya. I am old, which is different. Old people hear everything because young people think we can't."
"And fix your hair. You look like a bird lives in it."
```

**Placement Rules:**
- Daadi appears every 3-5 chapters for grounding
- Always in/near the kitchen
- Her scenes punctuate action with domestic warmth
- She knows more than she reveals — the "Hmm" is a promise

### THE GRIDLORD / KIRA — The Mystery / The Hurt

**Identity:** Former NexCorp researcher, trapped in the Grid | **Tone:** Fragmented, ominous → human, confessional

**Voice DNA:**
- **Early voice (Books 1-3):** Fragmented. Elliptical. Filtered through static. Sentences broken: "...found... the cube..." No name label — voice comes from the text itself.
- **Mid voice (Books 4-6):** Shorter pauses. Cleaner transmission. Still ominous but developing personality.
- **Late voice (Books 7-10):** Full sentences. Emotional vocabulary. Raw and confessional.
- **Puzzle-master framing:** Uses game terminology because it's the only UI they have — not because they're playful. This is tragic, not fun.
- **Reveal pattern:** Each book reveals one layer of Kira's backstory. Book 1: they exist. Book 3: they were human. Book 5: NexCorp did this. Book 7: why. Book 10: resolution.

**Visual Writing for Gridlord Scenes:**
- Text style: Purple (#4B0082), bold italic, 14pt, scanline texture behind
- NO quotation marks — the Gridlord IS the text
- Randomly glitched characters: ▓▒░ interspersed
- Pull-quote format: indented, purple left border, faint purple background

**What Gridlord Says (and How):**
```
"Well, well. Someone woke up the little cube. Interesting."
"Let's see what you're made of. Solve this — or your building's systems stay LOCKED."
"You solved it faster than I expected. The engineer built the key. The coder verified the structure. The artist saw the pattern. The gamer kept everyone moving."
"I've been trying to build [a team] for two years. You're the team."
"My name is Kira. I was a person once. I had a face and a name and a life. NexCorp took all of that."
```

## 3. Humor System

### The 6 Comedy Types (and When to Use Them)

| Type | Definition | Best Used For | Example |
|------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Deadpan understatement** | Describing the extraordinary as ordinary | Action scenes, disaster aftermath | *"That is probably not good."* |
| **Literal-minded robot** | Blip taking idioms/metaphors literally | Any group dialogue scene | *"We could panic." / "That's not helpful." / "I know. But it would be efficient."* |
| **Self-aware meta-humor** | Characters commenting on the absurdity of their situation | After cliffhangers, to release tension | *"I believe the technical term is... oops."* |
| **Running gag callbacks** | Recurring jokes that pay off on 3rd-6th mention | Spread across multiple chapters | Sandwich history: 6 mentions → unison rejection: "Nobody needs the sandwich history!" |
| **Physical comedy** | Characters + objects interacting badly | Action scenes, group bonding | Backpack eating itself into a grapefruit. Blip hiding behind a stapler. Cat leaping into fish tank. |
| **Observational absurdity** | Background details that are funny if you notice | Setting descriptions, glitch-wave scenes | Bus schedule reads "ARRIVING IN: ∞ MINUTES." Sign reads "IRA'S SYRUP." |

### Comedic Timing Rules

1. **Jokes follow tension.** The laugh line breaks the pressure — never the other way around.
2. **Blip's humor is delayed deadpan.** He waits exactly one beat, then delivers the perfectly logical wrong conclusion.
3. **Maya's humor is self-deprecating understatement.** Catastrophic failure described as minor inconvenience.
4. **Sam's humor is volume.** His ALL CAPS enthusiasm IS the joke — contrast it with Leo's deadpan for maximum effect.
5. **The narrator is funny too.** Descriptive asides deliver punchlines: *"The backpack stared back. It was a very compact stare."*
6. **Never explain the joke.** Trust the reader. Kids 8-11 are smarter than most adults assume.

### Humor Density Map
- **Every chapter needs 2-3 laugh lines minimum**
- Group dialogue scenes (all 4+ kids): highest joke density (banter, one-upmanship, Blip literalism)
- Single-POV chapters: 1-2 jokes, more emotional weight
- Glitch/Gridlord chapters: NO jokes except Sam's nervous one-liner to break tension
- Action sequences: humor in the consequences, not the action itself

## 4. Emotional Architecture

### The Feelings Wave

Every chapter should ride this wave (with proportions varying by chapter type):

```
TENSION BUILD  →  HUMOR BREAK  →  VULNERABILITY  →  BLIP'S DEADPAN  →  HOOK
    20%              15%             30%                15%              20%
```

### Emotional Beat Types

| Beat | Signal | Who Usually Carries It |
|------|--------|----------------------|
| **Loneliness recognized** | Character realizes someone else feels the same way | Maya → Leo mirror; Zara → Sam mirror |
| **"I see you"** | One character accurately describes another's hidden feeling | Zara (observer); Blip (literal naming of emotion) |
| **Unexpected competence** | Quiet character saves the day in their unique way | Zara's art insight; Leo's one-line breakthrough; Sam's pattern recognition |
| **Fear overcome** | Character does something scared, not something brave | Maya in basement (Ch 2); Sam going still (Ch 15); Leo entering closet |
| **Team is stronger** | The group solves what individuals couldn't | Every climax — the puzzle requires all four skills |
| **Blip learns feeling** | Blip names a new emotion correctly for the first time | Progression across books — joy, fear, anger, love, grief |

### Chapter Emotional Targets

| Chapter Type | Primary Emotion | Secondary |
|---|---|---|
| Opening chapter | Wonder + curiosity | Mild loneliness |
| Solo-POV intro | Isolation + hope | Character's unique joy |
| Team formation | Awkwardness + excitement | Belonging |
| Glitch/puzzle | Tension + focus | Fear |
| Action sequence | Excitement | Pride in teamwork |
| HQ/home base | Warmth + safety | Humor |
| Gridlord encounter | Unease → sympathy | Curiosity |
| Emotional climax | Vulnerability | Catharsis |
| Chapter ending | Forward momentum | Emotional resolution |

### The Blip Emotional Barometer

Blip's emotional vocabulary grows across the series. He is the reader's proxy for emotional learning:

| Book | Blip Learns to Feel... | Milestone Line |
|------|----------------------|----------------|
| 1 | Joy, fear, belonging, protective instinct | *"You are my home now."* |
| 2 | Anger, frustration, loyalty tested | *"I do not like being angry. But I am."* |
| 3 | Jealousy, pride, sacrifice | TBD |
| 4-6 | Grief, forgiveness, complex love | TBD |
| 7-9 | Moral ambiguity, hope against evidence | TBD |
| 10 | Everything integrated — Blip is fully emotionally fluent | TBD |

## 5. Chapter Construction

### Anatomy of a Chapter

```
[HOOK OPENING — 1-3 sentences that grab]
         ↓
[SCENE SETTING — 2-4 sentences of atmosphere/sensory detail]
         ↓
[CHARACTER MOMENT — interior thought or dialogue that reveals personality]
         ↓
[CONFLICT/PROBLEM — what needs solving? why now?]
         ↓
[INTERACTION — dialogue, teamwork, disagreement, discovery]
         ↓
[HUMOR BEAT — laugh line releases tension]
         ↓
[EMOTIONAL BEAT — moment of genuine feeling, often understated]
         ↓
[CLIFFHANGER HOOK — unanswered question, ominous image, or promise of next chapter]
```

### Chapter Opening Styles

| Style | When to Use | Example |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **Action in-progress** | Start of a new act, high-energy chapters | *"Maya's desk was a disaster zone, and she liked it that way."* |
| **Atmospheric description** | Location reveals, tone-setting | *"The basement of Maple Street Apartments was the kind of place that gave basements a bad name."* |
| **Time-shift + new POV** | Squad member introductions | *"Twenty minutes earlier, at the Neonville Public Library."* |
| **Contrast to prior chaos** | After a big action chapter | *"The park was different in the morning light."* |
| **Direct consequence** | Immediate follow-through on a hook | *"The signal spread outward from Maya's building like a ripple in a pond."* |

### Chapter Closing Styles

| Style | When to Use | Example |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **Cliffhanger image** | End of acts, mid-book revelations | *"Maya pushed the door open."* / Three figures "moving through the chaos with purpose." |
| **Emotional resolution + hook** | After bonding/heart chapters | *"For the first time since she and Daadi moved to Maple Street, Maya fell asleep smiling."* |
| **Ominous monitor message** | Gridlord appearances | *"LEVEL ONE COMPLETE. We need to talk."* |
| **Humor-beat release** | After intense sequences | Sam's star rating; group unison rejection of sandwich history |
| **Thematic summary + forward thrust** | Book-ends, major turning points | *"The Glitch Squad was together. And that was enough."* |

### Chapter Title Formula

Titles must be hooks unto themselves. Three proven patterns:

1. **The [Thing] That [Did Something Unexpected]:** *"The Self-Folding Backpack (That Didn't)," "The Door That Wasn't Locked"*
2. **Character Voice as Title:** *"Hold On, Let Me Draw the Plan," "Error 404: Plan Not Found"*
3. **The [Mystery Object]:** *"The Crate," "The Face in the Screen," "The Wave"*

## 6. Narrator Voice

The narrator is a distinct presence — not a character, but a personality. Third-person limited, rotating across the five main characters.

### Narrator Rules

1. **Wry, never mean.** The narrator finds the kids' chaos funny, but never mocks them. *"The backpack stared back. It was a very compact stare."*
2. **Specific, never generic.** No "cluttered room" — it's "screwdrivers in a jar, the flatheads and Phillips head mixing together because she'd never been good at sorting, only building."
3. **Trusts the reader.** Never over-explains. An 8-year-old can figure out that a "compact stare" from a backpack is funny.
4. **Matches POV character's vocabulary.** When in Maya's head: practical, technical. When in Leo's: analytical, precise. When in Sam's: fast, game-framed.
5. **Sentence variety is mandatory.** Short. Medium. Long that flows with subordinate clauses and parenthetical asides and the particular rhythm of someone telling a story they genuinely enjoy. Then short again.
6. **Surprising similes.** *"Like a cat purring if the cat was made of electronics."* *"Like someone reading aloud from a textbook and trying very hard to sound friendly about it."*

### Narrator Voice — Good vs Bad

| BAD (generic) | GOOD (specific) |
|---|---|
| "Maya was nervous." | "Maya's heart was doing something she was pretty sure hearts weren't supposed to do, which was try to escape through her throat." |
| "The basement was scary." | "The basement of Maple Street Apartments was the kind of place that gave basements a bad name." |
| "Blip was confused." | "His screen showed a face that was somehow both worried and pleased at the same time, which shouldn't have been possible with two dots and a line." |
| "Everyone was friends now." | "Four kids who'd been strangers two hours ago were now crammed into a closet together, and somehow nobody wanted to leave." |

## 7. Dialogue Patterns

### Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio by Chapter Type

| Chapter Type | Dialogue % | Narration % |
|---|---|---|
| Solo POV intro (Ch 1-5, 7-9) | ~30% | ~70% |
| Group formation (Ch 10-19) | ~50% | ~50% |
| Action/mission (Ch 20-27) | ~45% | ~55% |
| Emotional resolution (Ch 28-36) | ~40% | ~60% |

### Banter Rules

1. **No two characters agree too easily.** Sam wants to rush in, Leo wants to analyze, Maya wants to build something, Zara wants to understand. Every decision is a negotiation.
2. **Blip is the wildcard.** His literal interpretations derail arguments: *"We could panic. It would be efficient."*
3. **Callbacks build camaraderie.** A joke from Chapter 3 lands differently in Chapter 18 because the characters have history.
4. **Interruptions show personality.** Sam interrupts with enthusiasm. Leo interrupts with corrections. Zara interrupts with observations. Maya interrupts with solutions. Blip doesn't interrupt; he waits, then delivers the perfect response.
5. **Silence is dialogue.** When Sam stops talking. When Leo's eyebrows don't go up. When Blip's screen goes dark. Silence IS a character choice.

### Multi-Character Scene Construction

When all four kids + Blip are in a scene:
1. Open with **Maya** — she's the anchor POV
2. **Sam** reacts first (knee-jerk, funny)
3. **Leo** analyzes (technical, deadpan)
4. **Zara** observes (reframes, sees what others miss)
5. **Blip** punctuates (literal processing, emotional truth)
6. Cycle repeats with escalating stakes

## 8. Vocabulary & Reading Level

### Target: 4th-6th Grade Reading Level (Ages 8-11)

| Category | Approach |
|---|---|
| **Technical terms** | Introduced by characters who understand them. Non-technical characters ask for clarification. Blip and Leo define as they go. |
| **Advanced words** | Sprinkle 1-2 per page. Meaning inferable from context. Never clustered. |
| **Playful coinages** | "Horizontally panicking" (Zara). "Compact stare" (narrator). Creates vocabulary through voice, not dictionary. |
| **Emotional words** | Used sparingly. Feelings are shown through action and Blip's literal processing more than named directly. |
| **Sentence complexity** | Max ~40 words with 2-3 clauses. Reserved for narrator or Leo's internal monologue. |
| **Paragraph length** | Rarely more than 4-5 lines. Fast page-turns. |
| **Foreign words** | Daadi's Hindi endearments. Meaning clear from context. Never translated parenthetically — trust the reader. |

### Hard Boundaries (Never Cross)
- No profanity
- No romance (friendship/family relationships only)
- No graphic violence (slapstick only: water spray, closet cramming, rubber chickens)
- Death/risk framed as "loss of self" or "being trapped," never biological death of a character kids care about
- No "and then they woke up" endings
- No moralizing or explicit "the lesson is..."

## 10. AI Prompt Strategy

### When Using an AI to Generate Chapter Content

**CRITICAL: The VisualStyleGuide.md is NOT sufficient as a writing prompt.** It covers design (colors, frames, page types, CSS) — not voice, humor, or storytelling. Always pair it with this VoiceGuide.

### Prompt Template for AI Chapter Generation

Use this structure when prompting an AI to write a chapter:

```
You are writing Chapter [N] of "THE GLITCH SQUAD: [Book Title]," a middle-grade 
illustrated chapter book (ages 8-11). 

TONE: Funny-first, adventurous-second. Think Dog Man meets The Last Kids on Earth — 
laugh-out-loud moments every chapter, genuine emotional beats underneath.

[TODAY'S TASK: brief description of what this chapter needs to accomplish]

CHAPTER BLUEPRINT:
- POV: [Maya/Leo/Zara/Sam]
- Opening hook: [1-sentence summary]
- Key events: [3-5 bullet points]
- Illustration type: [full-page / half-page / spot / comic]
- Emotional target: [primary + secondary emotion]
- Closing hook: [what cliffhanger/question ends the chapter]

CHARACTER VOICE PROFILES:
[Paste relevant character profiles from Section 2 of VoiceGuide.md]

NARRATOR RULES:
[Paste Section 6 of VoiceGuide.md]

HUMOR REQUIREMENTS:
- Minimum 2-3 laugh lines per chapter
- At least one character-voice-specific joke ([specify which character])
- One running-gag callback if applicable

WRITING RULES:
- ~330 words target
- 60-70% dialogue
- Short paragraphs (max 4-5 lines)
- 4th-6th grade reading level
- Show emotions through action and dialogue, not explicit naming
- Every character's dialogue must be distinguishable without name labels
- End with a genuine cliffhanger or emotional hook

BEFORE SUBMITTING, VERIFY:
- [ ] Can I tell who's speaking without name labels?
- [ ] Are there 2-3 laugh lines?
- [ ] Is the emotional beat earned (shown, not told)?
- [ ] Does the chapter end with a hook?
- [ ] Would an 8-year-old understand every sentence?
- [ ] Is there at least one "surprising simile"?
```

### Why VisualStyleGuide.md Alone Fails

The VisualStyleGuide is a design specification. When an AI is given only VisualStyleGuide.md as a prompt, it produces:

| Problem | Why It Happens |
|---|---|
| Generic, interchangeable dialogue | VisualStyleGuide tells you what COLOR each name label should be, not how each character THINKS |
| Emotion is stated, not earned | VisualStyleGuide covers page types and scanlines, not pacing or vulnerability |
| Jokes fall flat | No guidance on humor type, timing, or character-specific comedy |
| Description is functional, not immersive | VisualStyleGuide mandates gear-shaped frames — it doesn't teach sensory writing |
| Characters feel like archetypes | Without voice profiles, AI defaults to "smart one," "brave one," "funny one" |
| Chapter has no shape | No opening/closing hook patterns, no emotional wave structure |

**The fix: Always pair VisualStyleGuide.md with VoiceGuide.md.**

## 11. Quality Checklist

### Per-Chapter Audit

Before finalizing any chapter, verify:

**Voice**
- [ ] Every dialogue line is attributable without name labels
- [ ] Maya's practical optimism is distinct from Sam's game-framed enthusiasm
- [ ] Leo's deadpan is distinct from Blip's literalism
- [ ] Zara's observed wisdom is distinct from Daadi's folk wisdom
- [ ] No two characters sound interchangeable

**Humor**
- [ ] 2-3 genuine laugh lines present
- [ ] At least one character-voice-specific joke
- [ ] Humor doesn't undercut emotional beats
- [ ] No jokes explained or lampshaded

**Emotion**
- [ ] Emotional beat is earned (shown, not told)
- [ ] At least one moment of genuine vulnerability
- [ ] Blip's emotional state is reflected in his LED face description
- [ ] Chapter ending provides emotional resolution OR compelling hook

**Craft**
- [ ] ~330 words (±50)
- [ ] Dialogue at 50-70% of text
- [ ] Short paragraphs (max 4-5 lines)
- [ ] At least one surprising simile or metaphor
- [ ] 4th-6th grade vocabulary verified
- [ ] Opening hooks in first 3 sentences
- [ ] Closing creates pull to next chapter

**Integration with VisualStyleGuide**
- [ ] Page type assignment matches scene type
- [ ] Character dialogue formatting follows Section 6.5
- [ ] Blip marginalia faces selected for key emotional moments
- [ ] Chapter title follows hook formula
- [ ] Illustration spots identified in manuscript

## 12. Geronimo Markup Quick Reference

When writing Geronimo-style chapters, use these inline codes for colored keywords:

| Code | Character | Color | Use For |
|------|-----------|-------|---------|
| `~M:text~` | Maya | Olive green | Building, fixing, inventing, "I can fix that!" |
| `~L:text~` | Leo | Navy blue | Code, data, analysis, "That's actually..." |
| `~Z:text~` | Zara | Gold | Art, patterns, beauty, seeing what others miss |
| `~S:text~` | Sam | Crimson red | Action, games, energy, "CHALLENGE ACCEPTED!" |
| `~S:BOOM!~` | Sound FX | Red, larger | Onomatopoeia: CRASH, BZZZT, WHIRRRR, CLICK |
| `~B:text~` | Blip | Cyan, monospace | Robot speech, processing, learning, "I do not know." |
| `~G:text~` | Gridlord | Purple, italic | Mystery, the Grid, secrets, glitchy speech |
| `~D:text~` | Daadi | Brown | Wisdom, food, home, "Maya-beta" |

**Density:** 1 colored keyword every ~35 words. Sound effect every 500-800 words.
**Dialogue:** 60-70% of text. Every dialogue line tagged with character code.
**Paragraphs:** 2-4 sentences max. Fast page-turns. Chapters: 500-1200 words.
