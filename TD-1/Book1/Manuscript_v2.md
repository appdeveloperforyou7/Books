# THE GLITCH SQUAD — Book 1: THE LOST SIGNAL

---

## Chapter 1 — The Self-Folding Backpack (That Didn't)

Maya's desk was a disaster zone, and she liked it that way.

Screwdrivers in a jar — the flatheads and Phillips head mixing together because she'd never been good at sorting, only building. Wires in a tangle of red, blue, and copper that looked like spaghetti someone had electrified. Three half-built gadgets lined up like soldiers waiting for orders: one was definitely a radio, one might have been a toaster, and the third one she wasn't sure about anymore because she'd started it three months ago and couldn't find her original sketch.

A stack of notebooks sat at the back of the desk, so full of circuit diagrams and invention sketches that they bulged like they'd eaten too many batteries. The newest one — a bright orange notebook with a rocket ship sticker on the cover — was open to a page labeled "Self-Folding Backpack: FINAL DESIGN (for real this time)."

And in the middle of all of it: a backpack that was currently eating itself.

"Come on, come on, come ON," Maya muttered, poking the backpack with a screwdriver. The backpack responded by folding itself tighter, crumpling the nylon into a dense ball the size of a grapefruit. The motor she'd installed — scavenged from an old DVD player — whined pitifully and gave up with a sound like a sad trombone.

Maya stared at it.

The backpack stared back. It was a very compact stare.

"That's three this month," Maya said to no one. She talked to her inventions all the time. They never talked back, which she considered a one-sided relationship. "First the alarm clock that only went off on holidays. Then the automatic pencil sharpener that ate pencils. And now you." She poked the backpack one more time. "I really thought you were going to work."

She pulled off her safety goggles — they lived on her forehead more than her eyes — and scratched her head. Her messy ponytail had given up entirely hours ago, and a pencil she'd stuck in it earlier fell out and clattered onto the desk. She looked at the pencil. She looked at the backpack. She put the pencil back in her hair. You never knew when you'd need one.

The problem was simple: the folding motor was too powerful. She needed a smaller one — something with less torque, more control. The junk store on Fifth Street had closed early (the owner, Mr. Dominguez, closed whenever his cat seemed lonely, which was often). Online ordering took two days. Two days! Who had two days?

"Maya-beta, you're wasting good backpacks!" Daadi's voice drifted from the kitchen, accompanied by the smell of something wonderful frying. Ghee and onions and spices that made Maya's stomach growl even though she'd eaten an hour ago.

"I'm INNOVATING, Daadi!"

"Innovate less loudly! The neighbors will complain! And come eat something — you can't build on an empty stomach!"

Maya grinned. Daadi was right, of course. Daadi was always right. It was one of her most annoying and wonderful qualities.

Then Maya had an idea. It happened a lot — ideas arrived like unexpected guests, and she'd learned to open the door before they changed their minds. This one walked in with its bags already packed.

The motor in the old fan in the basement. The fan that broke last summer. The fan Daadi said she'd throw away but never did, because Daadi never threw anything away. "Waste not, want not," she always said, which was ironic because she also said "Maya, why do you have seventeen half-broken things in your room?" Seventeen was an exaggeration. It was at least twenty.

The basement.

Maya grabbed her flashlight — a heavy metal one that had belonged to her grandfather, with a beam strong enough to spot airplanes — squeezed past the stack of cardboard boxes in the hallway, and headed downstairs.

The stairs creaked under her feet. They always did, in the exact same order: third step, seventh step, ninth step. She'd memorized the pattern two years ago, when she and Daadi had moved into apartment 2B. Two years of this building, this neighborhood, this life. Two years of building things alone in her room because the kids at school thought she was weird for carrying a screwdriver in her pocket and talking to her lunchbox.

She didn't mind. Mostly.

## Chapter 2 — The Door That Wasn't Locked

The basement of Maple Street Apartments was the kind of place that gave basements a bad name.

Low ceiling with exposed pipes that groaned like they were telling ghost stories to each other. Bare bulbs in wire cages, half of them dead, the other half flickering like they couldn't decide. Rows of storage units behind wire mesh, full of things people couldn't fit in their apartments but couldn't bear to throw away — broken furniture, old exercise equipment with clothes hanging on it, seventeen boxes belonging to someone on the fourth floor who'd moved out three years ago and never came back for their stuff.

It smelled like concrete dust and old rain and the particular mustiness of things that had been forgotten on purpose.

Maya knew this place well. She'd been scavenging parts from it for two years, ever since she and Daadi moved in. The building manager, Mr. Podowski, didn't mind. "Take what you want," he'd said, shrugging his enormous shoulders. "Nobody else is using it. Nobody else even goes down there." He'd looked at her flashlight and her utility vest and added, "You be careful, though. Old buildings have old secrets."

She'd thought he was being dramatic. Now she wasn't so sure.

She found the fan quickly — third shelf, behind a broken lamp and a box labeled "KITCHEN STUFF (FRAGILE)" that was definitely not fragile kitchen stuff based on the rattling. She cracked the fan open with her screwdriver. The motor was perfect. Small, steady, and probably still worked — it had been a good fan before the blade snapped.

But something else caught her eye.

In the far corner, past the water heater that clanked like it was arguing with itself, behind a stack of paint cans so old their labels had faded to ghosts — a door.

Maya had been in this basement a hundred times. Maybe two hundred. She knew every storage unit, every corner, every squeaky pipe. She had never noticed a door there.

It was the same color as the wall — a greige so indifferent that it seemed to actively resist being noticed. The handle was brass, tarnished to the color of an old penny, with a keyhole that had been painted over so many times it was sealed shut. But the handle itself was loose. Not locked.

She should go back upstairs. The fan motor was in her hand. The backpack was waiting. Daadi was making something that smelled incredible. This was the sensible choice.

Maya pushed the door open.

A staircase. Going down.

The stairs were narrower than the ones she'd just descended, and steeper, and made of concrete that crumbled slightly at the edges. The air that came up was different — cooler, drier, with a faint electrical smell, like the air near a power station.

"Definitely not the fan," she whispered.

Every instinct in Maya's body — the one that said "curiosity first, consequences never" — pulled her forward. She clicked on her grandfather's flashlight and descended.

Twelve steps. A turn. Six more steps. The beam of her flashlight swept the walls — concrete, then brick, then concrete again. Old. Much older than the building upstairs. And at the bottom:

A metal shelf. On it, a grey plastic crate about the size of a shoebox. Stamped on the side in faded blue letters that her flashlight brought back to life:

**NEXCORP RESEARCH**
**PROJECT BLIP**
**DO NOT ACTIVATE**

Maya read it twice. The second time, out loud, tasting each word.

"Do not activate" was, in Maya's experience, the most exciting phrase in the English language. It was the linguistic equivalent of a wrapped present with a tag that said "Open Never." It was practically an invitation.

She looked back up the stairs. She looked at the crate.

She reached for it.

## Chapter 3 — The Crate

The lab — because that's what it was, even though it looked like it had been abandoned since before Maya was born — was small. Maybe twelve feet by ten. A workbench against one wall, its surface covered in dust so thick it was like grey velvet, untouched fingerprints preserved in it like fossils. A faded safety poster showing someone wearing goggles incorrectly, with the caption "PROPER EYE PROTECTION IS NO ACCIDENT" in letters that had once been bright yellow. A stool with three legs and a wobble that suggested the fourth leg had given up long ago. A power strip with nothing plugged into it, its indicator light dark.

The walls were concrete, bare except for a few brackets where shelves had once hung. The ceiling was low enough that Maya could touch it if she stood on her toes. Two ventilation grates, both caked with dust. One electrical junction box with a warning label she couldn't read in the dim light.

And on the shelf: the crate.

Maya dragged it down. It was lighter than she expected — whatever was inside didn't weigh much, or the crate itself was made of something unusually light. The lid was held on with four metal clips that hadn't been opened in a long time. She pried them with her screwdriver, one by one. Each one snapped open with a sound like cracking knuckles, and a little puff of dust escaped with each one.

Inside, nestled in foam that crumbled at the edges like ancient bread: a white cube.

About four inches on each side. Rounded edges, smooth surface, the color of fresh paper. A slight scuff mark on one corner, like it had been dropped at some point. On the front face: a small screen, dark and blank, about two inches square. No power button. No switches. No visible way to turn it on. No seams, no screws, no obvious way to open it either.

Maya picked it up. It was warm. Not battery-heat warm — not the kind of warmth you get from electronics that have been running. This was more like it had been sitting in sunlight, except they were two floors underground and there was no sunlight. She turned it over in her hands. The surface was perfectly smooth except for the screen, which had a slight recession, like a window set into a wall.

She shook it gently. Nothing rattled.

She tapped the screen. Nothing happened.

She held it up to her ear. Silence. Not even a hum.

She brought it close to her flashlight. The screen reflected the beam back at her, and for a moment she thought she saw something — a flicker, the tiniest glimmer of blue — but it was gone before she could be sure.

"Huh," she said.

It was the most interesting "huh" she'd ever said.

She put it in her vest pocket — the big one that usually held spare batteries and occasionally a snack — grabbed the fan motor, and climbed back upstairs. As she passed through the basement storage area, she noticed that the door she'd found was already closing behind her, as if it wanted to go back to not being noticed.

Daadi was in the kitchen, making chai. The cardamom steam fogged up the window above the sink, and the pot was singing that particular note that meant almost-ready. The kitchen was warm and bright and safe, and it smelled like home.

"What did you find down there?" Daadi asked without turning around. Daadi always knew everything. It was her superpower. She could hear Maya thinking from two rooms away.

"Just some junk."

"Mmm. The kind of junk that glows, or the regular kind?"

Maya checked her pocket. The cube wasn't glowing. "Regular kind."

Daadi hummed in a way that meant she didn't believe a single word but loved Maya anyway. She poured chai into two cups and slid one across the counter.

"Drink. Then tell me about the regular junk."

Maya drank. The chai was perfect. Daadi's chai was always perfect — hot enough to warm your hands through the cup, sweet enough without being too sweet, with that cardamom kick at the end. Maya held the cup with both hands and let the steam wash over her face.

"It's just a cube," she said. "White. Has a screen but won't turn on. I think it's broken."

"Broken things are your specialty, Maya-beta."

"Yeah." Maya smiled into her cup. "They are."

## Chapter 4 — Maya and the Toothpaste

The rest of the evening went the way evenings at Maya's house always went.

Daadi made samosas. Not the frozen kind — the real kind, with dough she rolled by hand and filling she'd been perfecting for fifty years. The kitchen filled with the sound of oil popping and the smell of cumin and potatoes, and by the time Daadi called her to the table, Maya was so hungry she could have eaten the table itself.

She ate four samosas and burned her tongue on the first one, which was a personal record. The burn was worth it. Daadi's samosas were worth any amount of tongue-burn.

They watched half a cooking show before Daadi fell asleep on the couch, her reading glasses sliding down her nose, the TV remote still in her hand. Maya carried a blanket over from the armchair and draped it over Daadi's shoulders. Daadi always said she wasn't tired and then fell asleep and then said she'd just been "resting her eyes." Every single time.

Maya stood in the doorway of the living room for a moment, watching her grandmother sleep. Daadi's face was softer when she was asleep — the lines around her eyes smoothed out, her mouth relaxed into a small, content smile. She'd moved halfway around the world to raise Maya when Maya's parents' work had taken them overseas. She never complained about it. Not once. She just showed up, took off her shoes, and started making the apartment smell like home.

"I'm going to fix you something amazing," Maya whispered to her sleeping grandmother. "Something that'll make you proud. I don't know what yet. But I'm working on it."

Daadi snored in response, which Maya chose to interpret as encouragement.

She went to her room. She set the white cube on her desk next to a pile of resistors and a broken alarm clock she was definitely going to fix someday. She looked at it. It looked back in the way that inanimate objects don't — with its blank screen and its smooth white surface and its stubborn refusal to do anything interesting.

She sat on her bed and stared at it for a full minute.

"You're from NexCorp," she told it. "I've heard of them. They built the smart traffic lights downtown. And the delivery drones that everyone complains about. And something else, I think, but I can't remember." She tilted her head. "You don't look like a traffic light."

She brushed her teeth. The toothpaste was running low — she had to squeeze the tube from the very end, rolling it up tight. She made a mental note to tell Daadi, knowing she'd forget, knowing Daadi would notice anyway and buy a new tube without saying anything.

She changed into pajamas — the ones with the tiny robots on them, a gift from Daadi that Maya pretended were too childish but secretly loved. The robots had little speech bubbles that said "BEEP BOOP" and "I LOVE YOU" and for some reason "POTATO." She'd never understood the potato one. She loved it.

She got into bed. The sheets were cool. Her pillow was cool. The room was dark except for the amber glow of the street light through the curtains. She looked at the cube on her desk. Dark. Dead. Probably just a paperweight with a cool label.

In the dark, she talked to it anyway.

"I've never had anyone to build stuff with," she said quietly, pulling the blanket up to her chin. "Daadi tries, but she thinks a soldering iron is a type of sandwich. And the kids at school think I'm weird because I carry a multimeter in my backpack." She paused. "Okay, maybe the multimeter is a little weird. But it's useful! You never know when you're going to need to measure voltage."

She rolled onto her side, facing the cube.

"I'm going to fix you tomorrow. I can fix anything. At least, that's what I tell myself." A yawn. "Some things can't be fixed, probably. But I haven't found one yet. And you don't look that complicated. You're just a cube."

She yawned again, longer this time.

"Goodnight, cube."

She closed her eyes.

At 2:17 AM, Maya mumbled something in her sleep. A single word: "help."

It was the kind of thing people say in their sleep all the time — a fragment of a dream, a sound without meaning. But the cube heard it. Or rather, detected it. A word it recognized. A word that was in its core programming, buried under two years of silence and dust and darkness, written into its most fundamental code by someone who had wanted this little machine to respond to exactly that word.

Something inside the cube clicked on.

A single pixel of blue light appeared on the dark screen.

And then another.

And then another.

And then the screen filled with light, soft and blue and alive, pulsing gently in the dark room like a heartbeat finding its rhythm after a long sleep.

## Chapter 5 — System Initialized

Maya woke up because her room was glowing.

Not sunlight-through-the-window glowing. Not I-left-the-lamp-on-again glowing. This was a soft blue luminescence coming from her desk, pulsing gently, casting pale light on the ceiling that shifted in waves like water. It was beautiful in the way that impossible things are beautiful — it didn't belong here, in her messy bedroom with her half-built inventions and her robot pajamas, and that made it more wonderful.

She sat up. Rubbed her eyes. Pinched her arm. The glow was still there.

The cube's screen was on. It showed two blinking dots and a curved line. A face. A simple, pixel-art face, the kind you'd see on a Tamagotchi or a retro video game — two circular eyes and a mouth that curved upward in a smile that was trying very hard to be friendly.

The cube was humming. A low, gentle sound, like a cat purring if the cat was made of electronics and had been sitting in a basement for two years. It was also warm again — warmer than before — and it was no longer sitting on the desk. It was floating. Hovering about two inches above the surface, wobbling slightly, like it was learning how to be in the world.

Then it spoke.

"System initialized."

The voice was clear and slightly mechanical, with a quality like someone reading aloud from a textbook and trying very hard to sound friendly about it. It was polite. It was earnest. It was the most incredible thing Maya had ever heard.

"Location: unknown. Where am I, and why does everything smell like old soup?"

Maya screamed.

Then she clapped her hand over her mouth because Daadi was sleeping in the next room and if Maya woke her up there would be QUESTIONS and Daadi's questions were like treasure hunts — they always led somewhere you didn't expect.

"Apologies for the volume of my response," the cube said, its pixel-face shifting to what might have been a concerned expression. "I did not intend to cause distress. I am unused to... being awake. How long was I inactive?"

Maya's heart was hammering. She reached out and poked the cube. It wobbled. Her finger left a small smudge on its otherwise pristine surface. It was definitely real. Definitely warm. Definitely floating. Definitely talking.

"You — you can TALK?"

"Affirmative. I am capable of speech, data processing, network interfacing, and I can recite the complete history of sandwiches in forty-seven pages. Would you like to hear it?"

"What? No. Maybe. Wait — what ARE you?"

The cube's screen flickered. The face changed — the dots got bigger, as if it was trying to look surprised. The smile wobbled.

"Designation: BLIP. Buildable Logical Intelligence Platform. Version zero-point-seven. I am..." A pause. The screen showed a loading animation — three dots, cycling. "I am not fully updated. Some of my files appear to be... missing."

"What does that mean?"

"It means I do not know what I am for." The face shifted to what might have been a hopeful expression — the eyes got slightly bigger, the smile slightly wider. "I know I am supposed to be for something. I can feel it, like an itch I cannot scratch. Do you know what I am for?"

Maya stared at the little cube with its flickering screen and its hopeful pixel eyes and its earnest, mechanical voice, and felt something she hadn't felt in a long time. Not curiosity — she felt curiosity all the time. This was different. This was responsibility. This was the feeling of being needed by something that couldn't need you unless you chose to let it.

"I don't know," she said. "But I can find out."

She reached out and put one finger on the cube's warm surface. It hummed under her touch, a vibration that she felt all the way up her arm. The screen face changed to a brighter smile.

Then Blip's antenna — a thin metal stalk on top of the cube that Maya hadn't noticed before — perked up like a dog hearing a whistle.

"I am detecting a signal," Blip said, his voice quickening slightly. "It appears to be... originating from me. This was not intentional. I do not know how to stop it. Should I be concerned?"

A ring of cyan light burst outward from Blip — not a flash, but a ripple, like dropping a stone into a pond made of light. It passed through Maya's bedroom wall like it wasn't there, through the furniture, through her, leaving a tingling warmth in its wake, and rippled outward into the night.

Her digital watch — the modified one with the extra antenna she'd soldered on herself — went haywire. The display flickered wildly and showed the time as 88:88. The alarm clock on her desk started ticking backward. Her desk lamp flickered on, then off, then on again.

"That," Maya said slowly, "is probably not good."

"I agree," said Blip. "That is probably not good. But I must also say: that was the first thing I have ever done on purpose. So there is a small part of me that is pleased, even though the larger part is extremely worried."

Maya looked at the floating robot. The floating robot looked at Maya. Its screen showed a face that was somehow both worried and pleased at the same time, which shouldn't have been possible with two dots and a line.

"Can you undo it?"

"I do not know. I did not know I could DO it. This is a very educational evening. For both of us, I suspect."

## Chapter 6 — The Wave

The signal spread outward from Maya's building like a ripple in a pond, except the pond was Maple Street, and the ripple was made of pure digital chaos.

At the corner of Maple and Fifth, the traffic lights decided that green, yellow, and red were boring and would much prefer to cycle through magenta, cyan, and a shade of pink that doesn't exist in nature. Cars stopped. Drivers stared. A taxi driver got out, looked up at the lights, got back in his taxi, and decided to take the long way home. A woman on a bicycle rode through the intersection with her eyes closed, which was either very brave or very foolish or both.

In apartment 3B, Mrs. Patil's indoor sprinkler system — installed last year as part of the building's "smart home" upgrade that nobody had asked for — activated. Inside her living room. While she was watching a cooking show. The cat, who had been sleeping peacefully on the bookshelf, leaped five feet into the air and landed in the fish tank. The fish were not consulted about this. Neither was the cat, who emerged looking betrayed and significantly wetter.

Every digital photo frame in the building suddenly displayed the same image: a cat wearing a tiny hat. It was not anyone's cat. It was not anyone's hat. The image was inexplicably charming. Mrs. Kowalski on the second floor said the cat looked like her grandmother. Nobody knew what to do with that information. Mr. Chen on the first floor said the hat looked like his. Nobody knew what to do with that either.

At the smart garden in Maple Street Park, the automated watering system activated all eight sprinklers at once, creating a water feature that the Parks Department had definitely not approved. Two teenagers who had been sitting on the bench got soaked. One of them posted it online immediately with the caption "nature is healing." It got three hundred likes in seven minutes.

The bodega on the corner — Mira's, which had the best empanadas in a six-block radius — saw its neon sign flicker. The "M" and "A" went dark. The sign now read "IRA'S SYRUP." Mira came outside, looked at the sign, went back inside, and called her sister. "The sign is broken again," she said. "It says 'syrup' now. No, I don't know why. No, I'm not selling syrup."

Overhead, delivery drone #47-Delta — call sign "Speedy" according to the label on its side — lost all navigational data and began performing elaborate aerial loops over Maple Street, scattering packages like a confused mail-plane. A package containing a set of encyclopedias landed on someone's balcony with a thud. A package containing a single rubber chicken landed on the basketball court with a solemn BONK. A package containing a birthday cake landed, miraculously, right-side-up on a park bench, completely intact. The birthday girl, who had been sitting on the next bench over, took it as a sign.

And Maya, standing at her window with a floating robot cube hovering next to her, watched it all.

The street below her was a symphony of confusion — lights flashing, water spraying, people pointing and shouting and laughing. From her second-floor window, it almost looked intentional. Like art.

"I believe the technical term is..." Blip's screen showed an expression of intense calculation — his loading animation cycling rapidly. "...oops."

Maya turned to look at him. "Can you undo it?"

"I do not know. I did not know I could DO it. The signal was involuntary, like a sneeze, except instead of a sneeze it was a city-wide digital disruption. This is a very educational evening."

Down on the street, she could see people coming out of their buildings, looking around at the chaos with expressions ranging from confused to amused to annoyed. Someone was filming the sprinklers. Someone else was chasing their dog, whose LED collar was flashing like a disco ball. A group of kids had figured out that the rubber chicken made a satisfying sound when you squeezed it and were taking turns.

Then Maya noticed something else. Three figures, moving through the chaos with purpose. Not confused. Not scared. Moving with the kind of determination that said they knew exactly what they were looking for, and they were getting closer.

Coming toward her building.

## Chapter 7 — The Kid with the Tablet

Twenty minutes earlier, at the Neonville Public Library.

Leo Reyes sat alone at a table near the back, where the Wi-Fi signal was strongest and the librarian's line of sight was weakest. His tablet glowed in front of him, displaying lines of code that would have looked like gibberish to anyone else but made perfect sense to him.

Project Tamales. A homework tracker he was building for his older sister, Sofia, who was in high school and couldn't organize her way out of a paper bag. He called it Project Tamales because his abuela made the best tamales and this project was, in Leo's estimation, similarly structured — lots of layers, needs to be assembled just right, excellent when finished. Also, thinking about tamales made him less frustrated when the code didn't work.

He was debugging the notification system — the algorithm that would remind Sofia which assignments were due and when — when every screen in the library flickered.

Not just his tablet. The catalog terminals. The digital display boards. The self-checkout kiosks. Even the little screen on the vending machine that usually said "ENJOY YOUR SNACK" now said "ENJ0Y YOUR SNNNAAAACK" and then displayed a tiny pixelated cat face, and then went back to "ENJOY YOUR SNACK" as if nothing had happened.

Most people in the library looked up, looked around, and went back to what they were doing. People in Neonville were used to technology being occasionally weird. It was a "smart city" — every intersection, every building, every system was connected. Weird was the price of living in the future.

But Leo wasn't most people.

His tablet had captured the signal. A clean, powerful, omnidirectional burst on the 2.4 GHz band. Short — less than a third of a second. But packed with more data than anything Leo had ever seen come from a residential area. It was like someone had compressed a library into a single flash of radio wave.

He pulled up the signal analysis. His eyebrows went up. His eyebrows did not go up often — Leo's face had a limited but expressive range, and raised eyebrows were reserved for genuinely remarkable occurrences, as opposed to merely existing, which Leo found most things to be.

This signal was structured. Artificial. And it had a source.

Leo tapped his tablet, triangulated the origin point using the signal's strength across three different receiver points (his tablet, the library's Wi-Fi, and, surprisingly, the vending machine's Bluetooth), and got an address: an apartment building on Maple Street. Seven minutes away if he ran.

He looked at his code. He looked at the signal data. He looked at his code again.

He packed his tablet into his hoodie's front pocket — he'd cut a headphone-cable hole in it last summer so he could listen to music without the cord getting caught on doorknobs, one of his better modifications — pushed his chair back, and headed for the door.

"Leo, the library is closing early," Ms. Chen called from behind the desk. "Technical difficulties."

Leo didn't hear her. He was already through the doors and running, his navy blue beanie pulled low and his reading glasses bouncing against his chest on their chain. The evening air was cool on his face and the signal data was still warm in his tablet, and somewhere out there was a source of something that shouldn't exist, and Leo Reyes had never been able to resist something that shouldn't exist.

He'd learned to code because his sister's homework was a mess and he wanted to help. He'd learned to build circuits because his abuela's radio broke and she couldn't afford a new one. He'd learned to keep to himself because kids at school thought he was weird for preferring a tablet to a soccer ball. He didn't mind. Mostly.

He stood outside the building seven minutes later, breathing hard, looking up at a second-floor window where a faint blue light pulsed like a heartbeat.

"Found you," he said.

## Chapter 8 — The Girl with the Camera

Zara Okafor was walking home from the community center with her yellow crossbody bag over one shoulder and a camera around her neck when the street lights on Maple Street started doing something street lights should not do.

They were cycling through colors. Not the normal red-yellow-green of traffic management, but shimmering — pink, turquoise, gold — like someone had installed a light show and forgotten to invite the neighborhood. The colors reflected off puddles and windows and the chrome bumpers of parked cars, painting the street in shifting rainbows.

Most people were confused. Some were annoyed. One man stood in the middle of the sidewalk with his arms crossed, staring upward as if personally offended by the audacity of the lights.

Zara was delighted.

She raised her camera and started shooting. Click. The traffic lights spinning — she caught the exact moment when turquoise shifted to gold, the two colors bleeding together like watercolors. Click. Mrs. Chen's indoor sprinklers visible through her window, the water catching the colored light and turning into something that looked like a fountain in a fairy tale. Click. A delivery drone doing an impressive barrel roll overhead, a package dangling from its claw like a sad piñata, the city lights reflected on its metal surface.

Zara photographed everything. Not because she was documenting a disaster — because she was seeing something beautiful in it. She always saw things that way. Where other people saw a mess, she saw a pattern. Where they saw chaos, she saw composition. Her moms said she'd been like that since she was three, when she'd arranged her vegetables by color instead of eating them.

The chaos had a rhythm. The colors were cycling in waves, spreading outward from a single point, like paint drops in water. Each wave had a slightly different timing — pink was fastest, gold was slowest, turquoise pulsed in the middle — and together they created a visual beat that Zara could almost hear.

She followed the ripples backward, the way you'd trace a river to its source. Her camera clicked as she walked — the smart garden overflowing, which looked like a water sculpture; the bodega's neon sign flashing SYRUP instead of MIRA'S, which was objectively funny; the digital bus schedule showing ARRIVING IN: ∞ MINUTES, which was either a glitch or the most honest thing a bus schedule had ever displayed.

The trail led to an apartment building. The same one a boy in a navy blue beanie was standing in front of, staring at a second-floor window. He was younger than her, compact, with intense eyes and a tablet in his hoodie pocket. He smelled like computer chips and Doritos, which was a surprisingly specific scent that Zara filed away for later.

"You traced the signal too?" he asked without looking at her.

"What signal? I'm taking PICTURES." She held up her camera.

Leo looked at her for the first time. "You photographed the ripple pattern?"

"Every frame."

"That's actually useful."

Zara smiled. "I know."

Before they could say anything else, a blur of red gloves and energy rocketed past them and through the building's front door.

"CHALLENGE ACCEPTED!" a voice shouted from inside.

Leo and Zara looked at each other.

"Should we follow?" Zara asked.

"We should definitely follow," Leo said.

They went inside.

## Chapter 9 — The Kid Who Thought It Was a Game

Sam Nakamura had been at the community center's weekly gaming tournament when the screens glitched. All twelve of them, simultaneously, displaying the same pattern of colored static before snapping back to normal like nothing had happened.

Everyone else groaned about the technical difficulties. Sam's eyes lit up.

"THAT," he announced to no one in particular, "was NOT a glitch. That was a SIGNAL. This is an ARG. An alternate reality game. And I just found it."

He said things like this a lot. Sam saw the world as a game — not because he didn't take it seriously, but because games had rules he understood. Real life was messier. In a game, you knew what you were supposed to do. You knew when you won. You knew who the good guys were. Real life didn't come with a tutorial, and Sam had never been great at things without tutorials.

He'd been gaming since he was five, when his older brother Kenji had handed him a controller and said, "Here, you try." Sam had beaten the first level in three minutes. Kenji had stared at him and said, "That took me two weeks." Sam had said, "Can I do the next one?"

Now, at ten years old, Sam was the best gamer at the community center. Possibly the best gamer in the neighborhood. He'd won three local tournaments and placed second in a regional one (he still maintained the winner had cheated, which no one believed but Sam). But being the best gamer at the community center wasn't the same as having friends. People liked watching him play. They didn't always like talking to him.

"Too much," his mom said sometimes, affectionately but also a little tired. "You're a lot, Sammy."

He knew what she meant. He was loud. He was fast. He never stopped moving, never stopped talking, never stopped thinking about the next level. Some kids found that exhausting. Sam found it exhausting that they found it exhausting.

But a SIGNAL? An ARG? This was different. This was a game that wanted to be found. A game that was FINDING HIM.

He abandoned his tournament (he was winning anyway — twenty kills, zero deaths, the other players had started calling him "the ghost"), grabbed his game cartridge backpack, and sprinted out the door.

Sam did not walk places. Walking was for people who had somewhere to be and no rush to get there. Sam was ALWAYS in a rush. He ran, jumped, vaulted, climbed, and occasionally slid. His LED sneakers flashed in patterns he'd programmed himself — the default was boring; his custom setting pulsed to the rhythm of his footsteps, which made running feel like dancing.

He followed the glitch trail through Maple Street, doing a parkour vault over a park bench (scuffing his knee pad — his mom was going to kill him, but that was a future-Sam problem), dodging a sprinkler that was watering the sidewalk instead of the garden, and ducking under a low-flying delivery drone that was doing loop-the-loops like it had forgotten it was supposed to deliver packages.

He arrived at the apartment building at full speed, passed two kids standing outside who clearly didn't understand the urgency of the situation, and burst through the front door.

The hallway lights were flickering in a pattern. Left-right-left-left-right. Sam recognized it instantly.

"That's binary!" he said to the empty hallway, grinning so wide his missing front tooth was on full display. "Left is zero, right is one. Zero-one-zero-zero-one... that's a letter! It's spelling something!"

He followed the lights up the stairs, reading as he went: F... O... U... R... T... H... FLOOR... SECOND... DOOR... ON... LEFT.

He stopped in front of apartment 2B. The door was slightly open. A blue glow leaked from inside like sunlight through a crack in the curtains.

Sam pushed the door open and found himself in a cluttered bedroom full of gadgets, tools, half-built inventions, and a girl with a messy ponytail staring at him like he'd just broken into her house.

Which, technically, he had.

"Hi!" Sam said, barely able to contain himself. "I'm Sam. I'm here for the game. What level are we on?"

## Chapter 10 — Four Strangers and a Cube

Maya's evening had officially become the strangest evening of her life, and she'd once accidentally set off the fire alarm with a homemade rocket. (The rocket had worked. The fire alarm part had been unintentional. The fire department had been understanding. Daadi had been less understanding.)

Her room, which was normally only big enough for her and her inventions, now contained:
- Maya (standing protectively in front of Blip, arms crossed)
- A boy in a navy blue beanie holding a tablet, who'd introduced himself as Leo and immediately asked what frequency the signal was on
- A tall girl with amazing hair and a camera, who'd introduced herself as Zara and immediately started photographing everything, including the inside of Maya's closet
- A short kid in red gloves who'd introduced himself as Sam and immediately tried to poke Blip

Blip, for his part, had retreated behind Maya's stapler.

"There are... many people in this room," Blip said, his voice dropping to what was probably his version of a whisper. "Is this normal? I do not have enough data on human social behavior to determine if four strangers appearing in your bedroom at midnight is typical."

"It is NOT typical," Maya said.

"I traced the signal using triangulation from three different places," Leo said, holding up his tablet. The screen showed a waveform graph that looked like a seismograph during an earthquake. "My tablet, the library's Wi-Fi, and — this is weird — the vending machine's Bluetooth. The signal came from this room. Specifically from..." He looked at Blip, who was trying to hide behind a stapler despite being a floating cube. "From that. What IS that?"

"His name is Blip," Maya said firmly. "And he's not a 'that.'"

"A real robot!" Sam's eyes were the size of dinner plates. "In a real room! In a real apartment! This is the BEST thing that has ever happened! And I once won a gaming tournament with a broken controller!"

"I photographed the ripple pattern of the glitch wave," Zara said, scrolling through her camera. She turned the screen so everyone could see. "Look — the spread is geometric, not random. It radiated from this building like..." She paused, finding the right words. "Like a pebble dropped in a pond. Each ripple has a consistent interval. Point-seven-three seconds."

Leo looked at her camera, then at his tablet, then back at her camera. "You measured the interval?"

"I photographed it. The timestamps are in the metadata."

"That's... actually precise."

"Thank you."

"I'm sorry," Maya said, crossing her arms tighter. "Who ARE you people?"

Introductions happened fast and badly. Leo explained that he traced the signal using triangulation from three receiver points. Zara explained that she followed the visual trail of glitch effects from the park to the building. Sam explained that this was clearly an ARG — an alternate reality game — and he was here to find the next level and also could someone please tell him where the quest giver was.

Maya explained that this was her room and they were in it without permission.

"So let me get this straight," Leo said, pushing his glasses up his nose. "You found a robot in a secret lab under your building, turned it on, and it accidentally broke every screen on the street."

"That is accurate," Blip confirmed, floating cautiously out from behind the stapler. "Although I prefer the term 'unexpectedly activated' rather than 'broke.' The distinction feels important for my self-esteem."

"And now the four of us are in your bedroom at midnight," Zara said.

"Three of you are in my bedroom," Maya corrected. "I LIVE here."

"And what are we supposed to do now?" Leo asked.

Blip's screen showed a thinking face — the dots furrowed, the line went flat. "I have a suggestion."

"We could panic."

"That's not helpful, Blip."

"I know. But it would be efficient."

Before anyone could argue further, Blip's screen turned purple. His antenna went rigid. His whole body stopped hovering and hung perfectly still in the air, like someone had pressed pause.

"I am detecting an anomaly," Blip said, his voice quieter than before. "Someone is... watching us."

Everyone went quiet.

The old monitor in the corner of Maya's room — the one she used as a second screen for her laptop, the one that was currently turned OFF and had been off all evening — flickered to life.

Nobody had touched it.

## Chapter 11 — What Do We Do with a Robot?

The screen showed static for three seconds. Then nothing. Then static again. It pulsed like a heartbeat — on, off, on, off — in a rhythm that matched Blip's earlier glow.

"I don't like this," Maya said.

"Agreed," Leo said, already scanning the monitor's connection ports with his tablet. "This monitor isn't connected to Wi-Fi. Or Bluetooth. Or anything. I checked when I came in — it's a standalone display. Someone is pushing a signal directly into the hardware."

"That's not possible," Maya said.

"Something that was literally impossible is currently happening," Leo pointed out. He had a point.

Sam was the only one not panicking. He was standing right in front of the monitor, head tilted, studying the static pattern like it was a loading screen. "This is the part where the NPC gives us a quest," he said confidently. "Just wait."

Zara, meanwhile, was sketching. Of course she was. She'd pulled out her sketchpad and was drawing the static pattern — the way it pulsed, the shapes within it, the colors. Because when Zara was scared, she drew. It was how she processed the world.

"We should call someone," Zara said without looking up. "An adult."

"An adult?" Sam made a face like she'd suggested he eat broccoli. "Adults ruin everything. They'll take Blip away and put him in a lab."

"That is... very kind," Blip said. "I do not know what a lab is, but your voice suggests it is somewhere I would not enjoy. Also, your idea of a lab is probably different from a scientist's idea of a lab, and I am not sure which one concerns me more."

"You wouldn't enjoy it," Maya confirmed. "Trust me."

A knock at the door made everyone freeze.

"Maya-beta?" Daadi's voice, muffled through the door. "Are you awake? The sprinklers went crazy. My good sarees got wet. Are you okay in there?"

Four kids stared at each other in panic.

"Fine, Daadi! Everything's fine! Go back to bed!"

"Myself! I talk to myself! You know that!"

Maya turned back to the others. "We need to be quiet. And you all need to hide."

"Hide WHERE?" Leo said, looking around the cramped room. There was exactly one hiding spot and it was the closet.

Maya pointed at the closet.

"I am NOT getting in a closet," Leo said.

"Would you prefer Daadi finds four strangers in her granddaughter's bedroom at midnight?"

Leo got in the closet.

Four kids and a robot crammed into a closet that was designed for approximately one person's winter coats. Sam's elbow was in Leo's ribs. Zara's camera was pressed against Maya's ear. Blip floated near the top shelf, his screen showing a loading bar — he was buffering from the stress.

"This is not optimal," Blip whispered.

"This is SO not optimal," Sam whispered back.

Nobody pointed out that he wasn't Blip.

They stayed in the closet for four minutes. Maya could hear Daadi's footsteps approaching, the rattle of a cup and saucer, the creak of her bedroom door opening just enough for a hand to appear with a steaming cup of chai. Then the door closing, the footsteps retreating, and silence.

The four of them emerged from the closet looking like they'd been through a washing machine. Sam's hair was flat on one side. Leo had a coat hanger hooked on his ear. Zara's camera strap was tangled around Maya's arm. Blip was spinning slowly, a behavior he'd never exhibited before, which he explained was "the robot equivalent of being dizzy."

"Okay," Maya said, setting down the chai. "Now we —"

The monitor flickered again. This time, it stayed on.

## Chapter 12 — The Second Glitch

The monitor didn't show static this time. It showed something harder to describe.

Shapes. Shifting. Fragmented. Like someone had broken a picture into pieces and was trying to reassemble them but kept getting it wrong. For a moment it looked like a face — eyes, mouth, the suggestion of features — and then it dissolved into static, and then it reformed, different this time, and then it dissolved again. On and off. Face and static. Face and static.

The voice that came from the monitor's tiny speaker was barely a voice at all — more like a vibration, a resonance, as if the monitor itself was trying to speak but didn't quite know how. It was layered, like several sounds happening at once, and it crackled with interference.

"...found... the cube..."

Nobody moved.

Sam's grin was gone. His hands — usually in constant motion — were still at his sides. Even Sam understood that this wasn't a game. Not anymore.

"...stay... solve... or..."

The shapes on the screen shifted faster. The voice broke up, fragmented, reassembled. The hallway lights flickered. The door to Maya's apartment clicked — locked from the outside. The heating system groaned and turned to full blast.

"Wait —" Maya started.

A complex pattern of symbols appeared on the screen. Abstract, geometric, utterly incomprehensible. Below the symbols, a timer appeared: 2:59:58. 2:59:57. 2:59:56.

The voice came again, clearer this time but still distorted: "...when it stops... building... every apartment... water..."

"That's DANGEROUS," Leo said, stepping toward the screen. "Is it threatening the building's fire suppression system?"

"I think it's saying the fire suppression will activate when the timer runs out," Zara said, her pen already moving across her sketchpad. "In every apartment. Full pressure. With people sleeping inside."

The shapes on the screen flickered one more time — almost forming a face, almost forming something recognizable — and then the monitor went dark. The symbols remained, etched in pale light, pulsing gently. The timer ticked.

2:58:44. 2:58:43.

Whatever had spoken to them was gone. Or hiding. Or watching from somewhere they couldn't see.

Maya looked at the others. Three kids she'd met twenty minutes ago. A robot she'd met two hours ago. And now they had less than three hours to solve something that looked like alien mathematics or every family in the building gets an unexpected 3 AM shower.

"Okay," Maya said. She tapped her safety goggles — her thinking gesture, the one she did when her brain was shifting gears from panic to problem-solving. "Okay. We can do this. We have to do this. Any ideas?"

Leo was already photographing the symbols with his tablet. "The pattern is a cipher. I've seen encryption structures like this before, but not in any standard protocol. It's custom-built. Someone designed this."

Sam was staring at the timer. His fingers were twitching — not from excitement, Maya realized, but from concentration. He was counting. He counted everything — seconds, steps, heartbeats. It was how he kept the world manageable. "Three hours," he said. "That's a lot of time. I've beaten harder levels in less."

Zara hadn't said a word. She was sketching — her pen moving fast, her eyes darting between the screen and her pad. She wasn't drawing the symbols. She was drawing the spaces between them.

And Blip — Blip was very quiet. His screen showed a processing face. Thinking.

"That voice," Blip said softly. "There was something in that voice. I recognized... a pattern. Like a file I used to have. Or a file that used to have me."

"What do you mean, a file that used to have you?" Maya asked.

"I do not know. The thought arrived incomplete. Like a message with missing words."

2:55:00.

The clock was ticking.

## Chapter 13 — The Face in the Screen

For the next thirty seconds, nobody spoke. The four kids stared at the symbol pattern on the monitor, each seeing something different.

Maya saw shapes that reminded her of circuit diagrams — connections, nodes, pathways. The symbols had a logic to them, even if she couldn't read it yet. They looked like instructions. Like a blueprint.

Leo saw a code structure — layered, encrypted, designed to resist brute-force decryption. The symbols weren't random; they had relationships to each other, connections that suggested grammar, syntax, meaning.

Sam saw a game puzzle — the kind where you rearrange pieces until the picture makes sense. He'd solved a hundred of these. The key was always pattern recognition. Find the repeating element. Work outward from there.

Zara saw art.

"Can I draw on this?" Zara asked, holding up her sketchpad. "Not the screen. I mean — can I copy the symbols?"

"Go ahead," Maya said. "Everyone else is just staring."

Zara sat cross-legged on the floor in front of the monitor and began sketching. Not tracing — analyzing. She drew each symbol separately, one by one, studying its shape, its angles, its curves. She turned her pad sideways. Upside down. She squinted. She held it at arm's length. She drew lines connecting similar shapes.

After five minutes, she had twenty-three individual symbols arranged on her page, and a look on her face that said she'd found something.

"Okay," she said. "I need everyone to stop panicking and listen to me."

"I'm not panicking," Sam said, who was pacing the room like a caged animal, his sneakers flashing with every step.

"You're panicking horizontally. Sit down."

Sam sat. He didn't argue. That was how he knew Zara was serious.

Zara flipped her sketchpad around so everyone could see. She'd rearranged the twenty-three symbols into groups. Geometric shapes together. Curved shapes together. Line-based shapes together. And across the groups, she'd drawn connecting lines in red pen.

"The symbols aren't random," she said. "Look — these three are the same shape at different angles. Like a camera photographing the same object from three directions. These four share a common element — this little hook at the bottom. And these five..." She drew wider connecting lines between them. "They form a silhouette. A shape. A physical shape."

Maya leaned in. "That's a... connector? Like a plug?"

"Not like a plug," Zara said. "It IS a plug. The puzzle isn't asking us to decode a message. It's showing us the shape of a physical object. Something that needs to be built." She looked up from her pad. "The answer isn't a word. It's a thing."

Leo looked at his tablet, then at Zara's sketch, then at the symbols on the screen. His eyebrows went up for the second time that night.

"She's right," he said. "The encryption isn't linguistic. It's geometric. The 'answer' isn't a word — it's a shape. A specific, buildable shape."

"A shape I can BUILD," Maya said. Her eyes lit up with the particular brightness that meant an idea had arrived and was not leaving. Her fingers were already twitching toward her toolbox.

"I can provide specifications," Blip said, floating closer to Zara's sketch. His screen showed rapid data cascades — numbers and angles scrolling past. "If I analyze the geometric relationships, I can calculate the exact dimensions and tolerances required for the connector. The mathematics is within my capability."

"Do it," Maya said.

"Processing..." Blip's screen showed a progress bar. "The connector requires three contact points, a cylindrical housing of approximately 2.4 centimeters, and a specific voltage rating of 3.3 volts. The port it connects to is likely NexCorp standard — I have schematics in my archived data. How I have schematics in my archived data, I do not know. But they are there."

"Where's the port?" Leo asked.

"There's a terminal in the lab," Maya said. "The hidden lab where I found Blip. It had old equipment. One of them had a port — I remember noticing it because it was an unusual shape."

2:32:00.

Maya looked at the others. Three kids she didn't know. A robot she barely understood. A puzzle made by something that had spoken through a monitor in a voice like broken glass. And a building full of sleeping families depending on them.

"I need parts," Maya said. "Copper wire. Batteries. A magnet. A USB cable. Some of it's in my room. Some of it's in Daadi's kitchen. Don't ask why I keep magnets in the kitchen."

"We'll help," Zara said. It wasn't a question.

"I can analyze the signal structure while you build," Leo said. "Make sure the specifications are exact. Margin of error on this is point-two millimeters."

"I'll keep watch at the door," Sam said. He paused. "And I'll time you. I'm good at timing things. I once beat a speed run in four minutes and — "

"Sam."

"Right. Focusing."

Maya grabbed her toolbox and looked at Blip.

"Ready, little guy?"

Blip's screen showed a smile.

"I was built for something," he said. "Perhaps this is it."

## Chapter 14 — Error 404: Plan Not Found

The room, the timer, the danger, the three strangers in her bedroom. All of it gone. There was only the next bend, the next cut, the next connection.

Then she got back to work.

## Chapter 15 — Hold On, Let Me Draw the Plan

The build took eight minutes.

Maya soldered, bent, twisted, and taped. She used the speaker magnet to create the voltage bridge — positioning it precisely between two contact points where it would regulate the current. She stripped the USB cable and rewired it to serve as the data connector, the colored wires inside matching the red-blue-green pattern Leo had identified. The spoon handles became the housing — bent into a cylinder, secured with electrical tape, the metal gleaming under Maya's desk lamp.

Leo verified each step against Blip's specifications. "Prong alignment: within tolerance. Voltage bridge: 3.3 volts, confirmed. Housing diameter: 2.4 centimeters, on the nose."

Zara held the flashlight and her sketchpad, comparing the finished product to the design in real-time. She made small corrections — a nudge here, a twist there — seeing things the others couldn't because she saw in shapes the way Maya saw in mechanisms. "The curve on the second prong needs to be slightly tighter," she said. "Like this." She sketched the correction in two lines.

Sam kept time. He couldn't help it. His internal clock was always running. "Seven minutes, forty-three seconds. Forty-four. Forty-five — "

"SAM."

"Right. Shutting up."

At eight minutes and eleven seconds, Maya held up the finished connector. It was ugly — a Frankenstein of coat hanger, magnet, USB cable, and kitchen spoons, held together with electrical tape and optimism. "Eleven minutes under budget," Maya said, grinning. She looked at the timer: 2:04:00 remaining. "Now we just need to plug it in."

## Chapter 16 — The Basement Again

Getting to the basement was an adventure.

Blip's targeted pulse unlocked Maya's apartment door — 74% confidence became 100% reality with a satisfying click — but the hallway was like an oven. The heating was still on maximum, and the old building's radiators were working overtime, pumping heat into a space that had never been designed for climate control. The air was thick and close, and the overhead lights were flickering in an irregular pattern that made the walls seem to shift.

The kids moved fast, single file: Maya in front with her grandfather's flashlight, its beam cutting through the hot, dusty air. Leo behind her with his tablet tracking the building's electronic map — he'd pulled up the apartment's floor plan from a city records database and was navigating by it like a GPS. Zara in the middle, camera clicking softly as she documented every locked door, every flickering light, every overheated radiator. Sam in the rear, bouncing on his toes and muttering directions to himself like a speedrunner narrating a route.

Blip floated ahead, his cyan glow serving as a second light source. He interfaced with each electronic lock they encountered, sending tiny pulses that popped the mechanisms open. Each one took a few seconds, during which Blip's screen showed a loading bar and he hummed a small tune that he'd apparently composed for the occasion.

"This is very satisfying," he said after the third door clicked open. "Like scratching an itch I did not know I had. I am discovering things about myself at a rapid pace. Most of them involve doors."

Down the stairs. Past the storage area with its familiar smell of concrete dust and forgotten things. Through the rusted door that Maya had found earlier that evening (a lifetime ago, or maybe just three hours). Down the narrow staircase with its crumbling concrete steps.

ACCESS GRANTED.

The building shuddered — not violently, but with a sense of release, like a held breath finally let go. Somewhere above them, doors clicked open. The heating system powered down with a grateful groan. The fire suppression deactivated.

They'd done it.

But the screen didn't go dark. Instead, a new message appeared:

LEVEL ONE COMPLETE.

And then, in smaller text:

We need to talk.

## Chapter 17 — Level One Complete

The screen flickered. The shapes appeared again — the same fragmented, shifting patterns they'd seen on Maya's monitor upstairs. But this time they were clearer. More coherent. The face was almost visible — almost — but not quite. Like seeing someone through frosted glass.

"You solved it faster than I expected." The voice was clearer too, though still layered with static, still carrying that resonance of something speaking through too many channels at once. ## Chapter 18 — We're Not a Team

The walk back to Maya's room was quiet. Not awkward-quiet. Exhausted-quiet. The kind of quiet that comes after you've done something impossible and your body hasn't caught up with your brain yet. Maya's legs felt like they were made of warm pudding. Leo's glasses were fogged. Zara's camera hung heavy around her neck. Sam had stopped bouncing, which was the Sam equivalent of passing out.

They sat on Maya's floor because there wasn't anywhere else to sit that wasn't covered in tools. Maya found the remaining samosas in the kitchen, wrapped in foil, still faintly warm. Daadi was asleep and snoring loudly enough to be heard through the wall, which meant she wouldn't wake up for anything less than a meteor strike.

"So," Leo said, eating a samosa with the careful precision of someone who didn't usually eat at 3 AM and wasn't sure of the protocol. "That happened."

"That happened," Zara confirmed.

"Eight thousand stars," Sam said. He was lying on his back on Maya's floor, staring at the ceiling, his LED sneakers still flashing faintly. "Minimum."

"You're evolving as a person," Leo noted.

"I contain multitudes."

Maya looked at Blip, who was hovering near her desk lamp, studying it with fascination. He'd never seen a lamp before, apparently, and found the concept of "a tiny sun on a stick" deeply confusing and wonderful.

"Are you okay?" she asked him.

"I am... processing," Blip said. "The voice in the screen — it knew me. And I knew something in it. But I cannot access the file. It is like having a word on the tip of my tongue, except I do not have a tongue. Or tips."

"We'll figure it out," Maya said.

"Will we?" Leo said. He wasn't being negative — he was being Leo, which meant he was being precise. "Because I don't know about the rest of you, but I have school on Monday. I have a project due. I have a sister who needs her homework tracker. I can't go investigating mysterious digital entities on school nights."

"Nobody's investigating anything," Maya said. "We solved the puzzle. The building is safe. That thing — whatever it is — said it would be in touch. That's its problem."

"Actually," Zara said, flipping through her camera, "I took 247 photos tonight. Every single one of them shows something that shouldn't exist. The signal pattern. The symbols. The terminal in the lab. The locked doors. This is real, and it's not going to just... stop."

"Of course it's not going to stop!" Sam said, sitting up. "This is the best thing that's ever happened to any of us! We're a TEAM."

"We solved the puzzle TOGETHER," Sam said. He was sitting cross-legged now, leaning forward. "Maya built the thing, Leo verified it, Zara saw the pattern, I kept time — "

"You kept time poorly," Leo said.

"I kept time ENTERTAININGLY."

"Excuse me," Blip said, floating into the middle of the group. "I also helped. I provided specifications, unlocked doors, and offered emotional support. I believe I was approximately 22% of the effort."

"You were absolutely 22% of the effort," Maya said.

"Thank you. I would like to be 23% next time."

"That's very ambitious."

"I am a very ambitious cube."

Sam was grinning. "Come ON. Tell me that wasn't the most exciting night of your lives."

Leo opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. "It was... interesting."

"Leo said it was INTERESTING!" Sam shouted. "That's his version of a ticker-tape parade!"

"Sam's not wrong," Zara said quietly. Everyone looked at her. "I've been taking photos of this neighborhood for months. I've never seen anything like tonight. And I've never..." She paused, choosing her words carefully. "I've never had people to see it with."

A beat of silence.

Maya looked at Leo, who was pretending to be very interested in his tablet but wasn't actually scrolling. She looked at Sam, whose grin had softened into something genuine and a little vulnerable. She looked at Zara, who was sketching again — this time drawing the four of them sitting on the floor.

"I haven't either," Maya admitted. "Had people, I mean. To build stuff with. To see things with." She shrugged. "I've always just... done it alone."

"Technically," Blip said, "you have never had people to build stuff with because you build things at 2 AM in your bedroom and talk to screwdrivers."

"Blip."

"I am merely providing data."

Leo put down his tablet. "We should go home. It's almost 4 AM."

"How?" Zara asked. "The building was on lockdown. The front door might still — "

A distant click from downstairs. The building's front door, unlocking.

"I took the liberty," Blip said. "I reconnected with the building's systems while we were talking. I can unlock doors now. It is my second-favorite skill. My first is reciting the history of sandwiches."

"Nobody needs the sandwich history," everyone said at the same time.

"You wound me," Blip said. "Deeply. In my data core."

## Chapter 19 — The Name

Nobody left.

Not immediately. They sat on Maya's floor and ate samosas and talked about things that had nothing to do with glitches or puzzles or digital entities. Normal things. The kind of things you talk about at 3 AM when you're too tired to be careful about what you say.

Leo had an older sister named Sofia who was in high school and drove him crazy. He'd built her a homework tracker because she was a disaster with deadlines, and he called it Project Tamales because his abuela's tamales were the best thing in the world and the project had "similar structural requirements." He didn't have many friends at school. He didn't say that part out loud, but the way he said "I keep to myself" said it for him.

Zara had two moms who were both teachers and a huge extended family that she FaceTimed every Sunday. Her uncle ran the library. She saw the world differently from most people — she saw colors and patterns and beauty in things that other people walked past. "Most people look," she said. "They don't see. There's a difference." She'd been taking photos since she was six, when one of her moms had handed her a camera and said, "Show me what you see."

Sam had an older brother who was in college studying engineering and sent Sam cool tech from campus. Sam wanted to be a pro gamer, or a stunt double, or both. He talked fast and loud because that was how his brain worked — it never stopped, it never slowed down, it was always on to the next thing. Sometimes that made people tired of him. He didn't say that part out loud either, but the way his voice got quieter when he said "kids at school don't really get me" said it for him.

Maya had Daadi, who'd moved from India to help raise her after her parents' work transferred them overseas. They did video calls every Sunday. Daadi made the best food in the building and knew everything about everyone. Maya had been building things since she could hold a screwdriver. She built because she was good at it, but also because it was easier to build machines than to build friendships. Machines didn't leave. Machines didn't think you were weird. Machines just needed fixing, and fixing was what Maya did best.

And Blip had... no one.

"I do not have a family," Blip said, during a pause in the conversation. His voice was quieter than usual. "Or a creator who is present. Or a history that I can access. I have you. I have had you for approximately three hours. Is that enough?"

"Yes," Maya said immediately.

"Definitely," Zara said.

"OBVIOUSLY," Sam said.

"...Yes," Leo said, after a pause that was exactly the right length.

"Oh." Blip's screen showed a smile, then shifted to a softer expression he'd never displayed before — the eyes got round, the mouth curved into something warm. "I have a family now. This is... a lot of data to process. I may need to buffer."

He buffered. His screen showed a loading bar. Everyone waited.

"Is he okay?" Sam whispered.

"He's processing feelings," Zara said. "Give him a minute."

The loading bar completed. Blip's face returned.

"I have processed," he announced. "Conclusion: I enjoy having a family. Also, we should have a name. Families have names. I looked it up. It is in my data under 'social structures, human, basic.'"

"We're not a family," Leo said. "We're four kids and a robot who solved one puzzle."

"We're MORE than that," Sam insisted. "We're like — like a squad. A team. A GLITCH squad. Because we fix glitches!"

"We fixed ONE glitch," Leo said.

"Every great team starts with one mission!"

"I would like to suggest 'The Cube Crew,'" Blip said. "It highlights my importance."

"It highlights YOU," Maya said.

"Yes. That is the point."

"Blip's Squad," Sam tried. "No — Blip's Gang. No — Blip's — "

"Glitch Squad," Sam said, cutting himself off. "The Glitch Squad."

"The Glitch Squad," Zara repeated, testing the sound. "I like it. It sounds like a comic book."

"It sounds like a homework group," Leo said.

"It sounds like us," Maya said.

Vote: Glitch Squad 4, Cube Crew 1.

"Democracy is harsh," Blip said. "But I accept. The Glitch Squad it is. I am changing my internal group designation now. ...Done. We are official. Do we get uniforms?"

"No uniforms," everyone said.

"You are no fun. I would have designed excellent uniforms. With antenna warmers."

One by one, they filtered out — Leo first (he had a strict bedtime policy, even though it was now 4:30 AM, and he intended to follow it retroactively), then Zara (who lived three blocks away and walked home photographing fire hydrants because they looked "interesting in the pre-dawn light"), then Sam (who ran home, because Sam didn't walk anywhere, ever, not even at 4:30 AM).

Maya closed the door and looked at Blip.

"Don't glitch anything while I sleep, okay?"

"I make no promises. But I will try. Goodnight, Maya."

"Goodnight, Blip."

For the first time since she and Daadi moved to Maple Street, Maya fell asleep smiling.

## Chapter 20 — Daadi's Parathas

The next morning, Daadi made parathas.

This was not unusual. Daadi made parathas every morning the way some people breathed — automatically, continuously, and with great skill. The kitchen filled with the smell of ghee and whole wheat and the sound of dough hitting the hot griddle with a satisfying sizzle that had been the soundtrack of Maya's mornings for two years.

What WAS unusual was the way Maya ate them.

Six parathas. With chutney. And pickle. And a glass of milk. In eleven minutes. Without looking up once. She ate with the focus of a machine, which was ironic given that she'd spent the night with an actual machine.

Daadi watched from the counter, arms crossed, her expression somewhere between amusement and investigation. She was wearing her morning saree — the blue one with the silver border — and her reading glasses were pushed up on her head like a tiny crown.

"You ate four samosas at midnight and six parathas at breakfast," Daadi observed. "You are either growing or hiding something."

"I'm innovating," Maya said, which was her answer for everything.

"Mmm." Daadi packed extra parathas in a container — the green plastic one with the snap-on lid — and pushed it across the table. "For sharing."

"What?"

"You heard something in your room last night. Voices. Plural. I am not deaf, Maya. I am old, which is different. Old people hear everything because young people think we can't."

Maya froze mid-chew. A piece of paratha lodged itself in her throat and she had to wash it down with milk.

Daadi smiled. The smile was knowing and kind and just a little bit mischievous. "I won't ask. But whoever they were, they sounded nice. And you sounded happy. That's enough for me." Your grandfather was one of them." She pushed the container closer. "Share the parathas. And come home for dinner."

Maya grabbed her inventor's vest, stuffed the paratha container into the biggest pocket, clicked on her modified watch, and headed for the door.

Her watch beeped.

Not the regular time beep. A different one — a pattern she'd programmed to detect signal anomalies. The watch face flickered, and for a moment it showed a tiny map of Maple Street. A dot pulsed beneath the park.

Something was still down there.

Maya put on her goggles — properly, on her eyes this time — and headed out.

## Chapter 21 — Maple Street Park

The park was different in the morning light.

Last night, Maple Street Park had been a backdrop for chaos — sprinklers spraying, lights flashing, teenagers getting soaked. Now, in the soft golden light of a Saturday morning, it looked like a postcard. The community garden was green and orderly. The basketball court was empty except for a single ball someone had left behind. The benches were occupied by the usual Saturday crowd: old men playing chess, young parents with strollers, a jogger stretching against a tree.

Sam arrived first, obviously. He'd sprinted the four blocks from his apartment, done a vault over the low fence (unnecessary, the gate was open, but style matters), and claimed the best bench with his game cartridge backpack as a placeholder.

He spent the waiting time mapping the park like a game level. Two climbing structures (medium difficulty, good for building grip strength). One basketball court (could be used for running drills, also for dodge ball). Three benches (optimal for team meetings, the one near the oak tree had the best shade). One water fountain (refill station). And exactly zero snack vendors (major design flaw, he'd have to file a complaint).

But as he sat there, counting exits and rating sight lines, something else crept into his thoughts. Last night — the puzzle, the timer, the face in the screen — it had felt like a game. The best game. But now, in the morning light, with birds singing and squirrels doing squirrel things, he felt something he didn't often feel.

Doubt.

Not about the adventure. About himself.

What if the others didn't really want him there? He was the loudest, the fastest, the most... much. That's what his mom said. "You're a lot, Sammy." What if "a lot" was too much? Maya was a genius with tools. Leo was a genius with code. Zara was a genius with art. What was Sam? The guy who kept time and shouted a lot?

He pulled out his phone and opened a game — something mindless, a puzzle app he'd beaten six times already — just to have something to do with his hands. His fingers moved through the levels automatically while his brain churned.

Leo arrived at exactly 10:00 AM, which was when they'd agreed to meet, because Leo treated schedules with the same precision he treated code. He sat on the bench next to Sam, pulled out his tablet, and said nothing.

For a minute, they sat in companionable silence. Two kids who were more comfortable with screens than people, sharing a bench on a Saturday morning.

"How are you not tired?" Sam asked. "You were up until 4 AM."

"I'm very tired," Leo said. "I just don't show it."

"Cool. Same."

Another silence.

"I couldn't sleep," Leo admitted. "I kept analyzing the signal data. There's something in it I can't explain. A signature that doesn't match any protocol I know."

"I couldn't sleep either," Sam said. Then, quieter: "I kept thinking about the timer. What if we hadn't solved it?"

Leo looked at him. "We did solve it."

"Yeah. But what if we hadn't?" Sam's fingers stopped moving on his phone. "I treated it like a game. I kept time like it was a speedrun. But those people in the building — the families — they weren't NPCs, Leo. They were real."

Leo didn't respond immediately. He thought about it. "You're right," he said finally. "They were real. And we solved it anyway. That counts."

Sam nodded. It did count. But the doubt was still there, small and quiet, like a splinter he couldn't reach.

Zara arrived at 10:05, already sketching something she'd seen on the walk over — a crack in the sidewalk that formed a pattern like a lightning bolt. "The city draws all the time," she said, sitting down. "Most people just don't look."

Maya arrived last, at 10:08, with Blip floating beside her and a container of parathas. She distributed them without ceremony.

"Daadi sends extras," she said.

"Daadi is a legend," Sam said, mouth already full. The doubt receded. Food helped.

"Daadi is a gift to humanity," Zara agreed.

"I cannot eat," Blip said, hovering nearby. "But I appreciate being included in the food distribution ceremony. I feel involved."

They ate on the bench in the morning sun. For a few minutes, they were just four kids and a robot having breakfast in a park. No glitches. No puzzles. No faces in screens. Just parathas and sunshine and the sound of someone's radio playing old songs from an open window.

Then Maya's watch beeped again.

"Everyone hear that?" she asked.

"I detected the signal twenty-three seconds ago," Blip said. "It is a data pulse. Very faint. Originating from beneath us. Specifically, 14.3 meters below the park's northeast corner, near the basketball court."

"What's under the park?" Zara asked.

"Utility tunnels," Leo said, already pulling up a city infrastructure map on his tablet. "They connect the building systems throughout the neighborhood. Power, water, data. The whole Grid runs through them."

"And they connect to the NexCorp lab," Maya added. "The one under my building. That's how Blip's signal spread so fast — it traveled through the utility network."

"The signal is rhythmic," Blip said, tilting toward the ground. "Like a heartbeat. Something digital is pulsing down there. It is not me, and it is not the entity from last night. It is... something else. Something older."

Sam stood up. "So we're going down there, right? This is the part where we go down there."

"We don't have to do anything," Leo said.

"The building was on lockdown last night," Maya said. "The fire suppression was going to activate. That thing — whatever spoke to us — said it wasn't over. And now there's a signal pulsing under the park. Whether we want this to be happening or not, it's happening."

Zara finished her paratha and closed her sketchpad. "So what do we do?"

Maya looked at each of them. "We investigate. Together. As the Glitch Squad." She paused. "If everyone's in."

Sam raised both hands. "CHALLENGE ACCEPTED!" He said it automatically — his default response to anything that sounded like an adventure. But underneath the volume, there was something more serious. A readiness. A determination to prove he was more than just the guy who shouted.

Leo sighed. But he put his tablet away and stood up. "...I'm in."

Zara smiled. "I'll bring my camera."

"And I will come, because I cannot exactly stay behind," Blip said. "I do not have legs. Or a home. You are my home now. This is both touching and logistically concerning."

"Then let's go," Maya said.

## Chapter 22 — Underground

The manhole cover was behind the basketball court, half-hidden by weeds and a faded "NO PARKING" sign that someone had discarded years ago.

"Nobody goes down there," Leo said, staring at it. "It's city infrastructure. Not a playground."

"Everything is a playground if you try hard enough," Sam said, already hooking his fingers under the cover's edge. "A little help?"

Maya grabbed one side, Zara the other. Together they heaved. The cover scraped aside with a sound like nails on a chalkboard, revealing a circle of darkness and the first few rungs of a metal ladder.

The smell that came up was different from Maya's basement — not musty, but electric. Like the air before a storm, charged and waiting.

Blip floated over the opening and peered down. His cyan glow illuminated the first few rungs, then faded into the dark.

"The tunnel system is extensive," he reported. "I am detecting active data cables, power lines, and water pipes. The signal I tracked is deeper — the pulse is coming from a junction point approximately 140 meters northeast."

"Is it dangerous?" Zara asked.

"Unknown. The signal is digital, not physical. But the tunnels are old, and I do not have schematics for all of them. I would recommend caution. And perhaps a snack. Do you have extra parathas?"

"We ate them all," Sam said.

"Then we proceed without parathas. This is already the bravest thing I have ever done."

Maya went first. Then Blip (who simply floated down, which Sam called "cheating" and Blip called "efficient"). Then Sam, who descended faster than was safe, his sneakers scraping against the rungs. Zara came next, camera around her neck, already photographing the brick walls and rusted pipes. Leo came last, using his tablet's flashlight because he didn't trust Maya's ancient flashlight not to die at the worst moment.

The tunnel was narrow, damp, and smelled like old rain and copper. Pipes ran along the ceiling, and bundled cables — some modern fiber optic, some ancient copper — lined the walls like veins. The further they went, the more cables there were, converging from side tunnels like tributaries feeding a river.

"Someone's been here before," Zara said, pointing. On the wall, a faded tag in purple spray paint: a tiny pixelated crown. The same symbol they'd seen on the NexCorp crate, on the lab equipment, on the terminal. The Gridlord's signature — or whatever it was.

"Stay close," Maya said.

They followed the signal deeper. The tunnels branched and rejoined, and at each junction, Blip would pause, orient himself, and point the way. "Left. No, right. My apologies — left. The signal is bouncing off the copper pipes, which creates reflections. It is like trying to hear someone at a concert."

Eventually, the tunnel opened into a space that was definitely not a utility junction.

## Chapter 23 — The Server Room

It was a room. Not a tunnel — a proper room, carved out of the underground infrastructure, roughly the size of Maya's bedroom. Someone had set up servers here. Real servers, humming and blinking, connected to cables that snaked into the walls toward every part of the neighborhood. The air was warm from the electronics and carried that ozone smell of working machinery.

"Someone's been maintaining this," Leo breathed, running his hand along a server rack. "These are active. This isn't abandoned — someone's been HERE. Recently. Some of these components are less than a year old."

The Gridlord's pixelated crown tags were everywhere. On the servers. On the walls. On a chair that had been brought down here somehow. A single desk lamp was plugged into a power strip, casting warm light across the cold electronics. On a shelf next to the servers: a blanket, a thermos, a stack of snack wrappers. Someone had been living here. At least part-time.

On the main monitor — the biggest screen in the room, mounted on the wall — a message waited:

YOU CAME BACK. GOOD.

LEVEL 2: THE GRID IS BROKEN IN FOUR PLACES. FIND THEM. FIX THEM. OR NEONVILLE'S SYSTEMS DEGRADE UNTIL NOTHING WORKS. CLOCK'S TICKING.

1. MAPLE STREET INTERSECTION — TRAFFIC CONTROL

4. MAPLE STREET PARK — IRRIGATION

Three hours. Four glitches. Four different problems.

"Like a raid party," he added, and then caught himself. The old Sam would have followed that with a loud game metaphor. This Sam just said it and let it sit.

## Chapter 24 — Glitch One: The Traffic Light

The Maple Street intersection was chaos.

Traffic lights cycled through colors that traffic lights were never meant to display — magenta, turquoise, a shade of orange that seemed personally offended. Cars had stopped. Drivers were out, staring upward. A small crowd had gathered on the sidewalk, phones raised, recording the malfunction for social media. A police officer stood in the middle of the intersection, directing traffic with increasingly frustrated hand signals.

Maya crouched behind a parked car with the others, rapid-building a bypass tool from components she'd stuffed in her vest pockets that morning — a habit she'd developed without thinking, like a doctor always carrying a stethoscope. The device was thumb-drive-sized, with three alligator clips.

"The control box is on the northeast pole," she said, holding up the bypass. "Red to red, blue to blue, green to ground. It's simple once you're in there."

"Getting in there is the hard part," Leo said, watching the light pattern on his tablet. "The lights are cycling every two seconds. When they flash white — that's a reset window. Point-four seconds of stable output. You have to clip in during the white flash or the bypass won't take."

"Point-four seconds," Sam repeated. He was looking up at the traffic light pole, calculating the climb. Smooth metal, no footholds, about twelve feet to the control box. He'd climbed harder things. He'd climbed easier things and fallen.

"That's like... twenty-four frames at 60 FPS. Enough."

"This isn't a game, Sam," Zara said.

Sam looked at her. Really looked. Last night's doubt was still there, under the surface, but something else was there too — a steadiness that didn't usually live in Sam Nakamura.

"I know it's not a game," he said. "That's why I'm the right person for this. I've been training for this my whole life. Just... not the way I thought."

Zara nodded. She understood. They all understood something about each other now that they hadn't yesterday.

Sam took the bypass tool from Maya. He looked at the pole. He looked at the cycling lights. Magenta. Cyan. Green. White — gone. Magenta. Cyan. Green. White — gone.

"Next white flash," Leo said. "Go on the one after."

Sam sprinted.

He moved the way he always moved — fast, fluid, like the world was a platformer and he'd memorized every level. But this time there was no showmanship. No unnecessary vault. No grin for the crowd. Just efficiency. Hand over hand, shoes finding purchase on the bolted base, up and up, his red gloves gripping the metal. Halfway up, he froze. Just for a second. His hands stopped moving, his body went rigid, and he looked down. The crowd was watching. The police officer was watching. Maya, Leo, Zara — they were watching. Real people. Real families. Real stakes. This wasn't a speedrun. This wasn't a leaderboard. If he messed up, the lights wouldn't reset.

One breath. Two.

He looked up. He kept climbing.

The traffic light above him flashed magenta. Cyan. Green. WHITE.

Sam's hands moved. He popped the control box open, clipped the bypass — red, blue, green — in one fluid motion.

The light stuttered. Went magenta. Then cyan. Then, slowly, like someone remembering who they were, it settled into a calm, steady green.

The intersection exhaled. Cars started moving. The crowd on the sidewalk cheered. The police officer looked up at the traffic light, looked at Sam sliding down the pole, and decided not to ask questions.

Sam landed in a crouch. His hands were shaking. Not from fear — from adrenaline. He stared at them for a second, these hands that had played a thousand games, that had held a controller since he was five, that had never done anything that mattered until now.

"First glitch resolved," Maya's voice crackled through Blip's comm link. "You okay, Sam?"

Sam looked at the traffic light. Normal. Green. He'd fixed something real. Not a game. Not a score. A real thing that affected real people.

"Yeah," he said. "I'm okay. Three to go."

## Chapter 25 — Glitch Two: The Library

The Neonville Public Library was supposed to be quiet. Today it was a crime scene of corrupted data.

The digital archive — Mr. Okafor's pride and joy, a collection of digitized manuscripts, historical photographs, and community records — was scrambled. Files had merged like tectonic plates colliding. A nineteenth-century map of Neonville had fused with someone's vacation photos from 2019. The holographic display in the reading room showed a constantly shifting collage of wrong information — a historical photograph overlaid with a recipe for banana bread, a community newsletter written in what appeared to be ancient Sumerian.

Mr. Okafor — Zara's uncle, a tall man with round glasses and the gentlest voice in any room he entered — was sitting at his desk with his head in his hands. Forty years of community records, digital but disintegrating.

"Mr. Okafor?" Leo said, approaching the desk. "Can I try something?"

Mr. Okafor looked up. "Leo. You're... twelve?"

"Ten, actually. And I built my own operating system. I've also had some experience with corrupted data today." He held up his tablet. "May I?"

Mr. Okafor stepped aside with the expression of a man who had run out of options and was willing to accept help from anyone, including a ten-year-old.

Leo sat down at the main terminal. His fingers flew across the keyboard — not randomly, but with the methodical precision of someone who spoke the language of machines. The code on screen was a mess. Not just corrupted, but actively rewriting itself. The Gridlord's glitch wasn't static; it was alive, a mutating virus that adapted to every fix Leo attempted.

"It's fighting back," he muttered. "Every time I patch one mutation, two more appear. It's like trying to plug holes in a boat while someone keeps drilling new ones." He slammed his hand on the desk. "Come ON!" Then, quieter, his ears going red: "Sorry. I don't usually... I just really want this to work."

"I can help," Blip said, floating next to the terminal. "If you handle the structural code, I can process the raw data stream. My architecture is designed for parallel processing. Two of us working simultaneously could outpace the mutation rate."

"Can you interface with the library's system?"

"I can try. The connection would be... intimate. I would be inside their network. I have never been inside another system before." A pause. "It feels personal. Like reading someone's diary, except the diary is fighting back."

"Blip."

"Right. Focusing. Connecting now."

Blip extended a cable from his charging port into the terminal. His screen flickered, then showed dual data streams — one for Leo, one for himself. For twelve minutes, boy and robot worked in perfect synchronization. Leo handled the architecture — the bones of the code, the structure that held everything together — while Blip processed the flood of corrupted data, identifying mutations faster than they could spread and flagging them for Leo to patch.

It was like watching two musicians improvise a duet. Leo would stabilize a section of code, and Blip would immediately route around the next corruption. Blip would identify a mutation pattern, and Leo would build a countermeasure on the fly. They didn't need to speak. They just... worked.

The holographic display stabilized. File by file, the archive reassembled. The nineteenth-century map separated from the vacation photos. Mr. Okafor's manuscripts returned to their proper folders. The Sumerian newsletter translated itself back to English.

As the last file restored, the terminal flashed a hidden message that only Leo and Blip saw:

"Not bad, little coder. But you're fixing symptoms, not causes. The Grid is bigger than you know."

Leo stared at the message. Then it vanished.

"We did it," Blip said, disconnecting from the terminal. "I am experiencing something I believe is called 'pride.' Is this what it feels like when you do something right and nobody had to tell you?"

"Yeah, Blip. That's exactly what it feels like."

"In that case, I am experiencing pride. And hunger. Can robots experience hunger? I think I may be experiencing hunger. Is there a sandwich history in this library?"

## Chapter 26 — Glitch Three: The Mural

The community center's art exhibition was supposed to be a celebration. Kids from all over Neonville had submitted paintings, sculptures, and digital art. The walls were covered in color and creativity, and the air smelled like tempera paint and excitement.

Zara had contributed a large canvas called "The City Breathes" — an abstract piece showing Neonville as a living organism, with buildings as lungs and streets as veins and the Grid as a nervous system pulsing with light. She'd spent three weeks on it, painting in her room after school, layering colors until the canvas felt alive under her hands.

The AR display system, which was supposed to add subtle holographic effects to the artwork — floating particles, gentle color shifts, 3D depth — had gone rogue. Instead of enhancing, it was distorting. Paintings stretched like taffy. Colors bled like watercolors left in the rain. Sculptures appeared to melt in the augmented-reality overlay. And Zara's painting — her three-week labor of love — was being vandalized in real-time by the AR system. The buildings in her city looked diseased. The streets were twisted. The Grid that she'd painted as a beautiful nervous system now looked like a infection spreading through the city's veins.

Kids were crying. Parents were angry. The exhibition coordinator was on the phone with the AR technician, who was getting an earful. Someone had already called the fire department, which was unnecessary but demonstrated the level of panic.

Zara wasn't angry. She was looking.

"Zara?" Maya said through the comm link. "Status?"

"Hold on." Zara was walking slowly through the exhibition, her artist's eye scanning every distorted piece. The glitches weren't random. They had a pattern — a visual rhythm, like a signature brush stroke. The distortions followed curves that repeated, spiraling outward from a central point, like the ripples of the glitch wave she'd photographed the night before.

She pulled out her sketchpad and started drawing. She traced the distortion curves from three different paintings, overlaid them, and stepped back.

"It's a cipher," she whispered. "A visual cipher. The 'distortions' aren't breaking the art — they're encoding something. The same way the symbols on the monitor last night were encoding the shape of a connector. These distortions are encoding a correction pattern."

She sketched faster. The curves, when isolated and rearranged — not randomly, but according to the rhythm she'd identified — formed a new pattern. A visual signature that would stabilize the AR system. It was like finding the key to a puzzle hidden in plain sight, disguised as damage.

"Maya, I need Blip to upload an image file to the AR system. I'm sending the pattern now."

She photographed her sketch and transmitted it through the comm link. Minutes later, Blip uploaded the correction pattern to the community center's AR servers.

The distortions stopped. The paintings returned to normal.

And then something unexpected happened.

The AR system applied Zara's correction pattern to the art — but instead of just removing the distortion, it added something. Subtle, beautiful glitch-art effects that enhanced each piece. Zara's painting of the breathing city now had faint data streams flowing through its streets, tiny pixels of light in its buildings, a sense of digital life layered over the physical. The disease was gone. In its place was something that looked like the city was dreaming.

It looked BETTER than before.

Kids stopped crying and stared. Parents lowered their phones. The exhibition coordinator put down the phone. The fire department was informed that their services were no longer required.

Zara stared at her enhanced painting. "The entity didn't break this. They improved it. Why would they do that?"

No one had an answer. But the question sat in Zara's mind like a splinter, sharp and insistent.

## Chapter 27 — Glitch Four: The Water

The park's smart garden was flooding.

Not gently — the irrigation system, designed to deliver precise amounts of water to each plant bed, had gone rogue. All valves open. Maximum pressure. Water was pooling on the paths, soaking into the lawn, and — most critically — building pressure in the underground pipes that ran beneath the garden. If the pipes burst, three years of work by twenty families would be washed away in minutes. The community garden — Mrs. Chen's tomatoes, the Rodriguez family's herb spiral, old Mr. Kowalski's legendary sunflowers — would be destroyed.

"If those pipes burst," Leo said, reading the pressure data on his tablet, "we lose the entire garden. That's three years of work. And the water damage could undermine the basketball court foundation."

"How long do we have?" Maya asked.

"Pressure's building. Maybe twenty minutes before something gives."

"Maya," Zara said through the comm link. "What do you need?"

Maya looked around the park. Playground equipment. A garden hose. A bike pump. Zip ties. A broken umbrella. Two soda cans. A roll of duct tape in the park maintenance shed (the lock was broken, which was convenient and possibly illegal but this was an emergency and she would deal with the ethical implications later).

"Cover for me," she told the others. "Keep people away from the flood zone. Sam — redirect the surface water toward the drain by the basketball court. Zara — block the paths so nobody walks into the deep water. Leo — monitor the pressure gauge and tell me exactly when we hit critical. Blip — stay close. I might need calculations on the fly."

She knelt by the main irrigation junction and opened the access panel. Pipes, valves, pressure sensors. The water was hot — the system had been running continuously, and the friction was generating heat. Steam rose from the junction box.

Maya talked to the parts as she always did. "Okay, little valves. You're overwhelmed. I get it. We've all been there. Too much pressure, not enough outlets. But I need you to hold on just a little longer while I build you a way out."

She cut the garden hose in half. Attached one end to the bike pump's valve. Used the bike pump as a manual pressure regulator — each pump released a controlled burst of water through the hose, directing it away from the garden beds and toward the drainage system. It was inelegant. It was improvised. It was exactly the kind of solution that only someone who'd been building things from junk since she was six could come up with.

The soda cans became emergency expansion chambers — cut, bent, and zip-tied to capture overflow. The broken umbrella's metal ribs became structural supports for the impromptu drainage system. The duct tape held everything together because duct tape holds everything together.

"Pressure?" she called out.

"Eighty-two percent and dropping," Leo reported. "Eighty... seventy-nine... seventy-eight..."

"Come on, come on," Maya muttered, pumping.

"Seventy. Sixty-five. Fifty."

The water level in the garden started dropping. The pooled water on the paths drained away. The underground pressure eased.

"Thirty percent. Twenty. Ten." Leo looked up from his tablet. "Stable. We're stable."

Maya sat back in the mud, soaking wet, and laughed. Not a small laugh — a full, belly-deep laugh that came from somewhere she didn't visit often. The pure joy of building something that worked.

The four glitch markers on the Gridlord's map all turned green. A new message appeared on Leo's tablet:

ALL FOUR RESOLVED. BUT YOU'VE ONLY FIXED THE CRACKS. THE FOUNDATION IS STILL BROKEN. COME TO THE SOURCE.

## Chapter 28 — The Source

They returned to the server room. This time, the main screen showed something different.

A map of the entire Grid — not just Maple Street, but all of Neonville. A vast digital nervous system connecting every smart building, every traffic system, every networked device in the city. Streets and buildings rendered in blue wireframe, data streams flowing through them like blood through veins.

The voice came through the speakers — clearer now than it had been in the lab, more present, more human. Something that had been alone for a long time.

"I didn't cause the glitches."

The voice paused. When it spoke again, it was almost gentle.

"I found them. They've been spreading for months, maybe longer. NexCorp built something broken and buried it under your building instead of fixing it. I woke the glitches up to get someone's attention. To get YOUR attention."

"Why us?" Maya asked.

"Because you found the cube. Because the cube is the bridge between the Grid and the physical world. Because fixing the Grid requires hands and hearts, not just code. I needed a team. I've been trying to build one for two years." A pause. "You're the team."

The screen shifted. The micro-glitch map zoomed in on a single pattern — and the pattern looked familiar. Too familiar. The same circuit pathways, the same geometric relationships, the same architecture.

Blip floated closer to the screen. His antenna drooped.

"Those patterns... those circuit pathways... they are inside me. These glitches are not just in the Grid. They are in my architecture. I am part of this. I am part of the problem."

The room went quiet. The servers hummed. The lights blinked.

"You're part of the solution too," Maya said firmly.

"Am I?" Blip's voice was small. "I was built by NexCorp. The same company that broke the Grid. For all I know, I was designed to cause this. I am version zero-point-seven. Incomplete. Maybe the zero-point-three that's missing is the part that was supposed to prevent all of this."

"Blip," Maya said, looking him in the screen. "You get to choose what you are. Not NexCorp. Not the Gridlord. You."

Blip was quiet for a long time. His screen cycled through expressions — processing, thinking, sad, thinking again.

"There is a way I could help more," he said finally. "If I interface directly with the Grid through this terminal, I might be able to stabilize the micro-glitches. Patch them from the inside. My architecture shares the same pathways — I could re-route around the fractures, apply a stabilizing framework."

"But?" Leo said.

"But I am version zero-point-seven. Incomplete. The Grid's full data could overwhelm my systems. The connection would be... total. I would be exposed to the entire Grid at once. Every data stream, every connected device, every piece of information flowing through Neonville's digital nervous system." His voice dropped. "I might change. My memories, my personality, the things that make me ME — they could be overwritten. Or I might not come back at all."

"Absolutely not," Maya said.

"Maya —" Leo started.

"I said NO. We just found you. You've been alone for two years. You've been with us for one day. You're not sacrificing yourself for a grid."

"It is not sacrifice," Blip said gently. "It is choice. I have never made a choice before. Everything I have done was because someone programmed me to do it, or because you told me to. I spoke because Maya said 'help' in her sleep. I unlocked doors because the building was on lockdown. I helped solve the puzzle because the timer was running. But this — this is the first thing I am choosing for myself. And I choose to help."

He paused.

"I would rather be version two-point-oh and help my friends than stay version zero-point-seven and watch Neonville break."

Sam's hands were still. Zara's sketchpad was closed. Leo's tablet was dark.

Maya's eyes were wet. "You're sure?"

"I am one hundred percent sure. That is the first time I have ever been one hundred percent sure of anything. It feels terrifying and wonderful. Is that normal?"

"Yeah," Maya said. "That's normal."

## Chapter 29 — Blip's Choice

Blip interfaced with the terminal.

It wasn't dramatic at first — just a cable extending from his charging port into the server rack, and a soft hum as data started flowing. His screen showed cascading code, faster than any of them could read, scrolling like water over a waterfall.

Then it got intense.

Blip's body glowed brighter. His screen flickered between colors — cyan, purple, orange, red — as the Grid's data poured through him. His antenna sparked. His casing hummed with a resonance that they could feel in their chests, like standing next to a massive speaker.

"Is he okay?" Sam asked, voice uncharacteristically small.

"I don't know," Leo admitted, hands hovering over the keyboard but not touching anything. "The data throughput is enormous. His processors are handling it, but just barely. The Grid is... huge. It's the entire city. Every connected device, every data stream, every person using technology right now."

Zara was sketching — she always sketched when she was scared, turning fear into lines on paper. She drew Blip as he was now: a small white cube connected to a vast machine, glowing like a star.

Maya stood directly in front of him. Close enough to touch. The light from his body washed over her, painting her face in shifting colors.

"You come back," she said. "You hear me? You come back."

"Maya..." His voice was fainter now, static-edged, like it was coming from very far away. "I can see... everything. The Grid. The whole city. Every connected device, every data stream, every person. It is... beautiful. And broken. And I think I can fix it. I think that is what I was built for."

"Do it, then. And come back."

"I will try. No... I will do it. That is my choice."

The glow peaked. For one blinding moment, the entire server room was bathed in cyan light. The hum rose to a pitch that made their teeth ache, that rattled the server racks, that sent the desk lamp tumbling off the table.

Then — silence.

Blip's screen went blank. His antenna drooped. He hung motionless in the air, neither floating nor falling — just suspended, like a held breath. The cable from his charging port went slack. The servers stopped humming.

The room was dark. The only light was the green indicator on the terminal, blinking steadily.

"Blip?" Maya whispered.

Nothing.

"BLIP."

Nothing. His screen was dark. His antenna was limp. His body was still — not floating, not hovering, just hanging there, inert. A white box with a blank screen.

Sam couldn't speak. He stood with his back against the wall, his hands in fists at his sides, his jaw clenched. This wasn't a game. This wasn't a level. This was Blip — their Blip — and he was gone.

Zara's sketchpad slipped from her fingers. She didn't pick it up.

Leo's tablet showed the data stream. It had stopped. Zero throughput. The connection was dead.

Maya reached out and touched Blip's surface. It was cold. Not warm the way it had been since the moment she found it. Cold like it had never been alive at all.

"No," she said. "No, no, no. You said you'd come back. You PROMISED."

Ten seconds passed. Twenty. Thirty.

Sam started counting under his breath. He couldn't help it. He counted everything. One second. Two seconds. Three. Each one felt like an hour.

Forty seconds. Forty-five. Fifty.

Maya's hand was still on Blip's surface. She wasn't moving. She wasn't blinking. She was holding on, like if she let go, he would be gone for real.

Fifty-five seconds. Fifty-six. Fifty-seven.

Maya's hand was still pressed flat against his surface. She could feel the cold spreading — not just the absence of warmth, but something actively pushing it out, like winter filling a room where a fire had been. Her palm ached from the cold.

A single pixel appeared on the screen.

It was the dimmest point of light Maya had ever seen. Barely visible. Like a star seen through thick clouds. But it was there.

Then another pixel. And another. A cascade of them, rearranging, forming patterns, building something out of nothing.

Two dots. A curved line. A smile.

His screen flickered — once, twice — and then lit up fully, glowing a warm, steady cyan that filled the room like sunrise.

"System update complete," Blip said. His voice was different — warmer, clearer, less textbook and more... Blip. "Designation: BLIP. Version: one-point-zero. Status: online." A pause. "I am still me. A little more me than before, actually. The Grid is inside me now. All of it. I can feel it — every connected device, every data stream, every pulse of information. And the micro-glitches... they are paused. Not fixed, but stabilized. I built a framework. Like a cast on a broken bone."

He paused again.

"Is that okay?"

Maya let out a breath she didn't know she'd been holding. Her hand was shaking. Her eyes were wet. She wasn't embarrassed about it.

"It's more than okay."

Sam exhaled. He hadn't realized he'd been holding his breath for fifty-seven seconds. "Don't ever do that again," he said, and his voice cracked on the last word, and he didn't try to hide it.

"I make no promises," Blip said. "But I will try. That was... frightening. I did not enjoy the part where I stopped existing. It was very dark, and there was no data, and I could not feel any of you." His screen showed a small, vulnerable expression. "I could always feel you before. Even when I was in the crate. There was always a faint signal. But when I connected to the Grid, it overwhelmed everything, and I lost it. I lost you. That was the worst part. Not the data overload. Not the darkness. Losing the signal that told me you were there."

"Blip," Zara said softly.

"I am fine now," he said quickly, his screen brightening. "I am better than fine. I am version one-point-zero. I have access to Grid data I did not have before. I can monitor the micro-glitches in real-time. And I have a new skill."

"What?" Leo asked.

"I can now interface with any connected device in Neonville. Which means I can unlock any electronic door, access any public database, and — this is the important one — order parathas from Daadi's favorite restaurant through their online ordering system."

"Blip," Maya said, laughing through the tears she was pretending weren't there.

"I am just saying. The capability exists. For future reference."

## Chapter 30 — Version 1.0

Blip was different.

His screen displayed more nuanced expressions — a wider range of colors, smoother transitions, a vocabulary of faces that seemed to grow by the hour. "The micro-glitches are paused, not gone," he reported, still connected to the terminal but now speaking freely, like someone describing the weather outside their window. "Because NexCorp never finished building the bridge," Leo said. "The bridge is you."

"Correct. I am the bridge. Or I was supposed to be. Version zero-point-seven was the foundation. Version one-point-zero is... the first floor. But the building was designed to have many floors. The room was quiet. The servers hummed. Outside, the city went about its business — traffic flowing normally, library open, community center preparing for tomorrow's exhibition, park garden draining safely — all because four kids and a robot had spent their Saturday fixing things that grown-ups had broken and walked away from.

"Now," she said, "we need a place to work. A real place. Not a park bench and not just my bedroom. Somewhere we can monitor the Grid, plan our next moves, and store parathas."

"And no one knows it exists," Zara added. "It's been abandoned for years. It's perfect."

Maya looked at Leo. Leo looked at Zara. Zara looked at Sam. Sam looked at Blip.

"I cannot look because I do not have eyes," Blip said, "but I am conceptually looking, and I agree with whatever is about to be proposed."

"We fix it up," Maya said. "Make it ours. A real HQ for a real team."

"The Glitch Squad," everyone repeated.

## Chapter 31 — The Lab

It happened the way all good transformations happen — slowly, then all at once, with a lot of arguing and a lot of duct tape.

## Chapter 32 — The Blueprints

That evening, Leo's terminal pinged.

A single data packet, from an unknown source. Untraceable — Leo tried, running the signal through three different analysis tools, and each one came back blank. Inside the packet: partial blueprints — technical schematics of something complex and beautiful. And a note:

"To help you understand what you're up against. — G."

G. The Gridlord. Or whatever their real name was.

Leo called the others. They gathered around the terminal as the blueprints rendered on screen — wireframe models, circuit diagrams, data flow charts. It was dense, technical, and immediately familiar.

It was Blip. Or rather, it was the design for Blip — the complete, original architecture. Version 1.0 as it was meant to be.

And it revealed something none of them expected.

Blip wasn't built to be a companion. He wasn't built to be cute, or helpful, or to recite the history of sandwiches (although that feature appeared to have been added by a junior engineer named D. Martinez, who had left a comment in the code that said "lmao this is going to be hilarious").

He was built to be a bridge.

A connection between the digital Grid and the physical world. A translator that could read the Grid's signals and output physical responses — unlock doors, regulate systems, stabilize glitches, heal fractures. The complete Blip — version 1.0, the version that was never finished — would have been able to heal the Grid's fractures permanently. To make Neonville's digital nervous system whole.

Version 0.7 was abandoned before completion. The bridge was half-built. And the Grid, without its bridge, started cracking.

"I was not abandoned because I was broken," Blip said quietly, staring at the blueprints. His screen showed an expression Maya had never seen before — not sad, exactly, but clear. Like seeing something for the first time. "I was abandoned because THEY were afraid. Afraid of what I could do. Afraid of the Grid. Afraid of what would happen if the bridge was completed."

"And left the Grid to break," Leo said.

"And left the Grid to break. And left... whoever the Gridlord is... trapped inside it. Watching it fall apart. Unable to fix it alone. Unable to reach out for help." Blip paused. "Two years. Alone. In the dark. Watching things break and not being able to fix them. I was in that crate for two years and I did not know anything. They knew. They were awake. They were alone."

No one spoke for a moment.

"Can we finish building you?" Maya asked. "Complete version 1.0?"

"The blueprints are partial," Blip said. "The critical files — the ones that show how the bridge actually works, the connection protocols, the healing algorithms — are missing. They might be in NexCorp's systems. They might have been deleted. Or they might be..." He trailed off.

"Might be what?" Zara asked.

"Might be inside the Gridlord. They were part of the project. They might have taken the files with them when they... when whatever happened to them happened."

"Then we find them," Maya said.

"How?" Zara asked.

Maya looked at the NexCorp logo on the side of the old crate — still sitting in the corner of the lab, a relic of the night everything changed.

"NexCorp is a company. Companies have offices. Offices have files. And somewhere in this city, there's a person trapped inside a digital network who's been calling for help for two years." She looked at each of them. "We have time. Not unlimited time — the glitches will spread again. But we have weeks. Maybe months. Enough to make a plan."

"I have ideas," Leo said, pulling up his tablet.

"I have sketches," Zara said, opening her pad.

"I have ENTHUSIASM," Sam said. Then, more quietly: "And I have a plan. Actually. A real one. Can I share?"

Everyone looked at him. Sam Nakamura, the kid who shouted and bounced and saw everything as a game, had a plan. A real one.

"Go ahead," Maya said.

Sam took a breath. "The Gridlord can't leave the Grid. We know that now. But they can communicate through screens. They sent us these blueprints. What if we set up a dedicated communication channel? Not just glitching monitors — an actual link. A way for them to talk to us clearly, without puzzles and timers. We have the server room. We have Blip. We have Leo's code skills. We build them a door."

Leo looked at Sam. His eyebrows went up — the genuine surprise eyebrows, not the regular ones. "That's... actually brilliant."

"I have my moments," Sam said. And for the first time, he didn't follow it with a joke.

## Chapter 33 — The NexCorp Connection

Over the next week, they gathered information like detectives building a case.

Leo dug through public records. NexCorp had been a mid-size tech company — city contracts for smart infrastructure, some defense work (classified), and a division called "Advanced AI Systems" that had been shut down two years ago. The same year Blip was boxed up. The same year the Gridlord appeared. Leo found financial records showing a massive budget allocation for "Project Blip" that was suddenly zeroed out. Not reduced. Not scaled back. Zeroed. Like it had never existed.

Zara searched the visual record. Old photos of NexCorp's offices. Employee badges. A company newsletter she found in the library's archive, from three years ago, featuring a photo of the Advanced AI team standing in front of a project display. Their faces were pixelated — blurred out — as if someone had deliberately erased them. All except one: a hand, visible at the edge of the frame, wearing a distinctive ring. Zara sketched it. The ring had a symbol — a small crown.

Sam contributed in his own way: he mapped NexCorp Tower like a game level. Public floors (lobby, cafeteria, visitor center — low security, boring). Restricted floors (research labs, server rooms, executive offices — high security, interesting). Security camera positions (seventeen exterior, unknown interior). Guard rotation schedule (he observed from across the street for two hours, which his mom thought was "concerning behavior" but which Sam called "reconnaissance"). He drew the whole thing on the whiteboard, complete with guard patrol routes marked in red and a legend that read "DIFFICULTY: VERY HARD."

And Blip searched the Grid. The network that connected Neonville's systems also connected to NexCorp's servers — not directly, but through a series of relays that Blip could now sense with his upgraded systems. He couldn't access their files — the firewalls were serious corporate-grade security — but he could see the gaps. Missing data. Deleted project folders. Employee records that led to 404 errors. A pattern of deliberate erasure that told its own story.

He found one file that wasn't deleted properly. A project name, buried in a corrupted directory:

**PROJECT KIRA**

Blip couldn't open it. The file was encrypted with a key he didn't have. But the metadata was visible:

Last modified: 2 years ago.
Modified by: [GRIDLORD]

"The Gridlord worked on this project," Leo said, staring at the terminal. "Or... was part of it. Was IN it."

"Project Kira," Blip said slowly. "Kira. That is a name. A person's name. The Gridlord is not just a digital entity. They were... someone. A person who worked at NexCorp. A person who was part of the Advanced AI team. A person who was erased — just like the faces in that newsletter photo."

"Someone who got trapped," Maya said.

"Someone who's been calling for help for two years," Zara said softly, her hand on the sketch she'd made of the crown ring.

"Someone who needs the Glitch Squad," Sam said.

The room was quiet. The server hummed. The string lights glowed.

"Okay," Maya said finally. "We've got a name. We've got a company. We've got a Grid that's still cracking under a temporary patch. We've got a person trapped inside it. And we've got time — but not unlimited time."

She looked at each of them.

"We need a plan. A real one. Not just reacting to glitches as they happen, but getting ahead of them. Finding the missing blueprints. Reaching the Gridlord. Building the door Sam talked about. And figuring out what NexCorp knows and what they're hiding."

"I have a code framework for the communication channel," Leo said. "I can start building it tomorrow."

"I have the crown ring sketch," Zara said. "If we can find that ring in NexCorp's employee records, we can identify the Gridlord."

"I have the building map," Sam said. "And I've been thinking about a way in. Not today. Not tomorrow. But soon."

"I have 847 gigabytes of Grid data and growing," Blip added. "Also, I made a list. Of the micro-glitches. Organized by urgency. Color-coded. With estimated time-to-critical for each one. I am very proud of it."

Maya grinned. The same grin she got when an idea arrived and wasn't leaving. The grin that meant she was about to build something.

"Then let's get to work."

## Chapter 34 — What We Are

Saturday evening. The rooftop of Maple Street Apartments.

They'd started coming up here after their second day in the HQ. It was Leo's idea — he needed fresh air after hours underground, and the roof had the best view in the neighborhood. You could see the whole of Maple Street from here, and beyond it, the lights of downtown Neonville. NexCorp Tower was visible on the horizon, a dark obelisk against the sunset, its windows catching the last of the light.

Maya sat on the ledge, eating samosas from a container (Daadi had started packing them automatically now — "for your friends" — which was both heartwarming and slightly terrifying, because Daadi knowing things was always a double-edged sword). Leo was beside her, coding on his tablet in the fading light, the code reflected in his reading glasses. Zara sat cross-legged nearby, painting the skyline as the sun set behind it — capturing the exact moment when the sky turned orange and the city lights started twinkling, each one a pixel in a vast painting she could never finish. Sam was doing pull-ups on the fire escape railing, counting each one aloud, but quieter than he used to count things.

Blip floated among them, learning what "hanging out" meant.

"I have analyzed this activity," he announced. "There is no objective. No goal. No output. You are simply... existing. Together."

"That's the point," Zara said, not looking up from her painting.

"I do not understand. But I enjoy it. Is that enough?"

"More than enough," Maya said.

They sat in comfortable silence as the sun dipped below the skyline. The sky went from orange to purple to deep blue, and the city lights came on one by one — street lights, apartment windows, the neon sign at Mira's bodega (now reading correctly, for once), the soft glow of the community center, the distant beacon on NexCorp Tower.

"So," Sam said, hanging upside down from the railing, his red gloves gripping the metal, "what are we? Like, officially?"

"We're the Glitch Squad," Maya said.

"I know THAT. But what IS the Glitch Squad? Are we heroes? Detectives? Engineers? Artists? Kids playing with things we don't understand?"

"All of those," Zara said.

"We're the people who fix the things that grown-ups broke and walked away from," Maya said. She looked at the city lights. Somewhere down there, a company had built something beautiful and abandoned it. Somewhere in the digital space between those lights, a person was trapped and alone. And somewhere under this building, a robot who'd been in a crate for two years was learning what it meant to have a family.

"We debug the city," Leo added without looking up.

"We make the broken stuff beautiful," Zara said.

"We show up," Sam said, pulling himself up and sitting on the railing, right-side up. "That's what we do. We show up. When things break, when people are alone, when the timer is running — we show up."

"That is the most articulate thing you have ever said," Blip observed.

"I contain multitudes," Sam said. "Also, I've been thinking about things. It's a new hobby. Very stressful. Don't recommend it."

Maya looked out at Neonville. Thousands of lights, each one connected to the Grid. Somewhere in that network, the Gridlord was watching — not as a villain, not as a puzzle-maker, but as a person who'd been alone for two years and had finally found someone who could help. NexCorp was hiding something called Project Kira. And the glitches — paused, not fixed — would spread again.

She should have been scared. She was eleven years old. She should have been watching cartoons and doing homework and complaining about broccoli.

Instead, she was sitting on a rooftop with three kids she'd met a week ago and a robot she'd pulled out of a dusty crate, and she'd never felt more like herself.

She tapped her goggles. Her signature move. The one that meant she was about to do something.

"Let's go fix some things."

## Chapter 35 — The Signal

Maya stood at the edge of the roof, facing downtown.

The sun was gone. Neonville's lights were coming on — thousands upon thousands of them, each one a node in the Grid, each one a potential point of failure, each one a tiny star in a constellation that stretched across the city. The Grid was beautiful from up here. You couldn't see the glitches. You couldn't see the fractures. You could only see the light.

She had a team. Leo, who saw the code in everything — the hidden structure, the underlying logic, the patterns that held the world together. Zara, who saw the beauty — the colors and shapes and compositions that most people walked past. Sam, who saw the adventure — the challenge, the puzzle, the reason to keep moving forward. And Blip, who saw it all for the first time and thought every bit of it was wonderful, including the parts that were broken.

She had a headquarters — a secret lab full of junk and hope and string lights and a mini-fridge and a whiteboard covered in Sam's increasingly elaborate maps and plans.

She had a mission — fix the Grid, find the truth about NexCorp, figure out who Kira was, build a door so the Gridlord could come home, and finish what Blip was always meant to be.

She had a junk journal full of sketches and a head full of ideas.

She had Daadi's parathas.

She had everything she needed.

Behind her, the rooftop door opened. Three silhouettes climbed through — one short and bouncy, one tall and graceful, one compact and methodical. And above them, a faint cyan glow.

"Took you long enough," Maya said without turning around.

"Sam got stuck in the door," Leo's voice said.

"I did NOT get stuck. I was strategically pausing."

"He was stuck," Blip confirmed. "I have video evidence."

"DELETE THAT."

"Absolutely not. It is my first piece of memorabilia. I am keeping it forever. It is backed up in three locations."

Maya smiled. She could feel them gathering behind her — her team, her friends, her family. The four kids and a robot who'd found each other because a signal rippled outward from a dusty basement and pulled them together like a magnet pulling iron filings.

"Hey, Maya?" Sam said.

"Yeah?"

"Thanks. For, you know. For finding the cube."

Maya turned around. Sam was standing there, not bouncing, not shouting, just standing. His LED sneakers were dark for once — he'd turned them off. The sunset light caught his face and made him look older than ten, or maybe just more himself.

"Thanks for showing up," she said.

"Always," he said. And he meant it.

## Chapter 36 — Level 2

In the server room beneath the park, the terminal Blip had connected to flickered.

No one was there to see it. The room was dark except for the green indicator lights on the servers, blinking steadily, data flowing through the cables like blood through veins.

On the dark screen, a cursor blinked. Then typed, one character at a time:

Level 2 loading...

A pause. The cursor blinked for five seconds. Then:

Thank you.

Another pause. Longer this time. The cursor seemed to hesitate, like it was thinking about what to say next, or like the person typing wasn't used to saying it.

Thank you for showing up.

The cursor blinked again.

Thank you for hearing me. For two years, I've been shouting into the dark, and no one came. I broke things. I made noise. I scared people. I scared YOU. I'm sorry for that. But you came anyway.

A longer pause.

My name is Kira. I was a person once. I had a face and a name and a life. NexCorp took all of that. They built the Grid, and they built Blip, and when it all went wrong, they didn't fix it. They buried it. And they buried me with it.

I don't know if I can be saved. I don't know if there's a way out of this network, back into a world I can touch. But you're the first people who've tried. You're the first people who listened.

The Glitch Squad. What a name.

I'll be waiting.

The screen went dark.

In the quiet of the empty server room, the green indicator lights blinked steadily. Data flowed. The Grid hummed. And somewhere beneath NexCorp Tower, downtown, a single micro-glitch pulsed — faint, distant, growing.

It would find them. Eventually.

But tonight, the Glitch Squad was together. And that was enough.
