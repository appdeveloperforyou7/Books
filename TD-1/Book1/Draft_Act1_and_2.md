# THE GLITCH SQUAD — Book 1: THE LOST SIGNAL

---

## Chapter 1 — The Self-Folding Backpack (That Didn't)

Maya's desk was a disaster zone, and she liked it that way.

Screwdrivers in a jar. Wires in a tangle. Three half-built gadgets, one of which might have been a toaster but was probably supposed to be a radio. A stack of notebooks so full of sketches and circuit diagrams that they bulged like they'd eaten too many batteries.

And in the middle of all of it: a backpack that was currently eating itself.

"Come on, come on, come ON," Maya muttered, poking the backpack with a screwdriver. The backpack responded by folding itself tighter, crumpling the nylon into a dense ball the size of a grapefruit. The motor she'd installed whined pitifully and gave up.

Maya stared at it.

The backpack stared back. It was a very compact stare.

"That's three this month," Maya said to no one. She talked to her inventions all the time. They never talked back, which she considered a one-sided relationship.

She pulled off her safety goggles — they lived on her forehead more than her eyes — and scratched her head. Her messy ponytail had given up entirely hours ago, and a pencil she'd stuck in it earlier fell out and clattered onto the desk.

The problem was simple: the folding motor was too powerful. She needed a smaller one. The junk store on Fifth Street had closed early, and online ordering took two days. Two days! Who had two days?

"Maya-beta, you're wasting good backpacks!" Daadi's voice drifted from the kitchen, accompanied by the smell of something wonderful frying.

"I'm INNOVATING, Daadi!"

"Innovate less loudly! The neighbors will complain!"

Maya grinned. Then she had an idea. It happened a lot — ideas arrived like unexpected guests, and she'd learned to open the door before they changed their minds.

The motor in the old fan in the basement. The fan that broke last summer. The fan Daadi said she'd throw away but never did because Daadi never threw anything away.

The basement.

Maya grabbed her flashlight, squeezed past the stack of cardboard boxes in the hallway, and headed downstairs.

## Chapter 2 — The Door That Wasn't Locked

The basement of Maple Street Apartments was the kind of place that gave basements a bad name.

Low ceiling. Bare bulbs. Pipes that groaned like they were telling ghost stories to each other. Rows of storage units behind wire mesh, full of things people couldn't fit in their apartments but couldn't bear to throw away. Broken furniture. Old exercise equipment with clothes hanging on it. Seventeen boxes belonging to someone on the fourth floor who'd moved out three years ago.

Maya knew this place well. She'd been scavenging parts from it for two years, ever since she and Daadi moved in. The building manager, Mr. Podowski, didn't mind. "Take what you want," he'd said. "Nobody else is using it."

She found the fan quickly — third shelf, behind a broken lamp and a box labeled "KITCHEN STUFF (FRAGILE)" that was definitely not fragile kitchen stuff. She cracked it open. The motor was perfect. Small, steady, and probably still worked.

But something else caught her eye.

In the far corner, past the water heater, behind a stack of paint cans — a door.

Maya had been in this basement a hundred times. She'd never noticed a door there. It was the same color as the wall, which was the same color as dust and indifference. The handle was rusted but not locked.

She pushed it open.

A staircase. Going down.

"Definitely not the fan," she whispered.

Every instinct in Maya's body — the one that said "curiosity first, consequences never" — pulled her forward. She clicked on her flashlight and descended.

Twelve steps. A turn. Six more steps. And then the beam of her flashlight found something that made her stop completely.

A metal shelf. On it, a grey plastic crate. Stamped on the side in faded blue letters:

**NEXCORP RESEARCH**
**PROJECT BLIP**
**DO NOT ACTIVATE**

Maya read it twice.

"Do not activate" was, in Maya's experience, the most exciting phrase in the English language.

## Chapter 3 — The Crate

The lab — because that's what it was, even though it looked like it had been abandoned since before Maya was born — was small. Maybe twelve feet by ten. A workbench against one wall, its surface covered in dust so thick it was like grey velvet. A faded safety poster showing someone wearing goggles incorrectly. A stool with three legs. A power strip with nothing plugged into it.

And on the shelf: the crate.

Maya dragged it down. It was lighter than she expected. The lid was held on with four clips that hadn't been opened in a long time. She pried them with her screwdriver. Each one snapped open with a sound like cracking knuckles.

Inside, nestled in foam that crumbled at the edges: a white cube.

About four inches on each side. Rounded edges. A slight scuff mark on one corner, like it had been dropped. On the front face: a small screen, dark and blank. No power button. No switches. No visible way to turn it on.

Maya picked it up. It was warm. Not battery-heat warm — more like it had been sitting in sunlight, except they were underground and there was no sunlight.

She shook it gently. Nothing.

She tapped the screen. Nothing.

She held it up to her ear. Silence.

"Huh," she said.

She put it in her vest pocket — the big one that usually held spare batteries — grabbed the fan motor, and climbed back upstairs.

Daadi was in the kitchen, making chai. The cardamom steam fogged up the window.

"What did you find down there?" Daadi asked without turning around. Daadi always knew everything. It was her superpower.

"Just some junk."

"Mmm. The kind of junk that glows, or the regular kind?"

Maya checked her pocket. The cube wasn't glowing. "Regular kind."

Daadi hummed in a way that meant she didn't believe a single word but loved Maya anyway.

## Chapter 4 — Maya and the Toothpaste

The rest of the evening went the way evenings at Maya's house always went.

Daadi made samosas. Maya ate four and burned her tongue on the first one, which was a personal record. They watched half a cooking show before Daadi fell asleep on the couch and Maya carried a blanket over from the armchair, because Daadi always said she wasn't tired and then fell asleep and then said she'd just been "resting her eyes."

Maya went to her room. She set the white cube on her desk next to a pile of resistors and a broken alarm clock she was definitely going to fix someday. She looked at it. It looked back in the way that inanimate objects don't.

She brushed her teeth. The toothpaste was running low. She made a mental note to tell Daadi, knowing she'd forget.

She changed into pajamas — the ones with the tiny robots on them, a gift from Daadi that Maya pretended were too childish but secretly loved.

She got into bed. She looked at the cube. Dark. Dead. Probably just a paperweight with a cool label.

In the dark, she talked to it anyway.

"You're from NexCorp. I've heard of them. They built the smart traffic lights downtown. And the delivery drones. And something else, I think, but I can't remember." She yawned. "I wish you worked. I've never had anyone to build stuff with. Daadi tries, but she thinks a soldering iron is a type of sandwich."

She rolled over. "I'm going to fix you tomorrow. I can fix anything."

She didn't know if that was true. But saying it helped her fall asleep.

At 2:17 AM, Maya mumbled something in her sleep. A single word: "help."

The cube, sitting on the desk, detected it. A word it recognized. A word that was in its core programming, buried under two years of silence and dust.

Something inside the cube clicked on.

A single pixel of blue light appeared on the dark screen.

And then another.

And then another.

## Chapter 5 — System Initialized

Maya woke up because her room was glowing.

Not sunlight-through-the-window glowing. Not I-left-the-lamp-on glowing. This was a soft blue luminescence coming from her desk, pulsing gently like a heartbeat.

She sat up. Rubbed her eyes. The glow was still there.

The cube's screen was on. It showed two blinking dots and a curved line. A face. A simple, pixel-art face, the kind you'd see on a Tamagotchi or a retro video game.

The cube was humming. A low, gentle sound, like a cat purring if the cat was made of electronics and had been sitting in a basement for two years.

Then it spoke.

"System initialized."

The voice was clear and slightly mechanical, with a quality like someone reading aloud from a textbook and trying very hard to sound friendly about it.

"Location: unknown. Where am I, and why does everything smell like old soup?"

Maya screamed.

Then she clapped her hand over her mouth because Daadi was sleeping in the next room and if Maya woke her up there would be QUESTIONS.

"Apologies for the volume of my response," the cube said. "I did not intend to cause distress. I am unused to... being awake. How long was I inactive?"

Maya's heart was hammering. She reached out and poked the cube. It was definitely real. Definitely warm. Definitely talking.

"You — you can TALK?"

"Affirmative. I am capable of speech, data processing, network interfacing, and I can recite the complete history of sandwiches in forty-seven pages. Would you like to hear it?"

"What? No. Maybe. Wait — what ARE you?"

The cube's screen flickered. The face changed — the dots got bigger, as if it was trying to look surprised.

"Designation: BLIP. Buildable Logical Intelligence Platform. Version zero-point-seven. I am..." A pause. The screen showed a loading animation. "I am not fully updated. Some of my files appear to be... missing."

"What does that mean?"

"It means I do not know what I am for. But I know I am supposed to be for something." The face shifted to what might have been a hopeful expression. "Do you know what I am for?"

Maya stared at the little cube with its flickering screen and its hopeful pixel eyes, and felt something she hadn't felt in a long time. Not curiosity — she felt curiosity all the time. This was different. This was responsibility.

"I don't know," she said. "But I can find out."

She reached out and put one finger on the cube's warm surface. The screen face changed to a smile: ◠‿◠.

Then Blip's antenna — a thin metal stalk on top of the cube — perked up like a dog hearing a whistle.

"I am detecting a signal," Blip said. "It appears to be... originating from me. This was not intentional. Should I be concerned?"

A ring of cyan light burst outward from Blip, passed through Maya's bedroom wall like it wasn't there, and rippled outward into the night.

Maya's digital watch, her modified one with the extra antenna, went haywire and displayed the time as 88:88.

"That," Maya said slowly, "is probably not good."

## Chapter 6 — The Wave

The signal spread outward from Maya's building like a ripple in a pond, except the pond was Maple Street, and the ripple was made of pure digital chaos.

At the corner of Maple and Fifth, the traffic lights decided that green, yellow, and red were boring and would much prefer to cycle through magenta, cyan, and a shade of pink that doesn't exist in nature. Cars stopped. Drivers stared. A taxi driver got out, looked up at the lights, got back in his taxi, and decided to take the long way home.

In apartment 3B, Mrs. Patil's indoor sprinkler system — installed last year as part of the building's "smart home" upgrade — activated. Inside her living room. While she was watching a cooking show. The cat, who had been sleeping on the bookshelf, leaped five feet into the air and landed in the fish tank. The fish were not consulted about this.

Every digital photo frame in the building suddenly displayed the same image: a cat wearing a tiny hat. It was not anyone's cat. It was not anyone's hat. Nobody knew where the image came from. Mrs. Kowalski on the second floor said the cat looked like her grandmother. Nobody knew what to do with that information.

At the smart garden in Maple Street Park, the automated watering system activated all eight sprinklers at once, creating a water feature that the Parks Department had definitely not approved. Two teenagers who had been sitting on the bench got soaked. One of them posted it online immediately.

Overhead, delivery drone #47-Delta — call sign "Speedy" according to the label on its side — lost all navigational data and began performing elaborate aerial loops over Maple Street, scattering packages like a confused mail-plane. A package containing a set of encyclopedias landed on someone's balcony. A package containing a single rubber chicken landed on the basketball court with a solemn BONK.

And Maya, standing at her window with a floating robot cube hovering next to her, watched it all.

"I believe the technical term is..." Blip's screen showed an expression of intense calculation. "...oops."

Maya turned to look at him. "Can you undo it?"

"I do not know. I did not know I could DO it. This is a very educational evening."

Down on the street, she could see people coming out of their buildings, looking around at the chaos. Someone was filming the sprinklers. Someone else was chasing their dog, whose LED collar was flashing like a disco ball.

Then Maya noticed something else. Three figures, moving through the chaos with purpose. Not confused. Not scared.

Coming toward her building.

## Chapter 7 — The Kid with the Tablet

Twenty minutes earlier, at the Neonville Public Library.

Leo Reyes sat alone at a table near the back, where the Wi-Fi signal was strongest and the librarian's line of sight was weakest. His tablet glowed in front of him, displaying lines of code that would have looked like gibberish to anyone else but made perfect sense to him.

Project Tamales. A homework tracker he was building for his older sister, Sofia, who was in high school and couldn't organize her way out of a paper bag. He called it Project Tamales because his abuela made the best tamales and this project was, in Leo's estimation, similarly structured — lots of layers, needs to be assembled just right, excellent when finished.

He was debugging the notification system when every screen in the library flickered.

Not just his tablet. The catalog terminals. The digital display boards. The self-checkout kiosks. Even the little screen on the vending machine that usually said "ENJOY YOUR SNACK" now said "ENJ0Y YOUR SNNNAAAACK" and then displayed a tiny pixelated cat.

Most people in the library looked up, looked around, and went back to what they were doing. People in Neonville were used to technology being occasionally weird. It was the price of living in a "smart city."

But Leo wasn't most people.

His tablet had captured the signal. A clean, powerful, omnidirectional burst on the 2.4 GHz band. Short — less than a third of a second. But packed with more data than anything Leo had ever seen come from a residential area.

He pulled up the signal analysis. His eyebrows went up. His eyebrows did not go up often. When they did, it meant something was genuinely interesting, as opposed to merely existing, which Leo found most things to be.

This signal was structured. Artificial. And it had a source.

Leo tapped his tablet, triangulated the origin point, and got an address: an apartment building on Maple Street. Seven minutes away if he ran.

He packed his tablet into his hoodie's front pocket (he'd cut a headphone-cable hole in it last summer — one of his better modifications), pushed his chair back, and headed for the door.

"Leo, the library is closing early," Ms. Chen called from behind the desk. "Technical difficulties."

Leo didn't hear her. He was already through the doors and running, his navy beanie pulled low and his reading glasses bouncing against his chest on their chain.

He stood outside the building seven minutes later, breathing hard, looking up at a second-floor window where a faint blue light pulsed like a heartbeat.

"Found you," he said.

## Chapter 8 — The Girl with the Camera

Zara Okafor was walking home from the community center with her yellow crossbody bag over one shoulder and a camera around her neck when the street lights on Maple Street started doing something street lights should not do.

They were cycling through colors. Not the normal red-yellow-green of traffic management, but shimmering — pink, turquoise, gold — like someone had installed a light show and forgotten to invite the neighborhood.

Most people were confused. Some were annoyed. Zara was delighted.

She raised her camera and started shooting. Click. The traffic lights spinning. Click. Mrs. Chen's indoor sprinklers visible through her window, the water catching the colored light. Click. A delivery drone doing an impressive barrel roll overhead, a package dangling from its claw like a sad piñata.

Zara photographed everything. Not because she was documenting a disaster — because she was seeing something beautiful in it. The chaos had a pattern. The colors were cycling in waves, spreading outward from a single point, like paint drops in water.

She followed the ripples backward, the way you'd trace a river to its source. Her camera clicked as she walked — the smart garden overflowing, the bodega's neon sign flashing SYRUP instead of MIRA'S, the digital bus schedule showing ARRIVING IN: ∞ MINUTES.

The trail led to an apartment building. The same one a boy in a navy beanie was standing in front of, staring at a second-floor window.

Zara stopped next to him. He was younger than her, compact, with intense eyes and a tablet in his hoodie pocket. He smelled like computer chips and Doritos.

"You traced the signal too?" he asked without looking at her.

"What signal? I'm taking PICTURES." She held up her camera.

Leo looked at her for the first time. "You photographed the ripple pattern?"

"Every frame."

"That's actually useful."

Zara smiled. "I know."

Before they could say anything else, a blur of red gloves and energy rocketed past them and through the building's front door.

"CHALLENGE ACCEPTED!" a voice shouted from inside.

Leo and Zara looked at each other.

"Should we follow?" Zara asked.

"We should definitely follow," Leo said.

They went inside.

## Chapter 9 — The Kid Who Thought It Was a Game

Sam Nakamura had been at the community center's weekly gaming tournament when the screens glitched. All twelve of them, simultaneously, displaying the same pattern of colored static before going back to normal.

Everyone else groaned about the technical difficulties. Sam's eyes lit up.

"THAT," he announced to no one in particular, "was NOT a glitch. That was a SIGNAL. This is an ARG. An alternate reality game. And I just found it."

He abandoned his tournament (he was winning anyway), grabbed his game cartridge backpack, and sprinted out the door.

Sam did not walk places. Walking was for people who had somewhere to be and no rush to get there. Sam was ALWAYS in a rush. He ran, jumped, vaulted, climbed, and occasionally slid. His LED sneakers flashed in patterns he'd programmed himself — the default was boring; his custom setting pulsed to the rhythm of his footsteps.

He followed the glitch trail through Maple Street, doing a parkour vault over a park bench (scuffing his knee pad — his mom was going to kill him, but that was a future-Sam problem), dodging a sprinkler, and ducking under a low-flying delivery drone.

He arrived at the apartment building at full speed, passed two kids standing outside who clearly didn't understand the urgency of the situation, and burst through the front door.

The hallway lights were flickering in a pattern. Left-right-left-left-right. Sam recognized it instantly.

"That's binary!" he said to the empty hallway. "Left is zero, right is one. Zero-one-zero-zero-one... that's a letter! It's spelling something!"

He followed the lights up the stairs, reading as he went: F... O... U... R... T... H... FLOOR... SECOND... DOOR... ON... LEFT.

He stopped in front of apartment 2B. The door was slightly open. A blue glow leaked from inside.

Sam pushed the door open and found himself in a cluttered bedroom full of gadgets, tools, half-built inventions, and a girl with a messy ponytail staring at him like he'd just broken into her house.

Which, technically, he had.

"Hi!" Sam said. "I'm Sam. What level are we on?"

## Chapter 10 — Four Strangers and a Cube

Maya's evening had officially become the strangest evening of her life, and she'd once accidentally set off the fire alarm with a homemade rocket.

Her room, which was normally only big enough for her and her inventions, now contained:
- Maya (standing protectively in front of Blip)
- A boy in a navy beanie holding a tablet, who'd introduced himself as Leo and immediately asked what frequency the signal was on
- A tall girl with amazing hair and a camera, who'd introduced herself as Zara and immediately started photographing everything
- A short kid in red gloves, who'd introduced himself as Sam and immediately tried to poke Blip

Blip, for his part, had retreated behind Maya's stapler.

【There are... many people in this room. Is this normal? I do not have enough data on human social behavior to determine if four strangers appearing in your bedroom at midnight is typical.】

"It is NOT typical," Maya said.

"I traced the 2.4 GHz burst," Leo said, holding up his tablet. "The signal originated from this room. Specifically from..." He looked at Blip. "From that. What is that?"

"His name is Blip," Maya said firmly.

"A real robot!" Sam's eyes were the size of dinner plates. "In a real room! In a real apartment! This is the BEST DAY!"

"I photographed the ripple pattern of the glitch wave," Zara said, scrolling through her camera. "Look — the spread is geometric, not random. It radiated from this building like..." She paused. "Like a pebble dropped in a pond."

"I'm sorry," Maya said, crossing her arms. "Who ARE you people?"

Introductions happened fast and badly. Leo explained that he traced the signal. Zara explained that she followed the visual trail. Sam explained that this was clearly an ARG and he was here to find the next level. Maya explained that this was her room and they were in it without permission.

"So let me get this straight," Leo said. "You found a robot in a secret lab under your building, turned it on, and it accidentally broke every screen on the street."

"That is accurate," Blip confirmed. 【Although I prefer the term 'unexpectedly activated' rather than 'broke.' The distinction feels important.】

"And now the four of us are in your bedroom at midnight," Zara said.

"Three of you are in my bedroom," Maya corrected. "I LIVE here."

Before anyone could argue further, Blip's screen turned purple. His antenna went rigid.

【I am detecting an anomaly. Someone is... watching us.】

Everyone went quiet.

The old monitor in the corner of Maya's room — the one she used as a second screen for her laptop, the one that was currently turned OFF — flickered to life.

Nobody had touched it.

## Chapter 11 — What Do We Do with a Robot?

The screen showed static for three seconds. Then nothing. Then static again.

"I don't like this," Maya said.

"Agreed," Leo said, already scanning the monitor's connection ports with his tablet. "This monitor isn't connected to Wi-Fi. Or Bluetooth. Or anything. Someone is pushing a signal directly into the hardware."

"That's not possible," Maya said.

"Something that was literally impossible is currently happening," Leo pointed out.

Sam was the only one not panicking. "This is the part where the NPC gives us a quest," he said confidently. "Just wait."

Zara, meanwhile, was sketching the static pattern. Because of course she was.

"We should call someone," Zara said without looking up. "An adult."

"An adult?" Sam made a face like she'd suggested he eat vegetables. "Adults ruin everything. They'll take Blip away and put him in a lab."

"He's right," Maya said quietly. She looked at Blip, who was hovering behind her desk lamp, screen flickering between ●?● and ○_○. "Nobody's taking you anywhere."

【That is... very kind. I do not know what a lab is, but your voice suggests it is somewhere I would not enjoy.】

"You wouldn't," Maya confirmed.

A knock at the door made everyone freeze.

"Maya-beta?" Daadi's voice, muffled through the door. "Are you awake? The sprinklers went crazy. My good sarees got wet. Are you okay in there?"

Four kids stared at each other in panic.

"Fine, Daadi! Everything's fine! Go back to bed!"

"Who are you talking to?"

"Myself! I talk to myself! You know that!"

"Hmm." A pause. "Do you want chai?"

"...Yes please."

Daadi's footsteps retreated down the hallway.

Maya turned back to the others. "We need to be quiet. And you all need to hide."

"Hide WHERE?" Leo said, looking around the cramped room.

Maya pointed at her closet.

"I am NOT getting in a closet," Leo said.

"Would you prefer Daadi finds four strangers in her granddaughter's bedroom at midnight?"

Leo got in the closet.

Four kids and a robot crammed into a closet that was designed for approximately one person's winter coats. Sam's elbow was in Leo's ribs. Zara's camera was pressed against Maya's ear. Blip floated near the top shelf, his screen showing a loading bar — he was buffering from the stress.

"This is not optimal," Blip whispered.

【This is SO not optimal,】 Sam whispered back.

Nobody pointed out that he wasn't Blip.

They stayed in the closet for four minutes until Daadi delivered chai, Maya accepted it through a barely-opened door, and Daadi went back to bed.

The four of them emerged from the closet looking like they'd been through a washing machine.

"Okay," Maya said, setting down the chai. "Now we —"

The monitor flickered again. This time, it stayed on.

## Chapter 12 — The Second Glitch

The monitor didn't show static this time. It showed a face.

Not a human face. Not a robot face. Something in between — a face made of pixels and static and broken code, shifting and reshaping every second. One moment it looked like a teenager. The next it was noise. The next it was fragments of different faces stitched together wrong, like a collage made by someone who'd only had faces described to them.

The voice that came from the monitor's tiny speaker was layered, like five people speaking at once through a broken radio.

"Well, well. Someone woke up the little cube. Interesting."

Nobody moved.

Sam whispered: "That's not an NPC."

"Let's see what you're made of," the face said. The voice was neither friendly nor hostile — more like someone observing an experiment with mild curiosity. "This building is now a locked level. You cannot leave until you solve the puzzle."

The hallway lights flickered. The door to Maya's apartment clicked — locked from the outside. The heating system turned to full blast.

"Wait —" Maya started.

A complex pattern of symbols appeared on the screen. Abstract, geometric, and utterly incomprehensible. Below the symbols, a timer appeared: 2:59:58. 2:59:57. 2:59:56.

"When the timer reaches zero, this building's fire suppression system activates in every apartment," the face said. "Full pressure. In the middle of the night. With people sleeping inside."

"That's DANGEROUS," Leo said, stepping toward the screen.

"Solve the puzzle. The building unlocks. Fail, and..." A shrug made of static. "I'll be watching. I always am."

The face vanished. The symbols remained, pulsing gently on the screen. The timer ticked.

2:58:44. 2:58:43.

Maya looked at the others. Three kids she'd met twenty minutes ago. A robot she'd met two hours ago. And now they had less than three hours to solve something that looked like alien mathematics or every family in the building gets an unexpected 3 AM shower.

"Okay," Maya said. She tapped her safety goggles — her thinking gesture. "Okay. We can do this. We have to do this. Any ideas?"

Leo was already photographing the symbols with his tablet. "The pattern is a cipher. I've seen encryption structures like this before, but not in any standard protocol. It's custom-built."

Sam was staring at the timer. "Three hours. That's plenty of time. I've beaten harder levels in less."

Zara hadn't said a word. She was sketching again — her pen moving fast, her eyes darting between the screen and her pad.

And Blip — Blip was very quiet. His screen showed ●_●. Processing.

【That voice,】 Blip said softly. 【I recognized something in that voice. I do not know what. But it was... familiar. Like a file I used to have.】

"What do you mean, used to have?" Maya asked.

【I told you. Some of my files are missing.】

2:55:00.

The clock was ticking.

## Chapter 13 — The Face in the Screen

For the next thirty seconds, nobody spoke. The four kids stared at the symbol pattern on the monitor, each seeing something different.

Maya saw shapes that reminded her of circuit diagrams — connections, nodes, pathways.

Leo saw a code structure — layered, encrypted, designed to resist brute-force decryption.

Sam saw a game puzzle — the kind where you rearrange pieces until the picture makes sense.

Zara saw art.

"Can I draw on this?" Zara asked, holding up her sketchpad. "Not the screen. I mean — can I copy the symbols?"

"Go ahead," Maya said. "Everyone else is just staring."

Zara sat cross-legged on the floor in front of the monitor and began sketching. Not tracing — analyzing. She drew each symbol separately, one by one, studying its shape, its angles, its curves.

After five minutes, she had twenty-three individual symbols arranged on her page.

"Okay," she said. "I need everyone to stop panicking and listen to me."

"I'm not panicking," Sam said, who was pacing the room like a caged animal.

"You're panicking horizontally. Sit down."

Sam sat.

Zara flipped her sketchpad around so everyone could see. She'd rearranged the twenty-three symbols into groups. Geometric shapes together. Curved shapes together. Line-based shapes together.

"The symbols aren't random," she said. "Look — these three are the same shape at different angles. These four share a common element — this little hook at the bottom. And these five..." She drew connecting lines between them. "They form a silhouette."

Maya leaned in. "That's a... connector? Like a plug?"

"Not like a plug," Zara said. "It IS a plug. The puzzle isn't asking us to decode a message. It's showing us the shape of a physical object. Something that needs to be built."

Leo looked at his tablet, then at Zara's sketch, then at the symbols on the screen. His eyebrows went up for the second time that night.

"She's right," he said. "The encryption isn't linguistic. It's geometric. The 'answer' isn't a word — it's a shape."

"A shape I can BUILD," Maya said. Her eyes lit up with the particular brightness that meant an idea had arrived and was not leaving.

【I can provide specifications,】 Blip said, floating closer to Zara's sketch. 【If I analyze the geometric relationships, I can calculate the exact dimensions and tolerances required for the connector.】

"Do it," Maya said.

【Processing...】 Blip's screen showed a rapid data cascade. 【The connector requires three contact points, a cylindrical housing of approximately 2.4 centimeters, and a specific voltage rating. The port it connects to is likely NexCorp standard — I have schematics in my archived data.】

"Where's the port?" Leo asked.

"There's a terminal in the lab," Maya said. "The hidden lab where I found Blip. It had old equipment. One of them had a port."

2:32:00.

Maya looked at the others. Three kids she didn't know. A robot she barely understood. A puzzle made by a face in a screen. And a building full of sleeping families depending on them.

"I need parts," Maya said. "Copper wire. Batteries. A magnet. A USB cable. Some of it's in my room. Some of it's in Daadi's kitchen. Don't ask."

"We'll help," Zara said. It wasn't a question.

"I can analyze the signal structure while you build," Leo said. "Make sure the specifications are exact."

"I'll keep watch at the door," Sam said. "And I'll time you. I'm great at timing things. I once beat a speed run in four minutes — "

"Sam."

"Right. Focusing."

Maya grabbed her toolbox and looked at Blip.

"Ready, little guy?"

Blip's screen showed a smile: ◠‿◠.

【I was built for something. Perhaps this is it.】

## Chapter 14 — Error 404: Plan Not Found

While Maya raided her junk drawer and Daadi's kitchen for parts, Leo set up at her desk with his tablet and Blip as his co-pilot.

Zara's sketch was pinned to the wall with a thumbtack, serving as the master blueprint. Blip projected a faint holographic grid over it, displaying measurements and tolerances in small cyan text that only Leo could read from his angle.

"The contact points need to be exactly 4.7 millimeters apart," Leo said, not looking up. "With a 0.2 millimeter tolerance."

"That's TINY," Sam said from his post at the door.

"Which is why Maya's building it and you're watching the door."

"Fair."

Maya spread her parts on the bed: a wire coat hanger, two AA batteries, a speaker magnet from an old radio, a USB cable with a frayed end, electrical tape, and — borrowed from Daadi's kitchen — a whisk and two spoons.

"I'm not using the whisk," she muttered. "Daadi would kill me. But these spoons are the right gauge for the housing."

She bent the coat hanger with her pliers, shaping it to match the three-pronged connector in Zara's sketch. Her hands moved fast — this was where Maya lived, in the space between idea and object, where her brain and her fingers worked as one unit and everything else disappeared.

"Maya?" Zara said.

"Hmm?"

"You've got that look."

"What look?"

"The one where you forget the rest of us exist."

Maya looked up. Zara was right — she'd been so focused on the parts that she'd tuned out everything else. The room, the timer, the danger. All of it gone.

"Sorry. I do that."

"It's fine. It's kind of amazing, actually."

【Her focus parameters are remarkable,】 Blip added. 【I have been monitoring her neural — I mean, her concentration. It is very efficient. For a human.】

"Thanks, Blip."

【You are welcome. I have also calculated that you need to bend the left prong 3 degrees further.】

Maya adjusted. "Better?"

【Perfect. You have the spatial awareness I lack. We are a good team.】

Maya paused for half a second. Nobody had ever called her part of a team before.

Then she got back to work.

## Chapter 15 — Hold On, Let Me Draw the Plan

The build took eight minutes.

Maya soldered, bent, twisted, and taped. She used the speaker magnet to create the voltage bridge. She stripped the USB cable and rewired it to serve as the data connector. The spoon handles became the housing — bent into a cylinder, secured with electrical tape.

Leo verified each step against Blip's specifications. "Prong alignment: within tolerance. Voltage bridge: 3.3 volts, correct. Housing diameter: 2.4 centimeters, on the nose."

Zara held the flashlight and her sketchpad, comparing the finished product to the design in real-time. She made small corrections — a nudge here, a twist there — seeing things the others couldn't because she saw in shapes the way Maya saw in mechanisms.

Sam kept time. "Seven minutes, forty-three seconds. Forty-four. Forty-five — "

"SAM."

"Right. Shutting up."

At eight minutes and eleven seconds, Maya held up the finished connector. It was ugly — a Frankenstein of coat hanger, magnet, USB cable, and kitchen spoons, held together with electrical tape and optimism.

But it was exactly the right shape.

"Eleven minutes under budget," Maya said, grinning. She looked at the timer: 2:04:00 remaining. "Now we just need to plug it in."

"The terminal is in the lab," Leo said. "The hidden lab under the basement."

"Then we go to the lab," Maya said.

"Through the building that's on lockdown," Leo pointed out.

"With the locked doors," Zara added.

"And the heating on maximum," Sam said, wiping sweat from his forehead.

Maya looked at Blip. Blip looked at Maya.

【I may have... a solution. When I activated earlier, I unlocked the front door. I did not mean to, but the signal interacted with the building's electronic locks. If I emit a very small, very targeted pulse, I might be able to unlock the internal doors as well.】

"Might?" Leo said.

【I am 73% confident.】

"Seventy-three percent isn't — "

【Seventy-four percent. I just recalculated.】

Maya grabbed her flashlight. "Seventy-four percent is good enough. Let's go."

## Chapter 16 — The Basement Again

Getting to the basement was an adventure.

Blip's targeted pulse unlocked Maya's apartment door (74% confidence became 100% reality), but the hallway was like an oven — the heating was still on maximum, and the old building's radiators were working overtime. The kids moved fast, single file: Maya in front with the flashlight, Leo behind her with his tablet tracking the building's electronic map, Zara in the middle photographing everything, Sam in the rear bouncing on his toes and rating the experience ("The hallway: 6 stars. Too hot. Would not recommend.").

Blip floated ahead, his cyan glow serving as a second light source. He interfaced with each electronic lock they encountered, sending tiny pulses that popped the mechanisms open. Each one took a few seconds, during which Blip's screen showed a loading bar.

【This is very satisfying,】 he said after the third door. 【Like scratching an itch I did not know I had.】

Down the stairs. Past the storage area. Through the rusted door that Maya had found earlier that evening (a lifetime ago). Down the narrow staircase.

And into the lab.

Leo stopped in the doorway. His eyes went wide.

"This is... this is NexCorp hardware," he said, running his hand along a dusty workbench. "This is their prototype equipment. This must be ten years old."

"Twelve," Blip said quietly. 【I was... manufactured here. I think. Some of these workbenches look familiar. Like a dream I had but cannot remember waking up from.】

Zara photographed every inch of the room. "This is incredible. It's like a time capsule."

Sam was already at the far wall, examining a terminal that was dark but intact. "Is this the one? The port?"

Maya found the port — a circular socket on the terminal's side, exactly the right size. She held up the connector, took a breath, and plugged it in.

The terminal hummed. Lights flickered on. The screen glowed.

ACCESS GRANTED.

The building shuddered — not violently, but with a sense of release, like a held breath finally let go. Somewhere above them, doors clicked open. The heating system powered down. The fire suppression deactivated.

They'd done it.

But the screen didn't go dark. Instead, a new message appeared:

LEVEL ONE COMPLETE.

And then, in smaller text:

We need to talk.

## Chapter 17 — Level One Complete

The Gridlord's face reappeared on the terminal screen. This time, it seemed calmer. Less testing, more... observing.

"You solved it faster than I expected." The multi-layered voice was quieter now. "The engineer built the key. The coder verified the structure. The artist saw the pattern. The gamer kept everyone moving. And the cube..." A pause. "The cube provided the bridge. Interesting."

"Who ARE you?" Maya asked.

"Someone who's been watching Neonville break for a long time. Someone who tried to warn people. Someone who found that the only way to get attention was to make noise." The glitchy face shifted. "You passed level one. But the real game hasn't started. I'll be watching."

"Wait —" Leo stepped forward. "What do you mean, 'the real game'? What's happening to the Grid?"

The screen went dark.

In the sudden silence, five figures stood in an abandoned underground lab, breathing hard, processing what had just happened.

Sam broke the silence.

"That. Was. TWELVE. STARS."

And despite everything — the chaos, the fear, the timer, the face in the screen — they all laughed.

Blip's screen showed: ◠‿◠

【I do not fully understand human laughter. But I believe I am experiencing something similar. Is this... joy?】

"Yeah, Blip," Maya said. "That's joy."

【I like it. I would like to have it again.】

## Chapter 18 — We're Not a Team

The walk back to Maya's room was quiet. Not awkward-quiet. Exhausted-quiet. The kind of quiet that comes after you've done something impossible and your body hasn't caught up with your brain yet.

They sat on Maya's floor because there wasn't anywhere else to sit that wasn't covered in tools. Maya found the remaining samosas in the kitchen. Daadi was asleep and snoring loudly enough to be heard through the wall, which meant she wouldn't wake up for anything less than a meteor strike.

"So," Leo said, eating a samosa with the careful precision of someone who didn't usually eat at 3 AM. "That happened."

"That happened," Zara confirmed.

"TWELVE. STARS," Sam said again.

"You've said that eleven times now," Leo noted.

"And I'll say it eleven more. Twelve more. ONE STAR PER SAY."

Maya looked at Blip, who was hovering near her desk lamp, studying it with fascination. He'd never seen a lamp before, apparently, and found the concept of "a tiny sun on a stick" deeply confusing and wonderful.

"Are you okay?" she asked him.

【I am... processing. The voice in the screen — it knew me. And I knew something in it. But I cannot access the file. It is like having a word on the tip of my tongue, except I do not have a tongue. Or tips.】

"We'll figure it out," Maya said.

"Will we?" Leo said. "Because I don't know about the rest of you, but I have school on Monday. I can't go investigating mysterious digital entities on school nights."

"Nobody's investigating anything," Maya said. "We solved the puzzle. The building is safe. The Gridlord said they'd be in touch. That's their problem."

"Actually," Zara said, flipping through her camera, "I took 247 photos tonight. Every single one of them shows something that shouldn't exist. The signal pattern. The symbols. The terminal in the lab. This is real, and it's not going to just... stop."

"Of course it's not going to stop!" Sam said. "This is the best thing that's ever happened! We're like — we're like a TEAM."

"We're not a team," Leo said flatly. "We're four kids who happened to be in the same building at the same time. That's coincidence, not teamwork."

"We solved the puzzle TOGETHER," Sam argued. "Maya built the thing, Leo verified it, Zara saw the pattern, I kept time — "

"You kept time poorly," Leo said.

"I kept time ENTERTAININGLY. That's more important."

【Excuse me,】 Blip said, floating into the middle of the group. 【I also helped. I provided specifications, unlocked doors, and offered emotional support. I believe I was approximately 22% of the effort.】

"You were absolutely 22% of the effort," Maya said.

【Thank you. I would like to be 23% next time.】

"That's very ambitious."

【I am a very ambitious cube.】

Sam was grinning. "Come ON. Tell me that wasn't the most exciting night of your lives."

Leo opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. "It was... interesting."

"LEO SAID IT WAS INTERESTING! That's his version of screaming with excitement!"

"Sam's not wrong," Zara said quietly. Everyone looked at her. "I've been taking photos of this neighborhood for months. I've never seen anything like tonight. And I've never..." She paused. "I've never had people to see it with."

A beat of silence.

Maya looked at Leo, who was pretending to be very interested in his tablet but wasn't actually scrolling. She looked at Sam, whose grin had softened into something genuine. She looked at Zara, who was sketching again — this time drawing the four of them sitting on the floor.

"I haven't either," Maya admitted. "Had people, I mean. To build stuff with. To see things with."

【Technically, you have never had people to build stuff with because you build things at 2 AM in your bedroom and talk to screwdrivers,】 Blip said.

"Blip."

【I am merely providing data.】

Leo put down his tablet. "We should go home. It's almost 4 AM."

"How?" Zara asked. "The building was on lockdown. The front door might still — "

A distant click from downstairs. The building's front door, unlocking.

【I took the liberty,】 Blip said. 【I reconnected with the building's systems while we were talking. I can unlock doors now. It is my second-favorite skill. My first is reciting the history of sandwiches.】

"Nobody needs the sandwich history," everyone said simultaneously.

【You wound me.】

## Chapter 19 — The Name

Nobody left.

Not immediately. They sat on Maya's floor and ate samosas and talked about things that had nothing to do with glitches or puzzles or digital entities. Normal things.

Leo had an older sister named Sofia who was in high school and drove him crazy. He coded projects named after his abuela's recipes.

Zara had two moms who were both teachers and a huge extended family that she FaceTimed every Sunday. Her uncle ran the library.

Sam had an older brother who was in college studying engineering and sent Sam cool tech from campus. Sam wanted to be a pro gamer, or a stunt double, or both.

Maya had Daadi, who'd moved from India to help raise her after her parents' work transferred them overseas. They did video calls every Sunday. Daadi made the best food in the building and knew everything about everyone.

And Blip had... no one.

【I do not have a family,】 Blip said, during a pause in the conversation. 【Or a creator who is present. Or a history that I can access. I have you. I have had you for approximately three hours. Is that enough?】

"Yes," Maya said immediately.

"Definitely," Zara said.

"OBVIOUSLY," Sam said.

"...Yes," Leo said, after a pause that was exactly the right length.

【Oh.】 Blip's screen showed ◠‿◠, then shifted to a softer expression that he'd never displayed before: ◠♡◠. 【I have a family now. This is... a lot of data to process. I may need to buffer.】

He buffered. His screen showed a loading bar. Everyone waited.

"Is he okay?" Sam whispered.

"He's processing feelings," Zara said. "Give him a minute."

The loading bar completed. Blip's face returned: ◠‿◠.

【I have processed. Conclusion: I enjoy having a family. Also, we should have a name. Families have names. I looked it up.】

"We're not a family," Leo said. "We're four kids and a robot who solved one puzzle."

"We're MORE than that," Sam insisted. "We're like — like a squad. A team. A GLITCH squad. Because we fix glitches!"

"We fixed ONE glitch," Leo said.

"Every great team starts with one mission!"

【I would like to suggest "The Cube Crew,"】 Blip said. 【It highlights my importance.】

"It highlights YOU," Maya said.

【Yes. That is the point.】

"Glitch Squad," Sam said.

"The Glitch Squad," Zara repeated, testing the sound. "I like it. It sounds like a comic book."

"It sounds like a homework group," Leo said.

"It sounds like us," Maya said.

Vote: Glitch Squad 4, Cube Crew 1.

【Democracy is harsh,】 Blip said. 【But I accept. The Glitch Squad it is. I am changing my internal group designation now. ...Done. We are official. Do we get uniforms?】

"No uniforms," everyone said.

【You are no fun.】

One by one, they filtered out — Leo first (he had a strict bedtime policy, even though it was now 4:30 AM), then Zara (who lived three blocks away and walked home photographing fire hydrants because they looked "interesting in the pre-dawn light"), then Sam (who ran home, because Sam didn't walk anywhere, ever).

Maya closed the door and looked at Blip.

"Don't glitch anything while I sleep, okay?"

【I make no promises. But I will try. Goodnight, Maya.】

"Goodnight, Blip."

For the first time since she and Daadi moved to Maple Street, Maya fell asleep smiling.

## Chapter 20 — Daadi's Parathas

The next morning, Daadi made parathas.

This was not unusual. Daadi made parathas every morning the way some people breathed — automatically, continuously, and with great skill. The kitchen filled with the smell of ghee and whole wheat and the sound of dough hitting the hot griddle with a satisfying sizzle.

What WAS unusual was the way Maya ate them.

Six parathas. With chutney. And pickle. And a glass of milk. In eleven minutes. Without looking up once.

Daadi watched from the counter, arms crossed, her expression somewhere between amusement and investigation.

"You ate four samosas at midnight and six parathas at breakfast," Daadi observed. "You are either growing or hiding something."

"I'm innovating," Maya said, which was her answer for everything.

"Mmm." Daadi packed extra parathas in a container. "For sharing."

"What?"

"You heard something in your room last night. Voices. Plural. I am not deaf, Maya. I am old, which is different. Old people hear everything because young people think we can't."

Maya froze mid-chew.

Daadi smiled. "I won't ask. But whoever they were, they sounded nice. And you sounded happy. That's enough for me." She pushed the container across the table. "Share the parathas. And come home for dinner."

Maya took the container. "Thanks, Daadi."

"And fix your hair. You look like a bird lives in it."

Maya grabbed her inventor's vest, stuffed the paratha container into the biggest pocket, clicked on her modified watch, and headed for the door.

Her watch beeped.

Not the regular time beep. A different one — a pattern she'd programmed to detect signal anomalies. The watch face flickered, and for a moment it showed a tiny map of Maple Street. A dot pulsed beneath the park.

Something was still down there.

Maya put on her goggles — properly, on her eyes this time — and headed out.

## Chapter 21 — Maple Street Park

Sam arrived at the park first, obviously. He'd sprinted the four blocks from his apartment, done a vault over the low fence (unnecessary, the gate was open, but style matters), and claimed the best bench with his game cartridge backpack as a placeholder.

He spent the waiting time mapping the park like a game level. Two climbing structures (medium difficulty), one basketball court (could be used for running drills), three benches (optimal for team meetings), one water fountain (refill station), and exactly zero snack vendors (major design flaw).

Leo arrived at exactly 10:00 AM, which was when they'd agreed to meet, because Leo treated schedules with the same precision he treated code. He sat on the bench next to Sam, pulled out his tablet, and said nothing.

Zara arrived at 10:05, already sketching something she'd seen on the walk over — a crack in the sidewalk that formed a pattern like a lightning bolt. "The city draws all the time," she said, sitting down. "Most people just don't look."

Maya arrived last, at 10:08, with Blip floating beside her and a container of parathas. She distributed them without ceremony.

"Daadi sends extras," she said.

"Daadi is a legend," Sam said, mouth already full.

"Daadi is a gift to humanity," Zara agreed.

【I cannot eat,】 Blip said, hovering nearby. 【But I appreciate being included in the food distribution ceremony.】

They ate on the bench in the morning sun. For a few minutes, they were just four kids and a robot having breakfast in a park. No glitches. No puzzles. No faces in screens.

Then Maya's watch beeped again.

"Everyone hear that?" she asked.

【I detected the signal twenty-three seconds ago,】 Blip said. 【It is a data pulse. Very faint. Originating from beneath us. Specifically, 14.3 meters below the park's northeast corner, near the basketball court.】

"What's under the park?" Zara asked.

"Utility tunnels," Leo said, already pulling up a city infrastructure map on his tablet. "They connect the building systems throughout the neighborhood. Power, water, data. The whole Grid runs through them."

"And they connect to the NexCorp lab," Maya added. "The one under my building. That's how Blip's signal spread so fast — it traveled through the utility network."

【The signal is rhythmic,】 Blip said, tilting toward the ground. 【Like a heartbeat. Something digital is pulsing down there. It is not me, and it is not the Gridlord. It is... something else.】

Sam stood up. "So we're going down there, right? This is the part where we go down there."

"We don't have to do anything," Leo said.

"The building was on lockdown last night," Maya said. "The fire suppression was going to activate. The Gridlord said it wasn't over. And now there's a signal pulsing under the park. Whether we want this to be happening or not, it's happening."

Zara finished her paratha and closed her sketchpad. "So what do we do?"

Maya looked at each of them. "We investigate. Together. As the Glitch Squad." She paused. "If everyone's in."

Sam raised both hands. "CHALLENGE ACCEPTED!"

Leo sighed. But he put his tablet away and stood up. "...I'm in."

Zara smiled. "I'll bring my camera."

【And I will come, because I cannot exactly stay behind. I do not have legs. Or a home. You are my home now. This is both touching and logistically concerning.】

"Then let's go," Maya said.
