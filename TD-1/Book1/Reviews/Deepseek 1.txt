# Review: THE GLITCH SQUAD — Book 1: THE LOST SIGNAL

## For Readers Ages 8–11

---

## VERDICT: Bestseller Potential — HIGH

This book has *the spark*. The kind that turns readers into fans and fans into kids who camp outside bookstores. It's not perfect—but the things it gets right, it gets *brilliantly* right.

---

## WHAT WORKS (And It's Most of It)

### The Characters

Maya, Leo, Zara, and Sam are not archetypes. They're *people*. That's rare in children's fiction, and it's everything.

- **Maya** talks to her inventions. She wears goggles on her forehead more than her eyes. She has a messy ponytail and a pencil in her hair. She's not "the smart one"—she's a kid who happens to be brilliant at building things and not brilliant at people. Her arc from isolation to reluctant leader is subtle and earned.

- **Leo** could have been the annoying know-it-all. Instead, his precision is a genuine strength *and* a genuine limitation. His "eyebrows up" as his tell is a wonderful little character detail that young readers will clock and love.

- **Zara** sees the world in patterns and beauty. Her photography isn't a quirk—it's her *operating system*. The way she solves the visual cipher through art rather than code is one of the book's most satisfying moments.

- **Sam** is the biggest risk. A loud, fast-talking gamer kid could be unbearable. But the manuscript gives him depth exactly when it needs to—his quiet moment on the bench, his "I contain multitudes," his realization that the timer wasn't counting down to a game over but to real consequences. His "I know it's not a game—that's why I'm the right person for this" is a line that will land.

- **Blip** is the heart of the book, and he shouldn't be—he's a cube with a pixel face. But his earnestness, his confusion about emotions ("is this... joy?"), his obsessive need to be included, and his devastating quiet moment when he says "I could always feel you before... losing the signal that told me you were there" will *wreck* your target audience in the best way.

### The Diversity

This is how you do it. Maya is Indian, with a Daadi who makes parathas and chai and knows everything. Leo is Latinx, with an abuela and tamales and a sister named Sofia. Sam is Japanese-American, with an older brother in college. Zara is Black, with two moms who are teachers. None of this is announced with a flag or a lesson. It's just *who they are*. The food, the family structures, the cultural details—they're woven into the fabric of the story, not stapled onto it.

This book will sell internationally because *everyone* can see themselves in it, and no one feels like a checkbox.

### The Pacing

Thirty-six chapters. Short. Punchy. Each one ends on a hook that makes you say "one more chapter." The 8-11 age range has notoriously short attention spans for prose, but this book *moves*. The action beats are clear without being chaotic. The quiet moments (the rooftop, the parathas, the bench in the park) land because they're earned.

The timer in Chapter 12 is a masterstroke. Kids love countdowns. Three hours feels urgent but not impossible. Every time the number updates, the reader feels it.

### The Emotional Intelligence

This book respects its readers. It doesn't explain emotions—it shows them.

- Sam's doubt on the bench isn't a lecture about self-worth. It's him playing a mindless puzzle game while his brain churns, wondering if he's "too much."
- Leo's "I keep to myself" says everything it needs to say.
- Maya's realization that "she'd never let anyone get close enough to form a team" is devastating because it's *shown*, not told.
- Blip's "I have a family now. This is... a lot of data to process" is funnier and sadder than any robot has a right to be.

The book understands that 8-11 year olds are experiencing real loneliness, real self-doubt, real fear. It doesn't dismiss those feelings. It gives them a story where those feelings are valid *and* where kids can still be heroes.

### The Villain (Sort Of)

The Gridlord isn't a villain. The book *nearly* tricks you into thinking it is—the voice through the monitor, the timer, the threats. But the reveal that they're a trapped person (Kira) who's been alone for two years and just needed someone to *listen* is genuinely affecting. This is sophisticated storytelling for this age range, and it works because it's seeded throughout (the Gridlord's messages get progressively less threatening, more desperate, more grateful).

NexCorp as the real antagonist—a corporation that built something broken and walked away—is smart. Kids understand adults letting them down. They understand systems that don't work and people who won't fix them. That's why the Glitch Squad's mission matters.

### The "Glitch" as Metaphor

The technological glitches in the story map beautifully onto emotional and social fractures. The Grid is breaking because the bridge (Blip) was never finished—abandoned because the builders were afraid. That's not subtle, but it doesn't need to be. Young readers will understand that the book is about something bigger than traffic lights and sprinkler systems. It's about what happens when people stop taking care of things—and what happens when new people show up to fix them.

## WHAT NEEDS WORK (Minor, But Important)

### Daadi's Role

Daadi is wonderful, but she's *too* knowing. She hears voices in Maya's room, says "old people hear everything," and then just... goes back to making parathas? A grandmother this perceptive would push harder, or we need a reason she doesn't. One line—"I won't ask. But you sounded happy, and that's enough"—is lovely but also conveniently dismissive. If she's this aware, her not investigating further feels like plot convenience.

**Suggestion:** Add one moment where Daadi almost catches them—a knock at the lab door, a call from upstairs that's too close—so her restraint feels chosen rather than convenient. Or reveal that she knows *exactly* what's happening and is letting Maya have this because she remembers being young and having secrets. Either works.

### Leo's Voice

Occasionally, Leo sounds like an adult explaining technology rather than a ten-year-old who's good at it. "The encryption isn't linguistic—it's geometric" is a line that works. "The signal appears to be originating from me. This was not intentional" is Blip's voice, not a kid's. Leo's dialogue could loosen up 10-15%. He can be precise without being stiff. Give him one moment of genuine kid frustration—not just "sighing" but actually snapping, then apologizing.

### Sam's Transformation

Sam's arc from game-brain to real-world hero is well-drawn, but it happens *fast*. One chapter he's treating everything like an ARG; the next he's saying "I know it's not a game—that's why I'm the right person." The bench scene with Leo does heavy lifting, but we need one more beat—maybe Sam failing at something small and realizing the stakes in his body, not just his head.

**Suggestion:** During the traffic light climb, have him freeze for half a second—not from fear of heights, but from the sudden awareness that *people are watching and this matters*. Then he climbs anyway. That half-second tells the whole story.

### The Middle Chapters (18–22)

The stretch between "Level One Complete" and the server room discovery is where the book breathes—maybe too much. Chapter 18 (We're Not a Team) and Chapter 19 (The Name) are lovely character moments, but together they slow momentum. Consider merging them or trimming 20%. The reader knows they're going to become a team. You don't need to convince them twice.

### Blip's Sacrifice

Chapter 29 (Blip's Choice) is the emotional climax, and it's *almost* perfect. But "losing the signal that told me you were there" is so good that it makes the preceding dialogue feel a bit procedural. The countdown (fifty-seven seconds) is effective, but the actual moment of darkness could be one line longer. Let the reader sit in the silence. Let Maya's hand on his cold surface linger. The relief when he comes back will be that much sweeter.

### The Ending

Chapter 36 (Level 2) ends on a teaser for Book 2—fine, expected. But the line "It pulsed near NexCorp Tower. Downtown. And it was growing" is a bit generic. The best part of the ending is Kira's message: "Thank you for showing up." That's the emotional button. End there, or end on that feeling. The "downtown glitch" could be the first line of Book 2, not the last line of Book 1.

## MARKETING & BESTSELLER POTENTIAL

### Why This Book Will Sell

1. **The "Found Family" Hook.** Kids are hungry for stories about friendship that feels earned. The Glitch Squad doesn't become friends instantly—they're thrown together, they argue, they almost don't trust each other, and then they choose each other. That's the stuff.

2. **The STEM/STEAM Appeal.** Girls who build things, boys who code, artists who see patterns, gamers who understand systems—this book has entry points for every kind of curious kid. Maya alone is a character who will inspire a thousand "inventor girl" Halloween costumes.

3. **The Diversity Is Organic.** No "very special episode" energy. This book will be adopted by schools and libraries not because it ticks boxes but because it's *good* and happens to reflect the real world.

4. **The Pacing.** Serialized, chapter-based, hook-heavy. This is the kind of book that becomes a "reluctant reader" success story—the one the teacher hands to the kid who "doesn't like reading" and then that kid reads it under the desk during math.

5. **The Robot.** Blip is merchandise gold. A cube with a pixel face that can show different expressions? That's a plushie. That's a backpack charm. That's the thing every kid at the book fair wants.

### Comparable Titles

- **The Wild Robot** by Peter Brown (emotional robot, found family, survival) — but Glitch Squad has more action and team dynamics.
- **The Mysterious Benedict Society** by Trenton Lee Stewart (gifted kids, puzzles, adult conspiracies) — but Glitch Squad is warmer and faster-paced.
- **InvestiGators** by John Patrick Green (team dynamics, humor, action) — but Glitch Squad has higher emotional stakes.
- **City Spies** by James Ponti (diverse kid team, technology, global stakes) — this is probably the closest comp, but Glitch Squad is younger and more accessible.

### Cover & Title Thoughts

**Title:** "The Glitch Squad — Book 1: The Lost Signal" works. "The Lost Signal" is slightly generic, but paired with the series title, it's fine. Consider "The Glitch Squad: Signal Lost" for more punch.

**Cover:** Needs four kids and a robot in a dynamic composition. Blip should be glowing cyan. The background should be the Grid—circuits, city lights, glitch effects in magenta and turquoise. This should *not* look like a "boys' book" or a "girls' book." Neutral, bright, action-oriented. Think graphic novel energy even though it's prose.

**Tagline possibilities:**
- "Four kids. One robot. A city about to break."
- "They debug the city."
- "The signal found them. Now they have to fix it."

## AGE RANGE VERIFICATION

**8-11 is exactly right.**

- Vocabulary: Accessible without being dumbed down. "Multimeter," "triangulation," "encryption," and "voltage bridge" are introduced in context. Kids will learn them, not stumble over them.
- Emotional complexity: Loneliness, self-doubt, found family, responsibility, sacrifice. These are 8-11 themes, not YA themes. The book doesn't romanticize trauma or overcomplicate relationships.
- Scare level: The timer, the locked building, the voice in the screen—it's tense but not terrifying. No real violence. No permanent loss (Blip comes back). Perfect for sensitive readers who still want stakes.
- Length: 36 chapters, ~45,000-50,000 words. That's a substantial book for this age range but not intimidating. The short chapters make it feel manageable.
- Humor: Blip's sandwich history, his obsession with being included, Sam's "strategic pausing"—the humor is age-appropriate and doesn't talk down.

---

## FINAL VERDICT

**Bestseller Potential: HIGH**

*The Glitch Squad: The Lost Signal* is the kind of book that becomes a franchise. Not because it's cynical or calculated—because it's *loved*. Kids will finish this book and immediately ask for Book 2. They'll draw the characters. They'll imagine themselves in the Glitch Squad. They'll look at their own weird interests—building, coding, drawing, gaming—and see them as superpowers.

The manuscript needs a polish pass on the middle chapters and one or two character beats, but the bones are *strong*. The voice is confident. The heart is in the right place.

Publish this. Market it hard to schools and libraries. Get the cover right. And watch the Glitch Squad find its audience.

Because here's the thing: every kid who's ever felt weird, or alone, or like their interests don't fit in—every kid who's ever talked to a machine because machines don't judge—every kid who's ever wanted a team but didn't know how to find one—

They're waiting for this book.

*Recommended. Strongly.*

---

**Rating: 4.5 / 5 stars** (Half-star deducted for pacing in the middle third and one or two convenience-driven plot moments. But that half-star is coming back in Book 2 if the series finds its rhythm.)
