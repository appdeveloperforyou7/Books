## 1. THE RATING FRAMEWORK

Fourteen dimensions. Each scored **/10**. Anchors:

- **9–10** — genre-defining; among the best ever done in this dimension.
- **7–8** — genuinely strong; clearly above average.
- **5–6** — competent; does the job, unremarkable.
- **3–4** — a weakness.
- **1–2** — a failure.

### The fourteen dimensions

| # | Dimension | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Puzzle ingenuity** | The cleverness/originality of the central mystery itself. Is the *idea* of the crime elegant? |
| 2 | **Fair-play clue planting** | Can the reader solve it? Are all clues laid out honestly, no withheld information, no cheat? |
| 3 | **Misdirection** | Quality of red herrings and the wrong-suspect trap. Does the obvious answer turn out wrong — fairly? |
| 4 | **Twist / reveal** | The payoff. Does the solution surprise *and* recontextualize everything before it? |
| 5 | **Detective** | Memorability, interiority, distinctive method and voice of the investigator. |
| 6 | **Supporting cast / suspects** | Richness, distinctness, and depth of the people around the crime. |
| 7 | **Prose / literary craft** | Sentence-level writing quality. The dimension critics weight most. |
| 8 | **Thematic depth** | What the book is *about* beyond the plot. Does it mean something? |
| 9 | **Pacing / tension** | Momentum; the page-turner quality; does every chapter pull forward. |
| 10 | **Atmosphere / setting** | Sense of place and mood; a world you can feel. |
| 11 | **Emotional resonance** | Does it move the reader? Do the stakes land as feeling, not just plot? |
| 12 | **Resolution satisfaction** | Does the ending earn itself? No deus ex machina, no unearned twist. |
| 13 | **Originality / concept** | Freshness of concept and structure vs. the genre's habits. |
| 14 | **Reread value** | Does it reward a second reading (clues hidden in plain sight, foreshadowing)? |

**Legend for our book's scores (actual, revised draft):**

- The honest pattern is unchanged: **structure is the strength (8s)**, **execution is the binding weakness (6.4–6.5)**, and **the puzzle is still unproven** by real readers. The revision narrowed the execution gap only slightly — the gap to the benchmarks is still almost entirely prose/atmosphere/emotion, and that gap is now the **human line-editor's mile**, not an AI-revision lever.

## 4. PER-BOOK NOTES (why each score)

### Magpie Murders (Horowitz) — 110
The genre's modern gold standard for *craftsmanship*. A book-within-a-book; two fair-play puzzles; clues you can actually solve. Loses points on prose (workmanlike), theme (clever but shallow), and emotion (cerebral, rarely moving). Wins on everything structural. **This is the book to beat on puzzle and fair-play.**

### The Thursday Murder Club (Osman) — 106
Wins on **heart and character** — the four retirees are beloved, the book is genuinely moving about aging and friendship. Loses on puzzle (decent, not ingenious), twist (solid, not shocking), and reread value. **The book to beat on warmth and emotional pull.**

### The Searcher (Tana French) — 110
Wins on **prose and atmosphere** — 10/10 on both; masterful sentence-level writing and a dense, living setting. Deliberately *not* a clever puzzle (low puzzle/twist scores) — its power is literary and moral. **The book to beat on prose, atmosphere, and theme.** (Note: its strengths are exactly our weaknesses — a useful ceiling-to-chase, though apples-to-oranges on puzzle.)

### The Guest List (Lucy Foley) — 97
The commercial domestic-thriller comp closest to Elena's shelf: a locked-circle whodunit, female-skewing, propulsive bestseller. Wins on **pacing (9)** and a strong twist (8); loses on detective (no singular investigator), theme (entertainment over depth), and originality (locked-circle is familiar). **The book to beat in Elena's own market** — and the reminder that our structural ambition (puzzle/theme/originality) is *above* what this neighborhood usually attempts.

## 5. GAP ANALYSIS — what we must actually do to surpass

To honestly beat the benchmarks, the plan is not "score higher." It is to **close the execution gap (the 6s) and validate the puzzle (the 7s).**

### A. Close the prose gap (the decisive battle) — STATUS: open, not closed
The drafted prose is **6.3**: competent and literate, with real flair in the reading-mechanic, but a first draft — tics, cadence-uniformity, aphoristic tendency, no professional line-edit. The ambition was French's 9–10; the reality is ~3 points short. Closing it:
- **Cut tics & cadence-uniformity** — the "the way I…" construction, the aphorism habit, the repetitive sentence shapes. Vary rhythm aggressively.
- **Add sensory specificity** — smell, weight, temperature, the body in the room. Convert *sketched* setting into *felt* setting (this is the prose→atmosphere lever; 6.2 → 7.5).
- **Show feeling, don't state it** — replace Elena narrating her cost with the physical, concrete moment that evokes it (the prose→emotion lever; 6.3 → 7.5).
- **Length/density** — ~50K is short; the revision should add genuine density toward ~60–65K.
- Target: **prose 6.3 → 8**. This is revision work, the highest-leverage move on the board.
- **Consistency & sensory immersion:** the best chapters (Ch 1, the music seam, the confession) are at 7.5–8; some middle chapters are at 6.5–7. A full revision pass must lift the floor to match the ceiling and push toward French's sensory specificity (smell, weight, the body in the room).
### B. Make the puzzle watertight in execution (validate the 7s)
A clever design fails if a clue is planted too late, too obviously, or not at all. The fair-play test:
- Every solving clue must appear **before** the midpoint, in plain sight.
- The false solutions (Diane, the jealous-mentor story) must account for the clues *more convincingly* than the truth, until the truth snaps into place.
- Beta readers must be unable to solve it early *but able to say "I should have seen it"* at the end. That is the target reaction.

### C. Earn the emotional resonance (beat Osman on heart)
Osman's 9 comes from characters you love. We must make the **Elena–Joss friendship** and the **Elena–Clara dark-mirror** land as feeling, not just theme. If Clara's grief moves the reader before the reveal, the twist devastates. If not, it's just clever.

### D. Build the atmosphere (approach French on place)
The Hudson Valley estate must be a **felt presence** — the locked-in weekend, the weather, the house's moods. A vivid, claustrophobic setting converts a puzzle into an experience.
