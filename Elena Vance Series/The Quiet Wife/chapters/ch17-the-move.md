# CHAPTER SEVENTEEN
## The Move

The record was in a cookbook.

I want that to sound more dramatic than it is, because the truth — that my husband had hollowed out the pages of a 1996 edition of *Marcella Hazan's Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* and slid a flat drive and a sheaf of photostats into the spine, and shelved it between two equally unremarkable volumes where no billionaire's errand-boy would ever think to look — is exactly the kind of truth that doesn't sound like a thriller. It sounds like a man. A quiet, careful, slightly funny man, hiding the most dangerous document in New York inside a recipe for ossobuco, because he knew, the way archivists know, that the safest place to hide a thing is where it looks like it belongs.

"Take it to the office," Arthur said, at six in the morning, in the dark kitchen, while the vans sat outside like patient dogs. "Go to your ten o'clock. Then your one o'clock. Be ordinary. Be the version of you they've been watching for a week. Then, when the street is clean, go to Carter."

Carter was the journalist. Not the grey man — the grey man had been the courier. Carter was the reporter at the end of the line, the one with the bylines and the Pulitzer nomination and, Arthur swore, the spine. The grey man's silence had terrified us, but Carter was a different animal: a woman who'd made a career of embarrassing powerful men, who had, Arthur said, been waiting for exactly this kind of proof for a year.

"If Carter's real," I said, "why hasn't she published already?"

"Because she didn't have the records. She had rumours. She had pieces. She needed the spine — the original chain that proves the forgery. That's what you're carrying."

I looked at the cookbook in my bag, between my laptop and my water bottle, and I thought: *I am carrying the spine of a billion-dollar theft inside a pasta cookbook, and I am going to walk it past two surveillance vans on the way to a coaching session about a man who drinks at lunch, and that is my plan.* It was, GreyHat would have said, exactly the kind of plan that worked: ordinary, boring, the shape of a life no one would think to stop.

I did the dry-cleaning first. I left the building at my normal time, in my normal coat, with my normal twelve-dollar coffee — the prop I had carried past the vans every morning for a week, the costume of a woman going to work. The bench man was in position: newspaper open, no page turned in twenty minutes. I did not look at him. I walked my usual route for two blocks, and at the third I turned — left, into a drugstore I had no reason to enter, and out the side exit — and the turn was the test. A person going about their day ignores it. A tail reacts. I caught the bench man's hand rise to his phone, his face dropping, and in the two seconds his eyes left the street I crossed against the light and dissolved into the current of a Wednesday morning, one more wool coat in a river of them.

Three stops past my office. Switch cars. Coat off, folded over my arm — a different silhouette, the cheapest disguise there is and the only one that works on a crowded train. By the time I surfaced at my real stop I was, as far as I could read the platform, clean. My pulse was not. My pulse did not believe in clean.

The office. The ten o'clock. A logistics CEO who could not stop interrupting his own reports, who had paid me for six months to teach him the value of the half-second pause. He sat across from me and spoke over me three times in the first minute, and I held the pause — the pause that says *I will wait, and you will feel the silence, and you will learn to fill it less* — and he slowed, and the room settled, and I was, against everything, good at it. The fear had not made me worse at my job. It had only made every room feel thin, like a set built over an empty stage, with the real play happening somewhere beneath. I coached him through his hour. I read his face the way I had read Slade's in the hearing room — for the gap between the performance and the thing underneath.

Between sessions I sat with the bag in my lap, the cookbook's spine pressing into my thigh like a second pulse, and I did not touch it, and I did not look at it, and I waited for the street outside to forget me.

At three o'clock I left for Carter. The address was a bar in the Village, the kind of place where journalists meet sources at hours no one else drinks. I walked. I dry-cleaned again, shorter this time, sharper. I was getting faster. The turns were becoming instinct. I reached the bar's block, and I stood across the street, and I looked for the pattern, for the face that followed logic instead of life, and I found nothing. Clean.

I went in.

The booth at the back was empty. I sat. I ordered nothing. I waited. Three-fifteen. Three-thirty. Three-forty-five. Carter did not come.

At four o'clock my phone buzzed — a number I didn't know, a text, three words that ended the plan.

> **SMS — unknown number — 4:01 PM**
> Carter is compromised. Don't go home.

I stared at it. The bar was quiet around me. The cookbook was in my bag. The street outside, which I had been so proud of cleaning, was now a question mark I could not answer. Carter — the spine, the exit, Arthur's whole reason for waiting — was gone, the way the grey man was gone, the way every safe channel had closed, one by one, like lights going out in a hallway.

The record was on me. Nowhere to take it. No one left to trust. And home, where Arthur was waiting for the all-clear, had just become the one place I could not go.

I opened the Notes app with hands that were not, for the first time in my life, even pretending to be steady.

```
NOTE — 4:09 PM — bar, Village
Carter compromised. Grey man silent. Both channels dead.
The record is on me. I am the only copy now.
Can't go home. Arthur is there alone.
They're closing every door. One at a time.
=> They are not trying to get the record anymore.
=> They are trying to get ME.
```

I read the last line and I understood, in the dim bar with my husband's ossobuco cookbook against my ribs, that the move had not failed because of bad luck. It had failed because they had read my notes. They had read my questions. They knew about Carter before I'd ever walked out the door, because they had been inside my head for days, and I had handed them the keys with every question I'd typed.

I just didn't know it yet.

That understanding was still an hour away, sitting in a message from a stranger, and when it arrived it would rearrange everything I thought I knew about the week I'd just lived. But right now, in the bar, all I knew was that the doors were closing, and my husband was alone, and the only person in the world who could help me was a man I'd never met whose username was a colour and a hat.

I typed the only message that mattered.

The three dots appeared. Disappeared. Appeared again.

Then GreyHat wrote the sentence that would send me to a warehouse in the dark, and to a shipping container, and to the worst night of my life.
