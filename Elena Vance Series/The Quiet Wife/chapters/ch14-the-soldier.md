# CHAPTER FOURTEEN
## The Soldier

I gave Arthur one day. Then I broke the promise, because promises made out of love are the first casualties of fear, and because by Saturday morning the thing I was most afraid of wasn't the secret anymore — it was the shape of the man living in 6B.

Royce. I had accepted him for three months as a nuisance: hedge-fund shoulders, entitlement, a man who wanted my wall. I had never once asked whether Royce was real, because it had never occurred to me that a neighbour might not be. Neighbours are the most boring fact in a person's life. That is exactly why you'd put a fake one there.

I started with the thing Royce told everyone: his name, his job, his story. He'd said "hedge fund" at the last board meeting, and "private equity" to the doorman, and "consulting" to me — three answers to one question, which is the first thing I teach my clients to notice about a liar. A truthful person is boring and consistent. A liar offers versions, because each version feels safer than the last.

LinkedIn found three people named Royce in New York finance. None of them matched the man in 6B — wrong ages, wrong faces, wrong firms. I tried the photo. I cropped his face from a building-event picture on the co-op's website and ran it through a reverse image search, the way GreyHat had quietly taught me in a DM: *most people don't know you can search by image. You can. It's how you find out if a face has been used before.*

The search returned nothing. No Royce. No profile. No history. The man next door had no footprint before approximately three years ago — no older photos, no mentions, no trail. He had, as far as the internet was concerned, walked into existence fully formed, like a character with no backstory, and moved directly into an apartment owned by one of Slade's shell companies.

I sat with that. A man with no past, placed by the network, in the unit beside the keystone. Not a neighbour. A post.

*How close is your husband to moving it.*

That was the question that sent me out of the bedroom and into the kitchen, where Arthur was standing at the counter with his phone in his hand and the colour of concrete.

"Arthur."

He looked up. The phone screen was facing me, and for once he didn't angle it away, and what I saw on it was a long message thread in which his contact — the grey man, the careful ally — had gone silent. The last message was from Arthur, sent at 6 a.m.: *Today?* No reply. The one before that, from the grey man, two days ago: *Not this line. Burn the phone. I'll find you.*

"He's gone dark," Arthur said. His voice was flat in the way voices go flat when the feeling underneath is too large to carry in a normal tone. "He's never gone dark. He always answers. Something's wrong."

I looked at my husband, and at the silent phone, and at the kitchen that had been the safest place in my life, and I understood that the two days he'd promised me had just collapsed to zero, and that the safe channel — the journalist, the exit, the plan — had just closed, and that we were now two people alone in an apartment with the only copy of the proof that a billionaire had stolen a city block, and the billionaire's men were already through the wall.

"We have to move it," I said. "Today. Now. GreyHat says they'll come through the door next."

Arthur stared at me. "GreyHat?"

"The man helping me. Online. Arthur, I know. I know everything. I know about the keystone and the shells and Royce and Slade. I've known for days. You don't have to protect me from the shape of it anymore. The shape of it is in my living room."

He sank into a chair. He put his face in his hands. And then, muffled, almost a laugh, almost a sob: "You've been investigating me."

"I've been investigating everything. And I'm good at it. Which you already knew, because you married a woman who reads rooms for a living, and then you were surprised when she read this one."

He looked up. Something between horror and a terrible, tired wonder. "Elena."

"Where is it. The original. You said two days. You have two hours. Where."

He held my gaze for a long moment. I watched the last of his need to protect me lose its fight with the size of the thing we were in. And then he told me.

Not the wall. Not a safe. Somewhere so ordinary I would never have looked, somewhere that was, I realised as he said it, quietly genius, and quietly the most dangerous place it could be, because it was the first place they'd look the moment they understood what they were looking for.

He told me. And outside the window, on the street below, a white van with no name pulled up to the kerb and did not move.
