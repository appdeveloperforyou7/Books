# CHAPTER FIFTEEN
## Dry Cleaning

"Don't look," Arthur said, without moving his lips. "Don't go to the window. Don't change what you're doing."

I had already done all three. I was standing at the kitchen counter with a kettle in my hand, frozen mid-pour, because the white van outside was the same van, or a van exactly like it, that had pulled away from 6B after the drilling, and it was sitting at our kerb now with its engine running and nothing about it moving except the faint curl of exhaust in the cold.

"You saw it," I said. Not a question.

"I've seen it three times this week." Arthur's voice was steady in the way a wire is steady before it snaps. "I didn't tell you. I thought—I thought if I moved the records fast enough, it wouldn't matter."

It mattered. It mattered very much that my husband had been watching a surveillance van circle our block for a week and had decided to carry that alone too, because Arthur's love expressed itself as a series of doors closed in my face, each one labelled *for your own good*, and I was discovering that this was the thing about him that could get us both killed.

I put the kettle down. I picked up my phone. I messaged the only person who would know what to do with a van that didn't move.

```
💬 DM — u/throwaway_v2781 → u/GreyHat_4 — 9:24 AM
There's a van outside my building. Unmarked. It's been
circling all week (my husband knew, didn't tell me).
They're watching us. How do I know if I'm being followed
when I leave, and how do I lose them if I am?
```

The reply came fast, and it was the most practical thing anyone had ever told me, and I have thought about it every day since.

```
💬 u/GreyHat_4 — 9:26 AM
Investigators call it "dry cleaning" — losing a tail.
Boring name, real skill. Here's the plain version:

1) ASSUME you're being watched. Don't try to "spot" the
obvious person. The good ones are invisible. Look for
PATTERNS instead — the same face, same car, same jacket
turning up in two places it has no reason to be.

2) To test it: take a route with corners and choices.
Turn. Pause in a shop window. Cross a street for no
reason. A person going about their day ignores you. A
tail has to react to every choice you make, and reactions
leave fingerprints. Do three unnecessary turns. If
something follows the logic of YOUR turns and not theirs,
you're made.

3) To LOSE them: get into a crowd, then change your shape
— hat off, coat off, bag switched. Step into a store with
two exits. Use a train you don't need. The goal isn't
speed. The goal is making them lose you in the ordinary,
because the ordinary is the one thing surveillance can't
organise fast enough.

4) NEVER go where you were heading once you know you're
being followed. Go to the plan only after you're clean.

Do not leave that apartment with the evidence until you
are CLEAN. If they follow you to the handoff, the handoff
is dead and so is whoever's waiting.
```

I read it twice. *If they follow you to the handoff, the handoff is dead and so is whoever's waiting.* The grey man had gone dark. The handoff — Arthur's plan to move the records to safety — had already broken once. And now there was a van.

"We can't move it today," I said.

"We have to."

"Arthur. There is a van. If we walk out of here with a folder under your arm, they will follow us, and they will know exactly what we're carrying, and the man you were going to give it to isn't answering his phone." I kept my voice level, the coaching voice, the voice that holds a room together when the room wants to fly apart. "We wait. We let them get bored. We move when the street is ordinary."

"Bored." He almost laughed. "Elena, these people don't get bored. They have budgets."

"Then we outlast the budget. We go to work. We live our lives. We make them watch a woman go to the office and buy groceries and come home, and on the day the street is clean, we move it. One shot. No rehearsal, no pattern, no warning. The ordinary is the only thing they can't organise for. GreyHat said that, and he's right."

Arthur looked at me — really looked, the way he hadn't since this started, the way a man looks at his wife when he suddenly sees her clearly and she is not the person he married, she is someone harder and sharper and more useful, and he doesn't know whether to be grateful or afraid.

"GreyHat," he said again.

"The man helping me. Yes. A stranger on the internet who has kept me alive this week while you were busy protecting me into a corner."

He flinched. I didn't soften it. I was done softening. The softening was how we'd got here — two kind people being kind at each other while the walls came in.

"Okay," he said. "Okay. We wait. We're ordinary."

"Ordinary."

I went to the window. I did not look at the van. I looked at the street the way GreyHat had taught me to look at a boardroom — for the pattern, not the person. The van. A man on a bench across the road reading the same page for twenty minutes. A second van, darker, further up the block, that I had not noticed until I stopped looking for the obvious one.

Two vans. A bench. They had built a net around my building, and they had done it quietly, the way they built everything — with patience, and paper, and the certainty that ordinary people never look up from their lives long enough to see the shape closing around them.

I had looked up. That was the only difference between me and every other person on this street. I had looked up, and now I could see the net, and the net could see me seeing it, and there was no unseeing it.

I closed the note. I went to work. I walked out of my building past the van with my coat on and my bag over my shoulder and my face arranged into the calm blank mask of a woman with a 10 a.m. client, and I did not look at the bench, and I did not look at the vans, and I felt, with every step, the weight of an attention I could not see but knew was there, the way you feel a gaze on the back of your neck in an empty room.

I was dry-cleaned before I'd even started.

And the record — the keystone, the proof, the one thing that could end a billionaire's theft — was sitting in my apartment behind a locked door that someone, very soon, was going to stop asking permission to open.
