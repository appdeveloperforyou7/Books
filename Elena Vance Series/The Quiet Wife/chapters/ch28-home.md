# CHAPTER TWENTY-EIGHT
## Home

We moved back in on a Tuesday, because the federal team finally released the apartment, and because I wanted to come home on an ordinary day, in daylight, with the builders still patching the wall, so that the return would feel like a renovation and not a rescue.

The wall was the first thing I touched. The two bore-holes were gone, filled and skimmed and painted by men who did not know what the holes had been for, and the plaster was the slightly wrong white of fresh work, and I put my palm flat against it the way I had the morning of the drilling, and this time it was still. No vibration. No coring rig. No eye in the cavity. Just brick, and lath, and a hundred years of ordinary life, restored to the only thing it had ever owed me: silence.

Arthur watched me from the doorway. "It's the same colour," he said. "Almost."

"It's close enough." I turned. "Nothing's ever exactly the same after. That's all right. Close enough is its own kind of whole."

He nodded. He understood, because he was an archivist, and archivists know that a restored document is not the original document — that the mend is visible if you look, and that the looking is the point, because the mend is the proof the thing survived.

We put the apartment back together slowly, over days, the way you reassemble a life after a storm: the books reshelved, the drawers returned to their small disorders, the kitchen relearnt. The cookbook went back on the shelf — *Marcella Hazan*, 1996 edition, the spine now slightly looser than it had been, the hollow inside it empty, the drive and the photostats long since in the hands of prosecutors. I slid it into its place between the other unremarkable volumes and I ran my finger along the spine and I thought about all the ordinary objects that hold extraordinary things, and how the safest place to hide a truth is where it looks like it belongs, and how that is true of cookbooks and of people both.

The building changed around us, quietly. 6B was sealed, then emptied, then handed to a legal process that would, eventually, return it to the cooperative. Gerald resigned. The new board president was a retired teacher named Min, thirty years in 4C, who took both my hands in the lobby and said, "I always knew that man was no good" — almost certainly a lie, and I loved her for it. The residents who'd been pressured out, the ones before me, began to come back, or sue, or tell their stories to Carter, whose series ran for a year and won the things journalists win and changed very little and very much at once.

And through all of it, Arthur and I learned, clumsily, to live without the closed doors.

It is harder than it sounds. A marriage that has run for years on a particular architecture — his protectiveness, my control — does not simply rewire because the crisis has passed. We argued, that first month, more than we had in seven years, because the things we had suppressed to survive now had air, and air makes things grow. He resented, briefly, the woman I had become — not because he didn't admire her, but because she was not the woman he had married, and grief for a version of a person is real even when the new version is better. And I resented, briefly, the years of doors, the months of carrying alone, the particular loneliness of being protected by someone who would not let you stand beside him.

We said these things. Out loud. In the kitchen, over dinners I did not burn. We let the half-second pause do its work between us, the way it does in a session — not to smooth the feeling, but to let it be true long enough to move. And slowly, clumsily, we built a new architecture: one with no doors between us, only rooms, and the agreement that we would walk into each one together, even the frightening ones, even the ones with vans outside.

One evening, a month after the gala, I stood at the kitchen window and watched the street below — the ordinary street, the kerbs empty of white vans, the benches occupied only by people resting — and Arthur came up behind me and put his chin on my shoulder the way he does when he is not performing anything, when he is simply a man leaning on his wife.

"Do you miss it?" he said.

"Miss what?"

"Being afraid."

I almost laughed. "No one misses being afraid."

"That's not what I mean." He was quiet for a moment. "I mean — the part of you that came alive. The reading. The pulling of threads. The sitting across from someone and letting them show you who they are. You were — Elena, you were extraordinary. And I wonder if the ordinary is going to be enough for you now."

I looked at the street. At the city, enormous and indifferent and full, I was beginning to suspect, of a great many unread rooms.

"I don't think I get to choose that," I said. "I think the thing that came alive is just me. I think it was always me. I just needed a reason to stop hiding behind the soft part of my job."

He kissed my temple. "Then don't hide."

"I'm not going to."

And I wasn't. Because the email was already in my inbox, waiting, from a name I did not yet recognise, about a man I did not yet know was missing, in a building I had not yet learned to read.

I just didn't know it yet. Not until morning.
