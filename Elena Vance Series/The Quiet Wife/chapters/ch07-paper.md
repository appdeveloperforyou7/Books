# CHAPTER SEVEN
## Paper

The city keeps a website. I want to say that more dramatically, because it's the thing that changed everything, but the truth is undramatic: the city keeps a website, and on it, for free, for anyone, is the record of who owns every building and every piece of land. You type in an address. It tells you who bought it, when, and from whom. That's it. That's the whole magic trick. The information has been sitting there the whole time, in a beige government database, waiting for someone to care.

I cared now.

Monday morning, between clients, I sat in my office with the door closed and I opened the site — the Automated City Register Information System, which everyone just calls by its acronym, ACRIS, and which looks exactly as exciting as that sounds — and I typed in *Meridian Holdings LLC*.

```
SEARCH — ACRIS — 9:47 AM
Entity: Meridian Holdings LLC
Result: Owns parcels on W 44th St (block 788).
Acquired: 14 months ago.
Seller: Crestline Properties LLC.
```

Meridian, the company that quietly assembled the West 44th block, had bought the land from Crestline. So I typed in Crestline.

```
SEARCH — ACRIS — 9:49 AM
Entity: Crestline Properties LLC
Result: Owns multiple parcels, Midtown.
Acquired via transfer from: Northbridge Capital Holdings LLC.
```

I stopped typing.

Northbridge. Northbridge Capital Holdings. The company that owns apartment 6B, next door to me, the apartment Royce lives in but doesn't own.

I sat very still. I read the two results again, side by side, the way the AI had told me to — two facts that mean nothing alone, everything together. Crestline had sold the 44th Street land to Meridian. Crestline had acquired things from Northbridge. Northbridge owned my neighbour's apartment. The names weren't random. They were a family. The same hands, wearing different gloves.

I messaged GreyHat. I'd been messaging him all weekend, short careful questions, and he'd been answering in his clipped, reluctant way, like a man handing out matches and hoping you wouldn't burn the house down.

```
💬 DM — u/throwaway_v2781 → u/GreyHat_4 — 9:58 AM
I found a chain. Company A bought land from B. B got it
from C. C owns the apartment next to mine. Same network,
different names. What IS this? Is this normal?
```

```
💬 u/GreyHat_4 — 10:04 AM
It's normal-ish. It's called a shell company. A company
that exists on paper only — no office, no staff, just a
name and a registration. People use them for boring
reasons (tax, liability) and for bad reasons (hiding who
really owns something). One is nothing. A CHAIN of them,
all handing property to each other, is a person trying
very hard not to be found.

The trick: keep pulling the thread. Every shell has a
registered agent or a signatory somewhere. You're looking
for the one human name at the bottom of the pile.
```

A shell. A name on paper, built to hide the name behind it. I understood the concept instantly, the way you understand a mask — and the understanding made the back of my neck prickle, because a person who builds a chain of masks is a person with something to lose.

I pulled the thread.

Crestline's registration listed an agent, a corporate services firm in Delaware. That firm represented four hundred other companies. A dead end, on purpose — that's what those firms sell, anonymity by the bushel. Northbridge was the same. Meridian, the same. I spent an hour chasing names that led to firms that led to nothing, and I felt the particular frustration of a puzzle designed never to be solved.

Then I did the boring thing again. I stopped looking for the secret and looked at the dates.

```
NOTE — 11:30 AM — office, door shut
The chain, in order of transfer dates:
Crestline → sold to Meridian (14 mo ago)
Northbridge → transferred to Crestline (22 mo ago)
All three registered same week, 6 yrs ago, same Delaware firm.
Nobody buys a thing from themselves for no reason.
They're shuffling the SAME property between the SAME hands.
=> One owner. Many masks. Why move it so often?
```

Why move it. That was the question GreyHat would have asked. Property shuffled between shells is usually property being hidden from someone — a creditor, an ex-spouse, an investigator, a court. The masks aren't there to impress. They're there to confuse anyone following the trail. And I was following the trail, and the trail was getting warm, and the warmth was coming from the fact that one of those masks — Northbridge — was sitting in the apartment wall away from my bed.

I pulled up the ownership record for my own building next. Just to look. Just to see whose names sat beside Northbridge on the same block, the way you'd glance at the other passengers in a train car because one of them is making you nervous.

```
SEARCH — ACRIS — 11:42 AM
Address: [my building], Unit 6A (MINE)
Owner: [me] — cooperative corporation share #6A.
Building sponsor / original holder of unsold shares:
Hargrove Residential Partners LLC.
```

Hargrove Residential Partners. A name I'd never thought about. The "sponsor" of a co-op is the developer who originally converted the building, decades ago, and who often keeps certain rights over unsold units. Boring. Background noise. The kind of fact that sits in your closing documents and you never read it.

Except.

I typed Hargrove into the search. I waited. The page loaded.

```
SEARCH — ACRIS — 11:44 AM
Entity: Hargrove Residential Partners LLC
Registered agent: [same Delaware firm as Crestline,
Northbridge, Meridian].
Original filing: 31 years ago.
```

The same firm. The same paper. My own building's sponsor shared a mailbox with the network assembling the block on West 44th, the network that owned my neighbour's apartment, the network that my husband was apparently so frightened of that he lied about the time he left work to go meet someone at the Aurelian.

I closed the laptop, very gently, the way you close a door you're not ready to walk through.

The three threads — Arthur, the West 44th land, the neighbour — were not three threads. They were one rope. And one end of it was tied to my own front door, and I had not known, and the not-knowing was the thing that had always frightened me most, the thing I'd spent my whole life building walls against.

I opened the note. I wrote the only honest line left.

```
NOTE — 11:51 AM — office
My building's sponsor = same network as everything else.
Hargrove = Crestline = Northbridge = Meridian. One owner.
The pattern runs THROUGH my address.
This is not Arthur's secret.
This is not Royce's grudge.
This is something bigger, and I live inside it.
```

I sat in my quiet office and I listened to the city outside the window and I understood, for the first time, what GreyHat had meant by *you're inside it now.* I had thought I was standing at a window looking in. I was wrong. The window was a mirror. I'd been looking at my own room the whole time.
