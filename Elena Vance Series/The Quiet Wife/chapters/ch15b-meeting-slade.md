# CHAPTER FIFTEEN (part two)
## Reading Him

> *(New chapter — slots between the current Ch 15 and Ch 16. Final renumbering shifts the rest by one.)*

I needed to meet Slade.

This is the part of the story that sounds, in the retelling, like a woman losing her mind, and maybe I was. But the logic was clean, the way the worst decisions always are. I had a name and a thirty-year reputation and a wall full of certainty, and certainty without a face is just anxiety. I could not out-think a man I had never laid eyes on. I read people for a living. To read Richard Slade, I had to be in a room with him, the way I am in a room with my clients — close enough to feel the weight shift, to catch the half-second before he decides to lie. I could not do that from a phone.

The trouble was access. You do not simply request a meeting with a man whose business is the quiet removal of people from buildings. But Slade was not a ghost; he was a fixture, and fixtures attend functions. The community board was holding a public hearing on the West 44th rezoning application — the very rezoning that would turn Slade's stolen block into a fortune — and Richard Slade, whose name was on none of the application papers and whose presence was therefore guaranteed, would be in the third row, doing what fixers do: watching, saying nothing, being the temperature of the room.

I went. I wore the grey dress, the coaching dress, the one that says *I am here in a professional capacity and I am no one's threat.* And because I was about to do a thing I had never done — read a stranger on purpose, for stakes — I asked the one tutor who never judged me how to do it.

*You already do this for a living.* I read that line twice, and it steadied me, and I did not yet understand that the machine writing those words belonged to the man I was about to help it hide.

---

The hearing room was municipal beige and bad acoustics and forty chairs, most of them full of residents who did not want their block rezoned and developers who did. Slade was in the third row, exactly where I'd been told he'd be, and I knew him before I was introduced, the way you know a predator in a room of prey — not by anything he did, but by the way the air arranged itself around him. He was sixty, maybe, silver, soft-spoken, wearing a suit that cost more than my car and a smile that never reached the part of his face where feeling lives. He had the stillness of a man who has not needed to raise his voice in thirty years, because the people he addressed already knew what a raised voice from him would mean.

I waited until the recess. I approached the way I approach a difficult client — oblique, admiring, offering him the one thing powerful men cannot resist: an audience that already considers him interesting.

"Mr. Slade. Elena Vance. I coach executives on the human side of large developments — the community friction, the reputational risk. I've followed the 44th file from a distance. I wanted to introduce myself." I offered my hand. He took it. His grip was dry and unhurried and exactly as long as it needed to be, and in the half-second before he spoke I read him the way the machine had told me to: I watched the pause.

The pause was the smoothest thing about him. No flicker. No tell. A man who had been read before and had long since sanded away the seams. "Elena Vance," he said, and his smile came, warm and empty. "The human side. That's a nice corner of the business. Most people only think about the numbers."

"Numbers are easy. People are the whole risk." I let the admiring tilt do its work. "I imagine a block like 44th — the assembly alone must have been an extraordinary piece of work."

And there it was. In the half-second before he answered, the smoothness held — but his eyes changed, just for a moment, the way a lake changes when something moves underneath. Not guilt. Pride. The pride of a craftsman being admired by someone who understood the craft. He liked that I'd noticed. He could not help liking it.

"Assembly," he said, lightly, "is patience. Everyone wants the dramatic part. The dramatic part takes care of itself. The patience is where the art is." He tilted his head, and the smile sharpened, just enough. "You live around there, Ms. Vance? You have a familiarity with the block."

It was a test. And it was, underneath the courtesy, a warning — the softest possible way of saying *I know where you live, and I am asking you to confirm it so that we both understand what kind of conversation this is.* My stomach turned to ice. But the coach in me held the half-second pause, and the coach in me smiled, and the coach in me said, "I coach a few clients in the neighbourhood. I keep my ear close to the ground."

He nodded, slowly, still smiling. "Good instincts are rare in that neighbourhood. Keep them close." He glanced past me — a dismissal dressed as a courtesy — and then added, almost as an afterthought, the line I would turn over for days: "Streets change, Ms. Vance. The people who see it coming do well. The people who don't — well. They usually find somewhere else to live."

He walked away. I stood in the beige hallway with the bad acoustics and the taste of metal in my mouth, and I was certain — absolutely, coldly certain — that I had just met the man who was trying to take my home. The pride when I mentioned the assembly. The warning about my address. The threat dressed as advice: *they find somewhere else to live.* This was the brain. This was the architect. I had read him, and he had shown me, in the half-second before his answers, exactly who he was: a man who considered the removal of families from their homes an art form, and who wanted me to know it.

I walked out into the noon light certain I had my villain, and his name was Slade, and I had read his seams, and I knew exactly how to crack him — the way I crack every proud man, by making him feel so admired that he explains himself to me.

That was the trap closing. Not Slade's — Slade was real, and guilty, and every reading I'd made of him was true. The trap was the certainty. The certainty that the man I had just read, and read correctly, was the end of the thread, when he was in fact the knot tied deliberately at the end to make me stop pulling.

I had met the villain. I had read the villain. I knew the villain's lever.

I did not know that the villain was an employee.

I went home, and I began, with the bright focused energy of a woman who has finally found her target, to build the case that would bring Richard Slade down — and in building it, I walked, eyes open and certain, straight past the quiet young man on the magazine cover who had lent Slade the drill and was, even now, reading every note I typed about the man he had hired me to chase.
