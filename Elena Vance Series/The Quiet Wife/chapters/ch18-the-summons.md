# CHAPTER EIGHTEEN
## The Summons

Arthur's phone went straight to voicemail. I called it seven times in the bar, pressing redial with the mechanical calm of a person who has passed through panic into the colder country beyond it, where everything is precise and slow. Voicemail. Voicemail. Voicemail. The seventh time I stopped, because GreyHat had said *they have your husband*, and GreyHat had been right about everything for two weeks, and no version of redialling was going to change that.

I sat in the booth and I did the thing I do in sessions. I separated the feeling from the fact. Feeling: my husband is gone, and it is my fault, and I am going to be sick. Fact: I had the record, they wanted the record, they had the only leverage that would make me hand it over. Arthur was not a casualty. Arthur was the price. That meant he was, for now, alive, because a dead husband buys nothing.

That thought was the only thing holding me upright.

The summons came at 5:40, while I was still sitting in the bar trying to think. It came as a photo, sent to my phone from another unknown number, and I did not need to open it to know what it was, but I opened it anyway, because you always look.

Arthur. Sitting in a chair in a room with no windows, his hands zip-tied in front of him, a bruise already darkening under one eye. He was looking at the camera, and his face was not frightened — it was furious, the specific fury of a gentle man who has been shown to be powerless, and the fury was aimed past the lens at me, at the version of me he imagined would be looking, and his eyes said *don't*.

Below the photo, a message.

> **SMS — unknown number — 5:40 PM**
> The book. Tonight. 10pm. 1100 Woodward Ave, Queens.
> Building C. Come alone. Walk in, leave it on the
> desk, walk out. Your husband walks out after. No cops.
> We'll know.

No cops. *We'll know.* The casual certainty of people who had done this before, who had budgets and patience and a van for every block, and who believed, with the faith of the powerful, that they had already organised every variable — including me.

They had not organised me. They had organised the version of me they'd read in my Notes app: the careful observer, the woman who documented, the one who followed rules and took notes and waited for the ordinary. That woman would walk into Building C at ten o'clock and leave the ossobuco cookbook on a desk and walk out and pray.

I was no longer that woman. That woman had gotten her husband zip-tied in a windowless room.

I left the bar. I walked in the wrong direction first, dry-cleaning out of pure reflex, and then I found a drugstore and I bought, with cash, the things GreyHat had taught me to want: a small audio recorder, the kind students use for lectures, no bigger than a thumb. A burner phone. A cap. A bottle of water. I put the recorder in my bra, where it would be warm and flat and mine. I put the cookbook in my bag. I left my real phone, the one they'd been reading, in a public library on Sixth Avenue, powered on, so that for the next four hours it would sit in a building three boroughs from where I was going, and anyone watching its signal would watch a building and not a woman.

```
NOTE — 7:55 PM — written on paper, drugstore receipt
Not using the phone. They read the phone. Don't know
how long. Burner only from here.
PLAN: go. Recorder on. Leave the book. Get Arthur.
But the recorder stays ON, hidden, the whole time.
GreyHat said: take nothing they expect you to take.
They expect a scared wife with a book.
They will get a scared wife with a book and a witness
they can't see.
```

I rode the train to Queens in the dark. I got off at an industrial stop where the platform emptied to nothing and the streets were warehouses and the streetlights were the orange kind that make everything look like a memory. I walked. The cap pulled low. The recorder warm against my skin, running, capturing the sound of my own breathing and the distant grind of a freight line and, underneath everything, my heartbeat, which was the only thing in the world I could not slow down no matter how many half-second pauses I took.

1100 Woodward. Building C. A long low structure with a roller door and no lights except the one above the entrance, a single yellow bulb swinging slightly in a wind I couldn't feel, and in its light I could see that the door was open. Just open. Waiting. The way a mouth waits.

I stood on the cracked asphalt and I thought about my father. I thought about the morning he left, and the door I never locked, and the child I'd been who decided that she would always, always see it coming. I had seen this coming. I had seen all of it. And it had come anyway, because seeing is not stopping, and the gap between those two things is the gap where a life can end.

I had no phone. I had no backup. I had a recorder I didn't know how to use and a cookbook that could end a billionaire and a husband in a windowless room and the absolute, freezing certainty that the people inside Building C had no intention of letting either of us walk out.

I went in anyway.

Because the alternative was the woman on the platform at 23rd Street, in a trench coat, spilling coffee — the woman who chased and failed and ran. That woman was gone. The woman who walked into Building C at 9:58 p.m. on a Wednesday in October had read every room she'd ever been in, and she had finally read this one, and she understood that the only way out of a trap built for a scared wife was to stop being the wife they'd written for her.

I crossed the threshold. The door closed behind me with a sound like a period at the end of a sentence.

And the dark took me in.
