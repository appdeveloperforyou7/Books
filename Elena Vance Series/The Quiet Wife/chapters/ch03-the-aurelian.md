# CHAPTER THREE
## The Aurelian

I start watching the dot.

I don't mean to. I would like, very much, to be the kind of woman who receives a suspicious location ping and shrugs and trusts her husband and goes to sleep. I am not that woman. I have never been that woman. The woman I am sits on the edge of the bed on Thursday morning and watches a small blue circle breathe inside a building called the Aurelian, and she hates herself, and she keeps watching.

The dot is Arthur. Arthur is asleep beside me, phone on the nightstand, breathing slow. So the dot is just . . . data, a little ghost of him standing in a place he shouldn't be, hours ago. I am watching a recording. That's what I tell myself.

Then I close the app and reopen it. Then I close it again. This is the thing about control: once you've lost it in one place, you can't stop checking the others.

I get dressed. I go to work.

---

I am bad at work on Thursday, and I am worse on Friday, and by Friday afternoon the people I am paid to read are beginning to read me back.

My two o'clock is a chief operating officer named Priya — sharp, anxious, the kind of client who notices everything and apologises for it. We're three minutes in when she stops mid-sentence and says, "Are you all right? You seem . . . somewhere else."

Nobody has said that to me in eleven years.

"I'm fine," I say, and I smile the smile I have smiled a thousand times, the one that says *I am the steady one in this room, you can fall apart, I will hold the shape of things*. It does not land. I can feel it not landing. The smile is the same; I am the one who's different.

Priya frowns. "Elena."

"It's a personal thing," I say. "Nothing to do with you. I'm sorry. Let's go back to the board meeting." And because she is kind, she lets me, and we limp through the session, and the whole time I am thinking about a penthouse and a folder and a blue dot, and the part of my brain that is supposed to be tracking Priya's breathing and her eye contact and the half-second pause is instead standing at a window on West 29th Street, staring up at a building I have never entered.

This is the cost I didn't count on. I built my whole life on being the one who watches. It turns out you can only watch so many things at once, and the thing I am watching now is eating every other thing alive.

I cancel my four o'clock. I never cancel.

I go home early, which I never do, and I sit on the sofa with my laptop and I do the thing I've been telling myself not to do. I look up the Aurelian.

```
SEARCH — 4:22 PM — home
"The Aurelian" + "W 29th" + "residences"
```

It takes four seconds to feel sick.

The Aurelian is not an apartment building in the way I am used to apartment buildings. It is a private residence club — the kind of place that doesn't advertise, doesn't list prices, and doesn't, so far as the internet can tell, allow anyone in who isn't invited by someone already inside. The public-facing material is all brushed steel and orchids and the word *discretion* used like a religion. There are no reviews, because the people who go there do not leave reviews. There are no floor plans. There is a single grainy photograph of a lobby so sparse it looks like a warning.

An archivist — a public servant on a public servant's salary, the man I married, the man who irons his shirts and takes the same sandwich to work every day for a decade — has no earthly reason to be in a place like the Aurelian. None. I knew that the moment I saw the address. But seeing it confirmed, in brushed steel and orchids, makes it solid. Makes it a wall I can't think my way around.

So he isn't there for the building. He's there for a person. And whoever that person is, my husband lied to me to see them, and then drove twenty blocks south afterward to put his dot back where it belonged, like a man re-shelving a book he'd been caught reading.

I close the laptop. I open the Notes app. The Notes app has become the one place I am still honest.

```
NOTE — 4:39 PM — home
The Aurelian = private members' residence club.
Ultra-discreet. No public access. No prices.
Arthur has NO reason to be there.
=> He's there for a person, not a place.
=> Who has access to a place like that?
```

I stare at the last line. I don't know anyone with access to a place like that. I coach people who might — billionaires, mostly, and the lawyers who clean up after them — but Arthur doesn't move in those circles. Arthur moves in circles made of old paper and quiet corridors and a sandwich.

Unless he does, and I just never watched closely enough.

The thought lands like a stone in water, and the ripples go everywhere at once, because if I missed this — if the woman who reads rooms for a living missed an entire secret life in her own marriage — then the foundation I built my whole self on, the *I will never be blindsided* foundation, is a lie. And if that's a lie, then I don't know what I am.

I get up. I pace. I do the breathing exercise I teach clients, the one I have never once needed myself, and I am humiliated to find that it helps, a little, which means I needed it, which means I am not in control.

Then, because the brain is a cruel machine, the other thread tugs at me. The folder. Gerald's regretful face. *Who benefits from me losing this apartment?*

I sit back down. I open a fresh tab. I type in Royce.

```
SEARCH — 4:51 PM — home
"Royce" + "6B" + "co-op" + "owner records"
```

This one is easy. Boring, even. The city keeps property ownership on a free public website — anyone can look it up, anyone, it's right there, which is the part that always astonishes me, how much of our lives is just . . . sitting there for anyone who bothers. I type in the address. I wait.

Unit 6B comes up. I expect Royce's name. The man is called Royce; he introduced himself as Royce; the building knows him as Royce.

The owner of 6B is not Royce.

The owner of 6B is a company. *Northbridge Capital Holdings LLC.*

I blink at it. I read it twice. A company owns the apartment next door — the apartment a man named Royce lives in, full-time, as if it were his. People rent, of course. That's normal. But the *name* — Holdings, LLC, the same flavourless corporate suffix as Meridian Holdings and Crestline Properties, the West 44th companies — it brushes against something, and the wrong-note instinct fires so hard I actually put the phone down and pick it up again to make sure I read it right.

```
NOTE — 4:56 PM — home
6B owned by Northbridge Capital Holdings LLC.
NOT Royce. Royce lives there. Doesn't own.
Same "____ Holdings LLC" shape as the West 44th companies.
Coincidence? Probably. LLCs are everywhere.
=> But I don't believe in coincidence. Not today.
```

I sit with that for a long time. Three threads now: Arthur's lie, the Aurelian, the wall of nameless companies. They don't connect. There is no reason on earth they should connect. One is my marriage. One is a land deal I overheard at work. One is a neighbour I barely know trying to push me out of my home. Three separate wrong notes in three separate rooms.

And yet.

I think about my father. I think about the Thursday morning he kissed my mother and left and didn't come back, and how afterwards everyone said the same thing, every single person, in the same helpless voice: *we had no idea.* And I think about how, when I was eleven, I swore that no one would ever say that about me. That I would always have the idea. That I would see it coming.

I think about the fact that, right now, three things in my life are moving that I do not understand, and the woman who sees everything coming cannot see where any of them are going.

That is the thing I cannot bear. Not Arthur's lie — though that. Not the folder — though that. It's the *not knowing*. The blind spot opening up in the middle of the life I built specifically to have no blind spots.

I make a decision, sitting on my sofa at five o'clock on a Friday with a cold cup of coffee and a phone full of notes. It is not a good decision. It is, I will later understand, the kind of decision that people in films make, and the kind of decision that real investigators laugh at, and the kind of decision that will, within forty-eight hours, humiliate me so completely that I will want to dissolve into the floor of a subway car.

But I don't know that yet. All I know is that I cannot sit in this apartment and watch blue dots and read public websites and call it control. Watching is not control. Watching is just a prettier kind of helpless.

I have to follow him.

I have to see, with my own eyes, what my husband does when he thinks no one who loves him is looking.

I pick up the phone. I open the Notes app. My hand is, for the first time, not entirely steady.

```
NOTE — 5:03 PM — home
I'm going to follow him.
I know that's insane. I know that's a film thing.
But I have to see it. I can't think from in here.
Saturday. He always says "archive" on Saturdays.
If the dot goes to the Aurelian again, I go too.
```

I lock the note. I set the phone face-down. And somewhere under my ribs, in the cold animal place, the alarm that has been ringing all week changes its tone — from *something is wrong* to *something is starting* — and I understand, with the clarity that only comes right before you do something stupid, that there is no version of this where I stay the woman I was before.

The blue dot is home. Arthur comes through the door at six, warm and apologetic and smelling of paper, and he asks what's for dinner, and I smile my steady smile, and I don't tell him that I have just decided to become the kind of wife who follows her husband through the city in disguise.

That's Saturday.

Today, I make dinner. I do not burn the garlic. I watch my husband across the table the way I watch everyone — every tilt, every pause, every half-second — and I wait for him to move his phone.

He doesn't. He leaves it face-up on the table between us, screen visible, the picture of a man with nothing to hide.

And then, halfway through the pasta, the phone lights — a message, preview hidden — and Arthur does not move it, does not glance at it, and for three full seconds he does not breathe at all, and then he breathes, and he asks me to pass the salt, and the hand he reaches with is the steadiest it has been all week.

He does not react. That is the tell. The man who reacts to nothing is the man who has trained himself not to, and you do not train yourself out of reacting to ordinary messages.

Saturday. I follow him.
