# CHAPTER NINE
## Envelopes

I did it properly this time.

That's the thing GreyHat gave me, more than any single technique: the understanding that watching is not chasing. Watching is arriving early, sitting still, and letting the world walk past you while you press a button with one finger and pretend to read. It is the least glamorous thing a human being can do, and it is, I discovered, almost meditative — the same quiet alertness I bring to a coaching session, except now the room is a street corner and the client is my own husband and I am not allowed to blink.

Wednesday. One week after the first ping. I knew the pattern now: Arthur leaves the archive sometime between 1:00 and 4:30. So on Wednesday I took a half-day from the office — a "migraine," which was becoming my cover for everything, which was its own kind of telling — and I positioned myself two hours early in a coffee shop on West 28th, one block from the Aurelian, with a window seat and a book I did not read and my phone face-up on the table.

The dot was at the archive. I watched it the way GreyHat had taught me: not anxiously, not refreshing, just checking the gap. Arthur's dot. The archive. 1:12. 1:40. 2:15. Still there.

At 2:51, the dot moved.

It travelled north — the same route, the same direction as last week — and I felt the small electric certainty of a pattern confirmed. Patterns are power. A person who does the same thing twice has told you something about themselves they didn't mean to. I left seven dollars on the table, tucked my hair under a cap, and walked, not toward the Aurelian but to a spot I'd scouted that morning: a pharmacy entrance across the street with a clear sightline to the Aurelian's discreet brass door. I stood in the pharmacy pretending to read sunscreen labels. I waited.

Arthur arrived at 3:09. He looked, even from across the street, like a man checking over his shoulder. He went in.

I waited. I read the back of every sunscreen in the store. I did not chase. I did not enter. I stood, and I watched, and at 3:34 the brass door opened again and Arthur came out — and he was not alone.

A man. Fiftyish, thin, grey, in a coat too warm for the weather, the kind of coat a person wears when they've stopped caring about anything except the thing they're carrying. And he was carrying something. A large envelope — the rigid kind, the kind you don't fold, the kind that holds documents — and he pressed it into Arthur's hands on the pavement with the quick furtive motion of someone passing a note in class, and Arthur took it, and they spoke for perhaps eight seconds, and then the grey man walked fast in one direction and Arthur walked fast in another, and I, across the street, pressed the volume button on my phone three times.

My phone, set to silent, took three photographs in quick succession. GreyHat had told me this trick: most phones will snap a picture with a volume button, so you never have to lift the screen to your face like a tourist. You hold it at your hip. You press. You look like everyone else.

I looked like everyone else. The grey man and Arthur did not. They looked like two people completing a transaction they'd both rather no one saw.

I didn't follow either of them. I went home. I sat on my bed and I zoomed into the three photographs on my camera roll and I studied them the way I study a face, looking for the gap.

The envelope. Rigid. Document-shaped. The grey man's hand shaking slightly as he let go. Arthur's face — and here was the thing, the thing that ended the affair theory for good — Arthur's face was not the face of a man meeting a lover. It was the face of a man at a funeral. Tight. Grey. Resigned. The face of someone doing a hard necessary thing they wish they'd never had to start.

There was no woman. There had never been a woman. There was paper, and a frightened man, and my husband's funeral face, and a brushed-steel building full of people who never left reviews.

I had been wrong about the shape of it. I had spent a week grieving a betrayal that wasn't there, and now the real thing — the thing underneath — stood up in the space where the affair had been, and it was worse, because an affair I could have survived. This, whatever this was, had turned my steady husband into a man who met strangers on pavements and carried their documents home in his coat.

I read it twice. *You are standing next to a man with evidence.*

Arthur came home at 6:30, smelling of paper, and kissed my forehead, and asked what was for dinner, and I looked at his coat — the coat that had held the envelope — and I understood that the man I had married was carrying something heavier than I could see, and that he was carrying it alone because he thought, the way good men think, that he was protecting me by keeping me out of it.

He was wrong about that. The keeping-me-out part was the one thing putting me most in danger. Because the people who'd hidden whatever was on that paper — they didn't know I didn't know. To them, Arthur's wife was a variable. And variables, in the hands of people who build paper chains to hide their names, get removed.

I smiled at my husband. I made dinner. I did not burn anything.

Afterward I stood at the kitchen window with a glass of water and looked down at the street — the ordinary street, the one I had looked at a thousand nights — and there was a car I did not recognise, dark, idling at the kerb directly below us, no lights, no movement, just the low hum of an engine left running. I turned to tell Arthur.

He was already watching me. And the look on his face — careful, tired, and older than I had ever seen it — said, before he spoke: *Don't. Don't look. Don't let them see you see.*

"Come to bed," he said, mildly, and reached past me and pulled the curtain.

He knew the car was there. He had been waiting for me to notice it. Which meant it was not the first.

I followed him to the bedroom, and I did not look at the window again, and I lay in the dark beside my husband and listened to the hum of the engine below, steady as a heartbeat, and I understood that Arthur had been living with this — the idling cars, the watched windows, the knowledge that someone wanted what he had found — for longer than I had known. The door he had closed to protect me had never kept any of it out. It had only kept me from seeing it.

The hum did not stop until three in the morning.

I did not sleep, and neither did he, and neither of us said so.
