# CHAPTER EIGHT
## Daniel

The thing about my job is that people tell me things they don't tell anyone else, and then they pay me to pretend they didn't.

This is not cynicism. It's the contract. A founder doesn't come to me to confess; he comes to me to *manage*, and somewhere inside the managing, the truth falls out, and I catch it, and we both look politely away, and he leaves feeling lighter and I keep his secret in a locked cabinet in my head where it never sees daylight. I have held a thousand confessions and repeated none of them. It's the part of the work I'm proudest of, and also the part that makes me, on certain Monday afternoons, very tired.

Daniel Okafor has been my client for two years. He is forty-four, a real-estate partner at a white-shoe firm, and he came to me in a way I never quite examined at the time — he sought me out specifically, asked for me by name, through a referral from someone at Arthur's archive, of all places, just as Arthur's evening hours there began to stretch long. I remember thinking it was a lucky coincidence, a nice collision of my world and his. I have stopped believing in coincidences. Daniel is precise, funny, and so anxious that I can sometimes see his pulse in his throat before he's said a word. I like him. He tries hard. He is the kind of client who actually does the homework.

Today, he sits down across from me at 2:00 p.m. and I know in the first three seconds that something is badly wrong, because Daniel Okafor, who irons his shirt cuffs, is wearing a shirt with a crease across the front, and his hands, which are usually folded neatly, are doing nothing at all — just lying on the arms of the chair, like they've forgotten their job.

"Hey," I say. "Rough weekend?"

He laughs. It's the wrong laugh — too high, too quick, the laugh of a man who needs to fill a silence before it fills him. "You could say that." He meets my eyes, then doesn't. "Elena, can I — can we not do the usual thing today? I can't. I can't sit here and talk about my — my relationship with the managing partner. I genuinely cannot."

"Okay." I keep my voice where I want it. Low. Slower than his. "Then we don't."

"I'm sorry. I know this is your time. I'll pay for the—"

"Daniel. We don't." I let the silence do its work for a second. "Talk to me about whatever this is. Or don't. But you don't owe me a session."

He looks out the window. His knee is going. I have seen this exact posture in deposition-prep executives — the body bracing for a blow it can't see.

"There's a deal," he says, to the window. "At the firm. It's — it's a big one. I can't talk about it, I can't, I signed things, but Elena—" He turns back to me, and for a second the careful lawyer is gone and there's just a frightened man. "Have you ever worked on something and realised, partway through, that it was — that it wasn't what you thought it was? That the shape of it was wrong?"

I go very still. Because the woman I am professionally would say *tell me more about that feeling*. But the woman I have become in the last eight days hears *the shape of it was wrong* and feels the hair lift on her arms, because that is exactly how I would describe the company chain I traced this morning — a shape that was wrong, masks hiding masks.

"West 44th," I say.

I shouldn't have said it. It's a breach of everything — I don't know his deal, I don't know his client, and I've just named a piece of confidential information I only have because another client vented in my office. I watch the words land on him and I watch his face change, and the change is not surprise. It's fear.

"How do you know that." It isn't a question. His voice has gone flat and careful, the lawyer's voice, the voice that builds walls.

"I don't know your deal. I overheard the address somewhere. Forget I said it." I hold his gaze. "Daniel. Whatever this is — you look like a man who hasn't slept."

He stands up. Just like that, mid-session, mid-sentence, he stands and buttons his jacket with hands that aren't steady. "I have to go. I'm sorry. Elena, I have to — I need to cancel our next few sessions. Something's come up. I'll be in touch."

"Daniel."

"I'm sorry." He's already at the door. "You've been — you've been great. It's not you." And then, with his hand on the handle, he turns back, and there's something in his face I will replay for weeks — a look that is almost a warning, almost an apology, almost a plea. "Be careful," he says. "With — with whatever you're doing. The way you knew that address. Be careful. Please."

And then he's gone, and I'm sitting in my office with two untouched glasses of water and the echo of a man who just told me to be careful without telling me why.

---

I sit there for a long time.

Daniel is a real-estate lawyer. Daniel works on big deals. Daniel has just walked out of my office shaking because of a deal with the wrong shape on West 44th — the same West 44th where a network of paper companies has quietly assembled a city block, the same network whose paper trail runs, I discovered this morning, through my own building's sponsor.

The threads weren't three anymore. They were one. And now the one thread had walked into my office in an un-ironed shirt and told me to be careful.

I opened the Notes app. My hand was steady. I was, for the first time in eight days, not frightened so much as focused — the way I get focused in a session when a client is circling the truth, when I can feel the shape of it in the dark just ahead. The fear was still there. But underneath it, finally, something useful: I knew what I was looking at. I didn't know its name yet. But I knew it was one thing, not many, and that it was bigger than a lie about a penthouse.

I read the last line back. *Now it knows I'm looking.*

That was the part I couldn't prove and couldn't shake. I had been careful. I had used a throwaway account. I had searched public websites anyone could search. There was no reason for anyone to know.

But Daniel had walked in, heard one address, and gone white. And Arthur had turned, fast, on a subway grate, the turn of a frightened man. And somewhere in a brushed-steel building on West 29th, someone had been waiting for my husband at a time he'd lied to cover.

*Someone's waiting*, GreyHat had written. And now Daniel was running, and I was sitting in a quiet office realising that the safe distance I'd imagined between myself and this thing — the distance of a curious wife with a Notes app — had never existed at all.

I had thought I was reading a mystery. I was standing inside one. And the walls, which had only just started to move, were closing faster than I could map them.

I locked the note. I picked up my bag. And as I left the office that afternoon, for the first time in my life, I checked behind me in the reflection of the lobby glass — not because I'd seen anything, but because Daniel Okafor, a man who reads contracts for a living, had looked at me as if I'd just stepped into traffic.

I had. I just didn't know which lane.
