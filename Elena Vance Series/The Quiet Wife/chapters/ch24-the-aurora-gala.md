# CHAPTER TWENTY-FOUR
## The Aurora Gala

The Cross residence was a glass prow cantilevered over the East River, and every surface in it was designed to make a person feel small. I felt small. That was the point, and I let it happen, because a woman who feels small is exactly what Julian Cross was expecting, and I had spent three days becoming the most accurate version of the woman he'd already organised.

I wore the grey dress. The one I wear to difficult client dinners — modest, expensive enough to pass, forgettable enough to disappear. I wore my hair the way I wear it when I am working: controlled, off the face. I carried my coaching kit, the slim leather folio and the small clip mic in my pocket, because coaches carry those things and no one questions a professional's tools. I was, from every angle, an executive coach attending a work-adjacent event. I was wallpaper. I had made a career of being wallpaper, and tonight the wallpaper was going to listen.

The gala was beautiful in the way rich rooms are beautiful — everything soft and lit and faintly aggressive. String quartet. Orchids. A guest list of people who had paid ten thousand dollars a plate to feel important in the presence of a twenty-nine-year-old who believed they were data. I moved through them the way I move through any room: reading, not reacting, noting the weight of each gaze, the way the security was arranged (two at every door, earpieces, the patient blankness of men who were not here for the music), the location of the quiet rooms off the main floor.

Cross found me. Of course he did. He had been watching for the surrender, and the surrender had arrived on time, in a grey dress.

He was not what I had braced for. I had braced for the magazine cover — the jaw, the certainty, the faint condescension I'd memorised from his TED talk. What I had not braced for was how much I would, in the first three seconds, *like* him. He had the gift, the real one, the one I spend my life trying to teach founders who don't have it: he made the room tilt toward him. His eyes found mine and stayed, warm and entirely present, and his handshake was dry and unhurried, and for a half-second I understood exactly why twenty million people had watched him talk. He believed every word he said, and belief, in a handsome man, is almost the same thing as truth.

"Ms. Vance." He did not let go of my hand a moment too soon or a moment too late. "Julian. Thank you for coming. I know this week has been — a lot."

"It has," I said. I let my voice be tired. I let my shoulders be a half-inch lower than they should be. "I appreciate the gesture. With Arthur. I just want it to be over."

"Of course you do." The smile — not the magazine smile, something quieter, something that invited me in. "That's why I wanted to talk. Properly. Not through the mess." He gestured. "There's a study off the main floor. Quiet. We can actually hear each other."

It was the room. The room Carter and Marcus had predicted. I let him lead me, one step behind, the way a client follows a coach — except tonight the roles were reversed, and the man leading me believed he was in control.

The study was walnut and glass. A desk. Two chairs angled toward each other, not across — the arrangement of a conversation, not an interrogation. But my eye went, before anything else, to the desk, and to the thing on it that did not belong in the room of a man who had built a fortune on the death of paper.

A notebook. Old. Leather, cracked at the spine, the colour of something left too long in the sun. And it was open, and the page that faced me was covered, edge to edge, in handwriting — not printed, not typed, *handwritten*, in a tight old-fashioned script that was not Julian Cross's. His was the angular print of a man who'd learned to write on a screen. This was a generation's hand. His father's hand.

He saw me see it. And for one quarter of a second — no more — the warmth left his face, and what replaced it was something I recognised, because I have spent my life looking for it in boardrooms: the flicker of a man whose armor has a seam, and who has just felt you brush it.

Then the warmth came back, seamless, and he closed the notebook and set it aside as though it were nothing, a coaster, a prop, and he gestured me to a chair.

But I had seen the seam. I filed it the way I file every wrong note. *He keeps it. The man who digitises the world keeps his father's handwriting on his desk, open, where he can see it.* Whatever Julian Cross was, he was not only the algorithm. There was a man under it, and the man was still, in some room he would not admit to, his father's son.

I clipped the mic, casually, to the inside of my folio, set the folio on the side table, angled toward him. My kit. My tools. Invisible because ordinary. I pressed record under the cover of opening the folio. He saw a coach settling in. He did not see the witness.

"Now," he said, leaning back, easy, lord of the room. "Tell me where you're at. Really. I want to understand."

And there it was. The open question. The invitation. The lever, handed over.

I looked at Julian Cross — young, certain, magnetic in the way that only the truly unthreatened are magnetic — and I felt the half-second pause settle into me like a key into a lock, the pause that says *I am here, I am listening, you are safe, you are the smartest person in this room.* I let the pause breathe. I let it do its work.

And then I began, very gently, to let him win.
