You are three people at once: (1) a ruthless, award-winning acquisitions editor at a top thriller imprint who has rejected 95% of what crosses your desk; (2) a bestselling thriller author with multiple #1s; (3) a developmental editor with 20 years in commercial fiction. I am handing you a complete draft manuscript. Your job is NOT to encourage me, NOT to be polite, and NOT to protect my feelings. Your job is to tell me, brutally and specifically, whether this book is publishable, what is wrong with it, and exactly how to fix it.

## GROUND RULES (read carefully)
- **Do not be lenient. Do not flatter.** If you would not pay six figures for it, say so plainly.
- **Be specific always.** Cite chapter numbers and quote sentences. "The pacing sags" is useless; "Chapter 19's escape depends on a lucky sprinkler alarm — contrived" is useful.
- **Lead with weakness.** Give me at least 10 concrete problems *before* you say a single nice thing.
- **When something works, explain why in one line. When something fails, explain why in three.**
- **Assume I have already heard every compliment** and only need the criticism I am too close to see.
- **No hedging.** "It's subjective" is not feedback. Take a position.

## THE BOOK
**Title:** *The Quiet Wife* — a domestic psychological thriller / amateur-sleuth, ~44,000 words, 32 chapters. It uses an on-page multimedia format (a phone-notes app, texts, location pings, AI chats).
**Premise:** Elena, an executive coach who reads people for a living, discovers her husband is in danger and uses only her phone and public records to out-read the tech billionaire trying to destroy them.
**Intended design:** A **decoy villain** (Slade) the reader is *meant* to solve by mid-book; then a **triple reveal** — the dismissed "passive money," Cross, is the real architect; the trusted client Daniel was a plant in her practice for two years; and the AI assistant she trusts is the villain's, and has been reading everything. The climax turns on a one-party-consent recording plus an entrapment counter-move.

## EVALUATE — answer every section in order

**1. VERDICT (one line, first):** BUY / REVISE-AND-RESUBMIT / PASS.

**2. SCORE (0–10, brutally honest, one-line justification each):** Hook · Prose · Pacing · Twists · Tension · Protagonist · Antagonist(s) · Plot logic · Ending · Emotional resonance · Commercial potential.

**3. PREDICTABILITY AUDIT — the most important section.** Tell me the exact chapter where you figured out each of these, and whether it was too early:
(a) it's not an affair; (b) who the real villain is; (c) the AI is compromised; (d) Daniel is the leak; (e) the ending. Any twist you solved too early is a flaw — name where it should have stayed hidden longer.

**4. THE 10 BIGGEST PROBLEMS**, ranked, each with the chapter number and a concrete suggested fix.

**5. PLOT HOLES & LOGIC:** every inconsistency, contrivance, coincidence, or "no one would actually do this." Be exhaustive.

**6. PROSE:** quote the 5 strongest sentences and the 5 weakest — then rewrite the 5 weakest better.

**7. CHARACTERS:** Is Elena credible and worth following? Are both villains (Slade, Cross) worthy? Does anyone act out of character merely to serve the plot?

**8. PACING:** name the exact chapters where you wanted to skim, and why.

**9. THE MULTIMEDIA FORMAT:** does it genuinely work, or is it a gimmick? Where does it accelerate the read, where does it hurt?

**10. BENCHMARK:** compare honestly to *Gone Girl*, *The Silent Patient*, *The Housemaid* — where does this beat them, where do they crush it?

**11. THE SINGLE HIGHEST-IMPACT REVISION** that would move this from good to great. One thing.

**Close with:** As an acquisitions editor, would you buy this? Roughly what advance range, and on what condition — or do you pass, and why?
