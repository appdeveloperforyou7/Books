# VOICE & STYLE GUIDE — ELENA VANCE, BOOK 2

> The prose standard and house rules. Draft every chapter against this. The structure is locked in the bibles; this document is what makes the writing reach it.

---

## 1. POINT OF VIEW & TENSE

- **First person, past tense, Elena.** Matches Book 1. She is our only lens.
- She narrates from *inside* the perception, not above it. We are in her attention as it moves, not being told what she concluded.
- Rare, deliberate exceptions: a chapter may open with a short present-tense frame if it earns it. Default is past.

---

## 2. ELENA'S NARRATIVE VOICE

**Three words:** precise, controlled, weighted.

- **Observant, not flowery.** She notices the specific physical thing — the half-turn of a wrist, the glass placed a quarter-inch too symmetrically — and lets it carry the meaning. Metaphor is earned and exact, never decorative.
- **The pause, rendered.** Her signature half-second pause appears in the *prose rhythm* — a held sentence, a beat of white space, before the next line lands. Control is her character; let the syntax embody it.
- **Dry, understated wit.** Not jokes. The wry recognition of a woman who has read ten thousand rooms and finds the repetitions gently absurd. Humor is pressure relief, never broad.
- **Moral weight underneath.** Even in calm scenes, the awareness that reading is a lever sits beneath the surface. She is not cozy about her gift.
- **Economical.** No filler, no throat-clearing, no summarizing what was just shown. If a detail doesn't advance detection, deepen character, or build dread, cut it.

**The benchmark sentence:** specific sensory image + the perception it triggers + the controlled withholding of the conclusion. *Example:* "She set the glass down in the exact centre of the coaster, and I watched her do it, and I did not yet know that symmetry is what a guilty hand reaches for when the rest of it is coming apart."

---

## 3. THE PROSE STANDARD (Tana French level)

What "literary execution" concretely means here:

- **Sensory specificity.** Name the exact thing — the sound, the smell, the weight. Vagueness is the enemy of dread. "The house was quiet" dies; "the grandfather clock in the hall had stopped, and its silence was louder than its ticking had been" lives.
- **Interiority that earns emotion.** Elena's thought is perception laced with feeling, never abstract rumination. We feel her fear/control through *what she notices and how*, not through her announcing it.
- **Rhythm.** Long accumulating sentences that gather tension, broken by short ones that land like a door closing. Vary within every page.
- **Every paragraph does work.** Detection, character, or dread — ideally two of three. A paragraph that only describes is a paragraph to cut.
- **No cliché.** If a phrase has been used before, find the fresh image. "Heart pounding" → show the specific way her body fails her this time.
- **End on the image, not the explanation.** Let the concrete detail hold the meaning; trust the reader.

---

## 4. RENDERING "READING" (the book's signature — get this right)

The whole book depends on making Elena's perception vivid and fair. Rules:

- **Show the tell, don't name the conclusion.** Never "I could tell she was lying." Instead: render the specific micro-behavior, then let Elena *file* it without stating what it means yet. The reader feels the wrong note before Elena names it.
- **The gap is the unit.** Every read is a *gap* — between face and words, between performance and truth, between what a witness says and what their hands do. Render both sides; let the space between them hum.
- **Reading is physical.** It happens through eyes, posture, breath, the skin. Keep it embodied, never clinical.
- **Fair-play lives in the read.** The solving clues are *shown* in these moments (the uniform midnight music; Clara's too-correct grief; the staged glass). Plant them as specific, quotable details a reader could note and later say "I should have seen it."
- **Elena is sometimes wrong.** She locks onto Diane and is wrong. Render her certainty convincingly — a reader who is always right is dull and unfair. Her reading is formidable, not infallible.

---

## 5. DIALOGUE RULES

- **The under-conversation.** Every interview is a contest: Elena reads; the suspect controls what's read. Write the *surface* line and let the subtext live in the action/beats around it. The real conversation is in the pauses and the body.
- **Distinct voices** (per character-bible):
  - **Elena:** low, unhurried, the question that lands after a pause.
  - **Joss:** fast, flat New York, blunt, swears; drops to a murmur when reading a scene.
  - **Clara:** warm, breathy, gentle — the listening voice; *too* perfect.
  - **Diane:** dry, cutting, no politeness left.
  - **Thea:** sharp, clipped, adrenalized.
  - **Hugh:** quiet, cultured, lost.
  - **Dr. Voss:** measured, clinical-warm, authoritative.
- **No speech tags overkill.** "Said" is invisible; use it. Reserve "asked/replied" and action beats for rhythm. Never use adverbial tags ("she said angrily") — the line should carry its own tone.

---

## 6. ARTIFACT FORMATTING (match Book 1 conventions)

Artifacts carry clues, never decoration. Use the established visual language:

- **Notes app** — Elena's reasoning spine; terse, timestamped, location-stamped, often ending on an open question (`=>`). Mono-spaced code block.
  ```
  NOTE — 9:42 PM — estate, my room
  Clara's grief = too correct. No mess in it.
  Maren "despondent lately" — but 3 witnesses said
  "wired," "on a mission." 
  => Gap. Whose story is the despair?
  ```
- **Texts/SMS** — bold header block, sender + time.
- **The e-reader log / device data** — a small clean block (timestamp + event).
- **Interview excerpts / transcripts** — rendered when a key statement must be scannable for seams (replay value + fair-play).
- **Archive document** — the authenticated original; render as a described image or a quoted excerpt.
- Keep artifacts short; they read in seconds and accelerate pace. **Every artifact reveals something.**

---

## 7. TONE REGISTER

- **Dread over action.** This is a reading-detective; the tension is perceptual ("what is really going on"), not kinetic. Sustained, accumulating unease.
- **Cerebral but felt.** Smart, never cold. The emotion lives in Elena's stakes (the moral cost; Clara as mirror; the marriage).
- **Feminine, adult, clean.** No gore, no erotica. Power, grief, rivalry, sisterhood — the serious emotional territory of women's lives.
- **One physical set-piece** (Ch 22, the archive) supplies the genre's body-tension; everywhere else, the body is in service of perception.

---

## 8. CHAPTER SHAPE (enforcement of the engagement guide)

Every chapter must pass:
1. **Opens mid-tension** — mid-scene, mid-exchange, mid-thought. Never waking, weather, travel summary.
2. **Ends on a hook** (Q/R/V/T/E/C) — never resolution.
3. **Carries micro-tension** — something contested in every scene.
4. **Closes one question, opens a bigger one.**
5. **Something changes** by the chapter's end.
- **Length:** 2,000–2,500 words standard; **800–1,200** for the four beat-chapters (Ch 8, 14, 22, 28).

---

## 9. WHAT TO AVOID

- Cliché and stock thriller phrasing.
- Info-dumps; weave exposition into perception and dialogue.
- Opening on waking up, the weather, or "the next morning."
- Over-explaining a read ("I knew then that she was guilty…"). Show, file, move on.
- Melodrama; earn every emotion through specificity.
- Repetition of the same image/tell; each clue appears in fresh language.
- Sidekick-ifying Joss; she leads half the detection.

---

## 10. HOUSE-STYLE CHEAT SHEET

- POV: Elena, 1st person, past tense.
- Voice: precise, controlled, weighted; dry wit; moral undertow.
- Unit of perception: the **gap** (performance vs. truth).
- Clues: shown as specific details, filed not named.
- Dialogue: under-conversation; distinct voices; "said."
- Artifacts: Notes/texts/logs/transcripts/doc — every one a clue.
- Tone: perceptual dread; cerebral but felt; clean.
- Chapters: mid-tension open, hook close, 2–2.5k (beats 0.8–1.2k).
- Standard: specific sensory image + perception + withheld conclusion. Cut anything that only describes.
