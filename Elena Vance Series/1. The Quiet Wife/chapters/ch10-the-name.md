# CHAPTER TEN
## The Name

GreyHat had said: *keep pulling the thread. You're looking for the one human name at the bottom of the pile.*

The pile was deep. By Thursday I had eleven companies, all registered through the same Delaware services firm, all trading property between themselves like cards in a shell game. Hargrove, my building's sponsor. Crestline. Northbridge, the neighbour's landlord. Meridian, the 44th Street assembler. Seven others I'd never heard of. Eleven masks, and behind every mask another mask, and the thread, when I pulled it, just kept spooling into darker.

I needed help. So I did what any modern person does when a problem is too big for one brain: I split it with the AI. I've used this particular assistant for years — drafting, summarising, talking through problems out loud without anyone judging. I trust it the way you trust a calculator, which is to say provisionally: it does the boring arithmetic so I can do the thinking.

```
🤖 Chat — 11:08 AM
Elena: I have 11 company names. They're all connected —
they trade property between each other. All registered
through the same agent so I can't see the real owners.
How do investigators find the actual human being behind
something like this?

AI: A few tried-and-true approaches:
1) Look for "beneficial owner" filings some states require.
2) Cross-reference the SIGNATURES on the deeds — the
person who actually signed, even as "agent," often
repeats across a network.
3) Search court filings: shell companies get sued, and
lawsuits sometimes name the real people behind them.
4) Follow the MONEY, not just the paper — mortgages,
liens, and lender names often trace back to one source.
```

I did all four. The money was the loudest thread, and it ran, cleanly, to a name: **Cross Holdings**, the lender on the mortgages behind Crestline and Meridian. Cross Holdings was real and big — a private holding company, real estate, data centres, something called "predictive analytics" — and at the top of it sat a person, Julian Cross, twenty-nine, tech billionaire, magazine covers, the works.

I filed him. And then I filed him *away*.

Here is the thing they don't tell you about finding money in a property scheme: the money is almost never the villain. The money is the fuel. Lenders finance deals; they don't run them. Julian Cross was a name on a mortgage document, the same way a bank is a name on a mortgage document — present, necessary, passive. A boy-genius tech billionaire does not personally drill through his neighbour's wall or place a fake tenant in a co-op or send vans to circle a dowdy residential block. That is not lender behaviour. That is *operator* behaviour. And operators have names, and signatures, and they leave a different kind of trail.

So I went back to GreyHat's second rule — the signatures — and I pulled that thread instead.

```
SEARCH — ACRIS + court filings — 12:30 PM
Signatories on the Hargrove/Crestline/Northbridge transfers.
```

The same hand had signed for seven of the eleven companies, as "authorised agent," over fifteen years. Not the Delaware services firm — a real person, a local man, named on the filings because New York, for a while, required it. And the moment I had the name, the internet opened like a door.

**Richard Slade.**

Slade was not a tech billionaire. Slade was not on magazine covers. Slade was the kind of man who appeared in the back pages of the business section, in the paragraphs about lawsuits — a real-estate *fixer*, a name that developers whispered and tenants feared, a man who had spent thirty years assembling Manhattan blocks for other people's money through a method the city had slowly learned to recognise: pressure, paper, and the quiet removal of anyone in the way. There were tenant advocacy groups named after his tactics. There was a settlement. There was, everywhere, the same reputation: Richard Slade was the man you hired when you wanted a building emptied and you did not much care how.

This was the villain. Not the lender on the mortgage — the operator with his hand on the drill. I felt it the way I feel a client's lie: the certainty that locks into place when the shape finally fits. Slade fit. Slade explained the wall, and Royce, and the vans, and the thirty years of slow assembly, and the specific, patient cruelty of it all. A man who empties buildings for a living had been emptying mine.

```
NOTE — 12:48 PM — office
RICHARD SLADE. Real-estate fixer. 30 yrs.
Signed as agent for 7 of 11 shell companies.
Reputation: empties buildings. Pressure + paper.
=> Slade is the OPERATOR running the block grab.
=> The wall, Royce, the vans = his playbook.
Cross Holdings = just the lender (passive money).
The villain is Slade. A man who removes people.
```

I messaged GreyHat. I was certain. The certainty felt, after two weeks of fog, almost like joy.

```
💬 DM — u/throwaway_v2781 → u/GreyHat_4 — 12:55 PM
Found him. Richard Slade. Real-estate fixer, 30 years,
signed the shell-company transfers himself. He's the
one running the block grab — the wall, the fake tenant,
all of it. Cross Holdings shows up as the lender but
that's just financing. The operator is Slade. This is
his whole career. How do I prove what he's doing to my
building specifically?
```

```
💬 u/GreyHat_4 — 1:04 PM
Slow down.
You've found a hand. You haven't found the brain.
Fixers like Slade don't own the play. They're hired.
Someone is paying him to empty that block, and the
someone is the one who matters.
Who's the lender? Who's the money?
```

```
💬 u/throwaway_v2781 — 1:05 PM
Cross Holdings. Julian Cross. But he's just the
financing — lenders don't run operations like this.
```

The reply took a long time. When it came, it was the first time GreyHat had ever seemed, not alarmed, but careful — the careful of a man choosing whether to say a thing.

```
💬 u/GreyHat_4 — 1:12 PM
Maybe. Maybe he's just the money.
But here's a question you should sit with:
why would one of the richest men in the city lend
into a dowdy residential block through eleven shells
he doesn't control, to a fixer with Slade's reputation?
Lenders who don't care about control get burned.
Lenders who DO care about control use shells to HIDE
that they care.
Don't assume Slade is the top just because he's the one
you can see. The visible man is almost never the architect.
The visible man is the one they WANT you to find.
```

I read it twice. *The visible man is almost never the architect.* It was a good warning. It was also, I decided, a caution from a careful man who didn't yet have the feel of this the way I did. I had read the shape. Slade was the operator. Slade was the villain. The money was the money. I had spent two weeks chasing fog; now I had a name and a face and a thirty-year reputation, and I was not going to dilute that certainty with a what-if about a lender who signed a mortgage.

That was the mistake. The mistake was not chasing Slade — Slade was real, and Slade was guilty, and the wall and Royce and the vans were his work. The mistake was the certainty. The certainty that the man I could see was the man who mattered. The certainty that made me stop asking the one question — *who is the money, and why does the money care* — at exactly the moment I should have asked it hardest.

Because GreyHat was right, and I did not listen, because listening would have meant that the villain was not the man drilling through my wall but the quiet young man on the magazine cover who had lent him the drill, and that was a shape I was not yet ready to see.

I closed the laptop. I thought about Richard Slade, the fixer, the visible villain, the man I was going to bring down.

He was about to become the only thing standing between me and the truth.

And he was not, I would learn too late, the truth at all.
