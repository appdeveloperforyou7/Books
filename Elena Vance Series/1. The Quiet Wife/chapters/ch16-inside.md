# CHAPTER SIXTEEN
## Inside

I knew before I opened the door.

I want to be clear about that, because later people will ask how I could tell, and the honest answer is that I couldn't tell you a single thing that was wrong, the way I can never tell you a single thing that's wrong when a client is lying. It was a feeling. The feeling of a room that has been breathed in by someone who wasn't supposed to breathe in it. The air was the same air, the light was the same light, and every object was exactly where I had left it, and none of them were mine anymore.

I stood in the doorway of my own apartment and I knew.

I went in slowly. I did not call out. My keys were still in my hand, and I held them between my fingers the way GreyHat had once offhandedly mentioned you hold keys in a stairwell, and I am not a violent woman but in that moment I understood, for the first time, that I could be.

The living room: undisturbed. The kitchen: undisturbed. The bedroom — I stopped. The drawer of my bedside table was closed. I had left it open a half-inch, the way I always do, because the runner sticks and I never bother to push it flush. It was closed now. Flush. Perfect.

I opened it. Everything was there. My passport. My spare cards. The little notebook — the paper one, the one I'd been using for the questions I didn't trust to the phone. It was there. In the same place. Looking untouched.

I picked it up. The pages were slightly warmer than they should have been, the way paper is warm after it's been under a bright light, or a camera.

They had photographed it. Every page. And put it back. And closed the drawer. So that I would know.

I went through the apartment room by room, and the horror of it was not what they'd taken, because they'd taken nothing. The horror was the precision. They had been inside my home, my bedroom, my drawer, and they had touched everything just enough to let me understand that they had touched everything, and then they had restored it all to its place, like curators, like a warning written in the language of rearrangement. *We were here. We can be here again. There is no room in your life we cannot enter.*

The wall. I went to the wall. The new bore-hole from Friday was there, but beside it, fresh, was a second hole — neater, smaller, the kind made by a camera on a fibre cable, the kind that lets you see inside a cavity without breaking it. They had looked into my wall. They had sent an eye into the space between my apartment and Royce's, looking for the thing Arthur had hidden, and they had not found it, and the not-finding was the only reason, I understood with a cold clarity, that they had not yet taken the wall apart entirely.

They were still searching. Which meant they did not know where it was. Which meant Arthur's hiding place — the ordinary, genius, terrible place he had told me about that morning — had held.

For now.

I sat on my bed, in my violated bedroom, with my photographed notebook in my lap, and I did the thing I had spent my whole life training other people not to do: I let the feeling come, all of it, without managing it. The fear. The fury. The grief for the apartment that had been my safe place and was now a crime scene I couldn't prove. The sick understanding that my Notes app — my confessional, my one honest place — had probably been read too, because if they'd photographed the paper notebook they'd had time for the phone, and if they'd had the phone they'd had my questions to the AI, and if they'd had my questions to the AI—

I stopped.

My questions to the AI.

I had been asking the AI everything. About shell companies. About the keystone. About losing a tail. About my apartment. I had poured my entire investigation into a chat window, the way you pour water into a glass, trusting the glass to hold it. And the AI was a product, made by a company, and companies were owned by people, and the person at the top of the company that made this particular, popular, helpful assistant was—

I couldn't finish the thought. Not yet. It was too large. It sat at the edge of my mind like a door I was not ready to open, because if I opened it, everything changed, and I had already had enough everything-change for one Tuesday.

I opened the Notes app. My hand was steady. Steadier than I felt.

```
NOTE — 7:18 PM — bedroom, door locked
They were inside the apartment today while I was at work.
Nothing taken. Everything photographed. The paper notebook.
Probably the phone.
Wall has a 2nd hole now — a camera cable. They looked inside.
They did NOT find the record. Arthur's hiding place held.
BUT: they've read my questions. They know what I know.
They know I know about Slade, the keystone, Royce, all of it.
The watching is over. The warning is over.
This is the part where it stops being a mystery and
starts being a war.
```

I stared at the last line. *A war.* It was melodramatic. It was also, I realised, accurate, and accuracy was the only thing keeping me upright.

Arthur came home at seven-thirty. He saw my face and he didn't ask. He looked at the drawer, at the wall, at the place where the second hole was, and I watched the same journey I'd made — the horror, the fury, the cold — cross his face in five seconds, and then he did something I did not expect.

He took my hand. He held it. He said, very quietly, "I'm sorry. I'm sorry I brought this here. I'm sorry I didn't tell you. I'm sorry I thought I could carry it. I can't. We're in it. We're in it together, and from now on I don't close a single door on you, I swear it, Elena, I swear."

I held his hand. My steady, paper-reading, sandwich-eating husband, who had tried to save a stolen city block and brought the weight of it home to the woman he loved. I forgave him. I forgave him because the alternative was drowning, and because he was right: we were in it together now, and the time for closed doors was finished.

"We move it tomorrow," I said. "The record. Out of this apartment. And then we find someone real — a journalist, a federal agent, someone whose whole career is built on embarrassing people like Richard Slade — and we hand it over, and we let the law do what the law does. And we stop being the people hiding the evidence and start being the people who used it."

"How?" he asked. "They're watching the building. They're inside the walls."

"Then we don't move it the way they expect." I looked at him. "We move it the way I move. The way I've been moving all week. We use the ordinary. We use the boring. We use the fact that I am a woman with a coaching practice and a calendar and a coat, and that no one in a van has ever, in the history of surveillance, looked twice at an executive coach on her way to a client session."

Arthur stared at me. I saw it again — that terrible tired wonder — and then, for the first time in two weeks, something that was almost a smile.

"You've changed," he said.

"I read the room," I said. "I always read the room. I just finally read this one."

Outside, the two vans sat at the kerb in the dark, patient as paper, and the man on the bench turned the collar of his coat up against the cold, and the building breathed around us with its old brick and its stolen title and the keystone hidden somewhere a billionaire's men had not yet thought to tear apart.

We were inside it now. All the way.

And tomorrow we were going to stop running from the shape of it, and start breaking it instead.
