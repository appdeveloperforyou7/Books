# CHAPTER ELEVEN
## The Wall

The drilling starts at 7:14 on Friday morning and it sounds like someone is putting a hole in my skull.

I am in the shower. The sound comes through the tiles, through the pipe, through the bones of the building itself — a high industrial whine that I feel in my teeth before I hear it in my ears. I stand frozen under the water for three seconds, because the sound is coming from the wrong place. Not above (the roofers, last spring). Not below (the lobby renovation, two years ago). From the side. From the wall I share with 6B. From Royce's side.

I get dressed fast, hair wet, and I press my palm flat against the shared wall of my bedroom, and the wall is *vibrating*, a deep mechanical hum that means something heavier than a picture hook is being driven through brick, and I think, with a clarity that surprises me, *they are coming through*.

I go next door. I bang on 6B.

Royce opens it in a tracksuit, gym-sweaty, and behind him the hallway is full of dust and two men in masks with a coring rig, and the noise is twice as loud here, and Royce is smiling the smile I have come to hate, the wide obliging smile of a man who is never, ever sorry.

"Elena! Hey. God, sorry — is it loud over there? They're running a cable. Building code thing. Landlord insisted. Should be done by lunch."

"A cable," I say. "Through a load-bearing wall. Without notice."

"It's a thin bore, honestly, you won't even—"

"Did the board approve this?"

His smile doesn't move. That's the tell. A man who is telling the truth about a thin bore and a building code lets his face react to a reasonable question. Royce's face holds its shape the way a mask holds its shape. "Gerald signed off. Ask him." He shrugs, big and slow, the shrug of a man who has already won. "Sorry again. You know how buildings are."

He closes the door. The drilling continues.

I do not go back to my apartment. I go to the window at the end of the hall and I watch the street, and within four minutes a white van pulls away from the kerb below — unmarked, no company name on the side, just a smeared number on the door that I photograph before it turns the corner, pressing the volume button at my hip the way GreyHat taught me. No name. A number. A van that exists the way a shell company exists: to do a job and leave nothing behind.

I go home. I stand in my bedroom and I look at my wall, my wall, which now has a faint circle of fresh plaster dust blooming at the join, and I press my hand to it again, and the vibration is still there, and I understand something I didn't understand an hour ago.

This is not a cable.

Royce wants my apartment. I knew that. But a man who merely wants to buy an apartment does not core through the wall between them at 7 a.m. on a Friday. A man who wants to *combine* the units waits until he owns both. You do not renovate a wall you don't yet control unless the wall itself is the point — unless there is something about this particular wall, this particular join between 6A and 6B, that someone needs to reach from Royce's side before they have any legal right to be there.

```
NOTE — 7:48 AM — bedroom, dust on the floor
Royce drilling into our shared wall. "Cable." Lie.
No notice. Gerald "signed off" (under pressure, again).
Van: unmarked, number only, left fast. Ghost vehicle.
They are not trying to get me OUT.
They are trying to get IN. Into the wall. From his side.
=> What is in this wall that they need to reach?
```

I stared at the question. It was insane. Walls don't have things in them. This is a Manhattan co-op built in 1928; the walls are brick and lath and a hundred years of bad wiring. There is nothing *in* my wall.

And yet Royce — Royce, whose apartment is owned by Northbridge, owned by the network Slade signed for, the man whose entire existence in 6B is starting to look like a deployment rather than a tenancy — was drilling into it with an industrial coring rig at dawn, lying about it, and vanishing the evidence.

I thought about Arthur. About the envelope of records. About an archivist who'd found a seam in the paper. And I thought, for the first time, a thought I couldn't make neat: *what if the seam isn't only in the paper? What if there's something physical here — a record, a survey, a plan — that proves what Arthur found, and it's in this building, and they need to get to it through this wall?*

I was guessing. I knew I was guessing. But the guesses were getting less wild by the day, and the wall was vibrating under my hand, and somewhere on the other side of the brick Royce's men were boring toward something I couldn't see.

I picked up my phone. I messaged GreyHat.

```
💬 DM — u/throwaway_v2781 → u/GreyHat_4 — 7:55 AM
New development. The neighbour is drilling through our
shared wall. No permits I can find. Lied about why.
Unmarked van. He doesn't want to push me out of the
apartment. He wants to get INTO the wall. From his side.
Am I losing my mind, or does this mean something?
```

The reply was almost instant, and it was the first time GreyHat had ever sounded alarmed.

```
💬 u/GreyHat_4 — 7:57 AM
You're not losing your mind. This is escalation.
They're not pressuring you anymore. They're working
AROUND you. That means they've decided you're not
leaving on your own, and they're not waiting.
Whatever is in that wall, or whatever that wall gives
them access to — they need it, and they need it now.
Document everything. Photos. Times. The van number.
And Elena — change your locks. Today.
```

I read the last two words twice. *Change your locks.*

A stranger on the internet, who knew me only as a panicking throwaway account, had just told me to change the locks on my own home, and the terrible thing was that it did not sound paranoid. It sounded like the first sensible advice anyone had given me in a fortnight.

I looked at the wall. I looked at my door. I thought about my father, and the morning he left, and the door I never thought to lock because I believed, with a child's faith, that the people who loved me would always come back through it.

I picked up my keys. I went to the locksmith.

And on the way, in the lobby, I passed Gerald Mott, who would not meet my eye, and I understood — finally, fully — that the man who signed off on a hole in my wall was not my neighbour's ally and not my own. He was afraid, the way Daniel was afraid, the way Arthur was afraid, the way everyone who touched the edges of this thing became afraid. The fear was the shape of it. The fear was how I knew it was real.
