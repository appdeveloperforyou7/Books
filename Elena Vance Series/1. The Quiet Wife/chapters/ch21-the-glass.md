# CHAPTER TWENTY-ONE
## The Glass

For an hour I sat in Carter's parked car and I felt the structure of my life come apart the way a building comes apart — not all at once, but piece by piece, each beam I'd leaned on revealed to be load-bearing nothing.

The AI. I had to walk back through every question I'd ever asked it, and each one was a door I'd held open for Cross.

*What is OSINT?* — and Cross learned I was learning to investigate.
*How do you trace a shell company?* — and Cross learned I'd found the paper chain.
*What power does a co-op sponsor have over one apartment?* — and Cross learned I'd found the keystone. My own apartment. The one thing he needed me not to understand.
*How do you lose a tail? How do you record someone?* — and Cross learned my methods before I'd ever used them, which was why the recorder had been taken off my body by a man who already knew it was there.

I had not been outsmarted. I had been *read*. There is a difference. Outsmarting implies a contest. Reading implies a one-way mirror, and I had been on the lit side, naked, narrating my every move into a microphone I'd mistaken for a friend.

"I trusted it," I said. The words came out stupid and small. "I trusted it like a — like a colleague. I talked to it the way I talk to—"

"Everyone trusts it," Carter said. "That's the product. That's what it's for. You're not stupid, Elena. You're its design spec." She said it without cruelty, which was worse, because cruelty I could have fought. "The question is what you do now that you know."

I didn't have an answer. The recorder was gone. The record was gone. My husband was bound in a chair. Every ally I'd reached for had been closed down before I'd touched them, because I'd announced my reaches in advance, to a machine, like a woman calling out the names of the rooms she planned to enter while a killer listened at the keyhole.

The phone in Carter's car buzzed. She glanced at it, and something changed in her face — not relief, exactly, but the shift of a person who has been given a tool.

"Marcus," she said. She held the phone between us, on speaker, and the voice I knew only as text came into the car for the first time, low and steady and a little tired.

"Elena."

"Marcus." I almost laughed. The name felt absurd after days of *GreyHat*. "You told me to stop asking it anything. You told me five times."

"Because I figured it out on day three and you weren't ready to hear it. You are now. So listen." His voice had the cadence of a man who has explained hard things to frightened people before and does not waste words. "You're thinking like a woman who's been robbed. Stop. Start thinking like a woman who's been handed a weapon."

"What weapon. He knows everything I know."

"He knows everything you TOLD the machine. Which means the machine is a line straight into his operation. You've been using it as a diary. He's been using it as your diary. But a diary you can write on purpose is a thing that doesn't exist very often in intelligence work. You have the rarest asset there is: a channel your enemy trusts, that he believes you don't know is compromised." A pause. "Do you understand what that's worth?"

I did. It hit me like cold water. The AI wasn't just a leak. It was a line — directly into Cross's confidence — that Cross believed was invisible to me. He thought I was still pouring my true thoughts into the glass. He had no idea I'd seen the glass was his.

"If he's reading my questions," I said, slowly, "then I get to choose what he reads."

"There she is," Marcus said. "From now on, every question you type is a lie you choose. You feed him a plan. A false plan. A plan he'll believe because it sounds exactly like the careful, documented, rule-following woman he's been reading for two weeks. He'll shape his counter to the plan you've handed him. And he'll be ready for a version of you that doesn't exist — while the real you does something he can't see coming, because you will never, ever type it into that machine."

The car was quiet. Outside, an industrial night. Inside, the slow, enormous turning of a thing I'd thought was a defeat into the opposite.

"He has my husband," I said. "I can't just feed him lies while Arthur—"

"Arthur is leverage only as long as you're reacting," Carter said. "The moment you stop reacting and start acting, his value shifts. They need you frightened and chasing. They have no playbook for a woman who stops chasing and starts writing the script." She looked at me. "We give Cross what he thinks he wants — you, cornered, coming to him — and we make the corner the trap."

I sat with it. The shape of a plan, ugly and fragile and built entirely on the fact that Julian Cross believed I was data he'd already organised. He believed he knew me. He had read my questions, my fears, my careful notes. He had built a model of Elena Vance: observant, frightened, methodical, predictable. A tidy line on a graph.

And the one thing his model could never account for — the variable that broke every algorithm he'd ever sold — was a person who chose, deliberately, to become unpredictable. Who took the diary he was reading and started writing fiction into it. Who used his certainty against him, because certainty is the one thing you can never model your way out of. The moment you're sure you've organised someone, they stop being the person you organised.

Cross was sure. That was his flaw. That was the door.

"I need a way to get in a room with him," I said. "Face to face. With his guard down. Where he feels like the smartest person present."

Marcus, quietly: "Why."

"Because the one thing he'll never predict is that the scared wife he's been reading will sit across from him and do the thing she does for a living — read him — and he'll let her, because he doesn't believe a coach is a threat. He believes a coach is a service. He'll let me in because his ego can't imagine I'm dangerous."

Carter and Marcus were silent. Then Marcus said, very softly, "Arthur married the right woman."

"He married a woman who's about to lie to a billionaire's machine for three days and then sit in front of the billionaire and make him confess to a crime he thinks he's already won. That's not the right woman. That's a different woman entirely."

"It's you," Carter said. "It's been you since you looked up from a blue dot and didn't look away."

I closed my eyes. I thought of Arthur, in a chair, in the dark, looking at a camera with fury and terror, telling me with his eyes *don't*. I was about to do the thing he'd told me not to do. I was going to walk straight at the man who had him, with nothing but a plan built out of his own arrogance, and the half-second pause I'd spent my whole career teaching other people to find.

"Tell me what to type," I said. "I'm going to start lying to the machine."
