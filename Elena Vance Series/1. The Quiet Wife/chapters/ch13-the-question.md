# CHAPTER THIRTEEN
## The Question

I waited until the dishes were done and the flat was quiet, because the Question deserved a quiet room. You learn this in coaching: never ask the real thing in motion. Sit. Still. Let the silence do half the work. People fill silence with the truth because the truth is easier than the sound of nothing.

Arthur was reading. I came and sat across from him, on the edge of the sofa, and I didn't pick up my phone, and I didn't smile, and after a moment he lowered his book because the absence of my normal fidgeting was louder than any question.

"Elena?"

"I need to ask you something. And I need you to not lie to me. Not this time."

The colour left his face in a slow tide. I watched it go. He knew. He'd known, maybe, since the subway grate, that this conversation was coming the way you know a storm is coming — not the hour, just the certainty.

"Okay," he said. Very quietly.

I asked the Question. The only one that mattered. "What did you find at the archive, Arthur?"

He didn't move. But his hand, the one holding the book, tightened until the knuckles went white, and the book trembled, very slightly, the way a man trembles when every instinct tells him to run and his love tells him to stay in the chair.

"Where—" he started.

"The Aurelian. A man with an envelope. You leave early. You lie about the time. You come home with documents in your coat." I kept my voice low and even, the voice I use in sessions, the voice that says *I already know, the only thing left is for you to say it.* "I'm not angry. I was, about the affair. There's no affair. I know that now. So whatever this is, Arthur — it's bigger, and it's scaring you, and I need you to tell me what it is, because I can't help you if I don't know, and I can't protect myself if I don't know, and people who love each other are supposed to know."

The book lowered to his lap. He looked at me, and I saw him weighing it — the cost of telling me against the cost of carrying it alone — and for a long moment I thought he was going to choose the door he always chose, the brave lonely door, the one marked *protect her*.

"Elena." His voice cracked on my name. "You need to stop. Please. Whatever you think you've found — and I can see you've found something, because you're sitting like a woman who's found something — you need to stop looking. Right now. Tonight."

"I can't do that."

"You have to."

"Arthur. Someone drilled through our wall yesterday."

He went still. A different stillness. Not the stillness of a man caught. The stillness of a man whose worst fear has just been confirmed.

"I know," he whispered. "I know they did."

"Then you know I can't stop. Tell me. Tell me what they want."

He stood up. He paced to the window and back, the way he paces in his archive when a record won't align, and I saw his hands shaking, and I felt my heart break a little, because Arthur does not shake, Arthur is the steady one, and to see the steady one shake is to feel the floor turn to water.

"I found a discrepancy," he said, to the window. "In the property records. The chain of title for this block. It's been altered. Decades ago, and again recently. Someone forged transfers to make it look like a whole chain of ownership that never existed. The real records — the original surveys, the original deeds — they were supposed to be destroyed. They weren't. They're misfiled. Buried. And I found them."

He turned back to me, and his face was the face from the pavement outside the Aurelian — the funeral face, the duty face.

"The records prove the current ownership of this block is fake. All of it. Your building. The whole street. It's stolen land, Elena. Stolen with paperwork. And the people who stole it — they will do anything to keep that paper from ever seeing daylight."

"Who are they?"

He shook his head. "I don't know the top. I know it's big — money, patient, older than both of us. I've been trying to get the records to a real journalist, someone who can't be bought. That's the man you saw. He's moving the copies out piece by piece, because I can't take it all at once without them noticing."

"And the original? The real records?"

Arthur looked at me, and in his eyes I saw the thing he had done — the brave, foolish, loving, terrifying thing — and I knew the answer before he said it.

"It's here," he said. "In the apartment. Somewhere they'll never think to look. I put it somewhere I could get to it, somewhere—" He stopped. He read my face. "Elena. Don't."

"Where."

"Don't. If you know, and they find out you know—"

"Arthur. They are already drilling through the wall. They already know it's here. The only person who doesn't know where is me, and I am the one person in this apartment who can't leave, because it's my name on the keystone share and my door they're trying to take."

He closed his eyes. He looked, for a moment, like a man who had carried a boulder up a mountain and was being asked to set it down into someone else's arms and couldn't decide if that was mercy or the cruelest thing he'd ever been asked to do.

"Not yet," he said. "Not tonight. Let me move it first. Let me get it somewhere safer. Give me two days." He crossed the room and took my hands, and his were cold, and I let him hold them, because he was my husband and he was frightened and he had done all of this, every lie and every envelope, to keep a piece of paper alive that could right a theft, and he had done it for people he would never meet, and I loved him so fiercely in that moment that it hurt.

"Two days," I said. "And then you tell me where. And you stop protecting me by keeping me blind. That part is over, Arthur. That part has to be over."

"Two days," he promised.

He didn't sleep that night. I lay beside him and listened to him not sleep, and I thought about keystones and corners and a grey frightened man passing envelopes, and I thought, *two days*, and I thought about all the things that can happen in two days when the walls are already moving.

I didn't know then that two days was optimistic. I didn't know that the people who wanted what was hidden in my home had already stopped being patient. I didn't know that the grey man — Arthur's ally, the careful journalist's contact — had, by the time Arthur promised me two days, already stopped answering his phone.

I found that out the next morning.

When it was already too late to plan, and only just in time to run.
