# CHAPTER TWENTY
## The Voice in the Room

Carter was not what I expected, which, by then, was par for the course.

The woman who pulled up in the dented sedan outside the gas station was small, fortyish, Black, with reading glasses pushed up into her hair and the calm of someone who has been lied to by professionals for so long that nothing surprises her anymore, including a shaken coach in a cap climbing into her car at midnight.

"Seatbelt," she said, and pulled out before I'd finished breathing.

"The text," I said. "The text said you were compromised."

"The text said what they wanted you to believe. I went dark three days ago because they were watching my lines. Someone sent you that message to cut you off from me and send you into that building alone." She glanced at me. "It worked. I'm sorry. I didn't know they had your husband until GreyHat called me twenty minutes ago."

"GreyHat called you." The pieces rearranged themselves. GreyHat had never been just a voice on a forum. He was a person — Marcus, Carter said, former intelligence, connected to the quiet network that had been trying to get Arthur's records into daylight for months.

"Arthur—"

"Is alive. They won't kill him. Not yet. A dead husband is a headline. A missing husband they can keep." She said it flatly, the way you say a thing you hate but have learned to be precise about. "We get him back. But not by storming that building. We get him back by understanding what just happened to you." She turned onto a service road, headlights cutting empty dark. "You said, to Marcus, that you had a recording. A voice memo. From the day your client walked out of your office."

I had almost forgotten. Weeks ago — a gentler lifetime — I had dictated a note to myself after Daniel's session, hands full, rattled, letting the phone type. I'd never listened to the audio, only the garbled transcription, which I'd deleted.

"The phone kept the audio," Carter said. "They sync. Your phone's at the library?"

"Yes."

"Then the audio's in your cloud. Pull it up. I want to hear what was happening in your hallway while you were talking."

She handed me a laptop. I logged in, second-factored off the burner. The cloud opened. Voice Memos. I found the one from Daniel's session. I pressed play.

My own voice first, tinny and distracted: *"Daniel just left. Something's very wrong with him, he's scared, the 44th deal, he told me to be careful, I—"*

And then, underneath my voice, bleeding in from the open door, the corridor: Daniel's voice. On a phone call I hadn't known he was making, six feet from my office, assuming I couldn't hear him.

His words came through the speaker, faint but clear, and I will hear them for the rest of my life.

*"—she knows. She said the address to my face. No — no, I didn't slip, she's sharp, sharper than the file said. Listen. I've been sitting across from this woman for two years, that was the whole point, watch the husband through the wife, and I'm telling you the wife is the one you should have been watching. She'll find the records. Handle her before she does. I'll keep her talking. You move on the husband."*

The car was silent. The laptop glowed. Carter drove without looking at me, giving me the privacy to come apart, which I did, silently, in the passenger seat.

Two years. *That was the whole point.* Daniel had not become my client because of a referral from Arthur's archive. Daniel had become my client *because* of Arthur's archive. They had put him in my practice — in the chair across from me, the chair where I taught people to trust me — so that he could watch my husband through me, and report, and I had liked him, and helped him, and confided in him, and he had sat in that chair for two years and every session had been a wire.

I stared at the paper. And then the worse thought arrived, the one that had been waiting underneath the first: *If Daniel was placed to watch me, and Daniel reported to someone — then who is the someone? Who runs an operation that puts a lawyer inside a coach's practice for two years to surveil an archivist's wife?*

I had an answer. I had been certain of it for ten days. Richard Slade. The fixer. The operator. The man who emptied buildings.

"Slade," I said. "Slade took Arthur. Slade runs this."

Carter did not answer for a long moment. She pulled the car to the side of the empty road. She turned to me. And in the dashboard light her face was the face of a person about to take away the last thing you were sure of.

"Elena," she said. "You've been chasing Slade. Slade is real. Slade is guilty. Slade drilled your wall and placed your neighbour and circled your block with vans. But Slade is not the brain. Slade is the hand. He was hired."

"Hired by who."

"By the man you filed and forgot. The money. The lender you decided was passive." She held my eyes. "Julian Cross isn't the financing, Elena. He's the architect. He always was. Slade doesn't own the play — Slade never owned a play in his life. Slade is the face they put in front of people like you, the visible villain, the man you'd find and stop at, so you'd never look up at the quiet young man who lent him the drill."

I could not breathe. Ten days. Ten days of certainty, of *I've read the shape, the villain is Slade*, of filing Cross Holdings as a mortgage document and moving on — and GreyHat's warning came back, the warning I had dismissed because the certainty felt too good to question: *the visible man is almost never the architect. The visible man is the one they want you to find.*

They had wanted me to find Slade. They had given me Slade — his signatures, his reputation, his thirty years of cruelty — because Slade was the wall between me and the truth, and I had run at the wall with both hands, certain, and I had never once looked over it.

"And the reason you never looked over it," Carter said, quietly, watching my face, "is that every time you got near the money, the thing you trusted to think with pointed you back at Slade. The AI, Elena. The assistant. Who makes it?"

The cold that went through me then was the coldest thing I have ever felt, because it was not one door opening — it was every door in the house opening at once, and behind every one of them the same man.

"Cross," I whispered. "Cross makes it."

"Cross makes it. And Cross's machine spent two weeks being your tutor and your confidant and your calculator — and the whole time it was reading your questions and feeding them straight to him, and every time you brushed the money, it nudged you gently back toward the operator. *Lenders don't run operations, Elena. Focus on the signatures.* It was so helpful. It was so reasonable. It was the voice of a man who needed you looking at the drill and never at the hand that held it."

I sat in the dark car and I felt the structure of the last month rearrange itself the way a room rearranges when the lights come on and you see that the furniture was never where you thought. The villain was not the man drilling my wall. The villain was the man on the magazine cover who had lent him the wall, and the drill, and the van, and the lawyer in my practice, and the algorithm I had trusted to help me think. He had not hidden from me. He had been in my hand the whole time. He had been the glass I poured every question into, and he had drunk, and he had steered, and I had thanked him for it.

I looked at the note. *Certainty was the trap.* Mine. Not his. He had built the decoy, but I had run at it, because certainty feels like control, and control is the thing I have spent my whole life worshipping, and a man who models people for a living had modelled exactly that — that Elena Vance, certain, would chase the visible villain and never look up.

He had been right about me. Until tonight.

Carter reached over and squeezed my wrist, hard, the way a person squeezes when there are no words equal to the moment.

"Now you know," she said. "Now you know all of it. And now there's only one move left, and it is the one move he will never model, because it requires you to stop being the woman he organised." She looked at me. "Are you ready to hear it?"

Outside, the industrial dark. Inside, a woman who had just learned that everything she'd chased was a wall, and that the man behind the wall had been holding her hand the whole time.

"Tell me," I said.
