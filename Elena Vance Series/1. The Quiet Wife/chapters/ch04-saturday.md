# CHAPTER FOUR
## Saturday

I own a trench coat. I bought it in 2019 because a magazine told me it was timeless, and I have worn it perhaps four times, because it turns out I am not a trench coat person. I am a wool-blend-blazer person. But on Saturday morning I take the trench coat out of the back of the wardrobe, and I put on the oversized sunglasses I keep for migraines, and I look at myself in the mirror, and the woman looking back is not an executive coach with a graduate degree and a reputation for composure. She is a person in a Halloween costume.

"You look insane," I say to the mirror.

The mirror does not disagree.

Arthur leaves at 11:40. He kisses my forehead — "Back by four, probably, archive stuff" — and he means it, I think, or he has said it so many times he believes it, and then the door closes and I count to sixty because that is what people do in films and I have decided, against all evidence, that films know things I don't.

I follow.

Following a person in Manhattan is not like following a person in a film. In a film there are alleys and corners and helpful crowds. In Manhattan there are eight million witnesses, every one of them looking at their phone, and a man walking at a perfectly ordinary pace down Sixth Avenue, and me, in a trench coat and migraine sunglasses, twenty feet behind him, trying to look like I am going somewhere specific and failing.

The first six blocks are fine. He doesn't turn around. Nobody would turn around; New York is a city of people walking behind other people. I almost relax. I think, *this is easy, I was worried about nothing.*

Then, at the subway entrance on 23rd, he stops.

He stops the way a person stops when they've forgotten something, a small sudden halt, and he turns — not all the way, just the shoulders, just enough to scan — and I do the only thing my body knows how to do, which is to keep moving directly toward him at exactly the same speed, because my brain has left the building and my legs have decided that the best camouflage is commitment.

I walk straight into the back of a man with a briefcase.

The coffee goes first. My coffee, the nice one, twelve dollars, all over a stranger's trouser leg and the pavement and the corner of my trench coat. The briefcase man turns on me with the fury of a person whose morning has been ruined by a woman in movie-star glasses, and I am apologizing, hands up, and in the two seconds my eyes leave Arthur he has turned fully around and he is looking right at me.

There is a long, terrible moment.

I am crouched on a subway grate in a puddle of oat milk flat white. My sunglasses have slipped. My husband is fifteen feet away, and he has seen me, and there is no version of reality in which this is a coincidence — a coincidence would require me to be somewhere I had a reason to be, and I have no reason to be crouching on a grate in a trench coat outside his subway stop except the reason that I am, humiliatingly, following him.

He doesn't move. He doesn't call out. He just looks at me, and then he does something I will think about for days: he turns away, quickly, and walks down the stairs into the subway, and it is the quickness that catches me. Not surprise. Not the slow double-take of a man catching his wife somewhere unexpected. A fast, clean turn, the way you turn from a door you don't want to be seen opening.

I do not follow him down the stairs. I have used up all my bravery on the coffee.

I go home. I throw the trench coat on the bed like it's evidence of a crime, which it is, and the crime is against my own dignity. I sit on the edge of the mattress and I wait for the text, because there is going to be a text, because my husband is not stupid and I just committed an act of espionage visible from space.

The text comes at 12:31.

> **WhatsApp — Arthur 💛 — 12:31 PM**
> Funny running into you. You ok? That looked like a good coffee.

I stare at it. I read it four times. Here is what I am looking for, and what I do not find: the accusation. The joke that bites. The *what were you doing there.* Any version of the text a guilty man sends when he's been caught in his own lie — sharp, defensive, controlling the narrative.

This isn't that text. This text is light. Careful. It bends over backward to make what just happened into nothing, a joke, a funny coincidence. It is the text of a man working very hard to make sure I don't look closer.

I type back with hands that are not quite steady.

> **Elena — 12:33 PM**
> Ugh, so clumsy. Migraine coming on, couldn't see. Got it all over some poor man's suit 🙈 Sorry, didn't even register it was you! See you at four.

I send it. I hate myself for sending it. We are two married people performing a play for an audience of two, and neither of us will say the line that's actually true.

I put the phone down. And I think about the quickness of his turn. Because the thing about Arthur — the thing I have spent seven years learning — is that Arthur does not startle. Arthur is the steady one. Arthur is the man who reads a 130-year-old property dispute without raising his pulse. If Arthur turns fast, something has moved him, and I have spent the last hour trying to decide what.

Here is the problem with reading people for a living: you see the feeling, but the feeling does not come labelled. Fear looks like guilt. Guilt looks like fear. A man caught with another woman turns fast; a man caught doing something worse turns fast; a man who is *afraid* turns fast. The turn is the same turn. The cause is not.

I open the Notes app. I am aware, dimly, that the Notes app has become a kind of confessional, the one place I say the unsayable, and that this is possibly not healthy.

```
NOTE — 12:41 PM — home, coat still damp
The turn was FAST. Too fast for surprise.
But fear and guilt move the same muscles.
He could be:
  (a) caught — affair, something sordid
  (b) afraid — something being done TO him
  (c) guilty — something being done BY him
The turn can't tell me which. Nothing yet can.
=> Don't pick a story. Stories are how you go blind.
```

I read it back, and the last line is the one that stays. *Don't pick a story.* Because here is the thing I don't want to admit, the thing the coaching career is built to paper over: I have spent twenty-two years telling myself I see everything, and the truth is I only see the surface of everything, and the surface of Arthur right now is a man who turned too fast, and I have no idea — none — whether the man I married is in trouble, or whether he is the trouble.

That second possibility is the one I keep circling, the way your tongue finds a cracked tooth. Because an affair I can survive. An affair has a shape. But a husband who is *frightened* — a steady, paper-reading, sandwich man who is frightened of his own wife finding something out — that could be anything. It could be something done to him. It could be something he did. And I am not ready, I find, to look too hard at the difference, because if Arthur has done a thing and hidden it behind that warm steady face for seven years, then the woman who reads rooms has been living in one she never read at all.

He comes home at 4:02, smelling of paper, and he asks what's for dinner, and he does not mention the subway, and I do not mention the subway, and we eat in the warm careful silence of two people who have agreed, without a word, to pretend the morning didn't happen.

I watch him over the pasta. Every tilt. Every pause.

He is steady. He is warm. He is himself.

And I cannot tell — for the first time in seven years, I genuinely cannot tell — whether the warmth is the truth, or whether the warmth is the thing he wears over whatever he is hiding, the way all of us, every one of us, wear something over the gap.

I don't know my husband tonight. That is the hook in my chest, sharper than any affair. Not that he might love someone else. That I might not know him at all.
