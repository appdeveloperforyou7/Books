# CHAPTER TWENTY-SEVEN
## The Fall

It took three days for the city to learn what had happened in the walnut room, and when it learned, it learned the way cities learn — in headlines, in fragments, in the slow shocked voice of anchors who had spent years calling Julian Cross a visionary and now had to call him something else.

I watched it from Carter's apartment, because my own apartment was, for a little while longer, evidence. The federal team sealed Building C and the Cross residence and the records office where Arthur had found the seam, and they pulled the thread the way GreyHat had taught me to pull it — patiently, from the human name outward — and the thread, as Cross had promised, did not end at Cross.

Royce was arrested first. The soldier in 6B, the man with no past, picked up at a private airfield with a bag and a ticket that had been purchased the night of the gala, before he could know his employer had confessed to a coach in a walnut room. He went quietly. They always do, Carter said, when the operation above them has already been named out loud. There is no one left to protect, and so there is nothing left to fight for.

Gerald came to me — not to the authorities, to me, at the safe house, wearing the same regretful face he'd worn when he served me the noise complaint, except now the regret was unburdened, the way a face looks when a weight has finally been set down. He had not been bought. He had been squeezed: a favour called in on his mortgage, a quiet threat to a son's career. He had done what frightened men do — the small wrong thing instead of the right hard thing — and hated himself for it for three months. He wept in Carter's doorway, and I did what I do: held the pause, let him feel heard, and told him the only way out was the truth, spoken now while it was still worth something. He went to the prosecutors the next morning. His testimony was the second voice on the federal desk.

The network, the thing Cross had called *they*, was bigger than I had feared and slower than I had hoped. It sprawled across shells and decades and people who had never met Julian Cross and did not know they worked for the same architecture. The prosecutors told Carter, off the record, that untangling it would take years. The block would be returned, eventually, to its rightful owners — to us, to the residents, to the city. The fraud would be undone in courtrooms I would never enter. Arthur's records were the spine; my recording was the voice; and the two together had cracked a door that a great many powerful people had kept shut for a very long time.

But a cracked door is not an open one. And the people on the other side of it — the *they*, the generations, the architecture that predated Cross's father — were, the prosecutors believed, still there. Still organised. Still watching.

I did not sleep well that week. Not from fear, exactly. From the new, heavier knowledge that the thing I had broken was a piece of something larger, and that pieces, when broken, are noticed by the whole.

Arthur stayed close. He was healing — the ribs, the bruise, the particular wear of a good man who had carried a boulder and set it down at last. We did not talk much about the warehouse. We did not need to. Some doors, once opened between two people, do not need to be walked through again; they stay open, and the air moves through them, and that is enough.

One night, lying in the dark of Carter's guest room, he said, "You were right. About the doors."

"Which doors?"

"All of them. The ones I kept closing on you. I thought they protected you. They just left you alone in the rooms that mattered."

I took his hand in the dark. "You found a stolen city. You tried to give it back. You hid it in a cookbook. You married the woman who read the room. None of that was wrong, Arthur. The only wrong part was thinking you had to do it behind a door."

"Will you stop reading rooms now?"

"No."

"Good." He squeezed my hand. "Because I think there are a few more rooms coming."

He was right, though neither of us knew yet quite how many. The block was not finished with us. The *they* was not finished with anyone. And I had discovered, in the worst month of my life, a thing about myself that could not be put back: that I was good at this — not the coaching, which I had always been good at, but the other thing, the reading of gaps and the following of threads and the sitting across from a certain man and letting him convict himself. I was good at a thing I had never meant to learn.

And the city, it turned out, was full of rooms that needed reading.

I looked at the last line. *The first one.* It had arrived in the note without my deciding to write it, the way the truth always arrives — not summoned, but finally allowed. And I sat with it in the dark, beside my healing husband, and I understood that the woman who had walked into a walnut room and let a billionaire confess was not going back to being only a coach.

She couldn't. The alarm had changed key.

And it was not going to stop playing.
