# CHAPTER SIX
## The Half-Second

On Sunday I do what GreyHat said. I list the facts.

I sit at the kitchen table with Arthur out getting groceries, and I open the Notes app, and I force myself to do the thing I make my clients do: separate the feeling from the fact. Feelings are the weather. Facts are the ground. You can't navigate by weather.

```
NOTE — 10:08 AM — kitchen
FACTS ONLY. (GreyHat's rule.)
1. A's phone pinged The Aurelian, W29, 6:04–9:38 PM Wed.
2. A texted he was at the archive. He was not.
3. A then went TO the archive (Chambers) before coming home.
4. A angled his phone away from me at dinner. First time ever.
5. A tired ~3 weeks. Won't say why.
6. I tailed him Sat. He saw me. He turned FAST = afraid.
7. His text after = careful, light. Not angry. Overcorrecting.
8. 6B owned by Northbridge Capital Holdings LLC, not Royce.
9. West 44th block = Meridian Holdings + Crestline. No humans.
10. Co-op serving me notice. Royce behind it. Gerald pressured.

That's it. That's everything I actually KNOW.
Everything else is weather.
```

I read it over. It's a short list for a week of agony. But it's true, every line, and that makes it stronger than every theory I've tortured myself with. I look at line 3, especially: *he went to the archive before coming home.* That's the fact I keep circling. He didn't just lie. He corrected. He put himself back where he was supposed to be, like a man re-shelving a stolen book before anyone noticed it was gone.

I need to know more. And I don't know how.

GreyHat talked about "public records" and "the data" like those words meant something obvious, and maybe they do to him, but to me they're a door with no handle. I am a coach. I read faces, not filings. The only place I know to ask a stupid question without being judged is the little AI assistant on my phone, the one I use to draft awkward emails and summarise long articles, the one that never makes you feel small for not knowing.

I open it. I type the question that's been sitting under everything since the forum.

```
🤖 Chat — 10:22 AM
Elena: What does OSINT mean? Someone used it online and I
don't want to ask them and look stupid.

AI: It stands for Open Source Intelligence — but ignore the
spy-sounding name, it's misleading. All it means is:
finding information that's already public.

Think property records, court filings, company registrations,
 licences, news archives, social media — even the location
data hidden inside a photo. It's what journalists and
investigators use, because most people leave a surprisingly
large trail, and almost all of it is free and legal to look
at.

Elena: So it's just... looking things up.

AI: Essentially, yes. Anyone can search. The real skill isn't
access — it's knowing WHAT to look for, WHERE, and how to
CONNECT separate facts that don't obviously belong together.
That part takes practice. Two pieces of information that mean
nothing on their own can mean a lot side by side.

Elena: how do I learn the connecting part

AI: Start small. Pick one person or one question. Gather every
public fact you can find. Write them down in order. Then look
for the gaps — the places where the facts DON'T line up with
the story you've been told. The gaps are usually where the
truth is hiding.
```

I sat back.

*The gaps are usually where the truth is hiding.*

That sentence landed in me like a key turning. Because here's the thing nobody tells you about being good at reading people: it's the same muscle. Reading a boardroom is connecting separate facts — who looked at whom, who spoke first, who went quiet — and watching for the gap between what they say and what they mean. I've been doing OSINT on human beings for eleven years. I just never called it that, and I never thought to aim it at paper.

I'd spent a week feeling like an imposter in a spy movie. But this — looking things up, lining them up, finding the gaps — this was my job. I read gaps for a living. I'd simply assumed it only worked on faces.

I was wrong.

I picked up the phone and I went back to the facts. Line 3. The archive. And I did the boring thing GreyHat and the AI were both pointing at — I didn't chase Arthur, I didn't tail him, I just looked up the building he claims to work in.

```
SEARCH — 10:41 AM
"City Municipal Archives" + "hours" + "closing time"
```

The Municipal Archives, the public reading rooms where Arthur works, closes to the public at 4:30 p.m. on weekdays. That's the official closing time. I've known this for years; I've dropped off dinners, picked him up, sat in the lobby reading a book. 4:30. Always 4:30.

Now I went to the photos on my own phone. Because here is a thing about modern life that most people forget and that I, apparently, had also forgotten: every photograph you take stamps itself with the date, the time, and — if your location services are on, which mine always are — exactly where it was taken. It's called metadata. It's tiny and invisible and it's on every picture in your camera roll, free for anyone who looks.

I scrolled to Wednesday. Two photos. One I'd taken of a client's email at 3:47 p.m. in my office. And one — the one I was hunting — Arthur's lunch, which he'd sent me at 1:12 p.m. from the archive cafeteria. Geotagged: Municipal Archives, Chambers Street.

So at 1:12 p.m. Wednesday, Arthur was at the archive. Good. That part was true.

But the ping — the location ping — had him at the Aurelian at 6:04. And he'd told me he was working late "at the archive" all evening. And the archive closes at 4:30.

I did the arithmetic slowly, because the answer was strange.

Arthur could not have been at the archive at 6:04, or 7, or 8, because the archive closes at 4:30. Which means the lie wasn't *he was somewhere else*. The lie was smaller, and stranger. The lie was about *time*. He'd left the archive — at some point after 1:12 and before 4:30 — and gone to the Aurelian, and when he came home at 10:20 he'd told me he'd been there all evening, and the only reason to add the *all evening* part was to cover the hours he'd actually been somewhere else.

I opened a new note. My hands were steady. For the first time, I wasn't guessing. I was reading a gap.

```
NOTE — 10:58 AM — kitchen
ARCHIVE closes 4:30 PM. (Confirmed. Always has.)
A's lunch photo Wed = at archive, 1:12 PM. (True.)
A pinged at Aurelian 6:04 PM. (True.)
=> A left archive sometime 1:12–4:30.
=> But told me he was there "all evening."
The lie is the TIME, not the place.
He didn't hide WHERE he went after.
He hid WHEN he left.
=> Why does the TIMING matter more than the place?
```

I sat with the question. It was a good question. It was the kind of question that, a week ago, I wouldn't have known how to ask, because a week ago I was still trying to chase a man down a sidewalk instead of reading the quiet, boring, truthful data he was leaving behind him.

The AI had said it. GreyHat had said it. Find the gap between what you're told and what the data says.

The gap was an hour and a half, sometime between lunch and closing, on a Wednesday. Somewhere in that window, my steady, paper-reading, sandwich-eating husband had walked out of his job and into a brushed-steel penthouse, and the only thing he'd bothered to hide was the clock.

Why would a man lie about *when* he left, but not *that* he left?

I didn't have the answer yet. But I had the question, sharp and clean, and for the first time in seven days I felt the thing I'd been missing — not control, exactly, but its quieter cousin: direction. A line to walk.

I closed the note. I opened the forum.

```

You were right. I stopped chasing. I looked at the gap.
The lie isn't where he goes. It's the time he leaves.
He leaves work early but tells me he stayed late.
Why would someone hide the timing and not the place?
```

The reply took twenty minutes. When it came, it was three words long, and it made the hair on my arms stand up.

