# Image Prompts for "The Bhagavad Gita Made Clear"

> *Art direction: modern, premium, calm — NOT kitschy, NOT cartoonish, NOT literal-realist religious art.
> Think: editorial fine-line illustration meets Penguin Classics deluxe meets DK reference. Subtle colour,
> generous white space, sacred but contemporary. The aesthetic of the book is "calm · layered · living."*

---

## 1. MAP — Ancient India at the Time of the Mahābhārata

**Purpose:** Front-matter spread (2 pages) showing the geographical context of the Gita.

**Prompt:**
> A double-page-spread antique-style map of North-West India at the time of the Mahābhārata
> (approximately 1000 BCE), rendered in a modern editorial illustration style. The map should feel
> like a premium book illustration — not a tourist map, not a school textbook. Hand-drawn quality
> with fine ink lines on a warm cream background (matching #FAF6EC). Key locations marked with
> elegant small icons (not generic pins): Kurukshetra (the battlefield, marked with crossed swords
> or a chariot wheel), Hastināpura (the Kuru capital, marked with a palace silhouette),
> Indraprastha (the Pāṇḍava capital), Dvārakā (Krishna's city on the western coast, marked with
> a peacock feather), the Sarasvatī river (dried/fading), the Gaṅgā (Yamunā rivers prominent).
> The map should include the Kuru kingdom territory shaded subtly in saffron (#C77A1A at 8%
> opacity). A small compass rose. Labels in a clean serif font (like Source Serif). No modern borders,
> no modern cities. The overall mood: scholarly, beautiful, slightly aged but crisp — like a
> hand-coloured engraving from a 1940s limited-edition book. Dimensions: 16 inches wide × 10 inches
> tall (double-page spread at 8×10 trim). Resolution: 300+ DPI. Colour: warm cream, saffron accents,
> slate-blue rivers, dark brown ink lines. No people, no decorative borders, no Sanskrit script
> (English labels only). Leave a 0.5-inch margin around all edges for bleed.

## 2. CAST OF CHARACTERS — Illustrated Portrait Cards

**Purpose:** A visual "who's who" spread in the front matter. Each character gets a small
illustrated portrait card with their name, role, and one-line description.

### 2a. Krishna
> A small portrait illustration of Krishna as charioteer — not the blue-skinned deity of calendar
> art, but a dignified, serene young man in a simple charioteer's dhoti, holding the reins loosely,
> a subtle smile on his face. His skin tone is a deep warm brown (not literal blue). He wears a
> peacock feather tucked in his hair — the only explicitly divine attribute. The style: fine-line
> ink illustration with minimal watercolour wash, warm saffron and cream tones, on a cream
> background (#FAF6EC). The mood: calm authority, approachable wisdom. No jewellery, no discus,
> no multiple arms. Just a man who happens to be God, driving a chariot. Portrait format: 3 inches
> wide × 4 inches tall. 300 DPI.

### 2b. Arjuna
> A portrait illustration of Arjuna — a strong, handsome warrior in light armour, holding the
> Gāṇḍīva bow lowered at his side, his face showing the strain of moral anguish (not
> battlefield rage, but the grief of conscience). His eyes are downcast, not fierce. A quiver on
> his back. Fine-line ink with minimal wash, slate-blue and cream tones. The mood: noble sorrow, a
> good man at his breaking point. Portrait format: 3×4 inches. 300 DPI.

### 2c. Bhīṣma
> A portrait illustration of the grandsire Bhīṣma — an elderly, white-bearded warrior of immense
> stature, standing with a massive bow, wearing minimal armour over white robes. His expression is
> resolved but sorrowful — he fights for the wrong side out of oath-bound loyalty. White-haired,
> dignified, tragic. Fine-line ink, warm grey and cream tones. Portrait: 3×4. 300 DPI.

### 2d. Droṇa
> A portrait illustration of Droṇa, the martial guru — a middle-aged brahmin with a topknot,
> wearing simple priestly garments over armour, holding a bow with scholarly precision. His face is
> stern but kind — a teacher who must fight his own students. Fine-line ink, sage-green and cream.
> Portrait: 3×4. 300 DPI.

### 2e. Karṇa
> A portrait illustration of Karṇa — a proud, golden-armoured warrior with a radiant earring
> (his birth-gift from the sun god), standing alone, chin raised. His expression: defiant dignity
> masking deep insecurity about his unknown birth. Golden-amber and cream tones. Fine-line ink.
> Portrait: 3×4. 300 DPI.

### 2f. Duryodhana
> A portrait illustration of Duryodhana — a broad, powerfully built prince in royal regalia, his
> expression set in stubborn pride with a flicker of fear behind the eyes. Crowned, jewelled, but
> with a subtle asymmetry suggesting inner disorder. Dark maroon and cream tones. Fine-line ink.
> Portrait: 3×4. 300 DPI.

> Gustave Doré meets modern editorial illustration — detailed, atmospheric, sacred but not
> sentimental. No human figures. No text labels (the book's text will provide captions). 300 DPI.

## 4. THE GUṆA WHEEL — Chapter 14 Diagram

**Purpose:** A diagram showing the three guṇas (sattva/rajas/tamas) and how they interact.

**Prompt:**
> A circular diagram (8×8 inches, centered on an 8×10 page) illustrating the three guṇas of Sāṃkhya
> philosophy. The wheel is divided into three equal sectors: Sattva (top, rendered in warm gold/
> saffron with a subtle radiating light — "purity, clarity, luminosity"), Rajas (right, in warm
> rust/orange with dynamic angular lines suggesting motion and restlessness — "passion, activity"),
> Tamas (left, in cool grey-blue with heavy, dense texture — "inertia, darkness"). At the centre of
> the wheel: a small, still, uncoloured circle — the Self (puruṣa), beyond the guṇas. Around the
> outer rim: a thin saffron ring suggesting the path of transcendence (guṇātīta). The style:
> modern, geometric, minimal — think Bauhaus-inspired sacred geometry, not a traditional yantra.
> Fine ink lines, flat colour fills, cream background. No Sanskrit text. No human figures. The
> mood: analytical, clear, balanced — a teaching diagram that is also beautiful. 300 DPI.

## 5. THE COSMIC FORM (Viśvarūpa) — Chapter 11

**Purpose:** A full-page atmospheric illustration evoking the terrifying beauty of the cosmic
revelation.

**Prompt:**
> A full-page (8×10 inches) abstract/semi-abstract illustration evoking the Viśvarūpa — the cosmic
> form of Krishna revealed in Bhagavad Gita Chapter 11. NOT a literal depiction of many mouths and
> eyes (that becomes horror-movie kitsch). Instead: a vast, swirling composition suggesting infinity
> — galaxies, stars, cosmic fire, and within the fire, the faint suggestion of countless faces and
> eyes dissolving into light. The centre of the image is a blinding white-gold radiance (like a
> thousand suns) that fades at the edges into deep indigo and black. Tiny silhouettes of warriors
> appear at the edges of the radiance, being drawn inward "like moths into a blazing fire." The
> colour palette: white-gold centre, saffron-amber inner ring, deep indigo-blue outer ring, black
> edges. The mood: sublime terror and awe — beautiful and frightening simultaneously. Style: think
> the cosmic art of Chesley Bonestell crossed with the spiritual abstraction of Mark Rothko — vast,
> luminous, overwhelming but not literal. No clear human form at the centre (the form is beyond
> form). 300 DPI.

## 8. THE FIELD AND THE KNOWER — Chapter 13 Diagram

**Purpose:** A diagram illustrating the kṣetra (field) / kṣetrajña (knower) distinction.

**Prompt:**
> A conceptual diagram (8×8 inches centered on page) illustrating the relationship between the
> field (kṣetra = body/mind/world) and the knower of the field (kṣetrajña = the Self). The image

> gold point-light for the knower, cream background. Style: conceptual, modern, slightly poetic —
> not a literal anatomy diagram but a visual metaphor. Fine ink + watercolour. 300 DPI.

## Production notes for the illustrator/AI:
- All images should be at 300+ DPI for print quality.
- Colour profile: CMYK (for print) or sRGB (for digital preview).
- Background colour must match the book's cream: #FAF6EC (warm off-white).
- Avoid: stock-mandala patterns, cartoon gods, literal blue-skinned deities, Sanskrit script (the
  book provides its own), overly busy compositions, any religious imagery that feels sectarian or
  devotional-kitsch.
- Target: editorial illustration quality — think The New Yorker meets Penguin Classics meets a
  premium wellness publication. Calm, intelligent, beautiful, and respectful without being
  literalist.
