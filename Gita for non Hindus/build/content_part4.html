9.22THE PROMISE
अनन्याश्चिन्तयन्तो मां ये जनाः पर्युपासते ।
तेषां नित्याभियुक्तानां योगक्षेमं वहाम्यहम् ॥
ananyāś cintayanto māṃ ye janāḥ paryupāsate / teṣāṃ nityābhiyuktānāṃ yoga-kṣemaṃ vahāmy aham
GĪTĀ 9.22
LLiteral

Those who, thinking of nothing else, ever-worship Me with undivided devotion — to those ever-united ones I Myself carry their yoga-kṣema.

MIn plain English

If you keep your mind on Me — not as one thing among many, but as the centre — then your needs become My responsibility. I carry what you have not yet acquired (yoga) and I protect what you already hold (kṣema).

CCommentary

Notice what it does not say. It does not promise wealth, success, or comfort. It promises that the One behind the universe personally undertakes your welfare. The condition is precise: ananyāḥ — “without another.” Not that you must think of God every second, but that he is your refuge, the fixed star by which you navigate. To such a one, the cosmic Lord becomes a porter.

For the new reader

Every parent knows this promise from the other side. A small child does not earn its food or fear the rent; it simply trusts the one who carries it. Chapter 9 says the universe is built on that same trust — and God is the one carrying.

Try this — the handover practice

Once a day — at a moment of genuine anxiety — deliberately hand one worry over. Say inwardly: “This is now Yours.” The verse is not a theory to believe; it is an experiment to run. Run it for thirty days and watch what changes — not in the world, but in you.

A common misreading

This is not a prosperity teaching. Krishna is not a cosmic ATM. Yoga-kṣema means your true welfare — which may include hardship that saves you, and loss that frees you. To read it as “devotion gets you riches” is to make the Gītā small.

9.23 – 9.25 · Every sincere prayer reaches the One
9.23
येऽप्यन्यदेवता भक्ता यजन्ते श्रद्धयान्विताः ।
तेऽपि मामेव कौन्तेय यजन्त्यविधिपूर्वकम् ॥
Pye'py anya-devatā-bhaktā yajante śraddhayānvitāḥ / te'pi mām eva kaunteya yajanty avidhi-pūrvakam
LEven devotees of other gods worship with faith — they too worship Me, Kaunteya, though by an indirect path.
MSincerity, not the correct name, is what reaches the Divine — even worship of 'other gods' comes to him.
9.24
अहं हि सर्वयज्ञानां भोक्ता च प्रभुरेव च ।
न तु मामभिजानन्ति तत्त्वेनातश्च्यवन्ति ते ॥
Pahaṃ hi sarva-yajñānāṃ bhoktā ca prabhur eva ca / na tu mām abhijānanti tattvenātas cyavanti te
LI alone am the enjoyer and Lord of every sacrifice; not knowing Me in truth, they fall.
MHe alone receives every offering made anywhere; to miss this truth is to fall short.
9.25
यान्ति देवव्रता देवान् पितॄन्यान्ति पितृव्रताः ।
भूतानि यान्ति भूतेज्या यान्ति मद्याजिनोऽपि माम् ॥
Pyānti deva-vratā devān pitṝn yānti pitṛ-vratāḥ / bhūtāni yānti bhūtejyā yānti mad-yājino'pi mām
LWorshippers of the gods go to the gods; of the ancestors, to the ancestors; of the spirits, to the spirits — but My worshippers come to Me.
MYou reach what you worship — the gods, the ancestors, the spirits — but his devotee reaches him alone.
9.26The smallest offering
पत्रं पुष्पं फलं तोयं यो मे भक्त्या प्रयच्छति ।
तदहं भक्त्युपहृतमश्नामि प्रयतात्मनः ॥
patraṃ puṣpaṃ phalaṃ toyaṃ yo me bhaktyā prayacchati / tad ahaṃ bhakty-upahṛtam aśnāmi prayatātmanaḥ
GĪTĀ 9.26
LLiteral

Whoever offers Me a leaf, a flower, a fruit, or water with devotion — that devoted offering, from a pure-hearted one, I accept.

MIn plain English

You do not need a temple, a priest, or a fortune. A single leaf, offered with love, is enough. God is not impressed by the size of the gift but by the size of the heart behind it.

CCommentary

This verse democratized Indian spirituality forever. After 9.26, no one is too poor to worship. The emphasis falls entirely on bhakti (devotion) and prayatātman (a pure, concentrated heart). The leaf and the water are metaphors for anything offered wholeheartedly — your work, your attention, your forgiveness of someone who hurt you.

For the new reader

A child’s crayon drawing, given with love, is treasured by a parent more than a gallery masterpiece bought by a stranger. The economy of love runs on a different currency than the economy of things.

Try this — the leaf practice

Offer one small thing today as if to God — a cup of tea, a few quiet breaths, an act of kindness done in secret. Not for reward; simply given. Do this and you are already performing the central ritual of Chapter 9.

9.27 – 9.28 · Offer every act, and be free
9.27
यत्करोषि यदश्नासि यज्जुहोषि ददासि यत् ।
यत्तपस्यसि कौन्तेय तत्कुरुष्व मदर्पणम् ॥
Pyat karoṣi yad aśnāsi yaj juhoṣi dadāsi yat / yat tapasyasi kaunteya tat kuruṣva mad-arpaṇam
LWhatever you do, eat, offer, or give, and whatever austerity you practise — do it as an offering to Me.
MMake your whole life an offering: every act, meal, gift, and discipline, given to him.
9.28
शुभाशुभफलैरेवं मोक्ष्यसे कर्मबन्धनैः ।
संन्यासयोगयुक्तात्मा विमुक्तो मामुपैष्यसि ॥
Pśubhāśubha-phalair evaṃ mokṣyase karma-bandhanaiḥ / saṃnyāsa-yoga-yuktātmā vimukto mām upaiṣyasi
LThus you shall be freed from the bondage of good and bad fruits; united by the yoga of renunciation, liberated, you attain Me.
MOffered thus, action no longer binds; the detached doer is freed and reaches the Divine.
9.29The equality of God
समोऽहं सर्वभूतेषु न मे द्वेष्योऽस्ति न प्रियः ।
ये भजन्ति तु मां भक्त्या मयि ते तेषु चाप्यहम् ॥
samo'haṃ sarva-bhūteṣu na me dveṣyo'sti na priyaḥ / ye bhajanti tu māṃ bhaktyā mayi te teṣu cāpy aham
GĪTĀ 9.29
MIn plain English

God plays no favourites. He does not love the saint more than the sinner, the priest more than the outcast. But those who turn toward him in love enter a different relationship: they abide in him, and he in them.

A common misreading

“None hateful, none dear” is not indifference. It means God’s care is not rationed by rank, birth, or virtue — which is precisely what makes the devotion of the next two verses so radical.
