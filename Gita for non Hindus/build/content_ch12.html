1Intro Card

What happens. Arjuna asks one of the Gita's great questions: is it better to worship the personal God with form, or the impersonal Absolute without form? Kṛṣṇa answers that both reach Him — but the path of devotion to a personal form is easier for most, because the heart needs an object. He then gives a gradation of practices for those who cannot yet fix the mind (12.9–12), and closes with one of the most beautiful portraits in all of scripture: the marks of the devotee He holds dearest (12.13–20). These are not mystical achievements but ethical qualities — kindness, equanimity, freedom from ego.

Why this chapter matters to you. If you have ever loved something greater than yourself, you already know bhakti. This chapter says: that love, turned toward the Divine, is itself the path — not a preparation for it, not a consolation for those who cannot do philosophy. It is the path.

2The Big Picture

Three ideas hold Chapter 12 together — and each overturns a common assumption:

  1. God can be loved as a Person or contemplated as the formless — both reach Him. The personal path is easier because the heart needs an object; the impersonal is harder but also true. Neither is false; one is more practical.
  2. The highest path is undivided devotion. Not scholarship, not austerity, not ritual — but love, fixed on the One, opens the door that nothing else can. And if you cannot yet love fully, there is a gradation: practise, or act for Him, or simply let go of the fruit.
  3. The marks of the devotee are ethical, not mystical. The person God loves most is not the one who has visions but the one who harms no one, holds nothing, fears nothing — and loves.
3The Verses

All 20 verses of the chapter are given below in Sanskrit and translation. Eight key verses are opened in full — translation, commentary, a bridge for the new reader, a practice, and the deeper view.

12.1
अर्जुन उवाच ।
एवं सततयुक्ता ये भक्तास्त्वां पर्युपासते ।
ये चाप्यक्षरमव्यक्तं तेषां के योगवित्तमाः ॥
Parjuna uvāca / evaṃ satata-yuktā ye bhaktās tvāṃ paryupāsate / ye cāpy akṣaram avyaktaṃ teṣāṃ ke yoga-vittamāḥ
LThose devoteas who, ever united, worship You with form, and those who worship the imperishable, unmanifest — which are the better knowers of yoga?
MArjuna asks: personal God or impersonal Absolute — which path is higher?
12.2THE QUESTION ANSWERED
मय्यावेश्य मनो ये मां नित्ययुक्ता उपासते ।
श्रद्धया परयोपेतास्ते मे युक्ततमा मताः ॥
mayy āveśya mano ye māṃ nitya-yuktā upāsate / śraddhayā parayopetās te me yukta-tamā matāḥ
GĪTĀ 12.2
IIn plain English

Those who fix their mind on the Divine, ever united, worshipping with supreme faith — the Divine holds them most established in yoga. The personal path — fixing the mind on a form, a name, a presence — is declared supreme for the embodied soul.

CCommentary

Śaṅkara reads this as saguṇa upāsana (meditation on the qualified/personal Brahman) being the practical superior — not because the impersonal is false, but because the embodied mind needs an object to rest on. Rāmānuja reads 12.2 as the charter for prapatti — exclusive surrender to the personal Lord as the highest path. The verse says yukta-tamāḥ (most established) — a superlative that settles the question for the embodied: love a Person, not an abstraction.

↗ cf. 7.16-19, 9.13-15, 11.54
For the new reader

Think of love in human life: you do not love "love" in the abstract — you love a person. The Gita says the same is true of the Divine: the heart loves most truly when it has someone to love. The formless is real; but form is where love begins.

Try this — the fixation practice

For one minute today, fix your mind on the Divine in whatever form is natural to you — a name, an image, a feeling, a presence. When the mind wanders, bring it back gently. That is the practice of 12.2. Frequency matters more than duration.

Going deeper — saguṇa vs nirguṇa — the schools

Root: yukta-tamāḥ (superlative of yukta, from yuj = to yoke) — most yoked, most united. Śaṅkara: saguṇa is practice-superior; nirguṇa (the formless) is truth-superior — both are valid, at different stages. Rāmānuja: the personal Lord is objectively higher; the nirguṇa path, while valid, is more difficult (12.5) because it is less complete. Madhva: the personal path is commended because it includes knowledge of the Lord's excellence. Cross-refs: 7.16–19, 9.13–15, 11.54.

12.3 – 12.7 · The formless path and the easier path
12.3
ये त्वक्षरमनिर्देश्यमव्यक्तं पर्युपासते ।
सर्वत्रगमचिन्त्यं च कूटस्थमचलं ध्रुवम् ॥
Pye tv akṣaram anirdeśyam avyaktaṃ paryupāsate / sarvatragam acintyaṃ ca kūṭa-stham acalaṃ dhruvam
LBut those who worship the imperishable, indefinable, all-pervading, unthinkable, unchanging, fixed, immovable...
MOthers worship the formless Absolute — indefinable, all-pervading, changeless.
12.4
सन्नियम्येन्द्रियग्रामं सर्वत्र समबुद्धयः ।
ते प्राप्नुवन्ति मामेव सर्वभूतहिते रताः ॥
Psanniyamyendriyagrāmaṃ sarvatra sama-buddhayaḥ / te prāpnuvanti mām eva sarva-bhūta-hite ratāḥ
LMastering the senses, equal-minded everywhere, devoted to the welfare of all beings — they too attain Me.
MMastering their senses, equal to all, devoted to the good of all beings — they also reach the Divine.
12.5The harder path
क्लेशोऽधिकतरस्तेषामव्यक्तासक्तचेतसाम् ।
अव्यक्ता हि गतिर्दुःखं देहवद्भिरवाप्यते ॥
kleśo'dhikataras teṣām avyaktāsaktacetasām / avyaktā hi gatir duḥkhaṃ dehavadbhir avāpyate
GĪTĀ 12.5
IIn plain English

The path to the unmanifest is harder for embodied beings. The mind, which lives in forms, struggles to fix on the formless. This is not a judgment against the nirguṇa path — it is a compassionate acknowledgment of human nature.

CCommentary

Kleśo dhikataras — the sorrow (kleśa) is greater. The word is strong: it is not merely difficult but painful. Śaṅkara explains: the embodied mind is constituted by guṇas, which are forms of prakṛti; to fix a form-bound mind on the formless is like asking the eye to see its own seeing — possible, but requiring extraordinary refinement.

↗ cf. 7.13-14, 8.21-22, 13.12
Going deeper — the embodied limitation

Root: avyaktā hi gatir duḥkhaṃ dehavadbhir avāpyate — the unmanifest goal is attained with sorrow by the embodied (dehavadbhiḥ). Śaṅkara: the dehin (embodied one) has a mind conditioned by antaḥkaraṇa (inner instrument), which functions through forms; meditation on the formless requires first removing the form-tendency through prolonged sādhana. Rāmānuja: the embodied soul naturally relates to a personal God; the impersonal is approached through the personal, not instead of it. Cross-refs: 7.13–14, 8.21–22, 13.12.

A common misreading — 12.5 does not disparage

12.5 does not mean the nirguṇa path is inferior or wrong. It is harder — that is all. Both paths reach the same goal (12.3–4, 5.4–5). To read 12.5 as a rejection of the impersonal is to miss the Gita's consistent inclusivism.

12.6
ये तु सर्वाणि कर्माणि मयि संन्यस्य मत्पराः ।
अनन्यनेन योगेन मां ध्यायन्त उपासते ॥
Pye tu sarvāṇi karmāṇi mayi saṃnyasya mat-parāḥ / ananyenaiva yogena māṃ dhyāyanta upāsate
LBut those who surrender all actions to Me, devoted to Me, meditating on Me with undivided yoga — they worship Me.
MSurrender all actions to the Divine, fix your mind there — that is the easier path.
12.7
तेषामहं समुद्धर्ता मृत्युसंसारसागरात् ।
भवामि नचिरात्पार्थ मय्यावेशितचेतसाम् ॥
Pteṣām aham samuddhartā mṛtyu-saṃsāra-sāgarāt / bhavāmi nacirāt pārtha mayy āveśita-cetasām
LFor them I swiftly become the deliverer from the ocean of death and rebirth, O Partha — for those whose minds are absorbed in Me.
MFor those whose minds are on Him, the Divine personally becomes their deliverer.
12.8 – 12.12 · Gradations of practice
12.8Fix your mind on Me
मय्येव मन आधत्स्व मयि बुद्धिं निवेशय ।
निवसिष्यसि मय्येव अत ऊर्ध्वं न संशयः ॥
mayy eva mana ādhatsva mayi buddhiṃ niveśaya / nivasiṣyasi mayy eva ata ūrdhvaṃ na saṃśayaḥ
GĪTĀ 12.8
IIn plain English

Fix your mind and intellect on the Divine — you shall dwell in the Divine. No doubt. This is the Gita's most direct instruction: not a method, not a philosophy — an imperative. Lodge your mind here. Rest your intellect here. The result is assured.

CCommentary

Mana ādhatsva — lodge, place, establish your mind. The root dhā (to place) is deliberate: this is not passive hoping but intentional placement. You choose where to put the mind, as you choose where to set down a heavy load. Nivasiṣyasi — you shall dwell (from nivas, to abide). The one who fixes the mind on the Divine does not merely think about Him — he lives in Him.

↗ cf. 8.7, 9.34, 18.65
For the new reader

It is like choosing where to set up your home. You can dwell in anxiety, in ambition, in resentment — those are also "lodgings" of the mind. Or you can move. The Gita says: move your mind's address to the Divine. The lease is permanent; the rent is faith.

Try this — the one-minute reset

Several times today, pause and consciously lodge your mind on the Divine for one breath. Not a long meditation — just a deliberate return. Say inwardly: here. This is where I place my mind. Then return to your work. Frequency, not duration, builds the habit of 12.8.

Going deeper — ādhatsva — the deliberate act

Root: ādhatsva (imperative of ā-dhā, causative of dhā = to place/establish). Śaṅkara: the mana (mind) and buddhi (intellect) are the two faculties that must be fixed — the mind on the object (the Lord), the intellect on the conviction (that He is the supreme). Rāmānuja: this is the prapatti instruction — total placement of the inner being on the Lord. Cross-refs: 8.7 (remember Me and fight), 9.34, 18.65.

12.9If you cannot — the gradation begins
अथ चित्तं समाधातुं न शक्नोषि मयि स्थिरम् ।
अभ्यासयोगेन ततो मामिच्छाप्तुं धनंजय ॥
atha cittaṃ samādhātuṃ na śaknoṣi mayi sthiram / abhyāsa-yogena tato mām icchāptuṃ dhanañjaya
GĪTĀ 12.9
IIn plain English

If you cannot steady your mind on the Divine, then seek Him by practice. And if you cannot practise, act for His sake (12.10). And if you cannot do that, renounce the fruit of action (12.11). The Gita meets every reader where they are — and every starting point leads to the same summit.

CCommentary

Śaṅkara reads 12.9–12 as a gradation of means from most accessible to most advanced. The genius: no one is excluded. The one who can only let go of the fruit (12.11) is on the same path as the one who fixes the mind (12.8) — just at an earlier stage. Rāmānuja: each level is real and valid; phala-tyāga (12.11) is the universal baseline from which the higher stages grow.

↗ cf. 6.35, 3.19, 9.27, 18.11-12
For the new reader

A mountain has one summit but many trails. Some start at the base and walk slowly; some start halfway up. The Gita says: start wherever you are. Even the easiest trail (letting go of results) leads to the top.

Try this — identify your starting point

Which of the four can you do right now? (1) Fix your mind on the Divine? (2) Practise returning? (3) Act for His sake? (4) Let go of outcomes? Pick the one that is doable today — not the one you wish you could do. That is your trail.

Going deeper — the four-step hierarchy

Śaṅkara: 12.9 (abhyāsa) → 12.10 (mat-karma) → 12.11 (phala-tyāga) is listed in descending order of refinement but ascending accessibility. 12.12 then gives a different ordering (jñāna > abhyāsa; dhyāna > jñāna; tyāga > dhyāna → śānti), which Śaṅkara harmonises by noting that 12.9–12 describe different aspirants while 12.12 describes a single progression. Rāmānuja: the gradation shows the Lord's pastoral sensitivity — He never demands more than the devotee can give. Cross-refs: 6.35 (abhyāsa + vairāgya), 3.19, 9.27, 18.11–12.

12.10
अभ्यासेऽप्यसमर्थोऽसि मत्कर्मपरमो भव ।
मदर्थमपि कर्माणि कुर्वन्सिद्धिमवाप्स्यसि ॥
Pabhyāse'py asamartho'si mat-karma-paramo bhava / mad-artham api karmāṇi kurvan siddhim avāpsyasi
LIf you cannot even practise, then be devoted to My work; acting for My sake, you shall attain perfection.
MIf practice is too hard, then act for the Divine's sake — even that leads to perfection.
12.11
अथैतदप्यशक्तोऽसि कर्तुं मद्योगमाश्रितः ।
सर्वकर्मफलत्यागं ततो वा यतस्व भारत ॥
Pathaitad apy aśakto'si kartuṃ mad-yogam āśritaḥ / sarva-karma-phala-tyāgaṃ tato vā yatasya bhārata
LIf you cannot do even this, then take refuge in the yoga of renouncing the fruit of all action.
MIf even that is too much, then simply let go of the results of everything you do.
12.12
श्रेयो हि ज्ञानमभ्यासाज्ज्ञानाद्ध्यानं विशिष्यते ।
ध्यानात्कर्मफलत्यागस्त्यागाच्छान्तिरनन्तरम् ॥
Pśreyo hi jñānam abhyāsāj jñānād dhyānaṃ viśiṣyate / dhyānāt karma-phala-tyāgas tyāgāc chāntir anantaram
LKnowledge is better than practice; meditation better than knowledge; renunciation of fruit better than meditation — from renunciation, peace follows immediately.
MA gradation: practice → knowledge → meditation → letting go of fruit → peace.
12.13 – 12.20 · The marks of the devotee
12.13The portrait begins — ethics before mysticism
अद्वेष्टा सर्वभूतानां मैत्रः करुण एव च ।
निर्ममो निरहंकारः समदुःखसुखः क्षमी ॥
adveṣṭā sarva-bhūtānāṃ maitraḥ karuṇa eva ca / nirmamo nirahankāraḥ sama-duḥkha-sukhaḥ kṣamī
GĪTĀ 12.13
IIn plain English

The devotee: hating none, friendly, compassionate. Free from ego and possessiveness. Equal in pain and pleasure. Forgiving. Notice what comes first: not visions, not powers, not ecstatic experience — but kindness. The Gita's conviction: the spiritual life is not an escape from relationships but their perfection.

CCommentary

Śaṅkara reads these marks as the natural fruit of Self-realisation — the jñānī who sees the one Self in all cannot hate, because hatred presupposes otherness. Rāmānuja reads them as the marks of the bhakta — the devotee whose love for God overflows as love for all. Both agree: the marks are descriptive, not prescriptive — they describe what the devotee is, not what they must do to earn devotion.

↗ cf. 5.18, 6.9, 13.28, 2.55-72
For the new reader

Every wisdom tradition eventually says the same thing: the holy person is recognisable not by visions but by kindness. The Buddha's compassion, Christ's love, the Sufi's tenderness — they are all the same fruit on different trees. The Gita was there first. Adveṣṭā — non-hating — is the foundation of them all.

Try this — the no-hatred practice

Today, identify one person toward whom you feel hostility. Without forcing warmth, simply resolve: I will not add to the hatred in the world toward this person. That refusal to hate is the first mark of the devotee.

Going deeper — adroṓṣṭā — non-hating as metaphysical

Roots: adroṣṭā (a-droṣṭṛ, one who does not hate; from dviṣ = to hate), maitraḥ (friend, from mitra), karuṇa (compassionate, from kṛ = to do, via karuṇā), nirmamaḥ (without mine-ness; nir-mamatā), nirahaṅkāraḥ (without I-making; ahankāra = aham + kāra). Śaṅkara: non-hating is not an ethical achievement but a metaphysical recognition — when you see the one Self in all, hatred becomes impossible because there is no other to hate. Cross-refs: 5.18, 6.9, 13.28, 2.55–72.

12.14
सन्तुष्टः सततं योगी यतात्मा दृढनिश्चयः ।
मय्यर्पितमनोबुद्धिर्यो मद्भक्तः स मे प्रियः ॥
Psantuṣṭaḥ satataṃ yogī yatātmā dṛḍha-niścayaḥ / mayy arpita-mano-buddhir yo mad-bhaktaḥ sa me priyaḥ
LEver content, self-controlled, firm in resolve, mind and intellect surrendered to Me — that devotee of Mine is dear to Me.
MContent, steady, surrendered — such a one is dear to the Divine.
12.15One who disturbs no one
यस्मान्नोद्विजते लोको लोकान्नोद्विजते च यः ।
हर्षामर्षभयोद्वेगैर्मुक्तो यः स च मे प्रियः ॥
yasmān nodvijate loko lokān nodvijate ca yaḥ / harṣāmarṣabhayodvegair mukto yaḥ sa ca me priyaḥ
GĪTĀ 12.15
IIn plain English

One who disturbs no one and is disturbed by nothing — free from excitement, envy, fear, and anxiety. This is not passivity; it is stability. The devotee is not a doormat but an ocean — everything pours in, the surface does not move. Not because nothing is felt, but because the depth is greater than the wave.

CCommentary

Yasmān nodvijate loko lokān nodvijate ca yaḥ — a beautiful chiasmus: the world is not agitated by him; he is not agitated by the world. The freedom from fear and anxiety (bhaya-udvegair muktaḥ) is not denial of danger but depth of refuge — the devotee's security is not in circumstances but in the Divine.

↗ cf. 2.56, 4.10, 5.20-21
For the new reader

Think of the calmest person you know — not the one who feels nothing, but the one whose calm is deeper than the storm. They don't suppress reactions; they have a larger space inside. That spaciousness is what 12.15 describes.

Try this — the non-disturbance practice

Today, notice one moment when something agitates you — a comment, a delay, a slight. Instead of reacting, pause and ask: must I be disturbed by this? The devotee chooses non-disturbance, not because the world is harmless, but because the Divine is a deeper home than the disturbance.

Going deeper — udvij — the root of agitation

Root: udvij (ud-√vij, to be agitated/startled, literally to tremble upward). Śaṅkara: the world does not tremble around the devotee because his non-reactivity creates a zone of peace — others feel safe near him. And he does not tremble at the world because his refuge is internal. Harṣa (elation) and amarṣa (impatience/jealousy) are paired as the two extremes of reactivity; bhaya (fear) and udvega (anxiety) as the two forms of contraction. The devotee is free from all four. Cross-refs: 2.56, 4.10, 5.20–21.

12.16
अनपेक्षः शुचिर्दक्ष उदासीनो गतव्यथः ।
सर्वारम्भपरित्यागी यो मद्भक्तः स मे प्रियः ॥
Panapekṣaḥ śucir dakṣa udāsīno gata-vyathaḥ / sarvārambha-parityāgī yo mad-bhaktaḥ sa me priyaḥ
LFree from expectation, pure, skilful, indifferent, free from pain, renouncing all undertakings — that devotee is dear to Me.
MWithout demands, pure, capable, calm, free from pain, attached to no outcome.
12.17No elation, no hatred — renouncing good and bad
यो न हृष्यति न द्वेष्टि न शोचति न काङ्क्षति ।
शुभाशुभपरित्यागी भक्तिमान्यः स मे प्रियः ॥
yo na hṛṣyati na dveṣṭi na śocati na kāṅkṣati / śubhāśubha-parityāgī bhaktimān yaḥ sa me priyaḥ
GĪTĀ 12.17
IIn plain English

Who neither rejoices nor hates nor grieves nor desires — renouncing both good and bad outcomes, devoted. This is the devotee whose equanimity is not selective. Most of us can accept bad outcomes if we must; fewer can let go of good ones. The devotee lets go of both — not because nothing matters, but because everything is offered.

CCommentary

Śubhāśubha-parityāgī — renouncing good and bad. This is not nihilism; it is offering. The devotee does not suppress joy or manufacture indifference; rather, both pleasure and pain are placed at the Divine's feet. What remains is bhaktimān — filled with devotion, which is a fuller feeling than any passing joy or grief.

↗ cf. 2.38, 2.48, 4.22, 14.24-25
For the new reader

A parent watching a child succeed and a child fail feels both deeply — but underneath both is a love that does not change. That unchanging love is devotion; the successes and failures are the good and bad that are held but not clutched.

Going deeper — śubhāśubha-parityāga

Root: śubha-aśubha-parityāgī (one who has renounced — pari-tyāga — both the auspicious and inauspicious). Śaṅkara: the renunciation is of attachment to outcomes, not of the outcomes themselves — the devotee still acts, still experiences, but does not clutch. Rāmānuja: the devotee offers both success and failure to the Lord, as a servant brings both good and bad news to the king without personal claim. Madhva: equanimity is the mark of one who knows the Lord is the ultimate arbiter of all results. Cross-refs: 2.38, 2.48, 4.22, 14.24–25.

12.18
समः शत्रौ च मित्रे च तथा मानापमानयोः ।
शीतोष्णसुखदुःखेषु समः सङ्गविवर्जितः ॥
Psamaḥ śatrau ca mitre ca tathā mānāpamānayoḥ / śītoṣṇa-sukha-duḥkheṣu samaḥ saṅga-vivarjitaḥ
LEqual to friend and foe, in honour and dishonour, in cold and heat, pleasure and pain, free from attachment.
MEqual to friend and enemy. Equal in praise and blame. Equal in comfort and discomfort. Free from attachment.
12.19The portrait climaxes — praise and blame are the same
तुल्यनिन्दास्तुतिर्मौनी सन्तुष्टो येन केनचित् ।
अनिकेतः स्थिरमतिर्भक्तिमान्मे प्रियो नरः ॥
tulya-nindā-stutir maunī santuṣṭo yena kenacit / aniketaḥ sthira-matir bhaktimān me priyo naraḥ
GĪTĀ 12.19
IIn plain English

Praise and blame are the same. Silent. Content with whatever comes. Homeless. Steady. Devoted. These are not five separate qualities but one quality seen from five angles: the freedom of one whose identity is no longer located in the world's opinion, the world's supply, or the world's address. The devotee is at home everywhere because at home in the Divine.

CCommentary

Aniketaḥ — homeless — does not mean literally without shelter. It means inwardly unrooted, not anchored to any fixed identity or location. The devotee is stihira-matiḥ (firm-minded) precisely because aniketaḥ — not rooted in any shifting thing, the mind rests in the unshifting. Śaṅkara: this is the jñānī whose Self-knowledge makes all external anchors unnecessary. Rāmānuja: the devotee is so full of God that no external validation or comfort is needed.

↗ cf. 2.69-71, 4.22, 14.24-25
For the new reader

A tree that can grow anywhere — because it is not attached to one patch of soil. The devotee is like that: rooted not in a place, a role, or a reputation, but in the Divine. Uproot a tree and it dies. Uproot the devotee — and they simply grow elsewhere.

Try this — the contentment practice

Today, when something you wanted does not come, or something you did not want does — say inwardly: I am content with whatever comes. Not as resignation but as trust. The devotee who is santuṣṭo yena kenacit (content with whatever) has discovered a secret: contentment is not the result of getting what you want; it is the result of no longer needing to get what you want.

Going deeper — aniketaḥ — the unrooted devotee

Roots: tulya-nindā-stutiḥ (equal in censure and praise), maunī (silent, from mauna), santuṣṭo yena kenacit (content with whatever), aniketaḥ (without a fixed dwelling; a-niketa, from ni-ketana = a settled habitation), sthira-matiḥ (firm-minded). Śaṅkara: these five are the fruit of sāmya (evenness) — the mind that sees the same Self in praise and blame, in plenty and want, in home and homelessness. Rāmānuja: the devotee is aniketa because his true home is the Lord, not any earthly dwelling. Cross-refs: 2.69–71, 4.22, 14.24–25, Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.4.22 (parallel portrait).

4Takeaway
In Your Life

At work: Devotion is not a spiritual add-on; it is the quality of attention you bring to what you do. A doctor devoted to healing, a teacher devoted to students -- all are practising bhakti. The question is not whether your work is spiritual but whether your spirit is in your work.

In relationships: The marks of the devotee (12.13-19) are the world's best relationship ethic: hate none, be friendly, compassionate, forgiving. Bring these to even one difficult relationship.

In decision-making: When you cannot decide, ask: which option lets me fix my mind more steadily on what matters most? The answer is usually the simpler one.

In grief and loss: Santustho yena kenacit -- content with whatever comes. Not resignation but trust.

Chapter 12 turns love into the path.

  1. God can be loved as a Person or contemplated as the formless — both reach Him. The personal is easier; both are true.
  2. The highest door is undivided devotion. Not scholarship, not austerity — love. And if you cannot love fully yet, start where you are: practise, act for Him, or simply let go.
  3. The marks of the devotee are kindness, equanimity, and freedom from ego. Not visions — character. The person God loves most harms no one, holds nothing, fears nothing.

The person the Divine loves most is not the one who has extraordinary experiences but the one who, in the ordinary, harms no one, holds nothing, fears nothing — and loves.

5The 18-Step Sādhana Path — where you are

Step 12 of 18 · CULTIVATE THE DEAR-TO-ME QUALITIES. This week, take one quality from the portrait of the devotee (12.13–19) — no hatred, forgiveness, contentment, equal-mindedness, non-disturbance — and make it your deliberate practice. When you succeed, offer it. When you fail, begin again. That is bhakti.
