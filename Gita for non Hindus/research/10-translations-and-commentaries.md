# 10 — Translations, Commentaries & Recommended Reading

The book must rest on a small set of reliable sources and cite them consistently. This file inventories the
best ones and flags their character.

## 1. Classical commentaries (Bhāṣyas)

These are the traditional interpretive pillars. The book should acknowledge that **every** reading of the
Gita is filtered through one of these lenses.

| Commentator | Date | School | Emphasis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ādi Śaṅkarācārya** | c. 700–750 CE | Advaita (non-dual) | knowledge (jñāna); ātman = Brahman |
| **Rāmānujācārya** | c. 1017–1137 CE | Viśiṣṭādvaita (qualified non-dual) | devotion (bhakti); surrender (prapatti) |
| **Madhvācārya** | c. 1238–1317 CE | Dvaita (dualism) | eternal distinction of soul & God |
| **Abhinavagupta** | c. 950–1020 CE | Kashmir Śaiva / syncretic | aesthetic-devotional reading |
| **Vallabhācārya** | 1479–1531 CE | Śuddhādvaita | pure devotion (puṣṭi) |
| **Jñāneśvar (Jñāndev)** | 1275–1296 CE | Marathi bhakti | *Jñāneśvarī* — beloved vernacular commentary |
| **Keshav Kashmiri** | c. 1410 CE | Nimbarka school | devotional |

**Modern-era influential commentaries:**
- **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** — *Gita Rahasya* (1915): karma yoga as the Gita's essence; action for social good.
- **Mahatma Gandhi** — *Discourses on the Gita* (1926): non-violence; the war as allegory; *anasakti*
  (non-attachment) as the core.
- **Sri Aurobindo** — *Essays on the Gita*: integral spirituality; "divine life" in the world.
- **Swami Vivekananda / Swami Chinmayananda / Swami Prabhupada (ISKCON)** — devotional/neo-Vedantic popular
  readings (Prabhupada's is strongly Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava).

## 2. Recommended scholarly English translations

| Translator | Strengths |
|---|---|
| **J.A.B. van Buitenen** (1981) | Rigorous; situates in the epic context. |
| **Winthrop Sargeant** (1979/2009) | Word-by-word Sanskrit + grammar; gold standard for verse-level study. |
| **Robert Zaehner** (1966) | Scholarly, theologically literate. |
| **Barbara Stoler Miller** (1986) | Poetic, accessible; good for general readers. |
| **W.J. Johnson** (Oxford World's Classics, 1994) | Clean, reliable, affordable. |
| **Laurie Patton** (Penguin, 2008) | Readable, well-annotated. |
| **Eknath Easwaran** (1985) | Best for newcomers; clear prose + introductions (non-academic, slightly interpretive). |
| **Gambhirananda** (Advaita) / **Rāmānuja transl.** | For school-specific depth. |
| **Swami Chidbhavananda**, **Swami Sivananda** | Devotional-Indian exposition. |

## 3. Crucial historical translations
- **Charles Wilkins** — *Bhagvat-Geeta* (1785): **the first English translation**, presented to the East
  India Company; stirred Emerson, Thoreau, and Hegel. Historically important.
- **Persian translation** — *Sirr-i Akbar*, by Prince **Dara Shukoh** (1657), later translated into Latin
  (Anquetil-Duperron, 1785) — how the Gita first reached Europe.

## 4. Recommended scholarly studies (for the author's grounding)
- Robert N. Minor, ed., *Modern Indian Interpreters of the Bhagavadgītā* (1986).
- Eric Sharpe, *The Universal Gītā* (1985) — history of the Gita's reception.
- Jeaneane Fowler, *The Bhagavad Gita: A Text and Commentary for Students* (2012).
- Wilhelm Halbfass, *India and Europe* — for the cross-cultural reception.
- Graham Schweig, *Bhagavad Gītā: The Beloved Lord's Secret Love Song* (2007) — strong on bhakti.

## 5. Suggested primary working translation for THIS book
Recommendation: use **Sargeant** for verse-level accuracy (Sanskrit + literal), and **van Buitenen / W.J.
Johnson** for the book's flowing English, while producing the book's **own plain-English rendering** for the
final text. Cite the Sanskrit and the chosen literal translation in footnotes; keep the main commentary
jargon-free.
