# Deep Commentary — Chapters 16–18 (Daivāsura · Śraddhātraya · Mokṣa-Sannyāsa)

> *The closing arc: divine vs demoniac natures, the threefold faith, and the grand synthesis. Landmarks:
> 16.1–3, 16.21; 17.3, 17.23; 18.47, 18.61–62, 18.65, 18.66, 18.78.*

---

# CHAPTER 16: Daivāsura-Sampad-Vibhāga Yoga (Divine & Demoniac Natures) — 24 verses

**At a glance.** Kṛṣṇa catalogues the **divine** (*daivī*) endowments (16.1–3) and the **demoniac**
(*āsurī*) traits (16.4, 16.7–21), warns that the **threefold gate of hell** (*narakasya* traya-dvāra) is
**lust, anger, and greed** (16.21), and urges: **let scripture be your authority** (*śāstraṃ pramāṇam*,
16.24).

## 16.1–16.3 — The divine endowments
*abhayaṃ sattva-saṃśuddhir jñāna-yoga-vyavasthitiḥ / dānaṃ damaś ca yajñaś ca svādhyāyas tapa ārjavam* —
fearlessness, purity of heart, steadfastness in knowledge-yoga, charity, sense-control, sacrifice,
self-study, austerity, uprightness; non-violence, truth, freedom from anger, renunciation, tranquillity,
aversion to fault-finding, compassion, non-covetousness, gentleness, modesty, steadiness, **vigour,
forgiveness, fortitude, cleanliness, freedom from malice, absence of vanity** — "these belong to one **born
for the divine** (*abhijātasya... daivīm*)."

## 16.4–16.21 — The demoniac nature and the three gates of hell
**16.4:** *dambho darpo'bhimānaś ca krodhaḥ pāruṣyam eva ca / ajñānaṃ cābhijātasya... sampadam āsurīm* —
hypocrisy, arrogance, conceit, anger, harshness, ignorance — the āsurī endowments. **16.7–12:** the demonic
know not what to do or not do; they say "the world is without truth, without basis, **without God**
(*a-īśvara*)"; they are born to be destroyed; taking refuge in insatiable desire, **hypocrites**, **filled
with ego**, they **seize** wealth by **unjust means**. **16.13–15:** "this I have (*idam adrsta
labdham*)... this wealth is mine, and I shall have more." **16.16:** "bound by hundreds of hopes, given to
lust and anger, they **seek to amass wealth unjustly** for the gratification of the senses." **16.21 —
LANDMARK:** *tri-vidhaṃ narakasyedaṃ dvāraṃ nāśanam ātmanaḥ / kāmaḥ krodhas tathā lobhas tasmād etat trayaṃ
tyajet* — "threefold gate of hell, **destructive of the self** (*ātmanaḥ nāśanam*): **lust, anger, and greed**
(*kāma-krodha-lobha*) — therefore **abandon these three**."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 16.21 names the **root triad** of bondage — the same *kāma-krodha* diagnosed in
3.37 and the *kāma* that 2.62–63 traces to ruin; *lobha* (greed) is added as the third member. These three
are the "gates" because every āsuric act enters through them. Liberation requires their renunciation.
Rāmānuja: the daivī/āsurī distinction is **real** but not **birth-based** — it is a matter of the soul's
**choice and cultivation**. Abandoning the three gates is the **ethical precondition** of the spiritual path.
Madhva: the daivī endowments are marks of the soul destined for the Lord; āsurī traits indicate a soul turned
from Him. Abhinavagupta: the "demoniac" is the **contracted** (*saṅkucita*) state of consciousness; the
"divine" is its **expanded** (*vikāsita*) state.
**Cross-refs:** 2.62–63 (the chain from desire), 3.37, 14.7 (rajas born of *tṛṣṇā*).
**Layers —** W: "Fearlessness, truth, compassion, non-violence — these mark the divine character. Lust,
anger, greed — these three are the gates of hell. Abandon them." / S: the daivī/āsurī catalogue; the three
gates. / Sk: identify which of the three gates is your weakest and work on it. / A: Śaṅkara (the root triad)
vs Abhinavagupta (contraction vs expansion).

## 16.22–16.24 — Śāstra as authority
**16.23–24:** one who **casts off scripture** (*śāstra-vidhim utsṛjya*) and acts from **desire**
(*kāma-kārataḥ*) — attains neither perfection nor happiness nor the supreme goal. *tasmāc chāstraṃ pramāṇaṃ te
kāryākārya-vyavasthitau* — "therefore **let scripture be your authority** (*pramāṇaṃ*) in what to do and not
do."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: *śāstra* here means the **Vedānta śāstra** (the Upaniṣads and their auxiliaries) —
the authority that defines right and wrong action for the aspirant. The liberated *jñānī* is beyond śāstra's
binding (3.17–18) but the aspirant must follow it.

# CHAPTER 17: Śraddhātraya-Vibhāga Yoga (The Threefold Faith) — 28 verses

**At a glance.** Arjuna asks (17.1): what of one who has faith (*śraddhā*) but does not follow the scriptures
— is he sāttvic, rājasic, or tāmasic? Kṛṣṇa explains that **faith follows nature** (*satva-anurūpā sarvasya
śraddhā bhavati bhārata*, 17.3) — "man is made of his faith: as one's faith is, so is one" (*yo yac chraddhaḥ
sa eva saḥ*). He classifies **food** (17.8–10), **sacrifice** (17.11–13), **austerity** (17.14–19), and
**giving** (17.20–22) by the three guṇas. The chapter closes with the **OM TAT SAT** formula (17.23–28).

## 17.1–17.6 — Faith follows nature
**17.3:** *sattvānurūpā sarvasya śraddhā bhavati bhārata / śraddhā-mayo 'yaṃ puruṣo yo yac chraddhaḥ sa eva
saḥ* — "the faith of each follows their **inner being** (*sattva*); this person is **made of faith** — **as
one's faith is, so is one**." The sāttvic worship the gods, the rājasic the Yakṣas/Rākṣasas, the tāmasic the
spirits and the dead.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 17.3 is a profound psychological observation — **faith is not arbitrary but reflects
one's dominant guṇa**. One cannot fake a faith higher than one's level; but one can **cultivate** sattva to
elevate one's faith. Rāmānuja: faith is the **root** of all worship; its quality determines the fruit.

## 17.7–17.22 — Food, sacrifice, austerity, and giving — by guṇa
**Food** (17.8–10): sāttvic = **juicy, unctuous, substantial, agreeable** (*rasyāḥ snigdhā sthirā hṛdyā*) —
promotes life, purity, strength, health, joy. Rājasic = **bitter, sour, salty, hot, pungent, dry, burning**
(*kaṭv-amla-lavaṇa...*) — causes pain, sorrow, disease. Tāmasic = **stale, tasteless, putrid, left-over**
(*yāta-yāma gata-rasa*) — impure.
**Austerity of speech** (17.15): *anudvega-karaṃ vākyaṃ satyaṃ priya-hitaṃ ca yat* — speech that is
**truthful, pleasing, beneficial, non-disturbing** — plus the study of scripture. **Mental austerity** (17.16):
serenity (*manaḥ-prasāda*), gentleness (*saumyatvam*), silence (*maunam*), self-control, purity of motive.
**Giving** (17.20): sāttvic giving = at the right place/time, to one who **cannot repay** (*anupakāriṇe*), as
a duty. **OM TAT SAT** (17.23): *oṁ tat sat iti nirdeśo brahmaṇas tri-vidhaḥ smṛtaḥ* — "OM TAT SAT" — the
threefold designation of Brahman.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the entire life — eating, speaking, giving, sacrificing — is classified by the guṇas
to show that **every act has a spiritual quality**. The sāttvic mode of each is the one that purifies.
**Layers —** W: "Your faith reflects who you are — and even what you eat and how you give either purify or
pollute you." / S: the fourfold classification; OM TAT SAT. / Sk: make one act sāttvic this week (food,
speech, giving). / A: the guṇa-classification of daily life; 17.15 (speech) as a complete ethic of
communication.

# CHAPTER 18: Mokṣa-Sannyāsa Yoga (Liberation & Renunciation) — 78 verses

**At a glance — THE GRAND SYNTHESIS AND FAREWELL.** The longest chapter. Kṛṣṇa distinguishes true *tyāga*
(renouncing the fruit) from *sannyāsa* (giving up action, 18.1–6); revisits the **five factors of action**
(18.13–15); classifies **knowledge, action, and doer** by the three guṇas (18.20–28); defines **pleasure** by
guṇa (18.36–39); restates **svadharma** (18.47); and climaxes with the **final promise and surrender**
(18.65–66): *"abandon all dharmas and take refuge in Me alone — I shall free you."* The chapter (and the Gītā)
closes with Sañjaya's **benediction** (18.78).

## 18.1–18.6 — Tyāga vs Sannyāsa
**18.2:** *kāmyānāṃ karmaṇāṃ nyāsaṃ sannyāsaṃ kavayo viduḥ / sarva-karma-phala-tyāgaṃ prāhus tyāgaṃ vicakṣaṇāḥ*
— "giving up **desire-motivated** actions = *sannyāsa*; giving up the **fruit** of all action = *tyāga*."
**18.5–6:** sacrifice, charity, austerity must **not** be abandoned — they purify even the wise; "but even
these, **do without attachment** (*saṅgaṃ tyaktvā*)."

## 18.7–18.12 — The three-fold renunciation (by guṇa)
Sāttvic *tyāga*: done because **it must be done** (*kāryam ity*), abandoning attachment and fruit (18.9).
Rājasic: renouncing out of **fear of suffering** (18.8). Tāmasic: renouncing **what ought to be done** out of
**delusion** (18.7).

## 18.13–18.18 — The five factors of action
**18.14–15:** *adhiṣṭhānaṃ tathā kartā karaṇaṃ ca pṛthag-vidham / vividhāś ca pṛthak ceṣṭā daivaṃ caivātra
pañcamam* — the **five factors**: (1) **the locus** (*adhiṣṭhāna*, the body); (2) **the agent** (*kartā*);
(3) the **instruments** (*karaṇa*, the senses); (4) the **diverse efforts** (*ceṣṭā*); (5) **destiny/the
Divine** (*daivam*, the fifth).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the five-factor analysis **humbles the ego** — action is not the product of the
individual alone but of a complex of body, senses, effort, and cosmic destiny. This is the basis of
*akartṛtva* (non-doership): seeing all five factors, one does not claim "I alone did this."

## 18.20–18.28 — Knowledge, action, and doer (by guṇa)
**18.20 — LANDMARK:** *sarva-bhūteṣu yenaikaṃ bhāvam avyayam īkṣate / avibhaktaṃ vibhakteṣu taj jñānaṃ viddhi
sāttvikam* — "knowledge that sees **one imperishable reality, undivided** amid all divided beings — that is
sāttvic knowledge." Rājasic knowledge = sees **multiplicity** (*prthak-bhāvam*) in separate beings. Tāmasic =
clings to **one act as if it were all** (*ekaṃ kevalam*), without reason, without truth.
**18.26–28:** the **sāttvic doer** (*kartā*) — free from attachment, non-egoic (*anahaṃ-vādī*), **steadfast,
unaffected by success/failure** (*siddhy-asiddhyor nirvikāraḥ*). The rājasic — passionate, greedy,
**desiring the fruit**. The tāmasic — **unsteady, vulgar, deceitful, lazy, depressed**.

## 18.37–18.39 — Three-fold pleasure
**18.37–38:** sāttvic pleasure = **poison at first, nectar at the end** (*viṣam iva, amṛtopamam*) — born of
Self-knowledge, clear as crystal. Rājasic = **nectar at first, poison at the end** — from the contact of
senses with objects. Tāmasic = **delusive** from beginning to end — sleep, laziness, violence (*nidrā,
ālasya, pramāda*).

## 18.45–18.48 — Svadharma restated
**18.45:** *yataḥ pravṛttir bhūtānāṃ yena sarvam idaṃ tatam / sva-karmaṇā tam abhyarcya siddhiṃ vindati mānavaḥ*
— "by worshipping through one's **own duty** (*sva-karmaṇā*) Him from whom all proceed and by whom all is
pervaded — one attains perfection." **18.47 — LANDMARK:** *śreyān sva-dharmo viguṇaḥ para-dharmāt
sv-anuṣṭhitāt / svabhāva-niyataṃ karma kurvan nāpnoti kilbiṣam* — "better one's own dharma though imperfect
than another's well done; doing the action **prescribed by one's own nature** (*svabhāva-niyatam*), one
incurs **no sin**." **18.48:** no duty should be abandoned because it has **flaws** (*sadosam api*) — as fire
is obscured by smoke.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 18.47 restates the svadharma principle of 3.35 — one's own duty (*svabhāva-niyatam*)
suited to one's *prakṛti*; doing it is the path of karma-yoga. **Handle with file 11's caste treatment.**
Rāmānuja: 18.46 is the key — worshipping the Lord through one's own work (*abhyarcya*) is *karma-yoga =
bhakti*. Madhva: *svabhāva* is the Lord's ordination; following it is non-sinful.

## 18.49–18.55 — The path to Brahman
**18.50–54:** the one who is **devoid of doership** (*buddhyā viśuddhayā yuktaḥ*), whose mind is surrendered,
renouncing the fruit, attains **peace**; becoming Brahman (*brahma-bhūta*), one attains the supreme bliss of
Brahman; one neither grieves nor desires — **equal to all beings** (*sama-bhūta*). **18.55:** *bhaktyā mām
abhijānāti yāvān yaś cāsmi tattvataḥ / tato māṃ tattvato jñātvā viśate tad-anantaram* — "by **devotion**
(*bhaktyā*) one **knows Me in truth** (*tattvataḥ*) — how great I am and who; knowing Me in truth, one
**enters into Me** immediately."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 18.55 is the **culmination of the knowledge-devotion unity** — *bhakti* matures into
*jñāna* (knowing the Lord in truth) which IS *praveśa* (entry into Him). Rāmānuja: this is the supreme
statement — **bhakti** is the direct means of knowing and attaining the Lord.

## 18.58–18.63 — The Lord as charioteer of the heart
**18.61 — LANDMARK:** *īśvaraḥ sarva-bhūtānāṃ hṛd-deśe'rjuna tiṣṭhati / bhrāmayan sarva-bhūtāni yantrārūḍhāni
māyayā* — "the Lord dwells in the **hearts of all beings** (*hṛd-deśe*), causing all beings to **revolve as
if mounted on a machine** (*yantrārūḍhāni*) by His **māyā**." **18.62:** *tam eva śaraṇaṃ gaccha sarva-bhāvena
bhārata / tat-prasādāt parāṃ śāntiṃ sthānaṃ prāpsyasi śāśvatam* — "take **refuge in Him alone** (*tam eva
śaraṇaṃ gaccha*) with your whole being; by His grace, attain supreme peace, the **eternal abode**."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 18.61 is the Gītā's most vivid image of **divine providence and human agency** — the
Lord turns beings on the machine of prakṛti, but beings experience the turning as **their own** movement
(cf. 3.27, 13.21). 18.62 is the imperative: **surrender** (*śaraṇaṃ gaccha*) — the culmination of the entire
teaching.
**Cross-refs:** 13.22 (the paramātmā in all hearts); 9.10 (adhyakṣa); 15.15.

## 18.65–18.66 — THE FINAL PROMISE AND THE SUPREME SURRENDER
**18.65:** *man-manā bhava mad-bhakto mad-yājī māṃ namaskuru / mām evaiṣyasi satyaṃ te pratijāne priyo'si
me* — "fix your mind on Me, be My devotee, sacrifice to Me, bow to Me; you shall **come to Me** — truly I
promise (*satyam te pratijāne*); you are dear to Me." **18.66 — THE CARANA-ŚLOKA (THE CLIMAX OF THE ENTIRE
GĪTĀ):** *sarva-dharmān parityajya mām ekaṁ śaraṇaṃ vraja / ahaṃ tvāṃ sarva-pāpebhyo mokṣayiṣyāmi mā śucaḥ* —
"**abandoning all dharmas**, take refuge in Me **alone**; **I shall free you** from all sins; **do not
grieve**."
**Commentary:**
- **Śaṅkara:** "abandoning all dharmas" = abandoning all **karma** (including Vedic rituals and even
karma-yoga) as *means* of liberation, since only **knowledge of the Self** (*jñāna-niṣṭhā*) directly liberates.
The "surrender" is the **final settled rest** of the mind in the Self after all other means have done their
preparatory work (*sādhana*). The Lord's promise to "free" is His assurance that the Self-nature is already
free — ignorance alone binds.
- **Rāmānuja:** **18.66 is the supreme charter of *prapatti*** — total, exclusive surrender to the Lord as
the **sole means** (*upāya*) of liberation. "Abandoning all dharmas" = giving up the idea that **any action,
knowledge, or discipline** can earn liberation — and resting entirely on the Lord's grace. The Lord Himself
(*aham*) undertakes to free (*mokṣayiṣyāmi*) the surrendered soul. *Mā śucaḥ* ("do not grieve") = the most
tender assurance in the Gītā.
- **Madhva:** surrender is real and the Lord's promise is sure — but the soul remains **eternally distinct**
and **dependent**; "I shall free you" means the Lord, by His grace, removes the obstacles that prevented the
soul's natural dependence from bearing fruit.
- **Abhinavagupta:** the "abandoning" is the release of all conceptual frameworks (*sarva-dharma*) into the
recognition of one's identity with universal consciousness.
**Misreading:** (1) antinomianism — "abandon all dharma, so do whatever you want." The Gītā's 18.66 follows
66 verses of ethical/spiritual teaching; "abandoning dharmas" means abandoning them as the **means** of
liberation, not as **ethical living**. (2) "faith alone, no works ever" — the Gītā keeps the life of
*karma-yoga* as the **fruit** of surrender (cf. 18.56: "doing all actions for Me").
**Cross-refs:** 7.14 (māyā is hard to cross; only the surrendered cross); 9.22, 9.34; 11.53–55; 12.6–7.
**Layers —** W: "After all the teaching, the Lord says one final thing: **stop trying to earn it — just come
to Me. I will carry you. Do not grieve.**" / S: the caraṇa-śloka; the three schools' readings. / Sk: the
supreme handover — offer everything, even your spiritual effort, to the Divine. / A: Śaṅkara
(karma-abandonment as jñāna-niṣṭhā) vs Rāmānuja (*prapatti* charter) vs Madhva (grace that preserves
dependence) vs Abhinavagupta (release of all frameworks).

## 18.67–18.78 — The sealing and the benediction
**18.67:** "this must not be spoken to one who lacks austerity, or devotion, or who does not want to hear, or
who envies Me." **18.68–69:** "one who shall declare this supreme secret to My devotees — having performed
the highest devotion, he shall **come to Me**; no one among men does what is more dear to Me." **18.70:**
"whoever hears this with faith and without cavil — he too is **freed** and attains the worlds of the
meritorious." **18.71–72:** "have you heard this with a focused mind? Has your delusion, born of ignorance,
been **destroyed**?" **18.73** (Arjuna): *naṣṭo mohaḥ smṛtir labdhā tvat-prasādān mayācyuta / sthito'smi
gata-sandehaḥ kariṣye vacanaṃ tava* — "my **delusion is destroyed** (*naṣṭo mohaḥ*); my memory restored; I am
**steady, doubt-free** — **I shall act according to Your word**." **18.78** (Sañjaya): *yatra yogeśvaraḥ
kṛṣṇo yatra pārtho dhanur-dharaḥ / tatra śrīr vijayo bhūtir dhruvā nītir matir mama* — "wherever there is
Kṛṣṇa, the **Lord of Yoga**, and Arjuna, the archer — there are **fortune, victory, prosperity, and unfailing
justice** — such is my conviction."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 18.73 — Arjuna's delusion (*moha*) is destroyed **by Kṛṣṇa's grace**
(*tvat-prasādāt*), not by his own effort alone. The Gītā ends not with action (though Arjuna says "I shall
act") but with **grace** — the Lord's compassionate intervention that removes the root of paralysis. 18.78:
Sañjaya's benediction is not mere praise but a **statement of cosmic law**: where the Divine and the
dedicated human are united (yogeśvara + Pārtha), there is every auspiciousness. Rāmānuja: 18.78 is the
supreme *phala-śruti* — the fruit of the entire Gītā — union with the Lord ensures all good.
**Layers —** W: "Arjuna says: 'My confusion is gone. I will do as You say.' And wherever the Divine and a
willing human meet — there, everything good is guaranteed." / S: the sealing; 18.73 as Arjuna's
transformation; 18.78 as cosmic law. / Sk: locate your own 'naṣṭo mohaḥ' — when has your confusion lifted
through grace? / A: Śaṅkara (moha destroyed by grace, not effort alone) vs the schools on 18.78's meaning.
