# Deep Commentary — Chapter 9: Rāja-vidyā-rāja-guhya Yoga (The Royal Secret)

> *Verse-by-verse working reference for the author. Not for the reader verbatim — the source from which the
> Student/Seeker/Adept layers of Chapter 9 are authored. Sanskrit mūla verified against file 20 / Gita Press.
> Commentarial attributions follow the standard readings of each school; specific glosses marked [VERIFY]
> where I could not confirm the exact wording in translation. Intellectual honesty: the Gītā sustains several
> readings — I present them, I do not crown one.*

**Chapter at a glance.** Chapter 9 is the heart of the Gītā's bhakti revelation: Kṛṣṇa unfolds the relation of
the Lord to the world (immanent yet transcendent, 9.4–10), the two classes of humanity (9.11–15), the
universal reach of worship (9.16–25), the ethics of offering (9.26–28), the equality and grace of God
(9.29–34). Its emotional centre is 9.22 (the promise) and 9.30–31 (grace for the fallen).

**The schools' general stance on Chapter 9:**
- **Śaṅkara (Advaita):** the "king of knowledge" is the knowledge of the identity of ātman and brahman;
  *bhakti* is meditation on the qualified (*saguṇa*) Brahman that ripens into *parā-bhakti* (= jñāna).
- **Rāmānuja (Viśiṣṭādvaita):** this chapter is the charter of *prapatti* (total surrender); 9.22 shows the
  Lord Himself as the devotee's *upāya* (means); 9.30–31 and 9.34 are its pillars.
- **Madhva (Dvaita):** bhakti grounded in knowledge of the Lord's infinite excellence and the soul's eternal
  dependence (*pāratantrya*); grace is real but never erases the irreducible self–God distinction.
- **Abhinavagupta (Kashmir Śaiva):** reads the chapter as the recognition (*pratyabhijñā*) that all
  differentiation is the play of one consciousness; *bhakti* is the savouring of one's own true nature.

---

## 9.1
**Devanagari:** इदं तु ते गुह्यतमं प्रवक्ष्याम्यनसूयवे। ज्ञानं विज्ञानसहितं यज्ञात्वा मोक्ष्यसेऽशुभात्॥
**IAST:** idaṃ tu te guhyatamaṃ pravakṣyāmy anasūyave / jñānaṃ vijñāna-sahitaṃ yaj jñātvā mokṣyase 'śubhāt

**Anvaya:** anasūyave te aham idaṃ guhyatamam pravakṣyāmi; vijñāna-sahitam jñānam jñātvā tu tvam aśubhāt mokṣyase.

**Padārtha (key words):** *anasūyave* (dat. of *anasūyin*, "one free from *asūyā*" — *a-sūyā* = absence of
fault-finding/envy, the receptive mind); *guhyatamam* (superl. of *guhya*, "most secret"); *pravakṣyāmi*
(*pra-√vac*, fut. "I shall declare"); *jñānaṃ vijñāna-sahitam* ("knowledge together with realised
experience"); *mokṣyase* (*√muc*, pass. "you shall be freed"); *aśubhāt* ("from the inauspicious/evil").

**Literal:** To you who are free from fault-finding I shall declare this most secret [teaching] — knowledge
joined with direct realisation, knowing which you shall be freed from all that is inauspicious.

**Śaṅkara:** the qualification *anasūyin* is essential: this knowledge cannot take root in a carping mind.
*Jñāna-vijñāna* = scriptural knowledge plus its direct, experienced realisation (*anubhava*); only the latter
frees. The "most secret" is the knowledge of the Self as identical with Brahman.
**Rāmānuja:** the "secret" is the Lord's sovereign accessibility to the surrendered soul; *vijñāna* is the
lived conviction of God's being the sole means (*upāya*).
**Madhva:** stresses that the knowledge is "most secret" precisely because it concerns the Lord's
transcendent-excellent nature, veiled from the envious.
**Abhinavagupta:** the open, non-envious mind is the only vessel wide enough to hold the recognition.

**Textual note:** none material.
**Cross-refs:** 4.34 (approach a teacher *prasanna*), 7.1–2 (the same "king of knowledge").
**Significance:** sets the disciple's qualification — receptivity — before the revelation. Doctrine is
transmitted to a prepared mind.
**Misreading:** that knowledge *without* realisation (vijñāna) suffices; Śaṅkara is adamant it does not.
**Layers —** W: "Open your mind, and I'll share the deepest thing." / S: knowledge must become direct
experience to liberate. / Sk: examine where *asūyā* (cynicism) blocks reception. / A: the jñāna–vijñāna
distinction as śāstra-vs-anubhava.

## 9.2
**Devanagari:** राजविद्या राजगुह्यं पवित्रमिदमुत्तमम्। प्रत्यक्षावगमं धर्म्यं सुसुखं कर्तुमव्ययम्॥
**IAST:** rāja-vidyā rāja-guhyam pavitram idam uttamam / pratyakṣāvagamaṃ dharmyaṃ su-sukhaṃ kartum avyayam

**Anvaya:** idam rāja-vidyā rāja-guhyam; pavitram; uttamam; pratyakṣa-avagamam; dharmyam; su-sukham kartum;
avyayam.

**Padārtha:** *rāja-vidyā* ("king of sciences"), *rāja-guhyam* ("king of secrets"), *pavitram* ("purifying"),
*uttamam* (superlative, "supreme"), *pratyakṣa-avagamam* ("directly apprehended" — *pratyakṣa* direct
perception + *ava-gam* "descend into/realise"), *dharmyam* ("righteous, in accord with dharma"), *su-sukham*
("very easy"), *kartum* (inf. "to practise"), *avyayam* ("imperishable").

**Literal:** [This is] the king of sciences, the king of secrets, the most purifying, supreme, directly
realised, righteous, very easy to practise, imperishable.

**Śaṅkara:** the verse lists seven attributes of the knowledge of the Self. "Easy to practise" (*su-sukham*)
means that, once known, it requires no external apparatus — it is the ever-present Self. "Directly realised"
contradicts any merely theoretical reading.
**Rāmānuja:** the ease is the ease of *prapatti* — surrender is open to all, unlike arduous ritual/knowledge
disciplines.
**Madhva:** "easy" relative to its supreme fruit; the *dharmya* (lawful) nature ties it to the Lord's will.
**Abhinavagupta:** *pratyakṣa-avagama* — this knowledge is not belief but the very structure of one's
awareness, immediately present.

**Textual note:** none material.
**Cross-refs:** 9.1 (continues), 4.38 (nothing purifies like knowledge), 7.2.
**Significance:** the supreme valuation of this knowledge — purifying, direct, easy, eternal. The "easy" is
theologically charged: Śaṅkara = the Self is effortless; Rāmānuja = surrender is easy.
**Misreading:** "easy" = intellectually lazy. It is easy to *practise*, not shallow.
**Layers —** W: "The highest knowledge is also the most accessible." / S: seven attributes unpacked. / Sk:
what makes something "directly realised" vs merely believed? / A: the Śaṅkara-vs-Rāmānuja reading of
*su-sukham*.

## 9.3
**Devanagari:** अश्रद्दधानाः पुरुषा धर्मस्यास्य परन्तप। अप्राप्य मां निवर्तन्ते मृत्युसंसारवर्त्मनि॥
**IAST:** aśraddadhānāḥ puruṣā dharmasyāsya parantapa / aprāpya māṃ nivartante mṛtyu-saṃsāra-vartmani

**Anvaya:** he parantapa, asya dharmasya aśraddadhānāḥ puruṣāḥ mām aprāpya mṛtyu-saṃsāra-vartmani nivartante.

**Padārtha:** *aśraddadhānāḥ* (*a-śraddadhāna*, "lacking *śraddhā*—faith/trust"); *parantapa* (voc.
"scorcher of enemies," Arjuna); *aprāpya* (absol. "not having attained"); *nivartante* (*ni-√vṛt* "turn
back/return"); *mṛtyu-saṃsāra-vartmani* ("on the path of death-and-rebirth").

**Literal:** Those who lack faith in this dharma, failing to reach Me, return to the path of death and
saṃsāra.

**Śaṅkara:** *śraddhā* is the indispensable antecedent of knowledge — without trust in the scripture and
teacher, inquiry cannot begin, so one remains in transmigration.
**Rāmānuja:** faith here is trust in the Lord as the means; to lack it is to be shut out of surrender.
**Madhva:** faith is the gateway; without it the soul misses the Lord and revolves in saṃsāra.
**Abhinavagupta:** the faithless are those who refuse to recognise what they already are.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 4.39–40 (the faithful attain knowledge; the doubting self-destructs), 17.3 (faith = one's
essence).
**Significance:** the negative counterpart of 9.1–2 — the single door has a single key (*śraddhā*).
**Misreading:** that "faith" means blind belief. Here it is reverent trust that opens inquiry.
**Layers —** W: "Without trust, even the best map won't help you travel." / S: śraddhā as precondition of
jñāna. / Sk: notice where distrust of teaching blocks your progress. / A: śraddhā in the three schools.

## 9.4
**Devanagari:** मया ततमिदं सर्वं जगदव्यक्तमूर्तिना। मत्स्थानि सर्वभूतानि न चाहं तेष्ववस्थितः॥
**IAST:** mayā tatam idaṃ sarvaṃ jagad avyakta-mūrtinā / mat-sthāni sarva-bhūtāni na cāhaṃ teṣv avasthitaḥ

**Anvaya:** avyakta-mūrtinā mayā idaṃ sarvam jagat tatam; sarva-bhūtāni mat-sthāni; aham tu teṣu na
avasthitaḥ.

**Padārtha:** *mayā* (instr. "by Me"), *tatam* (*√tan* p.p.p. "spread/pervaded"), *avyakta-mūrtinā*
("with an unmanifest form"), *mat-sthāni* ("in Me situated"), *avasthitaḥ* (*ava-√sthā* "situated/dwelling").

**Literal:** By Me, in My unmanifest form, this whole universe is pervaded; all beings dwell in Me — yet I do
not dwell in them.

**Śaṅkara:** the classic statement of *avyakṛta-bhāva* — Brahman, without modification, pervades all as their
substance (the cause pervades the effect as clay pervades the pot), yet remains untainted ("not in them" =
not limited/localised by them). This is *apṛthak-siddhi* (non-separate, non-identical coexistence).
**Rāmānuja:** the Lord is the *antar-yāmin* (inner ruler) — He supports all as their material and efficient
cause (the *śarīra-śarīri* relation: beings are His "body"); "not in them" denies being *controlled* by them,
not absence.
**Madhva:** pervasion is real but God remains transcendent, distinct; "not in them" = He is never subject to
their limitations.
**Abhinavagupta:** the one consciousness pervades; "not in them" marks its freedom from the forms it takes.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 7.7 (all strung on Me), 13.13–15 (the all-pervading), 8.22.
**Significance:** establishes the doctrine of *immanence-without-limitation* — the hinge of the whole chapter.
**Misreading:** either crude pantheism ("God = the world") or pure transcendence ("God is elsewhere"). The
Gītā refuses both.
**Layers —** W: "God is everywhere, yet never trapped." / S: pervasion without containment. / Sk: contemplate
the wind-in-space image (9.6) as a felt sense. / A: apṛthak-siddhi vs śarīra-śarīri vs bhedābheda.

## 9.5
**Devanagari:** न च मत्स्थानि भूतानि पश्य मे योगमैश्वरम्। भूतभृन्न च भूतस्थो ममात्मा भूतभावनः॥
**IAST:** na ca mat-sthāni bhūtāni paśya me yogam aiśvaram / bhūta-bhṛn na ca bhūta-stho mamātmā bhūta-bhāvanaḥ

**Anvaya:** ca bhūtāni mat-sthāni na [santi]; me aiśvaram yogam paśya; aham bhūta-bhṛt, na ca bhūta-sthaḥ; mama
ātmā bhūta-bhāvanaḥ.

**Padārtha:** *yogam aiśvaram* ("divine power/yoga"), *bhūta-bhṛt* ("sustainer of beings"), *bhūta-sthaḥ*
("situated *in* beings"), *bhūta-bhāvanaḥ* ("originator/source of beings").

**Literal:** Yet beings do not (really) dwell in Me — behold My divine yoga: I am the sustainer of beings, but
not situated in them; My Self is the source of all beings.

**Śaṅkara:** the paradox deepens: the "in Me" of 9.4 was conventional; in absolute truth beings do not inhere
in Brahman (which is changeless). The *yogam aiśvaram* is the power (*māyā*) by which the cause appears as
effect without being altered.
**Rāmānuja:** resolves the paradox by the *śarīra-śarīri* doctrine — beings inhere as His body (real relation),
and "not in them" means He is their *support* without depending on them.
**Madhva:** God supports and originates all while remaining utterly distinct — "sustainer, not contained."
**Abhinavagupta:** the divine yoga is the freedom of consciousness to project and withdraw the world at will.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 9.4 (immediate), 13.14–15.
**Significance:** the Gītā's subtlest metaphysical move — reconciling "all in Him" with "He is in nothing,"
which each school does differently. This is the verse that most divides the commentators.
**Misreading:** reading the two verses as a flat contradiction. They are a deliberate paradox meant to defeat
one-sided doctrines.
**Layers —** W: "He holds everything without being held." / S: the support-without-containment paradox. / Sk:
sit with the felt impossibility; do not resolve it too fast. / A: the three schools' reconciliation compared.

## 9.6
**Devanagari:** यथाकाशस्थितो नित्यं वायुः सर्वत्रगो महान्। तथा सर्वाणि भूतानि मत्स्थानीत्युपधारय॥
**IAST:** yathā ākāśa-sthito nityaṃ vāyuḥ sarvatrago mahān / tathā sarvāṇi bhūtāni mat-sthānīty upadhāraya

**Anvaya:** yathā mahān sarvatragaḥ vāyuḥ nityam ākāśa-sthitaḥ [sati], tathā sarvāṇi bhūtāni mat-sthāni iti
upadhāraya.

**Padārtha:** *ākāśa-sthitaḥ* ("abiding in space"), *sarvatragaḥ* ("going everywhere"), *mahān* ("great"),
*upadhāraya* (imv. "understand/consider").

**Literal:** As the great, all-moving wind ever abides in space, so understand that all beings abide in Me.

**Śaṅkara:** the wind/space analogy illustrates the *apṛthak-siddhi* of cause and effect — wind is "in" space
yet space is untouched; so the world is "in" Brahman yet Brahman is unmodified.
**Rāmānuja:** the analogy shows real dependence — beings rest in the Lord as their ground — without implying
identity.
**Madhva:** supports pervasion-as-support, not identity.
**Abhinavagupta:** space = the vastness of consciousness; the "winds" = the vital movements that play within
it, never apart from it.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 9.4–5, 13.13.
**Significance:** an intuitive image for the immanence doctrine — the chapter's most teachable metaphor.
**Misreading:** pushing the analogy too far (wind is *material*; Brahman is not).
**Layers —** W: "Like wind moving in the sky — everything moves within Him." / S: cause/effect as wind/space.
/ Sk: use the image in meditation. / A: limits of the analogy (Śaṅkara's caution).

## 9.7
**Devanagari:** सर्वभूतानि कौन्तेय प्रकृतिं यान्ति मामिकाम्। कल्पक्षये पुनस्तानि कल्पादौ विसृजाम्यहम्॥
**IAST:** sarva-bhūtāni kaunteya prakṛtiṃ yānti māmikām / kalpa-kṣaye punas tāni kalpādau visṛjāmy aham

**Anvaya:** kaunteya, kalpa-kṣaye sarva-bhūtāni māmikāṃ prakṛtim yānti; punaḥ kalpa-ādau aham tāni visṛjāmi.

**Padārtha:** *māmikām* ("My own"), *kalpa-kṣaye* ("at the end of a *kalpa*—cosmic day"), *visṛjāmi*
(*vi-√sṛj* "emit/create").

**Literal:** At the end of a kalpa all beings enter My nature; at the dawn of the next I emit them again.

**Śaṅkara:** at cosmic dissolution beings resolve into *prakṛti* in the Lord; at creation He projects them
again — yet He remains the unchanging witness, the *nimitta-kāraṇa*.
**Rāmānuja:** the Lord is the efficient cause who, at dawn of each cycle, fashions beings from prakṛti
according to their past karma — real creation, real dependence.
**Madhva:** stresses the karmic continuity (souls retain their distinctness across cycles).
**Abhinavagupta:** creation/dissolution are the rhythms of consciousness's self-expression.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 8.18–19 (day/night of Brahmā), 14.3–4 (prakṛti the womb).
**Significance:** the cosmic rhythm — the Lord as author of creation and dissolution.
**Misreading:** fatalism; the Gītā elsewhere insists on free effort (this verse describes *process*, not
necessity for the individual).
**Layers —** W: "Worlds come and go; He is the constant." / S: the kalpa cycle. / Sk: place your life on the
cosmic scale to lighten anxiety. / A: nimitta vs upādāna-kāraṇa; karmic continuity.

## 9.8
**Devanagari:** प्रकृतिं स्वामवष्टभ्य विसृजामि पुनः पुनः। भूतग्राममिमं कृत्स्नमवशं प्रकृतेर्वशात्॥
**IAST:** prakṛtiṃ svām avaṣṭabhya visṛjāmi punaḥ punaḥ / bhūta-grāmam imaṃ kṛtsnam avaśaṃ prakṛter vaśāt

**Anvaya:** svām prakṛtim avaṣṭabhya aham imaṃ kṛtsnam bhūta-grāmam punaḥ punaḥ visṛjāmi; [saḥ] prakṛteḥ vaśāt
avaśaḥ.

**Padārtha:** *avaṣṭabhya* (absol. "having taken hold of"), *bhūta-grāmam* ("the multitude of beings"),
*avaśaḥ* ("helpless"), *prakṛteḥ vaśāt* ("by the sway of prakṛti").

**Literal:** Taking hold of My own prakṛti, again and again I project this whole multitude of beings,
helpless under the sway of prakṛti.

**Śaṅkara:** the Lord, *as* the inner ruler, sets prakṛti in motion; beings are "helpless" because they are
governed by guṇas — until knowledge frees them. The Lord is never bound.
**Rāmānuja:** the Lord uses prakṛti as His instrument; He projects souls *into* it according to their karma.
**Madhva:** the souls' dependence (*pāratantrya*) on the Lord's creative will and on prakṛti's modes.
**Abhinavagupta:** prakṛti is the spontaneous outpouring (*spanda*) of consciousness.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 9.7, 3.27 (the guṇas act), 13.21 (the puruṣa experiences the guṇas).
**Significance:** the mechanics of cyclic creation and the soul's subjection to prakṛti.
**Misreading:** that the Lord is the doer of bondage; He is the *overseer* — 9.10 clarifies.
**Layers —** W: "He renews the world; we get caught in its machinery unless we wake up." / S: prakṛti and the
guṇas. / Sk: notice the "helplessness" of being driven by moods. / A: nimitta-kāraṇatva; the Lord's
non-agency (9.9).

## 9.9
**Devanagari:** न च मां तानि कर्माणि निबध्नन्ति धनञ्जय। उदासीनवदासीनमसक्तं तेषु कर्मसु॥
**IAST:** na ca māṃ tāni karmāṇi nibadhnanti dhanañjaya / udāsīna-vad āsīnam asaktaṃ teṣu karmasu

**Anvaya:** dhanañjaya, tāni karmāṇi mām na nibadhnanti; aham teṣu karmasu asaktaṃ udāsīna-vad āsīnam.

**Padārtha:** *nibadhnanti* (*ni-√bandh* "bind"), *udāsīna-vat* ("as if indifferent/neutral"), *āsīnam*
("seated"), *asaktam* ("unattached").

**Literal:** These actions bind Me not, Dhanañjaya — I sit as one indifferent, unattached to those acts.

**Śaṅkara:** though the Lord oversees all action, He is never its *kartā* and so never bound — the model for
the liberated soul (3.28, 5.7).
**Rāmānuja:** the Lord acts only from compassion (līlā), never from need; hence no bondage.
**Madhva:** the Lord's freedom is absolute; His involvement is sovereign play, not karma-bearing.
**Abhinavagupta:** the witness-consciousness (*sākṣin*) remains ever untouched.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 3.28, 5.7–9 (the wise act unbound), 4.14, 13.29–31.
**Significance:** establishes the *akartṛtva* (non-doership) ideal the Gītā urges on Arjuna — "be like Me."
**Misreading:** "indifferent" = callous; it means *unentangled*, not uncaring.
**Layers —** W: "God is in everything but caught by nothing — be like that." / S: non-doership of the witness.
/ Sk: the practice of acting without inner claim. / A: akartṛtva and jīvanmukti.

## 9.10
**Devanagari:** मयाध्यक्षेण प्रकृतिः सूयते सचराचरम्। हेतुनानेन कौन्तेय जगद्विपरिवर्तते॥
**IAST:** mayādhyakṣeṇa prakṛtiḥ sūyate sacarācaram / hetunānena kaunteya jagad viparyavartate

**Anvaya:** mayā adhyakṣeṇa sacarācaram prakṛtiḥ sūyate; kaunteya, anena hetunā jagat viparyavartate.

**Padārtha:** *adhyakṣeṇa* ("with Me as overseer" — *adhi-akṣa*, "over-eye"), *sūyate* (*√sū* pass. "is
brought forth"), *sacarācaram* ("the moving and unmoving"), *hetunā* ("by this cause"), *viparyavartate*
(*vi-pari-√vṛt* "revolves/turns round").

**Literal:** Under My oversight, prakṛti brings forth the moving and unmoving; by this cause, the world
revolves.

**Śaṅkara:** the Lord is the *adhiṣṭhātṛ* (indwelling witness), not the agent; prakṛti does the producing.
This dissolves two errors: an absent God and a God entangled in matter.
**Rāmānuja:** the Lord is the efficient cause; prakṛti, animated by Him, the material cause — the *śarīra-śarīri*
frame.
**Madhva:** the Lord's supervision (*adhyakṣa*) is the sustaining will; prakṛti obeys.
**Abhinavagupta:** the "overseer" is the self-luminous consciousness in whose light prakṛti plays.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 9.4–10 as a unit; 13.22 (puruṣa as witness of the guṇas); 15.15.
**Significance:** the pivot of the immanence section — the bridge between metaphysics (9.4–9) and the human
response (9.11+). Often read as the Gītā's most concise theistic cosmology.
**Misreading:** making prakṛti autonomous (Sāṃkhya drift) or the Lord the direct agent.
**Layers —** W: "The world runs under a silent Witness." / S: the conductor/oversight image. / Sk: sense the
"still centre" behind a process today. / A: Sāṃkhya-dualist vs Vedānta-qualified-dualist vs Dvaita readings
of *adhyakṣa*.

## 9.11
**Devanagari:** अवजानन्ति मां मूढा मानुषीं तनुमाश्रितम्। परं भावमजानन्तो मम भूतमहेश्वरम्॥
**IAST:** avajānanti māṃ mūḍhā mānuṣīṃ tanum āśritam / paraṃ bhāvam ajānanto mama bhūta-maheśvaram

**Anvaya:** mūḍhāḥ mama paraṃ bhāvam ajānantas tu māṃ mānuṣīṃ tanum āśritam avajānanti; [te] mama
bhūta-maheśvaram [api] na jānanti.

**Padārtha:** *avajānanti* (*ava-√jñā* "despise/disregard"), *mūḍhāḥ* ("deluded"), *mānuṣīṃ tanum* ("a human
body"), *paraṃ bhāvam* ("supreme nature"), *bhūta-maheśvaram* ("great Lord of all beings").

**Literal:** Fools, not knowing My supreme nature as the great Lord of all beings, despise Me when I take a
human body.

**Śaṅkara:** applies to the *avatāra* — the Lord, though embodied, is never bound; the deluded see only the
body (the *adhyāsa* that mistakes appearance for reality).
**Rāmānuja:** the *avatāra*'s body is *divya* (divine, not karmic); despising it is to miss the Lord
accessible in human form.
**Madhva:** the Lord's incarnate form is real and sovereign; contempt reveals ignorance of His greatness.
**Abhinavagupta:** the "human body" is every form consciousness takes — to despise any form is to miss the
divinity wearing it.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 4.6–8 (avatāra doctrine), 7.24–25 (the deluded do not know His unborn nature).
**Significance:** the *avatāra*'s concealment-in-form — why divinity is missed when it is nearest.
**Misreading:** that the verse endorses contempt for the body or for other humans; it indicts the contempt of
*the divine appearing as* a body.
**Layers —** W: "Don't dismiss the divine just because it wears an ordinary face." / S: avatāra and adhyāsa.
/ Sk: where do I overlook the sacred because it looks familiar? / A: Śaṅkara's body-as-adhyāsa vs
Rāmānuja's divya-śarīra.

## 9.12
**Devanagari:** मोघाशा मोघकर्माणो मोघज्ञाना विचेतसः। राक्षसीमासुरीं चैव प्रकृतिं मोहिनीं श्रिताः॥
**IAST:** moghāśā mogha-karmāṇo mogha-jñānā vicetasaḥ / rākṣasīm āsurīṃ caiva prakṛtiṃ mohinīṃ śritāḥ

**Anvaya:** vicetasaḥ te moghāśāḥ mogha-karmāṇaḥ mogha-jñānāḥ [santi]; te caiva rākṣasīm āsurīm mohinīm
prakṛtim śritāḥ.

**Padārtha:** *moghāśāḥ* ("vain-hoped"), *mogha-karmāṇaḥ* ("vain-acting"), *mogha-jñānāḥ* ("vain-knowing"),
*vicetasaḥ* ("bewildered-minded"), *rākṣasīm…āsurīm* ("demonic…asuric"), *mohinīm* ("delusive").

**Literal:** [Their] hopes, acts and knowledge are vain; bewildered, they resort to the delusive demoniac and
āsuric nature.

**Śaṅkara:** without knowledge of the Self, all aspiration, action, even "knowledge" is fruitless (*mogha*);
the root is *moha* (delusion).
**Rāmānuja:** to ignore the Lord is to fall into *āsurī prakṛti* (16.4 ff.).
**Madhva:** the *āsuric* temperament (ch. 16) is the destiny of the contemptuous.
**Abhinavagupta:** the "delusive nature" is the contraction of consciousness that forgets its fullness.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 16.4–21 (the āsuric), 7.15 (the deluded who worship prakṛti/elements).
**Significance:** the consequence of 9.11 — blindness begets futility and a darkened nature.
**Misreading:** that *mogha-jñāna* condemns all worldly knowledge; it condemns knowledge severed from the
Self/the Lord.
**Layers —** W: "Without the sacred, even cleverness comes to nothing." / S: āsurī prakṛti. / Sk: audit your
"vain" pursuits. / A: moha as the root; ch. 16 typology.

## 9.13
**Devanagari:** महात्मानस्तु मां पार्थ दैवीं प्रकृतिमाश्रिताः। भजन्त्यनन्यमनसो ज्ञात्वा भूतादिमव्ययम्॥
**IAST:** mahātmānas tu māṃ pārtha daivīṃ prakṛtim āśritāḥ / bhajanty ananya-manaso jñātvā bhūta-ādim avyayam

**Anvaya:** pārtha, tu mahātmānaḥ daivīṃ prakṛtim āśritāḥ bhūta-ādim avyayam jñātvā ananya-manasaḥ māṃ bhajanti.

**Padārtha:** *mahātmānaḥ* ("great-souled"), *daivī…prakṛtiḥ* ("divine nature," opp. of 9.12's āsurī),
*bhajanti* (*√bhaj* "worship/serve/share in"), *ananya-manasaḥ* ("with undivided mind"), *bhūta-ādim*
("source/origin of beings"), *avyayam* ("imperishable").

**Literal:** But the great-souled, grounded in the divine nature, knowing Me as the imperishable source of
all beings, worship Me with undivided mind.

**Śaṅkara:** the *mahātman* is the one who has discerned the Self; *daivī prakṛti* is the sāttvic mind ripe
for *parā-bhakti*. "Undivided mind" = fixed on the one Brahman.
**Rāmānuja:** the devotee's *ananya-bhāva* (exclusive devotion) is the defining mark; *daivī prakṛti* is the
grace-given disposition of surrender.
**Madhva:** the great soul knows the Lord as *sarva-śreṣṭha* (supreme) and worships accordingly.
**Abhinavagupta:** the "divine nature" is the recognition of non-separation from the divine.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 7.16–19 (the wise devotee is rarest), 16.1–3 (daivī sampad).
**Significance:** defines the true devotee — knowledge + exclusive worship.
**Misreading:** that *ananya* means hatred of other paths; it means the heart's settled refuge.
**Layers —** W: "The great turn wholly toward the Divine." / S: daivī vs āsurī prakṛti. / Sk: examine the
undividedness of your own devotion. / A: parā-bhakti as Śaṅkara's term; ananya-bhāva.

## 9.14
**Devanagari:** सततं कीर्तयन्तो मां यतन्तश्च दृढव्रताः। नमस्यन्तश्च मां भक्त्या नित्ययुक्ता उपासते॥
**IAST:** satataṃ kīrtayanto māṃ yatantaś ca dṛḍha-vratāḥ / namasyantaś ca māṃ bhaktyā nitya-yuktā upāsate

**Anvaya:** dṛḍha-vratāḥ satataṃ māṃ kīrtayantaḥ, yatantaḥ ca, bhaktyā māṃ namasyantaḥ ca, nitya-yuktāḥ
[ mām] upāsate.

**Padārtha:** *satatam* ("constantly"), *kīrtayantaḥ* (*kīrt-√* "glorify/chant"), *yatantaḥ* ("striving"),
*dṛḍha-vratāḥ* ("of firm resolve"), *namasyantaḥ* (*namas-√yam* "bow/offer reverence"), *nitya-yuktāḥ*
("ever-united").

**Literal:** Ever chanting My glories, striving with firm resolve, bowing to Me in devotion, they worship Me,
ever united.

**Śaṅkara:** *upāsana* = meditation on the qualified Brahman; the disciplines (chanting, vow, bowing) steady
the mind until knowledge dawns.
**Rāmānuja:** this is the active life of *bhāgavata* devotion — *kīrtana*, *namaskāra*, *upāsana*.
**Madhva:** worship grounded in vows and constant remembrance.
**Abhinavagupta:** chanting (*kīrtana*) as the sound-vibration that re-tunes the mind to its source.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 10.9 (the wise ever chant), 12.8–9.
**Significance:** the *sādhana* of devotion described concretely.
**Misreading:** ritualism — the *bhāva* (devotion) is the substance, the acts are its expression.
**Layers —** W: "Their whole life becomes a continuous remembering." / S: the devotional disciplines. / Sk:
adopt one constant remembrance. / A: upāsana as saguṇa-meditation (Śaṅkara).

## 9.15
**Devanagari:** ज्ञानयज्ञेन चाप्यन्ये यजन्तो मामुपासते। एकत्वेन पृथक्त्वेन बहुधा विश्वतोमुखम्॥
**IAST:** jñāna-yajñena cāpy anye yajanto mām upāsate / ekatvena pṛthaktvena bahudhā viśvato-mukham

**Anvaya:** anye ca jñāna-yajñena māṃ yajantaḥ upāsate; [kecit] ekatvena, [kecit] pṛthaktvena, [kecit]
viśvato-mukham bahudhā [mām upāsate].

**Padārtha:** *jñāna-yajñena* ("by the sacrifice of knowledge"), *ekatvena* ("as the One"),
*pṛthaktvena* ("as the distinct/many"), *bahudhā* ("in many ways"), *viśvato-mukham* ("all-faced/
omni-present").

**Literal:** Others worship Me by the sacrifice of knowledge — some as the One, some as the many, in manifold
ways as the all-faced Universal Being.

**Śaṅkara:** the *jñāna-yajña* is the contemplation of the Self; "as the One" = the undifferentiated Brahman;
"as the many" = the qualified forms — both are legitimate stages, the former supreme.
**Rāmānuja:** the diversity of worship is real and accepted — saguṇa and viśva-rūpa devotion.
**Madhva:** worship varies by the soul's competence; all valid forms reach the one Lord.
**Abhinavagupta:** the "all-faced" is consciousness expressing every face — every form is His.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 7.21–22, 9.23–25 (worship in any form reaches Him), 11 (the viśva-rūpa).
**Significance:** the Gītā's striking pluralism — one truth, many devotional forms. The seed of interfaith
openness.
**Misreading:** that "all worship is the same" flatly (the Gītā still ranks jñāna highest in 7.16–19); it
accepts the forms while orienting them to the One.
**Layers —** W: "Sincere worship, in whatever form, reaches Him." / S: the unity-in-diversity of worship. /
Sk: respect forms unlike your own. / A: Śaṅkara's ranking of ekatva vs pṛthaktva upāsana.

## 9.16–19
**Devanagari (9.16):** अहं क्रतुरहं यज्ञः स्वधाहमहमौषधम्। मन्त्रोऽहमहमेवाज्यमहमग्निरहं हुतम्॥
**IAST (9.16):** ahaṃ kratur ahaṃ yajñaḥ svadhāham aham auṣadham / mantro'ham aham evājyam aham agnir ahaṃ hutam
**9.17–19 continue the "I am…" declarations: father/mother/sustainer, Oṁkāra, the Vedas; goal/support/witness;
the heat, the rain, immortality and death, being and non-being.**

**Anvaya (9.16):** aham kratuḥ, aham yajñaḥ, aham svadhā, aham auṣadham, aham mantraḥ, aham evājyam, aham agniḥ,
aham hutam.

**Padārtha:** *kratuḥ* (Vedic ritual), *yajñaḥ* (sacrifice), *svadhā* (oblation to ancestors), *auṣadham*
(herb/medicine), *mantraḥ* (sacred formula), *ājyam* (clarified butter), *hutam* (the offered oblation).

**Literal (9.16):** I am the rite, the sacrifice, the ancestral offering, the healing herb, the mantra, the
clarified butter, the fire, and the oblation.
**(9.17):** the father, mother, sustainer, grandsire; the knowable, the purifier, Oṁ, the Ṛg/Sāma/Yajur.
**(9.18):** goal, supporter, Lord, witness, abode, refuge, friend; origin, dissolution, ground, the seed.
**(9.19):** I give heat; I withhold and release rain; I am immortality and death, being and non-being.

**Śaṅkara:** these are the *vyūha* / *adhidaivata* identifications — the Lord as the inner Self of every
ritual element and cosmic function, establishing that all worship, knowingly or not, worships Him.
**Rāmānuja:** the exhaustive catalogue proves the Lord is the *material and efficient* cause and the *soul* of
all — the *śarīra-śarīri* universalised.
**Madhva:** the Lord pervades and owns all functions; the soul depends on Him for all this.
**Abhinavagupta:** consciousness is the substance of every symbol — the ritual and the cosmos are its display.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 7.8–12 (the earlier "I am…" list), 10.20–41 (the *vibhūtis*), 10.25 ("I am the mantra").
**Significance:** the *all-inclusive theism* of the Gītā — nothing lies outside the divine. The basis of
9.26 (even a leaf is accepted) and the universalism of 9.23–25.
**Misreading:** literal polytheism (the Gītā identifies the many *with* the One, not as separate gods).
**Layers —** W: "Every sacred thing, and the world's workings, are Him." / S: the adhidaivata identifications.
/ Sk: see one Process behind the many forms. / A: the move from many-gods to one-Brahman-as-all (Vedic→Vedāntic).

## 9.20–21
**Devanagari (9.20, triṣṭubh-ish long lines):** त्रैविद्या मां सोमपाः पूतपापा यज्ञैरिष्ट्वा स्वर्गतिं प्रार्थयन्ते …
**Literal (9.20):** Knowers of the three Vedas, cleansed of sin, worship Me through sacrifices and seek heaven;
attaining merit they enjoy the divine delights of Indra's world. **(9.21):** Having enjoyed that vast heaven,
when merit is exhausted they re-enter the mortal world — thus desire-driven Vedic worshippers gain only the
round of coming and going.

**Padārtha (key):** *traividya* ("versed in the three Vedas"), *somapāḥ* ("drinkers of soma"),
*pūta-pāpāḥ* ("purified of sin"), *svarga-gatim* ("the path to heaven"), *kṣīṇe puṇye* ("when merit is
exhausted"), *gatāgatam* ("coming-and-going").

**Śaṅkara:** a critique of ritual-motivated (kāmya) religion — it yields temporary heaven (*svarga*) but not
*mokṣa*; merit (*puṇya*) is finite, so the soul falls back.
**Rāmānuja:** even legitimate Vedic karma-yoga, if done for fruit, binds one to the cycle; only niṣkāma action
or bhakti liberates.
**Madhva:** the transience of svarga; liberation is the Lord's gift, not ritual's wage.
**Abhinavagupta:** heaven-hunting is still the restless pursuit of objects, never the rest of the Self.

**Textual note:** 9.20–21 are in a longer metre (often cited as slightly irregular).
**Cross-refs:** 2.42–46 (the flowery words of the Vedas promise heaven, not the Beyond), 8.25 (the two paths).
**Significance:** the Gītā's demotion of ritual-heaven religion — the most radical break with Vedic karma-kāṇḍa.
**Misreading:** that the Gītā rejects the Vedas; it rejects *fruit-motivated* ritual, not scripture itself.
**Layers —** W: "Even heaven is temporary; don't trade the eternal for it." / S: kāmya-karma and the finitude
of puṇya. / Sk: audit rewards you chase that won't last. / A: the Vedic→Vedānta shift; karmāṅga vs jñāna.

## 9.22 — THE PROMISE
**Devanagari:** अनन्याश्चिन्तयन्तो मां ये जनाः पर्युपासते। तेषां नित्याभियुक्तानां योगक्षेमं वहाम्यहम्॥
**IAST:** ananyāś cintayanto māṃ ye janāḥ paryupāsate / teṣāṃ nityābhiyuktānāṃ yoga-kṣemaṃ vahāmy aham

**Anvaya:** ye janāḥ ananyāḥ māṃ cintayantaḥ paryupāsate, teṣāṃ nitya-abhiyuktānāṃ aham yoga-kṣemam vahāmi.

**Padārtha:** *ananyāḥ* ("without other [object]"), *cintayantaḥ* (*√cit* "thinking/meditating"),
*paryupāsate* (*pari-upa-√ās* "worship intently"), *nitya-abhiyuktānām* ("ever-united"),
*yoga-kṣemam* (*yoga* = the unattained one acquires; *kṣema* = the attained is preserved), *vahāmi*
(*√vah* "I carry/bear").

**Literal:** Those who, thinking of nothing else, ever worship Me — to those ever-united ones I Myself carry
their yoga and kṣema.

**Śaṅkara:** for the one fixed on the Self, the Lord (as the *antaryāmin*) governs the provision and
preservation of what is needful for the body's sustenance; *yoga-kṣema* concerns material needs, not
spiritual attainment (which is already the Self's nature).
**Rāmānuja:** **the charter-verse of divine providence for the surrendered soul** — God Himself becomes the
devotee's protector; *yoga-kṣema* = He supplies all needs. This is the practical fruit of *prapatti*.
**Madhva:** the Lord, ever gracious, sustains His devotee; the soul's dependence is here answered by God's
care.
**Abhinavagupta:** the one absorbed in the divine finds the divine sustaining the whole of life spontaneously.

**Textual note:** none. Possibly the most-quoted verse of devotion in the entire Gītā.
**Cross-refs:** 9.34, 18.65–66 (the closing promises); 10.10 (I grant the lamp of wisdom).
**Significance:** the emotional core of bhakti — God's personal undertaking for the undivided devotee.
**Misreading:** the "prosperity gospel" — that devotion yields wealth. *Yoga-kṣema* = true welfare, which may
include the hardship that saves. Must be read with 9.30–31 (grace) and 5.22 (sense-joys as sorrow's womb).
**Layers —** W: "Keep your heart on Him, and He carries what you need." / S: yoga vs kṣema; ananya-bhakti. /
Sk: the handover practice. / A: Śaṅkara (material sustenance of the jñānī) vs Rāmānuja (prapatti's fruit).

## 9.23
**Devanagari:** येऽप्यन्यदेवता भक्ता यजन्ते श्रद्धयान्विताः। तेऽपि मामेव कौन्तेय यजन्त्यविधिपूर्वकम्॥
**IAST:** ye'py anya-devatā bhaktā yajante śraddhayānvitāḥ / te'pi mām eva kaunteya yajanty avidhi-pūrvakam

**Anvaya:** kaunteya, ye api anya-devatā-bhaktāḥ śraddhayā anvitāḥ yajante, te'pi mām eva avidhi-pūrvakam
yajanti.

**Padārtha:** *anya-devatā* ("other deities"), *śraddhayā anvitāḥ* ("endowed with faith"),
*avidhi-pūrvakam* ("indirectly, not according to the [correct] rule" — *a-vidhi*).

**Literal:** Even devotees of other deities, worshipping with faith, worship Me — but indirectly.

**Śaṅkara:** all worship, sincerely offered, ultimately reaches the one Brahman (the *adhiṣṭhāna* of all
deities); the difference is only the worshipper's *avidyā* in choosing a partial form.
**Rāmānuja:** the Lord receives all worship as its final enjoyer (9.24), but indirect worship yields only the
*finite* fruits of the deity invoked, not Himself — hence it is "not the right rule" (*avidhi*).
**Madhva:** worship of lesser gods reaches lesser fruits; direct worship of the Supreme alone reaches Him.
**Abhinavagupta:** the one consciousness is the secret recipient of every act of devotion.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 7.21–23 (those who worship other gods reach them, then fall back), 9.24–25, 4.11.
**Significance:** **the Gītā's universalism** — the theological basis for interfaith respect. Crucial to this
book's premise.
**Misreading:** "all religions are identical." The Gītā accepts sincere worship of *any* form as reaching the
One, while still distinguishing the fruit of knowing the Supreme directly. It is inclusivist, not flatly
pluralist.
**Layers —** W: "Sincerity, not the correct name, reaches the Divine." / S: avidhi-pūrvaka; adhiṣṭhāna. /
Sk: honour worship unlike your own. / A: Śaṅkara (One beneath all) vs Rāmānuja (right-rule vs indirect).

## 9.24
**Devanagari:** अहं हि सर्वयज्ञानां भोक्ता च प्रभुरेव च। न तु मामभिजानन्ति तत्त्वेनातश्च्यवन्ति ते॥
**IAST:** ahaṃ hi sarva-yajñānāṃ bhoktā ca prabhur eva ca / na tu mām abhijānanti tattvenātas cyavanti te

**Anvaya:** aham hi sarva-yajñānām bhoktā ca prabhuḥ eva ca; te tu mām tattvena na abhijānanti; ataḥ cyavanti.

**Padārtha:** *bhoktā* ("enjoyer/recipient"), *prabhuḥ* ("Lord/master"), *tattvena* ("in truth"),
*cyavanti* (*√cyu* "fall/slip away").

**Literal:** I alone am the enjoyer and Lord of all sacrifices; not knowing Me in truth, they fall.

**Śaṅkara:** the one Self is the true recipient of every act — ritual that ignores this is rooted in *avidyā*
and so "falls" (returns to saṃsāra).
**Rāmānuja:** because the Lord is the true enjoyer, only worship offered *to Him* is properly directed;
indirect worship misses the Supreme fruit.
**Madhva:** the Lord's sovereignty over all worship; ignorance of it forfeits liberation.
**Abhinavagupta:** to miss the Enjoyer (consciousness) behind every act is to remain bound.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 9.23, 9.27, 5.29.
**Significance:** grounds 9.23 — *why* all worship reaches Him (He is its true Enjoyer).
**Misreading:** that "fall" damns the worshipper; it means falling short of *mokṣa*.
**Layers —** W: "Every offering, anywhere, is received by the One." / S: the Lord as bhoktā. / Sk: offer your
actions to the One behind the act. / A: tattva-jñāna as the difference that makes the act liberating.

## 9.25
**Devanagari:** यान्ति देवव्रता देवान् पितृ़न्यान्ति पितृव्रताः। भूतानि यान्ति भूतेज्या यान्ति मद्याजिनोऽपि माम्॥
**IAST:** yānti deva-vratā devān pitṝn yānti pitṛ-vratāḥ / bhūtāni yānti bhūtejyā yānti mad-yājino'pi mām

**Anvaya:** deva-vratāḥ devān yānti; pitṛ-vratāḥ pitṝn yānti; bhūta-ijyāḥ bhūtāni yānti; mad-yājinaḥ api
mām yānti.

**Padārtha:** *deva-vratāḥ* ("vowed to the gods"), *pitṝn* ("the manes/ancestors"), *bhūtejyāḥ*
("worshippers of spirits/bhūtas"), *mad-yājinaḥ* ("My worshippers").

**Literal:** Worshippers of the gods go to the gods; of the ancestors, to the ancestors; of the spirits, to
the spirits; My worshippers come to Me.

**Śaṅkara:** *yaḥ yaḥ evaṃ bhāvam bhajati* (cf. 8.6) — you reach what your mind is fixed upon; only fixation on
the Supreme (the Self) yields the Supreme.
**Rāmānuja:** the law of correspondence — the *object* of devotion determines the *destination*; hence direct
devotion to the Lord is uniquely salvific.
**Madhva:** the lesser goals are real but finite; the Supreme goal is the Lord alone.
**Abhinavagupta:** consciousness gravitates to the form it most loves — choose the Highest.

**Textual note:** *pitṝn* uses the long-ṛ in some editions; nothing doctrinal.
**Cross-refs:** 8.5–8 (the last thought determines the path), 7.23.
**Significance:** the ethical-psychological law: we become what we worship. The missionary logic of bhakti.
**Misreading:** that other deities are "evil"; they are real but not the Supreme goal.
**Layers —** W: "You reach what you truly love." / S: the law of bhāva-gravitation. / Sk: examine the object
of your deepest attachment. / A: 8.6 (last thought) and the two-paths cosmology.

## 9.26
**Devanagari:** पत्रं पुष्पं फलं तोयं यो मे भक्त्या प्रयच्छति। तदहं भक्त्युपहृतमश्नामि प्रयतात्मनः॥
**IAST:** patraṃ puṣpaṃ phalaṃ toyaṃ yo me bhaktyā prayacchati / tad ahaṃ bhakty-upahṛtam aśnāmi prayatātmanaḥ

**Anvaya:** yaḥ me bhaktyā patram puṣpam phalam toyam [vā] prayacchati, tat prayatātmanaḥ bhakty-upahṛtam
aham aśnāmi.

**Padārtha:** *patram* (leaf), *puṣpam* (flower), *phalam* (fruit), *toyam* (water), *prayacchati*
(*pra-√yam* "offers"), *bhakty-upahṛtam* ("offered with devotion"), *aśnāmi* (*√aś* "eat/accept"),
*prayatātmanaḥ* ("of a pure/concentrated self").

**Literal:** Whoever offers Me a leaf, a flower, a fruit, or water with devotion — that devoted offering, from
a pure-hearted one, I accept.

**Śaṅkara:** the worth of an offering lies wholly in the *bhakti* (the one-pointed mind); the *sāttvic* purity
makes even the smallest act reach Brahman.
**Rāmānuja:** the great democratising verse — *prapatti* needs no wealth, only love; God accepts the smallest
gift of the sincere.
**Madhva:** the Lord's condescension (*saulabhya*) — He is pleased by devotion, not cost.
**Abhinavagupta:** the leaf/flower/fruit/water are metaphors for whatever the heart offers in purity.

**Textual note:** none. Among the most loved verses in the Gītā.
**Cross-refs:** 9.27 (offer *all* your acts), 12.8–11.
**Significance:** ritual economy overturned — devotion, not opulence, is the currency. The theological root of
*prasāda*.
**Misreading:** literalism (only these four items!); they stand for *any* sincere offering.
**Layers —** W: "A leaf, given with love, is enough." / S: bhakti vs the cost of the gift. / Sk: the leaf
practice (offer one small thing). / A: sāttvic-antarkaraṇa as the real offering (Śaṅkara).

## 9.27
**Devanagari:** यत्करोषि यदश्नासि यज्जुहोषि ददासि यत्। यत्तपस्यसि कौन्तेय तत्कुरुष्व मदर्पणम्॥
**IAST:** yat karoṣi yad aśnāsi yaj juhoṣi dadāsi yat / yat tapasyasi kaunteya tat kuruṣva mad-arpaṇam

**Anvaya:** kaunteya, yat yat karoṣi aśnāsi vā juhoṣi vā dadāsi vā tapasyasi vā, tat tat mad-arpaṇam kuruṣva.

**Padārtha:** *karoṣi* (*√kṛ* "do"), *aśnāsi* (*√aś* "eat"), *juhoṣi* (*hu-√ju* "offer/sacrifice"),
*dadāsi* (*√dā* "give"), *tapasyasi* (*√tap* "practise austerity"), *mad-arpaṇam* ("as an offering to Me",
*arpayati* "to fix upon/surrender").

**Literal:** Whatever you do, eat, offer, give, or practise as austerity — do it as an offering to Me.

**Śaṅkara:** the *kartavya-karma* consecrated to the Lord becomes *bhāvana* — the act, done as worship,
purifies the mind (*citta-śuddhi*) leading to knowledge.
**Rāmānuja:** *karma-yoga as bhakti* — every act offered to the Lord is freed from bondage and becomes worship.
**Madhva:** *arpāṇa* (dedication) makes all of life an act of devotion.
**Abhinavagupta:** to "offer" an act is to release the ego-claim on it — recognition of the one Doer.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 3.30 (renounce all actions in Me), 9.26, 9.28.
**Significance:** the operational formula of karma-yoga — how 9.26's "leaf" generalises to all of life.
**Misreading:** mechanical dedication without inner surrender; the *bhāva* is the offering.
**Layers —** W: "Make your whole life an offering." / S: karma-yoga = arpaṇa-bhāva. / Sk: dedicate one routine
act daily. / A: Śaṅkara's bhāvanā as citta-śuddhi for jñāna.

## 9.28
**Devanagari:** शुभाशुभफलैरेवं मोक्ष्यसे कर्मबन्धनैः। संन्यासयोगयुक्तात्मा विमुक्तो मामुपैष्यसि॥
**IAST:** śubhāśubha-phalair evaṃ mokṣyase karma-bandhanaiḥ / saṃnyāsa-yoga-yuktātmā vimukto mām upaiṣyasi

**Anvaya:** evaṃ śubha-aśubha-phalaiḥ karma-bandhanaiḥ mokṣyase; saṃnyāsa-yoga-yuktātmā vimuktaḥ mām upaiṣyasi.

**Padārtha:** *śubha-aśubha-phalaiḥ* ("by the fruits of good and bad [action]"), *karma-bandhanaiḥ*
("the bonds of action"), *saṃnyāsa-yoga-yuktātmā* ("the self united to the yoga of renunciation"),
*vimuktaḥ* ("freed"), *upaiṣyasi* (*upa-√aiṣ* "you shall attain/reach").

**Literal:** Thus you shall be freed from the bonds of action, good and bad; the self united to the yoga of
renunciation, freed, you shall attain Me.

**Śaṅkara:** *saṃnyāsa-yoga* = the knowledge that "I am not the doer"; this burns the seeds of past karma and
yields the Self (= Brahman).
**Rāmānuja:** renunciation of the *fruit* (tyāga) united with *bhakti-yoga* frees the soul and unites it with
the Lord.
**Madhva:** the Lord's grace, met by the devotee's disciplined surrender, releases from karma.
**Abhinavagupta:** freedom is recognising that the Doer was always the one consciousness.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 9.27 (the method), 18.10–12 (tyāga), 4.37 (knowledge's fire).
**Significance:** the result of 9.26–27 — liberation through consecrated action.
**Misreading:** that *both* good and bad karma are destroyed *automatically*; the condition is the yoked,
surrendered self.
**Layers —** W: "Offer your acts, and they stop binding you." / S: śubha-aśubha karma-bandhana. / Sk: feel the
lightness of non-claiming. / A: saṃnyāsa-yoga = jñāna-niṣṭhā (Śaṅkara) vs tyāga-bhakti (Rāmānuja).

## 9.29
**Devanagari:** समोऽहं सर्वभूतेषु न मे द्वेष्योऽस्ति न प्रियः। ये भजन्ति तु मां भक्त्या मयि ते तेषु चाप्यहम्॥
**IAST:** samo'haṃ sarva-bhūteṣu na me dveṣyo'sti na priyaḥ / ye bhajanti tu māṃ bhaktyā mayi te teṣu cāpy aham

**Anvaya:** aham sarva-bhūteṣu samaḥ; me na dveṣyaḥ asti na priyaḥ; ye tu māṃ bhaktyā bhajanti, te mayi, teṣu
ca api aham.

**Padārtha:** *samaḥ* ("equal/same"), *dveṣyaḥ* ("hated"), *priyaḥ* ("dear/beloved"),
*mayi…te / teṣu…aham* ("they in Me, and I in them").

**Literal:** I am the same to all beings; none is hateful to Me, none dear. But those who worship Me with
devotion — they are in Me, and I in them.

**Śaṅkara:** Brahman is impartial — It is the Self equally of all; the "devotion" simply removes the *avidyā*
hiding this ever-present identity, so the devotee "abides in" what was always the case.
**Rāmānuja:** the Lord's essential equality (no favourites by birth or merit) coexists with a special intimacy
toward the devotee — the *sāloka/sāmīpya* of surrender.
**Madhva:** God is just to all; the devotee earns, by grace-given devotion, a closer relation — but never
identity.
**Abhinavagupta:** the equality is the truth; the "in Me/I in them" is the devotee's recognition of it.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 5.18–19 (the sage sees the same Self in all), 6.9, 13.28.
**Significance:** the Gītā's deepest basis for equality and non-violence — God's impartiality.
**Misreading:** that "none dear" means indifference; it means *impartial*, and the devotion awakens a real
intimacy that was always available.
**Layers —** W: "God plays no favourites — but turns toward whoever turns to Him." / S: sama-bhāva vs
bhakta-priyatva. / Sk: extend equal regard to one "unloved" person. / A: Śaṅkara (identity unveiled) vs
Rāmānuja (essential equality + acquired intimacy).

## 9.30–31 — GRACE FOR THE FALLEN
**Devanagari (9.30):** अपि चेत्सुदुराचारो भजते मामनन्यभाक्। साधुरेव स मन्तव्यः सम्यग्व्यवसितो हि सः॥
**(9.31):** क्षिप्रं भवति धर्मात्मा शश्वच्छान्तिं निगच्छति। कौन्तेय प्रति जानीहि न मे भक्तः प्रणश्यति॥
**IAST (9.30):** api cet su-durācāro bhajate mām ananya-bhāk / sādhur eva sa mantavyaḥ samyag vyavasito hi saḥ
**(9.31):** kṣipraṃ bhavati dharmātmā śaśvac-chāntiṃ nigacchati / kaunteya prati jānīhi na me bhaktaḥ praṇaśyati

**Anvaya (9.30):** api cet su-durācāraḥ ananya-bhāk māṃ bhajate, saḥ sādhur eva mantavyaḥ; hi saḥ samyak
vyavasitaḥ. **(9.31):** saḥ kṣipram dharmātmā bhavati, śaśvac-chāntim nigacchati; kaunteya, [etat] prati
jānīhi — me bhaktaḥ na praṇaśyati.

**Padārtha:** *su-durācāraḥ* ("of very evil conduct"), *ananya-bhāk* ("of undivided devotion"),
*sādhur eva mantavyaḥ* ("is to be regarded as righteous"), *samyak vyavasitaḥ* ("rightly resolved"),
*kṣipram* ("quickly"), *dharmātmā* ("righteous-souled"), *śaśvac-chāntim* ("everlasting peace"),
*praṇaśyati* (*pra-√naś* "perish/is lost").

**Literal (9.30):** Even one of very evil conduct, who worships Me with undivided devotion, is to be regarded
as righteous — for he is rightly resolved. **(9.31):** He quickly becomes righteous and attains eternal peace;
know this for certain, Arjuna: My devotee is never lost.

**Śaṅkara:** sincere devotion (*ananya-bhakti*) is itself a mark of emerging right knowledge — the
*sattvic* resolve that, once turned Godward, purifies conduct "quickly"; grace and effort are partners
(devotion kindles the knowledge that transforms).
**Rāmānuja:** **the supreme charter of *prapatti*** — the Lord's unconditional acceptance of even the
fallen who surrender; salvation by grace, the path for those who cannot climb by knowledge or works.
**Madhva:** the Lord's grace can lift even the worst — yet the soul remains ever dependent and the
transformation is the Lord's gift, not the soul's achievement.
**Abhinavagupta:** the turning itself is the first tremor of recognition; the "evil conduct" cannot survive
the reorientation of the heart.

**Textual note:** none. The moral/emotional climax of the chapter.
**Cross-refs:** 9.22 (the promise), 18.66 (the final surrender), 9.34; the *prapatti* of Rāmānuja's *Śaraṇāgati
Gadya*.
**Significance:** **the Gītā's gospel of grace** — no one is beyond redemption. The basis for the book's
manifesto ("you are never too far gone").
**Misreading:** (1) antinomianism — "devotion excuses continued evil"; the verse says he *becomes* righteous
(9.31), i.e. grace begins transformation, not licenses sin. (2) "faith alone, no works ever" — the Gītā keeps
the life of dharma as the fruit of grace (cf. 3/4/5).
**Layers —** W: "You are not your worst day — turn, and you are received." / S: ananya-bhakti and the
grace/effort partnership. / Sk: the prodigal practice (offer your shame). / A: Śaṅkara (devotion =
emerging jñāna) vs Rāmānuja (*prapatti* charter) vs Madhva (grace that preserves dependence).

## 9.32
**Devanagari:** मां हि पार्थ व्यपाश्रित्य येऽपि स्युः पापयोनयः। स्त्रियो वैश्यास्तथा शूद्रास्तेऽपि यान्ति परां गतिम्॥
**IAST:** māṃ hi pārtha vyapāśritya ye'pi syuḥ pāpa-yonayaḥ / striyo vaiśyās tathā śūdrās te'pi yānti parāṃ gatim

**Anvaya:** pārtha, ye api pāpa-yonayaḥ syuḥ — striyaḥ, vaiśyāḥ, tathā śūdrāḥ — te'pi māṃ vyapāśritya parāṃ
gatim yānti.

**Padārtha:** *vyapāśritya* (absol. "taking refuge in"), *pāpa-yonayaḥ* ("of sinful/low birth"),
*striyaḥ* (women), *vaiśyāḥ/śūdrāḥ* (the two lower varṇas), *parāṃ gatim* ("the supreme goal").

**Literal:** Taking refuge in Me, even those of disadvantaged birth — women, vaiśyas, śūdras too — attain the
supreme goal.

**Śaṅkara:** the Self is equally available to all; no birth excludes the knowledge that liberates.
**Rāmānuja:** **the radical inclusivism of *prapatti*** — surrender is open to all, irrespective of birth or
gender (a striking claim for its time, and the basis of later Śrī-Vaiṣṇava egalitarianism).
**Madhva:** the Lord's grace is impartial; the soul's worth is its devotion, not its caste.
**Abhinavagupta:** consciousness has no caste or gender.

**Textual note:** the social categories reflect the Gītā's era — see file 11 for the caste handling.
**Cross-refs:** 5.18 (equal vision), 9.29, 6.9.
**Significance:** **the strongest egalitarian verse in the Gītā** — liberation is not gated by birth, gender,
or class. Must be read alongside 4.13 (handle honestly in file 11).
**Misreading:** that listing these groups *as* "sinful birth" endorses their inferiority; the verse's force is
*despite* the era's hierarchy, the Supreme is open to them — an inclusive move against the grain of its time.
**Layers —** W: "No one is barred from the highest." / S: the inclusive logic against caste hierarchy. / Sk:
release any sense of spiritual inferiority. / A: Rāmānuja's use of this in Śrī-Vaiṣṇava prapatti for all; the
caste question (file 11).

## 9.33
**Devanagari:** किं पुनर्ब्राह्मणाः पुण्या भक्ता राजर्षयस्तथा। अनित्यमसुखं लोकमिमं प्राप्य भजस्व माम्॥
**IAST:** kiṃ punar brāhmaṇāḥ puṇyā bhaktā rājarṣayas tathā / anityam asukhaṃ lokam imaṃ prāpya bhajasva mām

**Anvaya:** kim punar? tathā bhaktāḥ puṇyāḥ brāhmaṇāḥ rājarṣayaś ca [mām prāpnuvanti]; tvam anityam asukham
imam lokam prāpya māṃ bhajasva.

**Padārtha:** *kiṃ punar* ("how much more then"), *puṇyāḥ* ("pure/meritorious"), *rājarṣayaḥ*
("royal sages"), *anityam* ("transient"), *asukham* ("joyless"), *bhajasva* (imv. "worship").

**Literal:** How much more, then, the pure brāhmaṇas and royal sages who are devoted! Having come to this
transient, joyless world — worship Me.

**Śaṅkara:** if even the disadvantaged attain the Self, surely the learned and noble can; *anitya-asukha-loka*
motivates the turn inward to the eternal.
**Rāmānuja:** a fortiori argument — surrender is effortless for all; therefore you, Arjuna, a noble, worship.
**Madhva:** the call to a worthy soul to take the path open even to the lowly.
**Abhinavagupta:** the world's transience is the spur to recognise the changeless.

**Textual note:** none.
**Cross-refs:** 8.15 (the world is a place of suffering), 9.32 (a fortiori).
**Significance:** rhetorical climax of the inclusivism + a direct call to Arjuna (and the reader).
**Misreading:** that it reverses 9.32 (brāhmaṇas "better"); it says the path is *especially* open to the
advantaged — not that the disadvantaged are excluded.
**Layers —** W: "If it's open to all, it's open to you — so begin." / S: a-fortiori rhetoric; anitya-loka. /
Sk: use life's unsatisfactoriness as fuel. / A: the rhetorical structure; the call (anuśāsana) to the reader.

## 9.34
**Devanagari:** मन्मना भव मद्भक्तो मद्याजी मां नमस्कुरु। मामेवैष्यसि युक्त्वैवमात्मानं मत्परायणः॥
**IAST:** man-manā bhava mad-bhakto mad-yājī māṃ namaskuru / mām evaiṣyasi yuktvāivam ātmānaṃ mat-parāyaṇaḥ

**Anvaya:** mat-manāḥ bhava; mad-bhaktaḥ bhava; mad-yājī bhava; māṃ namaskuru; evam ātmānam mat-parāyaṇaḥ
yuktvā mām eva eṣyasi.

**Padārtha:** *mat-manāḥ* ("with mind on Me"), *mad-bhaktaḥ* ("My devotee"), *mad-yājī* ("My worshipper"),
*namaskuru* (*namas-√kṛ* "bow"), *mat-parāyaṇaḥ* ("devoted/surrendered to Me"),
*yuktvā* (*√yuj* "having yoked/united").

**Literal:** Fix your mind on Me, be My devotee, My worshipper, bow to Me; thus uniting your self to Me,
devoted to Me, you shall come to Me.

**Śaṅkara:** the fourfold sādhana (*smaraṇa*, *bhakti*, *yajana*, *namaskāra*) steadies the mind on the
qualified Brahman until it ripens into the knowledge of the unqualified Self.
**Rāmānuja:** **the fourfold formula of *bhakti-yoga***; its expanded form returns as 18.65 — the Gītā's final
imperative. The fruit (*mām evaiṣyasi*) is union with the Lord.
**Madhva:** the disciplines of devotion that, by grace, attain the Lord.
**Abhinavagupta:** remember, love, offer, surrender — the natural movements of the heart returning to its
source.

**Textual note:** none. The closing imperative of the chapter; recurs as 18.65 (the Gītā's penultimate verse).
**Cross-refs:** 18.65 (verbatim expansion), 9.22, 9.30–31, 12.8.
**Significance:** the distilled *sādhana* of the chapter — memory, love, offering, surrender — and its fruit.
**Misreading:** sectarianism ("worship *this* form only"); the formula is universal bhakti, the form a
concentration-point.
**Layers —** W: "Think of Me, love Me, offer to Me, bow — you will reach Me." / S: the fourfold bhakti yoga.
/ Sk: the four-breath practice. / A: Śaṅkara (saguṇa sādhana → nirguṇa jñāna) vs Rāmānuja (bhakti-yoga's
charter; = 18.65).
