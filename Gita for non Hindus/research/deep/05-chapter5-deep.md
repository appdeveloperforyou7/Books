# Deep Commentary — Chapter 5: Karma-Sannyāsa Yoga (Renunciation)

> *The resolution of karma-yoga vs sannyāsa. Verified (IITK). Landmarks: 5.10, 5.18, 5.22–23, 5.29.*

**At a glance.** Arjuna asks (5.1): which is better — renouncing action or karma-yoga? Kṛṣṇa says **both
lead to the same goal** but karma-yoga excels (5.2); they are **one** (5.4–5). The chapter then describes the
*akartṛ-bhāvanā* (the non-doer attitude, 5.7–9), the lotus-leaf image (5.10), the sage's **equal vision**
(5.18–20), the counsel against sense-pleasures (5.22–23), the meditation technique (5.27–28), and closes with
the Lord as *suhṛtam* (friend of all beings, 5.29).

---

## 5.1–5.6 — Both paths, one goal
**5.2:** *saṃnyāsaḥ karmayogaśca niḥśreyasakarāv ubhau* — "both renunciation and karma-yoga lead to the
highest good; but of the two, karma-yoga excels (*viśiṣyate*)." **5.4:** *sāṃkhya-yogau pṛthag bālāḥ
pravadanti na paṇḍitāḥ* — "only the childish call knowledge and action distinct; resolute in either, one
gains the fruit of **both**." **5.5:** the state reached by jñāna is reached by yoga too — "he who sees them
as one, **truly sees**." **5.6:** renunciation is hard to attain *without* yoga (*ayogataḥ*); the yoga-yoked
sage reaches Brahman quickly.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the two *niṣṭhās* are one in result — both purify the mind for knowledge;
karma-yoga is "better" because it is suitable for most (the householder), while pure *sannyāsa* without yoga
is impossible. Rāmānuja: both are *upāyas* (means) to the same Lord; their unity is in their common fruit.
Madhva: jñāna (knowledge of the Lord) must precede and accompany both.
**Misreading:** that 5.4 means all paths are **identical** (the Gītā says they reach the **same goal**, not
that they are the same method). Also: that karma-yoga is "easier" (it is more **practical**, not easier).
**Layers —** W: "Both the contemplative and the active path reach the same summit — but act, don't just
withdraw." / S: the unity of jñāna and karma. / Sk: which path suits your temperament? / A: Śaṅkara
(citta-śuddhi unity) vs Rāmānuja (common fruit).

## 5.7–5.16 — The non-doer: acting untainted
**5.7:** the yogī, purified, self-mastered, "the Self of all beings" (*sarva-bhūtātma-bhūtātmā*) — acts yet
is **untainted** (*kurvan na lipyate*). **5.8–9:** "I do nothing at all" (*naiva kiñcit karomīti*) — the
knower thinks this even while seeing, hearing, touching, eating, breathing — because "the senses move among
their objects" (*indriyāṇīndriyārtheṣu vartante*). **5.10 — LANDMARK:** *brahmaṇy ādhāya karmāṇi* — "one who
acts, surrendering works to Brahman and renouncing attachment, is untouched by sin — **as a lotus leaf by
water** (*padmapatram ivāmbhasā*)." **5.13:** the self-controlled one in the nine-gated body rests
content — "neither doing nor causing" (*naiva kurvan na kārayan*). **5.14–15:** the Lord creates neither
doership nor actions nor the fruit-binding — *svabhāvas tu pravartate* (nature alone brings these about); the
Lord assumes no one's sin or merit — ignorance veils knowledge.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the lotus-leaf image (5.10) captures the essence — the leaf **is in** the water but
**untouched** by it; so the yogī acts **in** the world but is **unstained** by attachment. 5.8–9 is the
**lived experience** of akartṛtva (non-doership). 5.14: the Lord as *adhiṣṭhātṛ* (overseer) who ordains the
framework but neither causes nor is tainted. Rāmānuja: "surrendering to Brahman" = to the Lord; the lotus is
untouched because offered. Madhva: the Lord's non-agency is real; the soul depends.
**Cross-refs:** 3.27 (guṇas act), 4.14 (actions taint Me not), 9.9, 13.14–15.
**Layers —** W: "Act in the world like a lotus leaf in water — in it, but untouched by it." / S:
akartṛ-bhāvanā; the lotus-leaf. / Sk: act without claiming; be in the world but not of it. / A: Śaṅkara
(lived akartṛtva) vs Rāmānuja (īśvara-arpaṇa).

## 5.17–5.21 — Equal vision and Brahman-bliss
**5.17:** those whose thought and self are fixed on That — their sins washed by knowledge — go to **no
return** (*apunarāvṛtti*). **5.18 — LANDMARK:** *vidyā-vinaya-saṃpanne brāhmaṇe gavi hastini / śuni caiva
śvapāke ca paṇḍitāḥ samadarśinaḥ* — "the wise see the **same Self** in a learned brahmin, a cow, an
elephant, a dog — and an outcaste." **5.19:** even here (*ihaiva*) creation is conquered by those whose minds
rest in **evenness** (*sāmye*); Brahman is flawless and equal — they abide in It. **5.20:** the steady,
undeluded knower of Brahman neither gladdens at the pleasant nor grieves at the unpleasant.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 5.18 is the Gītā's **deepest basis for equality** — the Self is **identically
present** in all; *samadarśin* (equal-seeing) is not sentiment but the **metaphysical fact** of one Self.
Rāmānuja: the Lord is the *antar-yāmin* (inner ruler) equally in all — equal vision sees Him. Madhva: the
Self is in all, but God is above; equal vision respects the soul's presence without erasing the soul-God
distinction. Abhinavagupta: *sāmya* is the recognition that all differentiation is the play of one
consciousness.
**Cross-refs:** 6.9, 13.28, 14.24–25, 18.20.
**Misreading:** that equal vision means all beings are **equally virtuous** or **interchangeable**; it means
the **Self** is the same — ethical discernment remains (16.1–24).
**Layers —** W: "The wise see the same spark in everyone — the scholar, the animal, the outcaste." / S:
samadarśin; the metaphysical basis of equality. / Sk: extend equal regard to one "unloved." / A: Śaṅkara
(identity of Self) vs Rāmānuja (the Lord within all) vs Madhva (soul present, God distinct).

## 5.22–5.26 — Sense-pleasures as sorrow's womb; Brahman-nirvāṇa
**5.22 — LANDMARK:** *ye hi saṃsparśajā bhogā duḥkhayonaya eva te* — "sense-born pleasures are wombs of
sorrow (*duḥkhayonaya*); they have beginning and end — the wise delight not in them." **5.23:** whoever can
withstand the urge (*vegam*) born of lust and anger **even before death** — "that disciplined one is happy."
**5.24–26:** the yogī whose happiness and refuge are **within** (*antaḥsukhaḥ*), the inner light
(*antar-jyotiḥ*) — becomes Brahman and attains *brahma-nirvāṇa*. The seers, sins exhausted, doubts cut,
devoted to all beings' welfare — attain nirvāṇa.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 5.22 is the Gītā's negative phenomenology of pleasure — every sense-pleasure is
*duḥkha-yoni* (sorrow-womb) because (a) it is transient, (b) craving for it is endless (*duṣpūreṇa*), (c) its
loss is pain. Only the ātma-ānanda (Self-bliss, 5.24) is imperishable. Rāmānuja: *brahma-nirvāṇa* = union
with the Lord in bliss. Madhva: the Lord's bliss surpasses sensory pleasure.
**Layers —** W: "Sense-pleasures breed sorrow because they end; true joy is found within." / S:
duḥkhayonaya; antaḥsukha. / Sk: trace one pleasure to its sorrow-tail. / A: Śaṅkara's
duḥkha-phenomenology; brahma-nirvāṇa across schools.

## 5.27–5.29 — Meditation technique and the Lord as friend
**5.27–28:** the meditation — closing off outer contacts, gaze between brows, breath equalised — the sage of
controlled senses, free from desire/fear/anger, is **ever free** (*sadā mukta eva*). **5.29 — CLOSING:**
*bhoktāraṃ yajña-tapasāṃ sarvalokamahēśvaram / suhṛdaṃ sarvabhūtānāṃ jñātvā māṃ śāntim ṛcchati* — "knowing
Me as the enjoyer of sacrifice, the Lord of all worlds, and the **friend** (*suhṛdam*) of all beings — one
attains peace."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 5.29's *suhṛdam* (friend/well-wisher of all) is remarkable — the Supreme is not a
distant judge but the **nearest friend** of every being, wishing their good. Knowing this dissolves fear and
brings peace. Rāmānuja: the devotee who knows the Lord as *suhṛt* attains *prāpti* (attainment).
**Layers —** W: "The Lord is the friend of all beings — know this and find peace." / S: suhṛdam; the
friendship of the Divine. / Sk: meditate knowing God is your friend, not your judge. / A: Śaṅkara on suhṛdam
as the Self's benevolence.
