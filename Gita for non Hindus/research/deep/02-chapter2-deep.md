# Deep Commentary — Chapter 2: Sāṃkhya Yoga (The Yoga of Knowledge)

> *The doctrinal heart of the Gītā — compressed. Sanskrit verified against IITK Supersite. Not for the reader
> verbatim; the source from which Chapter 2's layers are authored.*

**Chapter at a glance.** Chapter 2 is the Gītā in microcosm: Kṛṣṇa first rebukes Arjuna's faintness (2.1–3),
then delivers the **ātman / immortality** teaching (2.11–30), the **karma-yoga** teaching (2.31–53), and the
portrait of the **sthitaprajña** (2.54–72) — the person of steady wisdom. If the reader reads one chapter,
this is it. The signature verse 2.47 ("you have a right to action alone") sits at the centre.

---

## 2.1–2.10 — From collapse to surrender
Arjuna sits silent; Kṛṣṇa rebukes him ("whence this *kaśmala*, this faintness? it is *anārya*, unworthy of
the noble," 2.2–3). Arjuna's response (2.4–8) deepens into the fullest statement of his crisis: he names the
guru-figures he cannot slay (2.4–5), confesses confusion ("*kārpaṇya-doṣa*" — the fault of *kṛpaṇatā*,
cravenness, has gripped his nature, 2.7), and **makes his disciple-surrender**: *"śiṣyas te'ham śādhi māṃ
tvāṃ prapannam"* — "I am Your disciple; instruct me, who has taken refuge in You" (2.7). This is the
turning-point of the entire Gītā: from despair to discipleship. Kṛṣṇa smiles (2.10 — *prahasan iva*,
"as if smiling") and begins.
**Padārtha (key):** *kaśmala* (2.2, "faintness/stupor"), *anārya-juṣṭam* ("practised by the ignoble"),
*akīrti-karam* ("causing disgrace"), *klaibyam* (2.3, "unmanliness/impotence"), *kārpaṇya-doṣa* (2.7,
"the fault of cravenness"), *śiṣyas te'ham* ("I am Your disciple").
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara reads Arjuna's *kaśmala* as *moha* (delusion) that must be cured by knowledge;
Rāmānuja sees Arjuna's surrender (*prapanna*) as the disciple-posture that entitles him to the supreme
teaching; Madhva stresses that Arjuna, though confused, rightly turns to the Lord. The smile (2.10) — Śaṅkara:
Kṛṣṇa smiles at the irony that Arjuna speaks like a wise man (2.11 "you speak words of wisdom") while
drowning in ignorance. Abhinavagupta: the smile is the guru's delight at the disciple's readiness.
**Layers —** W: "Arjuna stops fighting and surrenders — and the teaching begins." / S: śiṣya-prapatti.
/ Sk: the moment of honest surrender as the gateway. / A: Śaṅkara on kaśmala as moha; the "smile" readings.

## 2.11–2.12 — The prologue to the Self-teaching
**2.11:** *"aśocyān anvaśocaḥ tvam prajñāvādāṃś ca bhāṣase"* — "You grieve for what should not be grieved
for, yet speak words of wisdom; the wise grieve neither for the dead nor the living." **2.12:** *"na tvevāhaṃ
jātu nāsam…"* — "Never was there a time when I did not exist, nor you, nor these kings; nor shall we ever
cease."
**Padārtha:** *aśocyān* ("not to be grieved for"), *anvaśocaḥ* (*anu-√śuc* "you grieve after/for"),
*prajñāvādāḥ* ("words of wisdom"), *paṇḍitāḥ* ("the wise").
**Significance.** The two-fold diagnosis: (a) Arjuna grieves for that which is *not* a proper object of grief
(the body which perishes), and (b) he speaks learnedly but does not *know*. 2.12 states the eternal existence
of the Self — the foundation on which 2.13–25 will build.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the *paṇḍita* is the one who knows the Self; grief is rooted in *dehātma-bhrānti*
(the error of equating the body with the Self). Rāmānuja: "not for the living" — the embodied soul persists
in both states.
**Misreading:** that the verse means "don't grieve, period" — it means grief based on misapprehension of the
Self; the Gītā does not forbid compassion.
**Layers —** W: "You grieve for what cannot die." / S: aśocyān; the Self's eternality. / Sk: distinguish
grief-for-the-body from genuine compassion. / A: dehātma-bhrānti; Śaṅkara's root-diagnosis.

## 2.13–2.18 — The immortality of the Self
**2.13** (*dehino'smin yathā dehe*): the soul passes from childhood → youth → old age → another body; the
*dhīra* (steadfast) is not deluded. **2.14–15:** sense-contacts give cold/heat, pleasure/pain — they come
and go; endure them (*titikṣasva*); the equanimous (*samaduḥkha-sukha*) become fit for immortality
(*amṛtatvāya kalpate*). **2.16:** *na asato vidyate bhāvo, na abhāvo vidyate sataḥ* — "of the unreal no
being; of the real no non-being" — the ontological distinction between the body (asat, transient) and the
Self (sat, eternal). **2.17:** *avināśi tu tad viddhi* — "know That which pervades all to be indestructible."
**2.18:** bodies end; the embodied Self (*śarīriṇaḥ*) is eternal (*nityasya*), indestructible (*anāśinaḥ*),
immeasurable (*aprameyasya*) — "therefore fight."
**Padārtha:** *dehinaḥ* ("of the embodied one"), *dhīraḥ* ("steadfast/wise"), *titikṣasva* ("endure"),
*asat/sat* ("unreal/real"), *avināśi* ("indestructible"), *anāśin* ("un-destroyed"), *aprameya*
("unmeasurable, beyond the means of knowledge").
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 2.16 is the Gītā's ontological cornerstone — the body belongs to the *vyāvahārika*
(pragmatic/empirical) level of reality; the Self to the *pāramārthika* (absolute). The *sat-asat-viveka*
(discrimination of real from unreal) is the essence of jñāna-yoga. Rāmānuja: the distinction is real but the
Self's relation to the body (*śarīra*) is governed by the Lord. Madhva: the Self is eternal, but eternally
*different* from God.
**Misreading:** that 2.16 supports a flat body-denial (the body is "unreal" in the sense of temporary, not
non-existent); or that "fight" (2.18) endorses violence without the karma-yoga framework that follows.
**Layers —** W: "The soul changes bodies like old clothes; the body dies, the Self does not." / S: sat/asat;
dehin; titikṣā. / Sk: endure the transient; locate the changeless. / A: Śaṅkara's vyāvahārika vs
pāramārthika satya; the three schools on the Self's relation to body.

## 2.19–2.25 — The Self cannot be slain
**2.19–20:** the Self neither slays nor is slain (*na hanti na hanyate*); it is *aja* (unborn), *nitya*
(eternal), *śāśvata* (abiding), *purāṇa* (ancient) — not killed when the body is killed. **2.22:** the
garment metaphor (*vāsāṃsi jīrṇāni*) — the Self casts off worn bodies like old clothes. **2.23–24:** weapons
cannot cleave it, fire cannot burn it, water cannot wet it, wind cannot dry it — *acchedyo, adāhyo, akledya,
aśoṣya*; eternal, all-pervading (*sarvagata*), fixed (*sthāṇu*), immovable (*acala*), everlasting
(*sanātana*). **2.25:** *avyakta* (unmanifest), *acintya* (unthinkable), *avikārya* (unchangeable).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: these attributes negate every possible modification — the Self is *nirguṇa*
(without qualities), *nirvikāra* (without change). The sixfold negation (uncut/unburnt/unwet/undry +
unmanifest/unthinkable) is the *via negativa* (*neti neti*) of the Upaniṣads applied to the Self. Rāmānuja:
the attributes show the Self's transcendence over matter. Madhva: the Self's indestructibility does not
erase its eternal dependence on the Lord. Abhinavagupta: these negations point to the Self as pure
consciousness, beyond all objectification.
**Cross-refs:** Kaṭha Upaniṣad 1.2.18–22 (the same Self-teaching); Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.4.
**Layers —** W: "You can't kill what doesn't die — weapons can't touch it, fire can't burn it." / S:
the six negations; the garment metaphor. / Sk: rest in the unchangeable when the world churns. / A:
Śaṅkara's neti-neti; the Upaniṣadic ground.

## 2.26–2.30 — "Even if you doubt…"
**2.26–28:** even if one accepts (for argument) constant birth and death, death is certain for the born and
birth for the dead — so grief is pointless (*aparihārye'rthe*). Beings are unmanifest before birth and after
death; only manifest in the middle — *tatra kā paridevanā* ("what ground for lamentation?"). **2.29:** one
sees it as a wonder, another speaks of it as a wonder, another hears of it — even having heard, none truly
knows it (*śrutvāpy enaṃ veda na caiva kaścit*). **2.30:** the embodied Self in every body is forever
*avadhya* (not to be slain).
**Significance.** Kṛṣṇa offers a *reductio*: even on the materialist hypothesis (no rebirth), death is
inevitable — so why grieve? 2.29 is one of the Gītā's most beautiful verses on the *mystery* of the Self:
it is accessible as *āścarya* (wonder) but not as *object* of ordinary knowledge.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 2.29 shows that the Self is *acintya* — not graspable by thought, only by direct
realisation (*anubhava*). Abhinavagupta: wonder (*camatkāra*) is the aesthetic of the Self's self-disclosure.
**Layers —** W: "Even on the grimmest view, death is certain — so live fully." / S: the reductio; āścarya.
/ Sk: sit with the wonder of awareness itself. / A: the Self as acintya; Śaṅkara on anubhava vs buddhi.

## 2.31–2.38 — The svadharma argument
**2.31:** considering your *svadharma* (a warrior's duty), you ought not falter — "there is nothing higher
for a *kṣatriya* than a righteous (*dharmyāt*) battle." **2.32:** fortunate (*sukhinaḥ*) are the warriors
who find such a war — "an open door to heaven" (*svargadvāram apāvṛtam*). **2.33–36:** if you don't fight,
you lose *dharma* and *kīrti* (fame), incur *pāpa* (sin), and people will speak of your infamy
(*akīrti*) "forever" (*anantaram*) — for the honoured, dishonour exceeds death (*marṇāt atiricyate*).
**2.37:** slain, you attain heaven; victorious, the earth — "arise, resolved." **2.38:** treating pleasure
and pain, gain and loss, victory and defeat as equal (*same kṛtvā*) — "fight, and you will incur no sin."
**Padārtha:** *svadharmam* ("own duty"), *kṣatriyasya* ("of the warrior"), *dharmyāt* ("righteous"),
*kīrtim* ("fame"), *akīrtiḥ* ("infamy"), *saṃbhāvitasya* ("of one who is honoured"),
*lābhālābhau jayājayau* ("gain and loss, victory and defeat").
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the *svadharma* argument is *sādhana* (preparatory) — it steadies the mind for the
jñāna that follows; *svadharma* is the duty prescribed by one's *varṇa-āśrama*. Rāmānuja: the duty is real
and binding; the battle is *dharmic*. Madhva: the warrior's *svadharma* is irrevocable. **2.38** introduces
*samatva* (equanimity) — the bridge from *dharma* to *yoga*. The argument shifts from "you must fight"
(2.31–37) to "fight *in a certain way* — equally" (2.38).
**Misreading:** that 2.31–37 are the Gītā's final word (they are provisional; 2.38+ transforms the "must"
into a "how"). Also: reading *svadharma* as "whatever your nature says" without the *śāstra* framework.
**Layers —** W: "As a warrior, your duty is to fight a righteous war — and if you do it with a steady mind,
you incur no sin." / S: svadharma; samatva; the bridge to yoga. / Sk: identify your svadharma and do it
with equanimity. / A: Śaṅkara's sādhana reading; 2.38 as the pivot to karma-yoga.

## 2.39–2.53 — Karma-yoga: the path of selfless action
**2.39:** the Sāṃkhya (knowledge) view has been given; now hear the **yoga** (action) view — "by which you
will cast off the bondage of karma." **2.40:** "in this path, no effort is lost (*nehābhikrama-nāśo'sti*),
no adverse result (*pratyavāyo na vidyate*); even a little (*svalpam api*) of this dharma protects from
great fear." **2.41:** the resolute (*vyavasāyātmikā*) understanding is one-pointed (*eka*); the irresolute
is many-branched (*bahu-śākhā*). **2.42–46:** critique of *veda-vāda-ratāḥ* (those wedded to Vedic ritual
for heavenly reward) — the Vedas deal with the three *guṇas* (*traiguṇya-viṣayā vedā*); "be free of the
three" (*nistraiaguṇyo bhava*); "as a well is useless when everywhere is flooded, so the Vedas to one who
knows Brahman" (2.46). **2.47 — THE VERSE:** *karmaṇy evādhikāras te mā phaleṣu kadācana / mā
karma-phala-hetur bhūr mā te saṅgo'stv akarmaṇi* — "You have a right to action alone, never to its fruits.
Let not the fruit be your motive, nor let your attachment be to inaction." **2.48:** *yogasthaḥ kuru karmāṇi*
— "established in yoga, perform action; equal in success and failure — this evenness (*samatvam*) is called
yoga." **2.49–50:** "seek refuge in *buddhi* (understanding); wretched are they who act for fruit"; "yoga is
skill in action" (*yogaḥ karmasu kauśalam*). **2.51–53:** the wise, renouncing fruit, are freed from
rebirth and attain the flawless state (*padaṃ anāmayam*); when understanding crosses the thicket of delusion
(*moha-kalilaṃ*) and is steady in *samādhi*, then one attains yoga.
**Commentary:**
- **Śaṅkara:** 2.47 means: act from *svadharma* (duty) with the understanding that the Self is the
non-agent (3.27, 5.8–9); the "non-attachment to fruit" is the fruit of jñāna, not a technique to earn
liberation by doing action-without-caring. Karma-yoga is *citta-śuddhi* (purification of mind) that prepares
for knowledge. "Skill in action" (2.50) = doing action as *yajña* (offering) without ego.
- **Rāmānuja:** 2.47 is the verse of *karma-yoga* — action offered to the Lord (*īśvara-arpaṇa*); the
renunciation of fruit (*phala-tyāga*) is real renunciation, not just mental posture. Karma-yoga and jñāna
are one path, not two.
- **Madhva:** the action must be done in devotion (*bhakti-pūrvaka*); "no right to fruit" means fruit is the
Lord's to grant.
- **Abhinavagupta:** to "not be the cause" (2.47c) is to recognise the one Doer behind all action.
**Misreadings:** (1) fatalism ("don't think about results") — the Gītā demands *more* effort, not less;
(2) "do your duty, God gives wealth" (prosperity gospel); (3) that karma-yoga is *separate* from bhakti
(the Gītā later fuses them in 9.27, 18.46).
**Cross-refs:** 3.9 (action not for yajña binds), 4.20, 5.10, 9.27, 12.8–11, 18.6.
**Layers —** W: "Do your best, then let go of the result — this freedom is itself the path." / S:
phala-tyāga; samatva-yoga; buddhi-yoga. / Sk: the "handover" practice; track where the ego claims the fruit.
/ A: Śaṅkara (citta-śuddhi → jñāna) vs Rāmānuja (īśvara-arpaṇa = real renunciation); "skill in action."

## 2.54–2.72 — The Sthitaprajña (the person of steady wisdom)
**2.54** (Arjuna): *"sthitaprajñasya kā bhāṣā?"* — "What is the mark of the person of steady wisdom? How
do they speak, sit, move?" **2.55** (Kṛṣṇa): the definition — when one casts off *all* desires that cling to
the mind (*prajahāti yadā kāmān sarvān pārtha manogatān*) and is satisfied in the Self by the Self
(*ātmany evātmanā tuṣṭaḥ*) — then one is *sthitaprajña*. **2.56–57:** untroubled in sorrow, free from
longing in joy, rid of attachment, fear and anger (*vīta-rāga-bhaya-krodhaḥ*) — neither rejoicing nor
loathing at pleasant or unpleasant. **2.58:** like a tortoise withdrawing its limbs (*kūrmo 'ṅgānīva*) —
withdrawing the senses from their objects. **2.59:** for the abstinent, objects withdraw but not the
*rasa* (taste/craving) — "even the taste ceases on beholding the Supreme" (*paraṃ dṛṣṭvā nivartate*).
**2.60:** the turbulent senses (*pramāthīni*) forcibly carry away even a wise person's mind. **2.61:**
restrain them all, sit yoked, devoted to Me (*mat-paraḥ*). **2.62–63 — THE PSYCHOLOGICAL CHAIN:**
*dhyāyato viṣayān* (dwelling on objects) → *saṅga* (attachment) → *kāma* (desire) → *krodha* (anger) →
*saṃmoha* (delusion) → *smṛti-vibhrama* (confusion of memory) → *buddhi-nāśa* (loss of intellect) →
*praṇaśyati* (ruin). **2.64–65:** moving among objects with senses free from attraction/repulsion
(*rāga-dveṣa-viyuktais*), self-controlled — one attains *prasāda* (serenity); in serenity, all sorrows
cease and the intellect is established. **2.66:** "there is no understanding for the unyoked (*ayuktasya*),
nor contemplation; without contemplation, no peace; without peace, how happiness?" **2.67:** as wind carries
off a ship on water, even one wandering sense carries off the wisdom (*prajñā*). **2.68:** whose senses are
fully held — wisdom is established. **2.69:** "what is night for all beings, in it the self-controlled is
awake; wherein all beings are awake, that is night for the seeing sage" — the *inversion* of values. **2.70:**
as waters enter the ocean which remains unmoved and ever-full, so all desires enter the sage — "he attains
peace, not the one who hankers after desires" (*na kāmakāmī*). **2.71:** abandoning all desires, free from
longing, without *mamatā* (possessiveness) and *ahaṅkāra* (ego) — that one attains peace. **2.72:** "this is
the *brāhmī sthitiḥ* (the Brahmic state); attaining it, one is not deluded; established in it even at death,
one reaches *brahma-nirvāṇa*."
**Commentary:**
- **Śaṅkara:** the *sthitaprajña* is the *jñānī* — one who has realised the Self; 2.55's "satisfied in the
Self by the Self" is the definition of Self-realisation (*ātmanā ātmānam anubhavati*). 2.62–63 is the
complete psychology of moral decline — a map of how a good person falls; it predates cognitive-behavioural
therapy by two millennia. 2.70 (the ocean) is the paradigmatic image of the desire-free mind. The *brāhmī
sthitiḥ* (2.72) = the state of Brahman-realisation = *jīvanmukti* (liberation while living).
- **Rāmānuja:** the sthitaprajña is the *bhakta* whose mind is fixed on the Lord; the Self-satisfaction
(2.55) is satisfaction *in the Lord* who dwells within.
- **Madhva:** the steady sage knows his eternal dependence on the Lord and rests in that.
- **Abhinavagupta:** the tortoise-withdrawal (2.58) is the *pratyāhāra* of Patañjali; the inversion of 2.69
is the tantric *viśvottīrṇa* (transcending the world of ordinary experience).
**Cross-refs:** 2.62–63 with the chain of dependent origination in Buddhism (similar psychology, different
ontology); 2.58 with Patañjali Yoga Sūtras 2.54 (pratyāhāra); 2.69 with Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad.
**Misreading:** that the sthitaprajña is a cold, emotionless automaton — the Gītā describes warmth
(satisfaction, peace, bliss), not deadness; the sage feels deeply but is not *driven*.
**Layers —** W: "The wise are like an ocean — everything pours in, but they remain full and still." / S: the
sthitaprajña's marks; 2.62–63 as the psychology of fall; prasāda. / Sk: trace the chain (2.62–63) in your own
life; practise the ocean-mind. / A: Śaṅkara (jīvanmukti) vs Rāmānuja (bhakta's fixed mind); the CBT parallel;
pratyāhāra; brāhmī sthitiḥ.
