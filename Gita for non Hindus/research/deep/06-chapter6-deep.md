# Deep Commentary — Chapter 6: Ātma-Saṃyama / Dhyāna Yoga (Meditation)

> *The meditation chapter — the mind as friend and enemy. Verified (IITK). Landmarks: 6.5–6, 6.16–17, 6.35,
> 6.47.*

**At a glance.** Chapter 6 defines true renunciation as **inner** (6.1–4), teaches the **mind as friend and
enemy** (6.5–6), gives the practical **meditation manual** (6.10–19), describes the yogī's bliss (6.20–29),
handles Arjuna's objection ("the mind is restless!," 6.33–36), and answers the fate of the **fallen yogī**
(6.37–45). It closes: the greatest yogī is the **devotee** (6.47).

---

## 6.1–6.4 — True renunciation is inner
**6.1:** one who acts without leaning on the fruit (*anāśritaḥ karmaphalam*) is the true *sannyāsī* — not
merely one who has dropped the sacred fire. **6.2:** "what they call renunciation, know to be **yoga**" —
without giving up *saṅkalpa* (resolves/mental proliferation), none becomes a yogī. **6.3–4:** for the
ascetic climbing, action is the means; once ascended, **calm** (*śamaḥ*) is the means. When one clings to
neither objects nor actions — a renouncer of all *saṅkalpa* — one has "risen to yoga."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: *saṅkalpa* (6.2) = the mind's proliferating thought-chatter that generates desire;
giving up *saṅkalpa* is the inner essence of both renunciation and meditation.
**Layers —** W: "Real renunciation is not outer withdrawal but inner quieting." / S: saṅkalpa-tyāga; the
karma/sannyāsa identity. / Sk: notice mental proliferation. / A: Śaṅkara on saṅkalpa.

## 6.5–6.6 — THE LANDMARK: the mind as friend and enemy
**6.5:** *uddhared ātmanātmānaṃ nātmānam avasādayet / ātmaiva hyātmano bandhur ātmaiva ripur ātmanaḥ* — "let
one **lift** the self by the Self; degrade it not; the Self alone is the self's **friend** — the Self alone
is its **enemy**." **6.6:** for the self-conquered (*ātmanā jitaḥ*), the Self is friend; for the unconquered
(*anātmanaḥ*), the Self acts as a foe (*śatruvat*).
**Padārtha:** *uddharet* (*ud-√dhṛ* "lift/raise up"), *avasādayet* (*ava-√sad* caus. "depress/sink"),
*bandhuḥ* ("friend/kinsman"), *ripuḥ* ("enemy").
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the verse is psychologically exact — the "lower self" (the mind-ego-complex) can be
either elevated or degraded by the "higher Self" (the pure witness-consciousness); trained, the mind is the
Self's ally; untrained, its saboteur. *Ātmanā* (instrumental) = "by the Self" — the higher lifts the lower.
Rāmānuja: the devotee's mind, offered to the Lord, becomes the friend. Madhva: the soul must cooperate with
the Lord's grace to master itself. Abhinavagupta: the "friend" is the recognition (*pratyabhijñā*) of one's
true nature.
**Misreading:** that 6.5 supports self-reliance without God — the Gītā immediately adds *mat-paraḥ* (devoted
to Me, 6.14, 6.47) and *mayi* (in Me). The Self-mastery and divine dependence are **not rivals**.
**Cross-refs:** 6.35 (abhyāsa + vairāgya as the method), 2.61.
**Layers —** W: "Your own mind can be your best friend or your worst enemy — train it." / S: ātmanā ātmānam;
higher/lower self. / Sk: is your mind your friend today? / A: Śaṅkara's higher/lower; the
self-reliance/divine-dependence non-contradiction.

## 6.7–6.10 — The self-conquered sage
**6.7:** in the tranquil (*praśāntasya*), self-conquered one, the Supreme Self (*paramātmā*) is poised
(*samāhitaḥ*) — alike in cold/heat, joy/sorrow, honour/dishonour. **6.8:** content with knowledge and
realisation (*jñāna-vijñāna-tṛptātmā*), changeless (*kūṭastha*), senses mastered — "equal to clod, stone and
gold" (*sama-loṣṭāśma-kāñcanaḥ*). **6.9:** supreme is the one of equal mind toward friend, foe, ally,
neutral, mediator, hater, kinsman — and toward the good and sinful. **6.10:** the yogī should ever unite the
self in solitude — alone (*ekākī*), controlled, desireless, possessionless.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 6.8's *kūṭastha* ("changeless, like an anvil") is a technical term for the Self
that remains unmoved while prakṛti flows; *sama-loṣṭāśma-kāñcanaḥ* is the test of non-attachment. 6.9: equal
mind toward *sādhūṣu pāpeṣu* (the virtuous and the sinful) is the summit of *sāmya*.
**Layers —** W: "The mastered mind is unshaken by heat or cold, praise or blame — steady as an anvil." / S:
kūṭastha; sama-loṣṭa. / Sk: test your equanimity with the clod/gold standard. / A: kūṭastha as
Self-technical-term.

## 6.11–6.19 — The meditation manual
**6.11–14:** seat firm, clean, not too high/low, cloth/skin/kuśa-grass; seated, mind one-pointed, restrain
thought and senses; body-head-neck erect, still; gaze at the nose-tip; tranquil, fearless, firm in the vow of
celibacy (*brahmacāri-vrate sthitaḥ*), mind restrained, fixed on Me (*mat-cittaḥ*), devoted to Me
(*mat-paraḥ*). **6.15:** "thus ever disciplining, the yogī of controlled mind attains the peace of nirvāṇa,
which abides in Me." **6.16–17 — THE MODERATION VERSES:** *nātyaśnatastu yogo'sti na caikāntam anaśnataḥ /
na cātisvapnaśīlasya jāgrato naiva cārjuna* — "yoga is not for the over-eater, nor for one who fasts to
excess; not for the over-sleeper nor the over-wakeful." **6.17:** *yuktāhāra-vihārasya yuktaceṣṭasya
karmasu / yuktasvapnāvabodhasya yogo bhavati duḥkhahā* — "for one **moderate** (*yukta*) in food and
recreation, in effort, in sleep and waking — yoga becomes the **destroyer of sorrow**." **6.18:** when the
ruled mind (*viniyataṃ citta*) rests in the Self alone, desireless — "then one is called **united**
(*yukta*)." **6.19:** as a lamp in a windless place (*dīpo nivātasthaḥ*) flickers not — such is the likeness
of the controlled mind.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the seat, posture, and gaze are **aids** (*sādhana*), not ends; the essence is
*cittaikāgratā* (one-pointedness, 6.12). 6.16–17's *yukta* (moderation) is crucial: the Gītā rejects both
self-indulgence and extreme asceticism — moderation is the path. The lamp-simile (6.19) captures the
*flicker-free* mind. Rāmānuja: the meditation is on the Lord (*mat-cittaḥ*), not on an impersonal principle.
**Cross-refs:** Patañjali Yoga Sūtras 2.46–53 (āsana, prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra); Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.4.
**Layers —** W: "Sit still, breathe, focus — and be moderate in everything." / S: the meditation manual;
the lamp-simile. / Sk: adopt 6.16–17's moderation in one area. / A: the Gītā vs Patañjali on method; Śaṅkara
on cittaikāgratā.

## 6.20–6.32 — The yogī's bliss and equal vision
**6.20–22:** the state where the mind, held by yoga practice, becomes still — one **sees the Self in the
Self**, rejoices in the Self; that infinite bliss (*sukham ātyantikaṃ*), graspable by *buddhi*, beyond the
senses — settled in it, one swerves not from the Real; having gained it, one deems no other gain greater;
not shaken even by heavy sorrow. **6.27–28:** supreme bliss comes to the calm-minded yogī, sinless — who
enjoys the touch of Brahman (*brahma-saṃsparśam*) and boundless bliss. **6.29:** the yogī sees the Self in
**all beings** and all beings in the Self (*sarvabhūtastham ātmānam sarvabhūtāni cātmani*). **6.30:** who
sees Me everywhere and all in Me — I am never lost to him, nor he to Me. **6.31–32:** the yogī who worships
Me dwelling in all beings, established in **oneness** (*ekatvam*) — though living in every way, abides in
Me. By taking the self as the measure of all (*ātmaupamyena*), seeing the same everywhere — that yogī is
**supreme** (*paramo mataḥ*).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 6.20–22 describe the **direct experience** (*sākṣātkāra*) of the Self — infinite
bliss that ends all seeking; this is the *jñānī*'s state. 6.29–32: the vision of the **one Self** in all —
the ethical fruit of Self-realisation: you cannot harm what you see as yourself. Rāmānuja: the vision is of
the **Lord** (6.30 "Me") immanent in all; oneness (*ekatvam*, 6.31) is unity of devotion, not identity.
Madhva: the yogī sees the **Lord** in all beings, not that all beings **are** the Lord.
**Layers —** W: "The meditator finds boundless joy within — and sees the same Self in everyone." / S:
sākṣātkāra; the one-Self vision. / Sk: the four-breath/meditation practice; equal regard. / A: Śaṅkara
(Self-experience) vs Rāmānuja (Lord-vision); *ekatvam* debate.

## 6.33–6.36 — Arjuna's objection: the mind is restless!
**6.33–34** (Arjuna): "this yoga of evenness — I see no firm abidance, for the mind is **restless**
(*cañcalam*)"; "the mind is **turbulent** (*pramāthi*), **vehement** (*balavad*), **stubborn** (*dṛḍham*);
curbing it is as hard as the wind (*vāyor iva suduṣkaram*)." **6.35 — THE ANSWER:** *abhyāsena tu kaunteya
vairāgyeṇa ca gṛhyate* — "doubtless, the mind is restless and hard to restrain — yet by **practice**
(*abhyāsena*) and **dispassion** (*vairāgyeṇa*) it is held." **6.36:** yoga is hard for the uncontrolled;
attainable by the self-controlled striving one.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: *abhyāsa* = the repeated practice of fixing the mind on the Self (or a chosen
object); *vairāgya* = the dispassion that arises from seeing the defects (*doṣa*) of sense-objects. Neither
alone suffices — both together. The wind-simile (6.34) is apt: as wind is vast and moving, so the mind; yet
as wind can be directed (by sails), the mind can be trained. Rāmānuja: *abhyāsa* = constant remembrance of
the Lord; *vairāgya* = detachment from the non-Lord.
**Cross-refs:** Patañjali YS 1.12 (*abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṃ* — the identical formula).
**Layers —** W: "Yes, the mind is restless — but it yields to practice and letting go." / S: abhyāsa +
vairāgya. / Sk: name your one practice and your one attachment to release. / A: Śaṅkara's two-method
analysis; the Patañjali parallel.

## 6.37–6.45 — The fate of the fallen yogī
**6.37–39** (Arjuna): what of the one who has faith but is **undisciplined** (*ayatiḥ*), whose mind slips
from yoga before perfection — "does he perish like a torn cloud (*chinnābhram iva*), fallen from both?"
**6.40:** *pārtha naiveha nāmutra vināśastasya vidyate* — "neither here nor hereafter is there ruin for him
— **none who does good ever falls to evil** (*na hi kalyāṇakṛt kaścid durgatiṃ tāta gacchati*)." **6.41–43:**
the fallen yogī, after long years in the heavens of the meritorious, is reborn in the home of the pure and
prosperous (*śucīnāṃ śrīmatāṃ gehe*) — or in a family of wise yogīs (rarer still). There he **recovers the
illumination** (*buddhi-saṃyogaṃ*) of his former body and strives again. **6.44:** by past practice
(*pūrvābhyāsena*) he is **drawn on even unwillingly** (*hriyate hy avaśo'pi saḥ*) — even a mere seeker of
yoga transcends the verbal Brahman (the Veda). **6.45:** the yogī who strives on, sins cleansed, perfected
over many births — attains the highest goal.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 6.44 is the Gītā's most **tender reassurance** — **no spiritual effort is ever
lost** (*nehābhikrama-nāśo'sti*, 2.40); past practice becomes an involuntary pull toward the good. The yogī
is "drawn unwillingly" because the momentum of past discipline is **stronger** than present inertia.
Rāmānuja: the Lord Himself ensures the fallen yogī's rebirth in favourable circumstances — His grace, not
chance.
**Layers —** W: "No spiritual effort is ever wasted — even if you fall, you will be carried forward by what
you've already done." / S: pūrvābhyāsa; the involuntary pull. / Sk: resume after a lapse — the past practice
draws you. / A: Śaṅkara's 6.44 ("drawn unwillingly"); the schools on grace vs momentum.

## 6.46–6.47 — The devotee is the greatest yogī
**6.46:** the yogī is higher than the ascetic (*tapasvibhyaḥ*), higher than the knower (*jñānibhyaḥ*),
higher than the ritualist (*karmibhyaḥ*) — "therefore, be a yogī." **6.47 — THE CLOSING LANDMARK:** *yoginām
api sarveṣāṃ madgatenāntarātmanā / śraddhāvān bhajate yo māṃ sa me yuktatamo mataḥ* — "of all yogīs, the one
of **faith** who **worships Me** with the inmost self absorbed in Me — that one is deemed **most united**
(*yukta-tamaḥ*) with Me."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the chapter that taught meditation (dhyāna) closes by saying the **bhakta** is the
greatest yogī — the Gītā's trajectory: jñāna → dhyāna → **bhakti** as the crown. The *mat-gata-antarātmanā*
("with the inner self gone to Me") = the jñānī whose Self-realisation has become devotion.
Rāmānuja: 6.47 is explicit — **bhakti is the highest yoga**; the one who worships the Lord surpasses all
others. Madhva: the devotee is greatest because devotion includes knowledge and action.
**Cross-refs:** 9.34, 12.2, 18.55, 18.65–66 — the bhakti crescendo.
**Layers —** W: "Of all paths, the greatest is the one of faith and devotion to the Divine." / S: the
jñāna→dhyāna→bhakti arc. / Sk: let your meditation become devotion. / A: Śaṅkara (jñānī as bhakta) vs
Rāmānuja (bhakti as crown).
