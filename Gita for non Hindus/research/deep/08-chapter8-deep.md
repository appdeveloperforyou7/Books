# Deep Commentary — Chapter 8: Akṣara-Brahma Yoga (The Imperishable Brahman)

> *The chapter of death, the last thought, and the two paths. Landmarks: 8.5–7, 8.13 (OM), 8.16, 8.21–22.*

**At a glance.** Arjuna asks seven questions (8.1–2): what is Brahman, *adhyātma*, *karma*, *adhibhūta*,
*adhidaiva*, *adhiyajña*, and how does the Lord work at death? Kṛṣṇa defines each (8.3–4), teaches the power
of the **last thought** (8.5–8), the practice of **OM** at death (8.9–14), the **cosmic cycles** of creation
(8.17–20), and the **two paths** of the departing soul — *devayāna* (to liberation) and *pitṛyāṇa* (to
rebirth) (8.24–26).

---

## 8.1–8.4 — Seven definitions
**8.1–2** (Arjuna asks): what is *Brahman*, *adhyātma*, *karma*, *adhibhūta*, *adhidaiva*, *adhiyajña*, and
how to know You at death? **8.3–4:** *Brahman* = the **imperishable** (*akṣara*); *adhyātma* = one's own
nature (*svabhāva*); *karma* = the creative force (*visarga*) that brings beings into being; *adhibhūta* =
the perishable (*kṣara*) physical nature; *adhidaiva* = the **Puruṣa** (cosmic Person, = Hiraṇyagarbha/the
Sun); *adhiyajña* = Kṛṣṇa Himself, dwelling in the body. **8.5–6 — THE LAST THOUGHT:** *anta-kāle ca mām eva
smaran muktvā kalevaram / yaḥ prayāti sa mad-bhāvaṃ yāti nāsty atra saṃśayaḥ* — "whoever, leaving the body
at the end, remembers Me alone, attains My state — no doubt." **8.6:** *yaṃ yaṃ vāpi smaran bhāvaṃ tyajaty
ante kalevaram* — "whatever state of being one remembers when leaving the body, that one **attains** — one
**becomes** that (*sadā tad-bhāva-bhāvitaḥ*)."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 8.6 is the law of *bhāvana* — the mind's accumulated **becoming** crystallises at
death into the next destiny; the last thought is not arbitrary but the **culmination** of one's life's
direction. 8.5's "remember Me" = the mind fixed on the Self/Brahman at death — and such a one, already
identified with Brahman, attains it. Rāmānuja: 8.5–6 is the *prapatti* doctrine at the hour of death — the
surrendered soul who remembers the Lord is assured of reaching Him.
**Misreading:** that 8.5 means "just think of God once at death and you're saved" regardless of how you
lived; Śaṅkara insists the last thought **reflects** the lifetime's direction — a life of attachment cannot
suddenly produce a mind fixed on God at death. Rāmānuja would allow God's grace to receive even the
last-moment turn (cf. 9.30–31), but the general principle holds: **you die as you lived**.
**Cross-refs:** 9.34, 18.65, Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad, Bṛhadāraṇyaka.
**Layers —** W: "What you think of at death is what you become — so fill your life with the Divine." / S:
the bhāvana-law; the last thought. / Sk: what occupies your mind daily — that is your likely last thought. /
A: Śaṅkara (lifetime-direction) vs Rāmānuja (grace at death).

## 8.7–8.8 — "Therefore remember Me and fight"
**8.7 — LANDMARK:** *tasmāt sarveṣu kāleṣu mām anusmara yudhya ca / mayy arpita-mano-buddhir mām evaiṣyasy
asaṃśayam* — "therefore, at **all times**, remember Me and **fight** (*yudhya*); with mind and intellect
offered to Me, you shall come to Me without doubt." **8.8:** "by constant meditation (*abhyāsa-yoga*), the
mind does not stray elsewhere."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 8.7 is the Gītā's distilled method — **remember the Divine while fully engaged in
duty** (*yudhya*); not withdrawal but interiorised action. *Arpita-mano-buddhiḥ* = the mind and intellect
fixed on the Self while the body acts. Rāmānuja: "fight" = do your duty with the mind on the Lord — the
quintessence of *bhakti*-infused karma-yoga. Madhva: remembrance of the Lord in all states.
**Layers —** W: "Remember God constantly — and do your work. These are not opposites." / S: anusmara + yudhya;
arpita-mano-buddhi. / Sk: weave remembrance into your workday. / A: Śaṅkara (Self-remembrance in action) vs
Rāmānuja (Lord-remembrance).

## 8.9–8.14 — OM at death
**8.9–10:** meditate on the all-pervading, omniscient, ancient ruler — subtler than the subtle — "whoever
at the time of death, with unwavering devotion and the power of yoga, fixes the life-breath (*prāṇa*) between
the brows, attains that divine Person." **8.13:** *oṁ ity ekākṣaraṃ brahma* — "the syllable **OM** (*oṁkāra*),
the one-syllable Brahman — uttering it and remembering Me, whoever departs the body attains the supreme goal."
**8.14:** "for one who is always absorbed, remembering Me without other thought — I am **easy to attain**
(*sulabhaḥ*), O Pārtha."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: OM (8.13) is the **name and symbol of Brahman** — the *praṇava*, the cosmic sound;
uttering it with the mind on the Self is the supreme practice at death. The *eka-akṣara* (one-syllable)
shows Brahman's indivisibility. Rāmānuja: OM is the **name of the Lord** (Nārāyaṇa); chanting it leads to
Him. Madhva: OM = Vāsudeva. Abhinavagupta: OM = the primordial vibration (*spanda*) of consciousness.
**Cross-refs:** Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (OM = all); 17.23 (*oṁ tat sat*).
**Layers —** W: "OM is the sound of the Divine — let your last word be His name." / S: praṇava; OM as
eka-akṣara brahma. / Sk: chant OM as a daily practice. / A: Śaṅkara (OM = symbol of Brahman) vs Rāmānuja (OM
= Lord's name); the Māṇḍūkya.

## 8.15–8.22 — The cosmic cycle and the supreme abode
**8.15:** the world of rebirth is a place of suffering — "having come to it, one returns" — but the one who
reaches Kṛṣṇa is **not reborn**. **8.16:** *ā-brahma-bhuvanāl lokāḥ punar āvartino 'rjuna* — "all worlds,
even Brahmā's, entail return; but the one who reaches Me is **not reborn** (*mām upetya tu kaunteya punar
janma na vidyate*)." **8.17–19:** the **day and night of Brahmā** — a thousand *yugas* (ages) = one day of
Brahmā; at the dawn of His day, all beings come forth from the unmanifest; at the twilight of His night,
they dissolve back — "again and again" (*bhūtvā bhūtvā pralīyate*). **8.20:** *paraḥ smāt tu bhāvo 'nyo
'vyakto 'vyaktāt sanātanaḥ* — "but **beyond** this unmanifest is another, eternal, unmanifest being — which,
when all beings perish, **does not perish**." **8.21–22:** that is the **supreme abode** (*paraṃ dhāma*); it
is attained by **undivided devotion** (*ananyayā bhaktyā*) — "by which one enters the Lord, the pervasive one
(*viśvataḥ-mukham*) — He is **not** attained by the divided (*aprapadyante*)."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 8.16 — even the highest heaven (*Brahmā-loka*) is within *saṃsāra*; only the
realisation of Brahman is beyond the cycle. 8.20: the *avyakta* (unmanifest prakṛti) of 8.18–19 is the
*prakṛti-pralaya* (cosmic dissolution); **beyond** that is the *para-bhāva* — the Supreme (Brahman/Ātman)
which is truly eternal. 8.22: "not attained by the divided" = not attained by one whose mind is scattered
among many objects; only *ekāgra-bhakti* reaches.
**Layers —** W: "All heavens are temporary — only union with the Divine is permanent." / S:
Brahmā's day/night; the para-dhāma. / Sk: locate your true home beyond every temporary heaven. / A:
Śaṅkara's avyakta vs para-bhāva hierarchy.

## 8.23–8.28 — The two paths
**8.23–26:** "I shall tell you the times at which departing yogīs **return** or **do not return**." The
**bright path** (*śukla-gati* = *devayāna*): fire, light, daytime, the bright fortnight, the six months of
the sun's northern course — taking the Brahman-knower to Brahman, **they do not return**. The **dark path**
(*kṛṣṇa-gati* = *pitṛyāṇa*): smoke, night, the dark fortnight, the six months of the southern course — taking
the yogī to the **lunar light**, whence he **returns**. "These two paths — bright and dark — are the world's
perennial ones." **8.27–28:** knowing these, "no yogī is deluded" — therefore at all times be yoked in yoga;
the merit of Vedic study, sacrifice, austerity — "all this is surpassed by knowing this truth."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the two paths are the *Chāndogya* (5.10) and *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* (6.2) doctrine of
*devayāna* vs *pitṛyāṇa* — the bright (northern) path leads the *jñānī* to immediate liberation (no return);
the dark (southern) path leads the *karmī* to Svarga (temporary heaven) and rebirth. The cosmic "timing"
(fire, light, etc.) may be symbolic of inner states or literally calendrical (Śaṅkara takes them literally).
Rāmānuja: the bright path = the path of the devotee who reaches the Lord; the dark path = the path of
ritual-merit that yields temporary fruits. Madhva: similar, with the Lord as the final arbiter.
**Cross-refs:** Chāndogya Upaniṣad 4.15–5.10; Bṛhadāraṇyaka 6.2.
**Layers —** W: "There are two roads after death — one leads home forever; the other brings you back." / S:
devayāna/pitṛyāṇa; the Chāndogya doctrine. / Sk: which path are you on now — the path of knowledge or the
path of merit? / A: Śaṅkara (literal cosmic timing vs inner states); the Upaniṣadic sources.
