# Deep Commentary — Chapters 13–15 (Kṣetra-Kṣetrajña · Guṇatraya · Puruṣottama)

> *The Sāṃkhya-knowledge section: the distinction of field/knower, the three guṇas, and the Supreme Person.
> Landmarks: 13.1–2, 13.8–12, 13.22, 13.28; 14.5, 14.26; 15.1, 15.7, 15.16–19.*

---

# CHAPTER 13: Kṣetra-Kṣetrajña-Vibhāga Yoga (The Field & the Knower of the Field) — 34 verses

**At a glance.** Arjuna asks (13.1): what is *prakṛti*, *puruṣa*, the field (*kṣetra*) and its knower
(*kṣetrajña*)? Kṛṣṇa answers: **the body is the field; the conscious Self is the knower of the field**
(*idaṃ śarīraṃ kaunteya kṣetram ity abhidhīyate / etad yo vetti taṃ prāhuḥ kṣetrajña iti tad-vidaḥ*, 13.2).
He catalogues the elements of the field (13.5–6) and then **defines true knowledge** not as information but
as a **character** — humility, non-violence, patience, self-control (13.7–11). He describes *prakṛti* and
*puruṣa* (13.19–22) and the soul's liberation by knowing their distinction (13.23–26). The chapter closes on
the vision of the **one Self in all** (13.27–35).

## 13.1–13.6 — The field and its knower
**13.1–2** (the question + the answer): the field = the body and world; its knower = the conscious Self.
Kṛṣṇa: "I am the **knower of the field** in **all fields** (*sarva-kṣetreṣu*, 13.2) — the knowledge of the
field and its knower — **that is what I call knowledge** (*taj jñānaṃ mati*)." **13.5–6:** the field's elements
— the five great elements (*mahā-bhūta*), *ahaṅkāra*, *buddhi*, the *avyakta*, the ten senses, mind, the five
sense-objects, desire/hatred/pleasure/pain, the body, consciousness (*cetanā*) and resolve (*dhṛti*) — this
is the field, summarised.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 13.2 is the Gītā's clearest **epistemological map** — the *kṣetra* (field) is the
entire material/psychological complex (body, mind, intellect, ego, all modifications of *prakṛti*); the
*kṣetrajña* (knower) is the **witness-consciousness** (*sākṣin*) that illuminates the field without being
modified by it. "I am the knower of the field in all fields" = the one Self, Kṛṣṇa, is the witness in every
being. Rāmānuja: the *kṣetrajña* is the individual *jīva*, supported by the Lord as the *antar-yāmin* (inner
ruler). Madhva: the *kṣetra* and *kṣetrajña* are eternally distinct; the Lord is the supreme *kṣetrajña* above
all.

## 13.7–13.12 — What true knowledge IS
**13.7–11 — LANDMARK (the definition of knowledge):** *amānitvam adambhitvam ahiṃsā kṣāntir ārjavam /
ācāryopāsanaṃ śaucaṃ sthairyam ātma-vinigrahaḥ* — humility, non-hypocrisy, non-violence (*ahiṃsā*),
forbearance (*kṣānti*), uprightness, service of the teacher, purity, steadfastness, self-control; detachment
from sense-objects, absence of ego; reflecting on the evils of birth/death/old age/sorrow; non-attachment,
**undivided devotion to the Lord** (*aniketo... bhaktiḥ* [VERIFY]); retreat from the company of the worldly;
**constancy in spiritual knowledge** — **THIS is knowledge; what is other than this is ignorance** (*etaj
jñānam iti matvā ajñānaṃ yad ato 'nyathā*, 13.11 [VERIFY]).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: this is one of the Gītā's most important redefinitions — **knowledge is not
information but transformation of character**. The twenty qualities listed are not "things to learn" but
"states to become." The sage who embodies them IS knowledge. Rāmānuja: these are the **preconditions** of
knowledge — the *sādhana-catvārtha* (four-fold means) of Vedānta.

## 13.13–13.18 — The Supreme described
With hands and feet everywhere, eyes, heads, faces everywhere — hearing everything — **dwelling in the heart
of all** (*sarva-bhūtāntar-ātmā*), the **support of all** (*bhūta-bhṛt*), **tasteless to the senses**
(*nirguṇam guṇa-bhoktṛ ca* [VERIFY]), outside and within all, moving and unmoving, **subtle beyond grasp**
(*sūkṣmatvāt*), **near and far** — undivided yet **seated as if divided** (*bhūtam a-vibhāktaṃ ca, vibhāktaṃ
iva* [VERIFY]) — the **light of all lights** (*jyotiṣāṃ jyotiḥ*), **beyond darkness** (*tamasaḥ param*) —
knowable by the **intellect** (*buddhyā*) — **dwelling in the heart of all** (*hṛdi sarvasya*).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 13.13–18 use the *neti-neti* (not this, not this) approach of the Upaniṣads —
Brahman is described through paradoxes that defeat every category. "Undivided yet as if divided" = the one
Self appears as many through *upādhi* (limiting adjuncts) without being modified. "Light of lights" = pure
consciousness, the illuminator of all illumination.

## 13.19–13.26 — Prakṛti and Puruṣa
**13.19:** *prakṛtiṃ puruṣaṃ caiva viddhy anādī ubhāv api / vikārāṃś ca guṇāṃś caiva viddhi prakṛti-sambhavān*
— know *prakṛti* and *puruṣa* both as beginningless; know that all modifications and guṇas are born of
*prakṛti*. **13.20:** *kārya-karaṇa-kartṛtve* — prakṛti is the cause of **effect and instrument** (the body
and its tools); *puruṣa* is the cause of **experience** (*bhoktṛtve*) of pleasure and pain. **13.21:** the
*puruṣa* **dwelling in prakṛti** (*prakṛti-sthaḥ*) **experiences** the guṇas; **attachment to the guṇas is
the cause of birth in good and evil wombs** (*guṇa-saṅgaḥ... sad-asad-yoni-janmasu*). **13.22:** the **Supreme
Puruṣa** (*puruṣaḥ paraḥ*) in this body is the **witness** (*sākṣī*), the **approver** (*anumantā*), the
**supporter** (*bhartā*), the **experiencer** (*bhoktā*), the **great Lord** (*maheśvara*), the **supreme
Self** (*paramātmā*).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 13.22 gives the **six functions** of the Self in the body — but note that *sākṣī*
(witness) is the fundamental one; the others are concessions to the embodied state. The *paramātmā* here is
the individual Self seen in its universality (or, for Rāmānuja, the Lord as *antar-yāmin*). Rāmānuja: the
*puruṣa paraḥ* of 13.22 is the **Lord Himself**, not the individual soul — the Lord dwells in every body as
the inner ruler. Madhva: the distinction is tripartite — *prakṛti*, the individual *jīva*, and the supreme
*paramātmā*; all three are eternally real and distinct.
**Cross-refs:** Sāṃkhya Kārikā; Bṛhadāraṇyaka; 14.5–6, 15.7.

## 13.27–13.35 — Seeing the one Self in all
**13.28 — LANDMARK:** *samaṃ sarveṣu bhūteṣu tiṣṭhantaṃ parameśvaram / vinaśyatsv avinaśyantaṃ yaḥ paśyati
sa paśyati* — "who sees the **imperishable Lord** dwelling alike in all **perishable** beings — **he truly
sees** (*sa paśyati*)." **13.29:** seeing the Lord everywhere, one does not **degrade the self by the self**
(*ātmanā ātmānam*) — and attains the supreme goal. **13.30:** seeing that all **action** is done by *prakṛti*
alone, and the **Self is the non-actor** (*akartāram*) — one realises. **13.33–34:** as the one sun lights up
the whole world, so the one Self illuminates the entire field; those who **see with the eye of knowledge**
(*jñāna-cakṣuṣā*) the distinction between the field and its knower — they are **released from prakṛti**.
**Layers —** W: "Your body is the field; the awareness watching it is the knower. Knowledge is not facts but a
transformed heart. See the one Self in all, and you are free." / S: kṣetra/kṣetrajña; the 20 marks of
knowledge; the 6 functions of the Self; *sa paśyati*. / Sk: find the witness behind your thoughts. / A:
Śaṅkara (sākṣī as fundamental) vs Rāmānuja (paramātmā as the Lord) vs Madhva (tripartite reality).

# CHAPTER 14: Guṇatraya-Vibhāga Yoga (The Three Guṇas) — 27 verses

**At a glance.** Kṛṣṇa explains the three *guṇas* — *sattva* (purity), *rajas* (passion), *tamas* (inertia) —
how they bind the soul (14.5–9), what each yields at death (14.14–18), and how one transcends them
(*guṇātīta*, 14.21–26). The path to transcendence: **undivided devotion** (*bhakti-yogena*, 14.26).

## 14.1–14.4 — The womb and the seed
**14.3–4:** *mama yonir mahad brahma tasmin garbhaṃ dadhāmy aham* — "My **great womb** (*mahad brahma*) is the
source; I place the **seed** (*garbham*); from that womb, O Bhārata, all beings are born." The *brahma* (14.3)
= the *mahat-tattva* / *prakṛti*; the *garbha* = the *jīvas*.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: God is both the **father** (seed = consciousness) and **mother** (womb = prakṛti)
of the cosmos. Rāmānuja: the Lord as the efficient cause places the *jīvas* in the *prakṛti*-womb.

## 14.5–14.9 — How the three guṇas bind
**14.5:** *sattvaṃ rajas tama iti guṇāḥ prakṛti-sambhavāḥ / nibadhnanti mahābāho dehe dehinam avyayam* —
"sattva, rajas, tamas — the three guṇas born of prakṛti — **bind the imperishable embodied soul** in the
body." **14.6:** *sattva* — **luminous** (*prakāśaka*) and **healthful** (*anāmaya*) — binds through
**attachment to happiness and knowledge** (*sukha-saṅgena, jñāna-saṅgena ca*). **14.7:** *rajas* —
**passion** (*rāga-ātmaka*), born of **thirst** (*tṛṣṇā*) and **attachment** (*saṅga*) — binds through
**attachment to action and its fruit** (*karma-saṅgena*). **14.8:** *tamas* — born of **ignorance**
(*ajñāna-jam*) — **deludes** all embodied beings (*mohanam sarva-dehinām*) — binds through **heedlessness,
laziness, and sleep** (*pramāda, ālasya, nidrā*).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the key insight — **even sattva binds**. Purity and knowledge are not the Self; they
are the highest *guṇa*, but attachment to them ("I am pure, I know") is still bondage. The *guṇātīta* goes
**beyond** even sattva — the Self is *nirguṇa* (guṇa-less). Rāmānuja: the guṇas are prakṛti's modes; the
soul must rise above them by devotion. Madhva: the guṇas are the Lord's creation; they bind through
attachment, not by themselves.

## 14.10–14.18 — The dominance, death, and fruit of each guṇa
When sattva predominates, the gates of **light** (*prakāśa*) shine; when rajas, **greed, activity,
restlessness** (*lobha, pravṛtti, ārambha*); when tamas, **darkness, inertia, delusion** (*pramāda,
ālasya, pramoha*). At death: sattva → the **pure worlds** of the knowers of the Highest (*uttama-vidāṃ
lokān*); rajas → among the **action-attached** (*karma-saṅgiṣu*); tamas → in **wombs of the deluded**
(*mūḍha-yoniṣu*). **14.14–15:** dying in sattva → attains the **spotless higher worlds**; in rajas → **born
among the action-driven**; in tamas → **born among the deluded/insentient** (*tiryaktvā*, taking animal-like
birth [VERIFY]).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the death-state reflects the **lifetime's dominant mode** — a life of sattva
naturally crystallises at death into a higher destiny (cf. 8.5–6, the last thought). But even the highest
sattvic world is still *saṃsāra* (cf. 8.16) — only going **beyond** the guṇas is liberation.

## 14.19–14.27 — The Guṇātīta: the one who transcends
**14.19:** *nānyaṃ guṇebhyaḥ kartāraṃ yadā draṣṭānupaśyati / guṇebhyaś ca paraṃ vetti mad-bhāvaṃ so'dhigacchati*
— "when the seer perceives **no agent other than the guṇas** (*guṇa* acting on *guṇa*), and knows what is
**beyond** the guṇas — he attains **My state** (*mad-bhāvam*)." **14.20:** "transcending the three guṇas —
freed from birth/death/old age/sorrow — the embodied one attains **immortality** (*amṛtatvam*)."
**14.21** (Arjuna): "what are the marks of the one who has **transcended** (*guṇātītaḥ*)? How do they behave?"
**14.22–25:** the marks — **equal** in **pleasure and pain** (*sama-duḥkha-sukhaḥ*), **content** (*sva-sthaḥ*)
— **same in a clod, a stone, and gold** (*sama-loṣṭāśma-kāñcanaḥ*); equal in **friend and foe** (*samaḥ
śatrau ca mitre ca*), in **honour and dishonour** (*māna-apamānayos tulyaḥ*); **equal in praise and censure**
(*tulya-nindā-stutiḥ*); **renouncing all undertakings** (*sarva-ārambha-parityāgī*). **14.26 — THE KEY VERSE:**
*māṃ ca yo'vyabhicāreṇa bhakti-yogena sevate / sa guṇān samatītyaitān brahma-bhūyāya kalpate* — "one who
**serves Me by unswerving bhakti-yoga** (*avyabhicāreṇa bhakti-yogena*) — that one **transcends the guṇas**
(*guṇān samatītyaitān*) and becomes **fit for Brahman** (*brahma-bhūyāya kalpate*)."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 14.26 reveals the **means** of transcendence — not by suppressing the guṇas (impossible
— they constitute mind-body) but by **unswerving devotion** to the Lord (as the Self) which disidentifies
from all three modes. *Brahma-bhūyāya kalpate* = becomes fit for the realisation of Brahman.
Rāmānuja: *bhakti-yoga* (14.26) is the supreme path to *guṇātītatva* — devotion to the personal Lord
transcends even the highest guṇa. Madhva: the devotee, by the Lord's grace, rises above prakṛti's sway.
**Cross-refs:** 6.8, 6.9 (the sama-loṣṭa, sama-śatramitra marks); 2.56–57 (the sthitaprajña's marks).
**Layers —** W: "Don't suppress your moods — rise above them by devotion. The free person is equal in pain
and pleasure, praise and blame, gold and dust." / S: the three guṇas; guṇātīta; 14.26 as the means. / Sk:
observe which guṇa dominates you today. / A: Śaṅkara (sattva also binds; transcendence via devotion = Self
knowledge) vs Rāmānuja (bhakti as the path to transcendence).

## 14.27 — Brahman as the ground
*brahmaṇo hi pratiṣṭhāham amṛtasyāvyayasya ca / śāśvatasya ca dharmasya sukhasyaikāntikasya ca* — "I am the
**resting-place** (*pratiṣṭhā*) of Brahman, of immortality and imperishability, of eternal dharma, and of
**absolute bliss** (*sukhasyaikāntikasya*)."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: this verse is central to the Śaṅkara-Rāmānuja debate — Śaṅkara reads it as: the
**personal** Lord (*bhagavān*) is the **ground** of the **impersonal** Brahman — the personal and impersonal
are two aspects of one reality. Rāmānuja: the Lord is superior to the impersonal Brahman — the personal God
is the ultimate. This verse is a key *prasthāna* (foundational text) in the Vedānta debates.

# CHAPTER 15: Puruṣottama Yoga (The Supreme Person) — 20 verses

**At a glance.** The shortest doctrinal chapter. The cosmic **Aśvattha tree** (the world-tree of saṃsāra,
15.1–5) to be felled; the **two puruṣas** — perishable (*kṣara*) and imperishable (*akṣara*) — and the
**third**, the **Puruṣottama** (Supreme Person, 15.16–19); and Kṛṣṇa's self-declaration as that Supreme.

## 15.1–15.5 — The Aśvattha tree
**15.1–2:** the *aśvattha* (peepul/banyan) tree — **roots above** (*ūrdhva-mūlam*), **branches below**
(*adhaḥ-śākham*) — eternal (*avyayam*); its leaves are the Vedas (*chandāṃsi*); its branches grow from the
guṇas, its buds are sense-objects; its secondary roots bind through action (*karma-anubandhīni*). **15.3:**
the **axe** of **detachment** (*asaṅga-śastreṇa dhṛeṇa*, [VERIFY]) fells this firmly-rooted tree. **15.5:**
once felled, one seeks the **supreme state** (*tat padam*) — taking **refuge in the Lord** (*taṃ eva
adhyāyam*, [VERIFY]) — going to which one **does not return** (*yad gatvā na nivartante*).
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the Aśvattha tree is **saṃsāra** — the world of becoming. Its "roots above" = the
Supreme as the source; "branches below" = the material worlds descending into density. It is the *kārya-jagat*
(the world-as-effect) that is cut by *asaṅga* (non-attachment). Rāmānuja: the tree is prakṛti sustained by
the Lord; the axe is *vairāgya* combined with *bhakti*.
**Cross-refs:** Kaṭha Upaniṣad 6.1 (the same *aśvattha* image); Muṇḍaka 3.1.1.
**Layers —** W: "The material world is like a vast tree — cut its roots of attachment, and seek the Divine
beyond it." / S: the aśvattha as saṃsāra; asaṅga-dhṛ. / Sk: identify one root of attachment and cut it. / A:
the Upaniṣadic source; Śaṅkara's symbolism.

## 15.6–15.15 — The Supreme abode and the Lord's immanence
**15.6:** *na tad bhāsayate sūryo na śaśāṅko na pāvakaḥ / yad gatvā na nivartante tad dhāma paramaṃ mama* —
"neither sun nor moon nor fire illumines that; having reached it, one does not return — that is My **supreme
abode** (*tad dhāma paramam*)." **15.7:** *mamaivāṃśo jīva-loke jīva-bhūtaḥ sanātanaḥ* — "an **eternal
fragment** (*amśa*) of Myself, becoming the *jīva* in the world of living beings — draws the **senses**
(*manaḥ-ṣaṣṭhānīndriyāṇi*) around the mind, which are in prakṛti." **15.15:** *sarvasya cāhaṃ hṛdi sanniviṣṭo*
— "seated in the hearts of all; from Me come memory, knowledge, and their loss; I alone am to be known by
all the Vedas; I am the author of the Vedānta (*vedānta-kṛt*) and the knower of the Vedas (*veda-vid*)."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 15.7 — the *jīva* (individual soul) is an **amśa** (fragment) of the Lord — NOT a
separate creation but a **limited manifestation** of the one consciousness, conditioned by the mind and
senses. Rāmānuja: *amśa* = a **real, eternal** mode (*prakāra*) of the Lord — related as spark to fire.
Madhva: *amśa* = the *jīva* is a real, eternally distinct **fraction** of God's power (*prabhāva*), never
identical.

## 15.16–15.19 — The THREE PURUṢAS and the Puruṣottama
**15.16–17 — LANDMARK:** *dvā imau puruṣau loke kṣaraś cākṣara eva ca / kṣaraḥ sarvāṇi bhūtāni
kūṭa-stho'kṣara ucyate / uttamaḥ puruṣas tv anyaḥ paramātmety udāhṛtaḥ* — "two beings in this world: the
**perishable** (*kṣara*) — all creatures; and the **imperishable** (*akṣara*) — the unchanging (*kūṭastha*).
But **beyond** both is the **Supreme Person** (*uttamaḥ puruṣaḥ*), called the **Paramātmā** — who, entering
the three worlds, **sustains all** (*bibharti*)." **15.18–19:** *yasmāt kṣaram atīto'ham akṣarād api cottamaḥ
/ ato'smi loke vede ca prathitaḥ puruṣottamaḥ* — "because I transcend the perishable and am higher than the
imperishable, I am celebrated in both world and scripture as **Puruṣottama**."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the **three-tiered ontology** — (1) *kṣara* = all mutable beings (body, world);
(2) *akṣara* = the unchanging Self (*kūṭastha*), identical in all; (3) *Puruṣottama* = the Lord in His
**total** nature (including the power to create/sustain/dissolve), which the individual Self does not have in
isolation. The *Puruṣottama* is the Self seen in its **universal** aspect (as the *Īśvara*).
Rāmānuja: the three are **really distinct** — (1) *acit* (matter); (2) *cit* (individual souls); (3) **Īśvara**
(the Lord). *Puruṣottama* = Nārāyaṇa, above both. Madhva: the five-fold distinction (*pañca-bheda*) — God,
soul, matter, the relation between them, the relation's nature — all are grounded here.
**Cross-refs:** 13.22; 10.20; 18.61.
**Layers —** W: "There are three levels — the changing world, the unchanging soul, and the Supreme Person who
is above both and sustains all. Krishna is that Supreme." / S: kṣara/akṣara/Puruṣottama; the three-tiered
ontology. / Sk: which level do you identify with — body, soul, or the Lord? / A: Śaṅkara (Puruṣottama =
Īśvara-aspect of Self) vs Rāmānuja (three real categories) vs Madhva (pañca-bheda).

## 15.20 — The closing call
*yo vetti sa puruṣottamaṃ... sa sarva-vid bhajati māṃ sarva-bhāvena bharata* — "whoever knows the
Puruṣottama — that one, **all-knowing** (*sarva-vid*), **worships Me with his whole being** (*sarva-bhāvena*)."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: knowing the Puruṣottama is not mere intellection but the realisation that **leads to
total devotion** — knowledge and worship are not rivals but one movement.
