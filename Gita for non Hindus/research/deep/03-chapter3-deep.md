# Deep Commentary — Chapter 3: Karma Yoga (The Yoga of Action)

> *The chapter of niṣkāma karma — selfless action. Sanskrit verified (IITK). Landmarks: 3.5, 3.19, 3.21, 3.27,
> 3.35, 3.37, 3.42–43.*

**Chapter at a glance.** Arjuna asks (3.1–2): if knowledge is superior, why push me to terrible action?
Kṛṣṇa replies that two paths exist (3.3) — jñāna-yoga (contemplation) and karma-yoga (action) — but **no one
can avoid action** (3.5). He teaches the doctrine of *yajña* (3.9–16), the *lokasaṅgraha* (world-welfare)
motive for the wise who need no personal gain (3.20–26), the *guṇa-karma* analysis (3.27–29), and ends with
the psychology of desire as the enemy (3.36–43).

**The schools on Chapter 3:** Śaṅkara reads karma-yoga as *citta-śuddhi* for jñāna; Rāmānuja as the path of
*īśvara-arpaṇa* (offering action to God) open to all; Madhva as *vi hita-karma* (ordained duty) done in devotion.

---

## 3.1–3.8 — Action is inescapable
**3.3:** two *niṣṭhās* (paths) — *jñāna-yogena sāṃkhyānāṃ, karma-yogena yoginām* (knowledge for the
contemplative, action for the active). **3.4:** not by abstaining does one attain *naiṣkarmya* (freedom from
action), nor by mere renunciation, perfection. **3.5 — LANDMARK:** *na hi kaścit kṣaṇam api jātu tiṣṭhaty
akarmakṛt* — "no one can remain even a moment without acting; all are **forced** (*avaśaḥ*) by the guṇas."
**3.6:** the hypocrite (*mithyācāraḥ*) who restrains organs but mentally dwells on objects. **3.7:** superior
is the one who acts with senses controlled. **3.8:** "action is better than inaction; even bodily maintenance
fails without it."
**Padārtha:** *niṣṭhā* (3.3, "firm abidance/path"), *naiṣkarmya* (3.4, "actionlessness"), *avaśaḥ* (3.5,
"helplessly/forced"), *mithyācāraḥ* (3.6, "hypocrite"), *karmendriyaiḥ* (3.7, "with organs of action").
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 3.5 is axiomatic — the mind-body complex (*prakṛti*) **must** act; *naiṣkarmya* is
not physical inaction but the **inner** freedom from the sense of doership (cf. 4.18, 5.8–9). Rāmānuja: the
*niṣṭhā* of karma-yoga is for those still in the world; both paths reach the same goal (5.4–5). Madhva: duty
is non-negotiable; even the jñānī must maintain the body.
**Misreading:** that 3.5 means fatalism ("I'm forced, so whatever I do is fine"); the verse establishes the
**necessity** of action, not its **indifference** — the *how* (karma-yoga) is taught next.
**Layers —** W: "You can't escape action — so learn to act rightly." / S: naiṣkarmya as inner freedom, not
outer inaction. / Sk: where do you fake restraint while the mind craves? (3.6). / A: Śaṅkara's
guṇa-vaśyatva; the jñāna/karma niṣṭhā debate.

## 3.9–3.16 — The doctrine of Yajña (sacrifice)
**3.9:** "action other than for *yajña* binds (*loko 'yaṃ karma-bandhanaḥ*); perform action for that end,
detached." **3.10–13:** Prajāpati created beings with sacrifice (*saha-yajñāḥ prajāḥ*); the gods nourished
by sacrifice nourish you in return (*parasparaṃ bhāvayantaḥ*) — mutual sustenance; he who eats without
offering is a *stena* (thief). **3.14–15:** the cosmic chain: beings ← food ← rain (*parjanyāt*) ← sacrifice
← action ← Veda ← the imperishable Brahman (*brahmākṣara-samudbhavam*). **3.16:** he who does not turn this
wheel of sacrifice "lives in vain" (*moghaṃ pārtha sa jīvati*), delighting only the senses.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: *yajña* = the *īśvara-arpaṇa-bhāvanā* — the spirit in which all action is offered;
the cosmic chain (3.14–15) shows that all action is rooted in Brahman, and thus should be offered back.
Rāmānuja: the *yajña* cycle is real cosmic interdependence; *lokasaṅgraha* (world-maintenance) is a duty.
Madhva: sacrifice is the Lord's ordinance; the cosmic order is His.
**Significance:** the **interiorisation of Vedic ritual** — the Gītā transforms external sacrifice into the
spirit of all action. "Even a leaf" (9.26) is the culmination.
**Cross-refs:** 4.24 (brahmārpaṇam — Brahman is the offering), 9.26–28.
**Layers —** W: "Everything is interconnected — take only what you've offered to." / S: the yajña-cycle;
interiorisation. / Sk: offer one daily act as sacrifice. / A: Śaṅkara's īśvara-arpaṇa-bhāvana; Vedic→Vedāntic
transformation.

## 3.17–3.19 — The liberated one has no *duty*, yet acts
**3.17–18:** one who revels in the Self (*ātmaratiḥ*), satisfied in the Self alone (*ātmany eva santuṣṭaḥ*)
— for such a one there is **no duty** (*kāryaṃ na vidyate*); no stake in action or inaction. **3.19 —
LANDMARK:** *tasmād asaktaḥ satataṃ kāryaṃ karma samācara* — "therefore, ever detached, duly perform action;
acting detached, one attains the Supreme (*paramāpnoti pūruṣaḥ*)."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: 3.17–18 describe the *jñānī* for whom *śāstra* (scriptural duty) has no binding
force — but 3.19 says even so, act (for *lokasaṅgraha*, 3.20). The apparent tension: the free one needs no
duty yet acts for the world's sake. Rāmānuja: 3.19 is the operative verse — detached action for all.
**Layers —** W: "Even the free keep acting — not from need, but for the world." / S: ātmarati; the tension of
3.17–19. / Sk: serve without needing to. / A: jñānī's non-obligation vs lokasaṅgraha.

## 3.20–3.26 — Lokasaṅgraha: the welfare of the world
**3.20:** *janakādayaḥ* (King Janaka and others) attained perfection through action alone. **3.21 —
LANDMARK:** *yad yad ācarati śreṣṭhas tat tad evetaro janaḥ* — "whatever the great do, others follow; the
standard they set, the world pursues." **3.22–24:** even Kṛṣṇa Himself has nothing to gain (*na me pārthāsti
kartavyaṃ triṣu lokeṣu kiñcana*) — yet He acts vigilantly (*atandritaḥ*), lest men follow His inaction and
worlds perish (*utsīdeyur ime lokā*). **3.25:** as the ignorant act attached, so the wise act unattached,
desiring the world's welfare (*lokasaṅgraham*). **3.26:** the wise should not unsettle (*na buddhi-bhedaṃ
janayed*) the ignorant attached to action — but by acting rightly make them act too.
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: the *jñānī* acts for *lokasaṅgraha* — to set an example (3.21), not from any personal
need; Kṛṣṇa's own action (3.22–24) is the model. 3.26 warns against confusing the unripe by teaching
non-action prematurely. Rāmānuja: *lokasaṅgraha* is real social duty. Madhva: the Lord's *līlā* (play) as
example.
**Significance:** 3.21 is the Gītā's **ethic of leadership** — the great are responsible for what they
normalise. Kṛṣṇa's self-action (3.22–24) grounds the duty of the liberated to remain engaged.
**Layers —** W: "What leaders do, the world follows — so act well, even when you need nothing." / S:
lokasaṅgraha; the leadership ethic. / Sk: whose example are you? / A: Śaṅkara's jñānī-lokasaṅgraha;
Kṛṣṇa's līlā-varta.

## 3.27–3.35 — Guṇa-karma: the guṇas act, not the ego
**3.27 — LANDMARK:** *prakṛteḥ kriyamāṇāni guṇaiḥ karmāṇi sarvaśaḥ / ahaṅkāra-vimūḍhātmā kartāham iti
manyate* — "all actions are done by the guṇas of prakṛti; the ego-deluded thinks 'I am the doer.'" **3.28:**
the knower of truth (*tattvavit*) sees "guṇas acting on guṇas" and is not attached. **3.29:** don't disturb
the ignorant deluded by guṇas. **3.30:** renounce all actions in Me (*mayi sarvāṇi karmāṇi saṃnyasya*),
desireless — fight! **3.31–32:** those who follow this with faith (*śraddhā*) are freed; those who cavil are
lost. **3.33:** even the wise act according to their *svabhāva* (own nature) — *prakṛtiṃ yānti bhūtāni*.
**3.34:** liking (*rāga*) and aversion (*dveṣa*) for sense-objects are fixed — don't come under their sway
(*paripanthinau*, "they are highway robbers"). **3.35 — LANDMARK:** *śreyān svadharmo viguṇaḥ paradharmāt
svanuṣṭhitāt* — "better one's own duty though imperfectly done than another's well done; death in one's own
duty is good (*śreyaḥ*); another's is fearful (*bhayāvahaḥ*)."
**Commentary:**
- **Śaṅkara:** 3.27 is the key to *akartṛtva* (non-doership) — the Self witnesses; the guṇas act. Realising
this ends bondage. 3.35's *svadharma* is one's *varṇāśrama-dharma* (stage/role duty); "another's duty"
(*paradharma*) is perilous because it is against one's nature and thus cannot steady the mind for knowledge.
- **Rāmānuja:** 3.27 shows the Lord as the *antaryāmin* behind the guṇas. 3.35: each soul has its own
*svekṣaṇa-dharma* (natural aptitude for worship).
- **Madhva:** 3.35 — *svabhāva* (inborn nature) is real; deviation from it is *adharma*.
- **Abhinavagupta:** 3.27 — the recognition that consciousness is not the doer liberates.
**Misreading of 3.35:** that it defends caste rigidity. Śaṅkara reads *svadharma* as the duty prescribed by
scripture for one's station; the "better than another's" principle is **vocational**, not necessarily
birth-based — the Gītā's own *guṇa-karma-vibhāga* (4.13) ties it to quality and action. Handle in file 11.
**Layers —** W: "The guṇas act, not 'you'; know this and be free. Do your own job, even badly, rather than
another's well." / S: akartṛtva; svadharma vs paradharma. / Sk: where do you claim doership? Where do you envy
another's role? / A: Śaṅkara's guṇa-akartṛtva; the svadharma/caste question (file 11).

## 3.36–3.43 — Desire (*kāma*) as the enemy
**3.36** (Arjuna): *atha kena prayukto'yaṃ pāpaṃ carati pūruṣaḥ* — "by what is a man impelled to sin
unwillingly, as if dragged by force?" **3.37 — LANDMARK:** *kāma eṣa krodha eṣa rajo-guṇa-samudbhavaḥ* — "it
is desire (*kāma*), which becomes anger (*krodha*), born of *rajas*; the great-devouring, great-sinful —
know it as the enemy." **3.38:** as fire is covered by smoke, a mirror by dust, a womb by the foetus — so
knowledge is covered by desire. **3.39:** desire is the **constant enemy** (*nitya-vairiṇā*) of the wise, an
unquenchable fire (*duṣpūreṇānalena*). **3.40:** its seat is the senses, mind, intellect — through them it
clouds knowledge and deludes. **3.41:** therefore restrain the senses first (*ādau*) and slay this destroyer
of knowledge and realisation. **3.42–3.43 — THE HIERARCHY:** senses → mind → intellect → the Self (*saḥ*)
above all. "Knowing the Self as higher than the intellect, steadying the lower self by the Self (*ātmanā*),
slay this enemy — desire (*kāma-rūpam*), hard to assail."
**Commentary:** Śaṅkara: *kāma* is not mere sexual desire but the **craving** (*tṛṣṇā*) that includes all
attachment; it is the *rajas*-born root of bondage. 3.42–43 gives the **soteriological map**: the Self is
above all instruments — rest there and the enemy dissolves. Rāmānuja: the Lord alone can truly slay desire;
approach through Him. Madhva: desire arises from *prakṛti*, not the Self; knowledge of the Lord's greatness
weakens it. Abhinavagupta: the "enemy" is the contraction of consciousness into ego-desire.
**Cross-refs:** 2.62–63 (the chain), 16.21 (lust/anger/greed = the three gates of hell).
**Layers —** W: "Desire is the enemy — it hides knowledge like smoke hides fire. Go above the mind to the
Self, and slay it." / S: kāma-krodha; the hierarchy; the coverings. / Sk: trace one desire through the
hierarchy; rest in the witness. / A: Śaṅkara's tṛṣṇā; the soteriological ladder; the schools on who slays.
