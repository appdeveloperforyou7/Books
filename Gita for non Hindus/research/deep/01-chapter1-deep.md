# Deep Commentary — Chapter 1: Arjunaviṣāda Yoga (Arjuna's Dejection)

> *Verse-by-verse working reference. Sanskrit verified against IITK Gita Supersite (Śaṅkara recension, 47
> verses) + Gita Press Gorakhpur. Two flagged variants below (1.26 ṝ; 1.38 pātakam). Not for the reader
> verbatim — the source from which Chapter 1's layers are authored.*

**Chapter at a glance.** Chapter 1 is the *human problem* the whole Gītā answers. It is largely narrative:
the blind king Dhṛtarāṣṭra's opening question (1.1), Sañjaya's battlefield report (1.2–1.27), Duryodhana's
war-council speech (1.3–1.11), the sounding of conches (1.12–1.19), Arjuna's request to survey the armies
(1.21–1.25), his sight of his own kin (1.26–1.28), and his collapse — physical, moral, theological — into
grief (1.29–1.46), ending with him dropping his bow and sitting down (1.47). The doctrine is not yet taught;
the *crisis* is established.

**Why the chapter matters for the layered reader:** it makes Arjuna's *viṣāda* (sorrow) legible as a *dharma*
dilemma, not cowardice — the universal human situation of conscience colliding with duty.

**The commentators on Chapter 1:** Śaṅkara reads Arjuna's collapse as *moha* (delusion/attachment) needing
the cure of knowledge; Rāmānuja stresses the pathos of *svajana* (one's own people) and the Lord's role as
charioteer-guide; Madhva emphasises that Arjuna's reluctance, though understandable, must yield to the Lord's
will; Abhinavagupta reads the battlefield as the field of the heart and Arjuna's grief as the soul's
anguish before awakening.

---

## 1.1 — Dhṛtarāṣṭra
**Devanagari:** धर्मक्षेत्रे कुरुक्षेत्रे समवेता युयुत्सवः। मामकाः पाण्डवाश्चैव किमकुर्वत सञ्जय॥
**IAST:** dharmakṣetre kurukṣetre samavetā yuyutsavaḥ / māmakāḥ pāṇḍavāś caiva kim akurvata sañjaya

**Padārtha:** *dharmakṣetre-kurukṣetre* (loc. dual, "at the field of dharma, at Kurukshetra"), *samavetāḥ*
("assembled"), *yuyutsavaḥ* (*yu-√yudh*, desid. "desiring to fight"), *māmakāḥ* ("mine — my sons"),
*pāṇḍavāḥ* ("the sons of Pāṇḍu"), *kim akurvata* ("what did they do").

**Literal:** On the field of dharma, at Kurukshetra, assembled and eager to fight, what did my men and the
Pāṇḍavas do, Sañjaya?

**Significance & commentary.** The whole Gītā opens through the blind king's anxious question — *blindness*
(here physical) is the book's recurring metaphor for *avidyā*. *Māmakāḥ* ("mine") vs *pāṇḍavāḥ* ("theirs")
already names the disease: possessiveness, *mamatā*. Śaṅkara notes the loaded place-name: *dharmakṣetra*
("field of dharma") signals that this battle will decide righteousness, not merely thrones.
**Misreading:** reading this as a neutral logistics question; it is charged with dread (he fears the outcome
precisely *because* it is dharmakṣetra).
**Layers —** W: "The blind king asks what is happening on the field of duty." / S: dharmakṣetra; mamatā. /
Sk: notice where possessiveness ("mine") distorts your sight. / A: blindness = avidyā; the frame as metaphor.

## 1.2 — Sañjaya
**Devanagari:** दृष्ट्वा तु पाण्डवानीकं व्यूढं दुर्योधनस्तदा। आचार्यमुपसङ्गम्य राजा वचनमब्रवीत्॥
**IAST:** dṛṣṭvā tu pāṇḍavānīkaṃ vyūḍhaṃ duryodhanas tadā / ācāryam upasaṅgamya rājā vacanam abravīt

**Padārtha:** *vyūḍham* (*vi-√ūh* p.p.p. "arrayed"), *upasaṅgamya* (absol. "approaching"), *ācāryam*
(teacher = Droṇa).

**Literal:** Seeing the Pāṇḍava army arrayed, Duryodhana then approached the teacher and spoke.

**Significance.** Sañjaya's report begins with *Duryodhana* seeing — and reacting with strategic anxiety
(approaching his martial guru). The narrative subtly contrasts Duryodhana's fear-driven action with the calm
that will follow. *Duryodhana* (etym. "difficult to fight" / [trad.] "ill-armed in the kingdom of light")
stands as the type of *adharma*.
**Layers —** W: "Duryodhana, alarmed, goes to his teacher to size up the foe." / S: the cast; Droṇa. / Sk:
notice fear as a driver of speech. / A: the "seeing" motif — who sees rightly begins in ch. 2.

## 1.3–1.9 — Duryodhana's war-council speech
These seven verses are Duryodhana's catalogue to Droṇa of the warriors on **both** sides. **Padārtha**
highlights: *dhīmatā* (1.3, "wise" — sarcastic, since Dhṛṣṭadyumna was Droṇa's destined killer, his own
pupil); *mahārathaḥ* ("great-chariot-warrior," a technical rank); *aparyāptam* vs *paryāptam* (1.10,
"unlimited / limited"). The names (Yuyudhāna, Virāṭa, Drupada, Dhṛṣṭaketu, Bhīṣma, Karṇa, Kṛpa, Aśvatthāmā…)
are the dramatis personae; many are *guru-figures* to Arjuna — the seed of his later grief.
**Significance.** The catalogue does double duty: it builds the epic scale, and it plants the moral trap —
these are not strangers but *teachers and kinsmen*. Duryodhana's anxiety ("our strength needs Bhīṣma's
protection," 1.10–11) reveals his unspoken doubt; Bhīṣma's lion-roar (1.12) is staged to hearten him.
**Layers —** W: "Duryodhana names the champions and betrays his own fear." / S: the warrior-ranks and the
kinship-web. / Sk: see how strategic talk can mask inner panic. / A: the dramatic irony — the very kin Arjuna
will grieve are listed here as foes.

## 1.10–1.11 — Duryodhana's doubt
**1.10:** अपर्याप्तं तदस्माकं बलं भीष्माभिरक्षितम् … पर्याप्तं त्विदमेतेषां बलं भीमारभिरक्षितम्
**Literal:** "Our army, guarded by Bhīṣma, is unlimited; theirs, guarded by Bhīma, is limited."
**1.11:** "Therefore all of you, at your posts, must protect Bhīṣma alone."
**Significance.** A famous crux: *aparyāptam* can mean "unlimited" (so Śaṅkara) OR "insufficient/limited"
(other readings) — Duryodhana's speech either boasts or betrays fear. Śaṅkara reads it as bravado masking
anxiety, which is why Bhīṣma must *hearten* him (1.12). Either way, the verse shows that the confidence of
*adharma* is brittle.
**Misreading:** treating 1.10 as a calm strategic assessment; it is panic dressed as command.
**Layers —** W: "Duryodhana's bravado barely hides his fear." / S: the aparyāptam ambiguity. / Sk: notice
your own over-compensating confidence. / A: Śaṅkara's reading of bravado-as-fear.

## 1.12–1.19 — The sounding of the conches
Bhīṣma's lion-roar and conch (1.12); the tumult of instruments (1.13); then the *divine* conches of the
Pāṇḍava side — Kṛṣṇa's Pāñcajanya, Arjuna's Devadatta, Bhīma's Pauṇḍra, Yudhiṣṭhira's Anantavijaya, etc.
(1.14–1.18). **1.19:** that sound shatters the *hearts* of Dhṛtarāṣṭra's sons — the first sign the Kauravas
are already inwardly losing.
**Padārtha:** *siṃhanādam* (1.12, "lion-roar"), *tumulaḥ* (1.13/19, "tumultuous"), *vyadārayat* (1.19,
"shattered/cleft").
**Significance.** Each named conch is iconic (Kṛṣṇa's from the demon Pāñcajana; Arjuna's a divine gift).
The *sound* is the first weapon — symbolic of *nāda*, the sacred vibration. Crucially, *Kṛṣṇa and Arjuna*
blow their conches *together* (1.14): the divine-human partnership is announced before a word is spoken.
The Kaurava hearts "split" (1.19) — a foreshadowing.
**Layers —** W: "The two sides sound their war-horns; the Pāṇḍava blast already shakes the foe." / S: the
named conches; Kṛṣṇa-Arjuna unity. / Sk: the power of a sacred sound to shift the inner field. / A: nāda;
the dramatic foreshadow in 1.19.

## 1.20–1.25 — Arjuna raises his bow; the chariot placed between the armies
**1.20:** Arjuna (*kapidhvaja*, "monkey-flagged") raises the Gāṇḍīva as weapons begin to fly.
**1.21–1.23:** Arjuna's *first* speech — "Place my chariot between the two armies… that I may see those who
stand eager to fight." **1.24–1.25:** Kṛṣṇa does so, and says "Behold these Kurus assembled."
**Padārtha:** *hṛṣīkeśa* (Kṛṣṇa, "Lord of the senses"), *guḍākeśa* (Arjuna, "lord of sleep/conquest of
sleep"), *acyuta* ("the unfalling"), *senayoḥ ubhayoḥ madhye* ("between both armies").
**Significance.** The pivot of the chapter: Arjuna's curiosity ("let me *see* whom I fight") becomes the
occasion of his undoing — to see one's kin as the enemy is to break. *Guḍākeśa* (conqueror of sleep =
disciplined) ironically collapses; *Hṛṣīkeśa* (master of the senses) will be the one to restore him.
Śaṅkara notes the dramatic irony: Arjuna asks to see, and seeing becomes the wound.
**Misreading:** thinking Arjuna's request is tactical; it is the beginning of his moral paralysis.
**Layers —** W: "Arjuna asks to look at the foe — and is broken by what he sees." / S: the name-ironies. /
Sk: the moments where clearer sight brings pain. / A: seeing-as-wound; the name-pair (sense-lord vs
sleep-lord).

## 1.26–1.27 — Arjuna sees his own people
**1.26:** *पितॄन् … पितामहान् आचार्यान् मातुलान् भ्रातॄन् पुत्रान् पौत्रान् सखīन्* — fathers, grandfathers,
teachers, maternal uncles, brothers, sons, grandsons, friends. **1.27:** fathers-in-law, friends, *both*
armies — "all those kinsmen." **(Variant flag:** 1.26 uses long vocalic ṛ — *pitṝn*, *bhrātṝn*; some
digital texts mis-render with a nukta. Use ṝ.)
**Significance.** The catalogue of relations makes the war a *fratricide*. The horror is not death but
*killing one's own*. This is the raw material of Arjuna's *dharma*-crisis: his *kṣatriya* duty (fight a just
war) collides with his duty to *kin* and *guru*. No abstract problem — a torn heart.
**Layers —** W: "He looks across and sees not enemies but family." / S: the relational ethics of dharma. /
Sk: a duty that conflicts with love. / A: the *svajana* motif; dharma-saṅkaṭa (dharma-dilemma).

## 1.28 — Arjuna's grief erupts
**Devanagari:** कृपया परयाऽऽविष्टो विषीदन्निदमब्रवीत्। दृष्ट्वेमं स्वजनं कृष्ण युयुत्सुं समुपस्थितम्॥
**IAST:** kṛpayā parayā 'viṣṭo viṣīdan idam abravīt / dṛṣṭvemaṃ svajanaṃ kṛṣṇa yuyutsuṃ samupasthitam
**Padārtha:** *kṛpayā parayā* ("with supreme compassion"), *āviṣṭaḥ* ("overwhelmed/seized"), *viṣīdan*
("grieving"), *svajanam* ("one's own people").
**Literal:** Seized by supreme compassion and grieving, he spoke: "Seeing my own kin assembled, eager to
fight…"
**Significance.** *Kṛpā* (compassion) is, for Rāmānuja, the very mark of a *sāttvic* soul — Arjuna's grief is
not ignoble but *misdirected*. Śaṅkara: this compassion is coloured by *attachment* (*rāga*) to "mine"; the
cure is not cruelty but *discrimination*. The chapter's title-word *viṣāda* (dejection) crystallises here.
**Misreading:** condemning Arjuna as weak; the Gītā treats him as a noble soul in genuine distress.
**Layers —** W: "Compassion overwhelms him — a good man frozen by love." / S: kṛpā as sāttvic but misdirected.
/ Sk: distinguish compassion from attachment. / A: Śaṅkara (kṛpā tainted by rāga) vs Rāmānuja (noble pathos).

## 1.29–1.30 — The body's collapse
**1.29:** limbs give way, mouth dries, body trembles, hair stands on end (*vepathuḥ, romaharṣaḥ*).
**1.30:** the Gāṇḍīva slips, skin burns, cannot stand, mind reels (*bhramatīva*).
**Significance.** A precise *psychosomatic* description of acute distress — the Gītā knows the body registers
moral crisis before the intellect names it. The slipping bow (1.30) anticipates the dropped bow (1.47): the
warrior's identity is dissolving. Abhinavagupta: the body is the visible surface of an inner dissolution that
precedes rebirth of understanding.
**Layers —** W: "His whole body fails him — grief is physical." / S: the physiology of crisis. / Sk: heed the
body's signals under moral strain. / A: the bow-as-identity; dissolution before insight.

## 1.31–1.32 — "I see no good in this"
**1.31:** "I see ill omens (*viparītāni nimittāni*); no good (*śreyaḥ*) in slaying my kin."
**1.32:** "I desire neither victory, nor kingdom, nor pleasures — what use, Govinda, is kingdom, enjoyments,
or even life?"
**Significance.** The deepest line in the chapter: the *goals* for which one would fight (victory, throne,
pleasure, even life) are suddenly worthless. This is the authentic beginning of *vairāgya*
(dispassion) — though, as Śaṅkara warns, Arjuna's is born of *dejection*, not *knowledge*, and so is unstable
until ch. 2 grounds it. Madhva: a true turning, but it must be completed by surrender to the Lord's will.
**Misreading:** that Arjuna has attained detachment; he has only its shadow — grief. Real detachment comes
later.
**Layers —** W: "All he was fighting for now means nothing to him." / S: vairāgya vs viṣāda. / Sk: when your
goals lose meaning — is it wisdom or burnout? / A: Śaṅkara's "grief-born dispassion" critique.

## 1.33–1.38 — The relational & moral argument
**1.33–1.34:** "For *whose* sake we wanted kingdom — those very people stand here to die."
**1.35:** "I would not kill them even for the three worlds, much less for the earth — not even if they kill
me." **1.36:** "What joy in killing the sons of Dhṛtarāṣṭra? Sin (*pāpam*) alone would cling to us — even
though they are *ātatāyinaḥ* (aggressors)." **1.37:** "How could we be happy after killing our own?"
**1.38:** "Though these, greed-blinded, see no fault, *we* who see should turn back." **(Variant flag:** 1.38
final word = **pātakam** (Gita Press/Śaṅkara) vs pāpakam (some texts). Use pātakam.)
**Padārtha:** *ātatāyinaḥ* (1.36, technical legal term — an *ātatāyī* is one of the six heinous aggressors whom
śāstra permits a king to kill; Arjuna concedes the Kauravas qualify yet still hesitates), *lobhāpahata-cetasaḥ*
(1.38, "hearts overpowered by greed").
**Significance.** Arjuna's reasoning is sophisticated, not cowardly: he argues from *relation* (they are
kin), *consequence* (sin will cling), and *asymmetry* (they are deluded by greed; we are not, so we are more
responsible). The *ātatāyī* term shows he knows the law permits the war — his refusal is moral, not legal.
This is why Kṛṣṇa cannot answer him with a legal ruling; the answer must be metaphysical (ch. 2+).
**Misreading:** that Arjuna is morally *right* and Kṛṣṇa overrides a correct conscience. The Gītā's position
is subtler: Arjuna's *feeling* is noble, his *conclusion* (to abandon duty out of attachment) is not.
**Layers —** W: "He argues it is wrong to kill one's own — even a just war." / S: ātatāyī; the moral
asymmetry. / Sk: when does compassion become an excuse to evade duty? / A: why Kṛṣṇa's reply must go beyond
law — the failure of *deontic* answers here.

## 1.39–1.45 — Theological argument: *kula-dharma* and *varṇa-saṅkara*
Arjuna shifts from the personal to the *social-theological*: war destroys *kula* (family/clan); with the clan
perish its *kula-dharmāḥ* (1.40); *adharma* breeds *strī-doṣa* (corruption of women) and thence
*varṇa-saṅkara* — mixture of castes (1.41); this dooms the family to hell and even the ancestors fall,
deprived of *piṇḍa-udaka* rites (1.42–1.44); "we are resolved to commit a great sin for the sake of royal
pleasure" (1.45).
**Padārtha:** *kulakṣaya* ("destruction of the clan"), *varṇasaṅkaraḥ* ("mixture of castes"),
*piṇḍodaka-kriyā* (the śrāddha rite of rice-balls and water to ancestors), *jāti-dharmāḥ* (caste-duties).
**Significance — and the book's responsibility.** These verses invoke the social order of their age (clan,
caste, women, ancestor-rites). They are *Arjuna's* argument, not necessarily the Gītā's final word — and
Kṛṣṇa will *not* answer by defending varṇa-saṅkara; He will reframe the whole question around the immortal
Self (2.x) and later address varṇa differently (4.13, handled critically in file 11). The author must present
these verses honestly as Arjuna's genuine fears, contextualise the social framework, and *not* use them to
defend caste. Abhinavagupta reads *kula* symbolically as the inner "family" of one's faculties.
**Misreading:** (a) citing 1.41 as the Gītā's "defence of caste/women's purity" — it is *Arjuna's* anxious
rhetoric, which the Lord will surpass; (b) dismissing it as mere patriarchy — it reflects a real ancient
social-religious logic (continuity of rites) that the reader must be helped to understand, not ridiculed.
**Layers —** W: "Arjuna fears the war will tear apart families, duties, and the social order itself." / S:
kula-dharma, varṇa-saṅkara, śrāddha — contextualised. / Sk: hold the question of social order vs the
individual conscience. / A: why Kṛṣṇa refuses the social frame; Abhinavagupta's symbolic kula; the caste
treatment (file 11).

## 1.46 — "Let them kill me unarmed; that is better"
**Literal:** "If the armed sons of Dhṛtarāṣṭra were to slay me, unresisting and weaponless, in battle — that
would be far better for me."
**Significance.** Arjuna prefers death to fratricide — the limit-case of his crisis. He would rather be the
*victim* than the *agent* of adharma. This sets up Kṛṣṇa's central correction: the question is not *whether*
to act but *in what spirit*; and the "I" who fears killing is itself imperishable (2.x). Rāmānuja: Arjuna's
heart is here fully disclosed — he is ready to renounce even life.
**Layers —** W: "He would rather die than kill his own." / S: the victim-vs-agent framing. / Sk: when
non-action is itself a choice with consequences. / A: the setup for Kṛṣṇa's "you cannot really kill" (2.19+).

## 1.47 — Sañjaya closes: Arjuna sits down
**Devanagari:** एवमुक्त्वाऽर्जुनः संख्ये रथोपस्थ उपाविशत्। विसृज्य सशरं चापं शोकसंविग्नमानसः॥
**IAST:** evam uktvā 'rjunaḥ saṃkhye rathopastha upāviśat / visṛjya sa-śaraṃ cāpaṃ śoka-saṃvigna-mānasaḥ
**Padārtha:** *rathopastha* ("the seat of the chariot"), *visṛjya* (absol. "casting aside"), *sa-śaram*
("with arrows"), *śoka-saṃvigna-mānasaḥ* ("mind overwhelmed/aggrieved by grief").
**Literal:** Having spoken thus on the battlefield, Arjuna cast down his bow with arrows and sat on the
chariot-seat, his mind overwhelmed with grief.
**Significance.** The chapter's iconic close: the warrior *drops his weapon and sits* — not a refusal out of
cowardice but a *paralysis of conscience*. The chapter titled *Arjuna's Dejection* ends on this image of a
great soul brought low by an unresolved moral wound. It is the perfect *setup*: the reader is now as desperate
as Arjuna for Kṛṣṇa to speak. Chapter 2 begins the cure.
**Misreading:** reading "sat down" as final surrender; it is the *beginning* of the dialogue — the posture of
a disciple about to receive teaching.
**Layers —** W: "He drops his bow and sits, undone — and the teaching is about to begin." / S: viṣāda as the
precondition of śravaṇa (receiving). / Sk: the times breakdown is the threshold of insight. / A: the
disciple-posture; why the Gītā could not start without this crisis.
