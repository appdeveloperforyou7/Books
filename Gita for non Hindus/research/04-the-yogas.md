# 04 — The Paths of Yoga (Karma, Bhakti, Jñāna, Dhyana)

The Gita's central teaching is that there are **multiple valid paths** (*mārgas*) to the same goal, suited
to different temperaments. The word **yoga** (योग) itself must be clarified first: it does not (here) mean
the physical postures of modern yoga studios.

## 0. What "yoga" means in the Gita

- **Yoga** = *yuj* ("to yoke/unite") → union of the individual self with the Divine; and, **a disciplined
  method/path** for achieving it.
- In the Gita, "yoga" often means **equanimity and skill in action**: *"yoga is skill in action"* (2.50);
  *"sameness is called yoga"* (2.48).
- **Physical postures (*āsana*)** belong to later Haṭha/Rāja yoga (Patañjali, *Yoga Sūtras*); the Gita's
  chapter 6 (*Dhyāna yoga*) gives a brief meditation practice but is not a posture manual.

---

## 1. Karma Yoga (कर्म योग) — the path of selfless action
*Chapters 3, 4, 5 are its core.*

- **Core:** do your duty (*dharma*) fully, but **renounce attachment to the results** (*phala*).
- **Key term:** *niṣkāma karma* — action without selfish desire; *karma-phala-tyāga* — giving up the fruit.
- **Why it works:** action binds only when fuelled by desire for its outcome. Act as an offering, and action
  *frees* rather than binds.
- **The emblematic verse (2.47):** *"You have a right to action alone, never to its fruits. Let not the
  fruit of action be your motive, nor let your attachment be to inaction."*
- **Bridge:** the Protestant ethic of vocation done as service; Stoic "do what is right, leave outcomes to
  Providence."
- This path **solves Arjuna's problem**: he can fight without spiritual corruption *if* he fights as duty
  offered to God, not for personal gain.

## 2. Jñāna Yoga (ज्ञान योग) — the path of knowledge/discernment
*Chapters 2 (Sāṃkhya section), 4, 13–15.*

- **Core:** liberation through **discriminating the real from the unreal** — knowing the eternal ātman is
  distinct from the changing body/mind (prakṛti).
- **Method:** study, reflection, meditation on the Upanishadic truth *"tat tvam asi"* ("that thou art").
- **Key idea:** the wise see the same Self in a learned brahmin, a cow, an elephant, a dog, an outcast
  (5.18).
- **Bridge:** the contemplative/mystical path in all traditions; Socratic "know thyself"; Platonic ascent to
  the Form of the Good.

## 3. Bhakti Yoga (भक्ति योग) — the path of loving devotion
*Chapters 7, 9–12, and the climax 18.65–66.*

- **Core:** surrender all action, knowledge, and life to God in love. The Gita increasingly elevates bhakti
  as the **most accessible** path for ordinary people.
- **Bhakti** (भक्ति) = loving devotion, participatory surrender (root *bhaj* = to share/serve/love).
- **God's promise (9.22, 18.65–66):** to the devotee who thinks of God with exclusive love, God becomes
  their supporter — "Abandon all dharmas and surrender to me alone; I will liberate you from all sins; do
  not fear" (18.66).
- **Note for balance:** the Gita's bhakti is **not blind** — it is paired with knowledge and right action;
  nor is it sectarian in a narrow sense, since Krishna is presented as the universal Supreme (7.7, 9.23–25:
  even those who worship other gods worship him, albeit indirectly).
- **Bridge:** Christian *agape*/devotion; Sufi love of God; the personal-God strand across religions. For
  readers from non-theistic backgrounds, present bhakti also as "the heart's orientation to the highest."

## 4. Dhyāna / Rāja Yoga (ध्यान योग) — the path of meditation
*Chapter 6 (Ātma Saṃyama Yoga).*

- **Core:** disciplining the mind through meditation to still its modifications and realize the Self.
- Often identified with **Rāja Yoga** (Patañjali's eightfold system). The Gita gives a concise meditative
  practice: seated, focused, gentle, mind fixed on the Self/God (6.10–15).
- **Key idea:** "the mind is the friend as well as the enemy of the self" (6.5–6); yoga is the mastery of
  the mind.
- **Bridge:** Christian contemplation (Cloud of Unknowing), Buddhist mindfulness, modern secular meditation.

---

## How the four paths interrelate (essential for the book)

- They are **not mutually exclusive** but complementary facets of one integral yoga. Krishna recommends
  *karma* purified by *jñāna* and offered through *bhakti*.
- A famous framing: **knowledge (jñāna) without devotion is dry; devotion (bhakti) without knowledge is
  blind; both must be expressed in action (karma).**
- The Gita thus **resolves the ancient Indian tension** between the householder's active life and the
  renunciant's withdrawal: you need not flee the world — act in it, selflessly, God-ward.
- Chapter 5 explicitly compares the *karma yogi* and the *sannyāsī* and concludes that both reach the same
  goal, but **the path of selfless action is superior for most** (5.2, 5.6).

## Pedagogical suggestion
Dedicate **one full chapter of the book to each path** with: definition → key verses → a worked example →
modern parallels. This becomes the conceptual spine of the commentary.
