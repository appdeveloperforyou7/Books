# 02 — The Mahābhārata Context (Setting of the Gita)

To explain the Gita to a non-Indian reader, the **story around it** must be told first. The dialogue makes
no sense without understanding *why* Arjuna is standing on a battlefield paralysed.

## 1. The Mahābhārata in one paragraph

The *Mahābhārata* is the **longest epic poem in the world** (~100,000 verses, ~10× the Iliad + Odyssey
combined). It tells of a dynastic struggle for the throne of **Hastināpura** between two branches of one
family — the **Kauravas** (100 brothers, sons of the blind king Dhṛtarāṣṭra) and the **Pāṇḍavas** (5
brothers, sons of Pāṇḍu). After a rigged game of dice, exile, and failed diplomacy, the conflict ends in a
catastrophic 18-day war at **Kurukshetra**. The Gita occurs in the moments just before the war's first
blow.

## 2. Principal characters (who's who)

### The Pāṇḍavas (the "good" side)
- **Yudhiṣṭhira** — eldest; son of Dharma (god of righteousness); the rightful king.
- **Bhīma** — son of Vāyu (wind god); immensely strong warrior.
- **Arjuna** — son of Indra (king of gods); the greatest archer; **the listener of the Gita.**
- **Nakula & Sahadeva** — twins, sons of the Aśvin gods.

### The Kauravas (the opposing side)
- **Duryodhana** — eldest Kaurava; ambitious, resentful, the chief antagonist.
- **Droṇa** — the martial guru (teacher) of both sides.
- **Bhīṣma** — grand-uncle of both clans; the supreme elder commander; bound by an oath.
- **Karṇa** — a secret half-brother of the Pāṇḍavas; fights for Duryodhana out of loyalty.

### Key others
- **Kṛṣṇa (Krishna)** — cousin and friend of the Pāṇḍavas; avatar of Vishnu; king of Dvārakā. He refuses to
  fight but offers his army to one side and **himself as charioteer** to the other. Arjuna chooses Krishna.
- **Draupadī** — the Pāṇḍavas' shared wife; her public humiliation is a catalyst of the war.
- **Dhṛtarāṣṭra** — blind king, father of the Kauravas; hears the Gita via Sanjaya.
- **Sañjaya** — the king's advisor; granted clairvoyant vision; narrator of the Gita.

## 3. Why the war is a moral crisis (the heart of the Gita)

This is the single most important point to convey to a foreign reader:

- Arjuna is a **Kṣatriya** (warrior), whose *dharma* (duty) is to fight a righteous war.
- But lined up against him are his **grand-uncle (Bhīṣma) who raised him, his teacher (Droṇa), cousins,
  friends, and kinsmen.**
- The war was forced on the Pāṇḍavas after every peaceful option failed. Yet at the moment of truth, Arjuna
  collapses psychologically — grief, horror, moral paralysis: *"What is the point of kingdom, pleasure, or
  life itself if it costs everyone I love?"*

Krishna's task is not merely to motivate a soldier — it is to answer the deepest question:
**How can a good person do the right thing when the right thing is also the painful thing?**

## 4. The deeper, allegorical layer

- **Kurukshetra** ("field of the Kurus") is read by many (e.g., Mahatma Gandhi) as **the field of the human
  mind/body**, where the forces of virtue and vice are constantly at war.
- The Bhagavad Gita thus moves from a literal war to a **universal spiritual drama**.
- This dual reading (historical + allegorical) should be presented honestly: the text *is* set on a real
  battlefield in the epic, but its enduring power is symbolic.

## 5. The place of the Gita within the epic

- It sits in **Book VI (*Bhīṣma Parvan*)**, chapters 23–40, immediately before fighting begins.
- The conches have sounded; both armies face each other; Arjuna asks Krishna to drive the chariot between
  the two armies so he can *see whom he must fight*. What he sees breaks him — and the Gita begins.

## Note for the book
Open the book with a short, vivid retelling of this setup (2–4 pages) so a reader with zero background can
enter the dialogue. Without it, chapter 1's grief is unintelligible.
