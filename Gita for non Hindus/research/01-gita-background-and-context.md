# 01 — Background & Context of the Bhagavad Gita

## 1. What the Gita is

The **Bhagavad Gītā** ("Song of the Divine") is a 700-verse Sanskrit scripture embedded inside the
*Mahābhārata*, the great epic of ancient India. It is a **philosophical and spiritual dialogue** delivered
on a battlefield — between the warrior-prince **Arjuna** and his charioteer **Krishna**, who is an *avatar*
(descent/incarnation) of the god **Vishnu**.

It is often called the essence of Hindu thought because it **synthesises** several older strands:
- the Vedic idea of **dharma** (duty / right action);
- the Upanishadic philosophy of **ātman** (self) and **brahman** (absolute reality);
- the **Sāṃkhya–Yoga** analysis of matter and spirit;
- and the devotional path of **bhakti** (loving surrender to God).

This synthesis is precisely why the book "for non-Hindus" matters: the Gita is the single most accessible
doorway into Hindu philosophy.

## 3. Authorship

- **Tradition:** attributed to the sage **Vyāsa** (also credited with compiling the Vedas, the Mahābhārata,
  and the Purāṇas). A legend says Ganesha broke a tusk to write it down.
- **Scholarship:** "Vyāsa" is also a *title* meaning "compiler/editor". Indologists (Basham, van Buitenen,
  McLeod, Minor) regard the Gita as a **composite work** by multiple authors/editors, but one that is
  philosophically and narratively well-woven **into** the Mahābhārata — not a stray text inserted later.
  Van Buitenen: it was conceived by the epic's authors "to bring to a climax and solution the dharmic
  dilemma of a war."

## 4. Manuscript & textual status

- Found in **Book VI (*Bhīṣma Parvan*), section 3, chapters 23–40** of the Mahābhārata.
- Manuscripts exist in many Indic scripts (Devanagari, Sharada, Grantha, etc.).
- **Variant readings are minor** — far more stable than the rest of the Mahābhārata.
- The **700-verse standard** was fixed by **Adi Shankara** (c. 8th c.) in his commentary, partly to prevent
  later insertions. Some traditions cite older counts up to 745, but no 745-verse manuscript survives.

## 5. Structure of the dialogue

- Total **18 chapters**, grouped in later commentary tradition into three sets of six:
  - **Ch 1–6:** emphasis on action & the individual self (Karma / Sāṃkhya).
  - **Ch 7–12:** emphasis on devotion & the Divine (Bhakti).
  - **Ch 13–18:** emphasis on knowledge & the distinction of matter/spirit (Jñāna).
  - *(Note: this three-fold grouping is a pedagogical convention, not in the text itself.)*
- Speakers & verse distribution (per some Mahābhārata recensions): Krishna ~574, Arjuna ~84, Sanjaya ~41,
  Dhritarashtra 1.

## 6. The "frame narrative"

The Gita is actually **narrated by Sañjaya**, who has clairvoyant sight granted by the sage Vyāsa, to the
blind king **Dhṛtarāṣṭra** (father of the rival Kaurava clan), as the battle begins. So the chain is:
**Krishna → Arjuna**, witnessed and reported by **Sañjaya → Dhṛtarāṣṭra**. Dhṛtarāṣṭra speaks the very first
verse of the Gita.

## 7. Why it is read as universal (relevance for non-Hindus)

- The battlefield is widely read as an **allegory** of the human condition: the inner war between duty and
  fear, self-interest and self-transcendence.
- Its core questions — *What is my duty? How do I act without being enslaved to outcomes? Who am I, really?*
  — are universal, not sectarian.
- The Gita influenced Thoreau, Emerson, Gandhi, Oppenheimer, Aldous Huxley, Carl Jung, and many others.
- It is NOT a "Hindu Bible" (Hinduism has no single canon), but it functions as the most universally
  cherished Hindu scripture.
