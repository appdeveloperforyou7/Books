# 03 — Core Philosophical Concepts of the Gita

These are the load-bearing ideas. A non-Hindu reader cannot follow the Gita without them. Each is defined
plainly, then located in the text, then given a Western/world parallel for bridging.

---

## 1. Dharma (धर्म) — *duty, right order, righteousness, the law of one's being*

- **Literal:** from root *dhṛ* = "to uphold/sustain". That which holds the cosmos and society together.
- **In the Gita:** Arjuna's crisis is a *dharma* crisis. He is a Kṣatriya; his *svadharma* (personal duty) is
  to fight a just war. Krishna's whole argument is that **abandoning duty out of fear is itself wrong**.
- **Nuance:** *dharma* is not "religion" (a common mistranslation). It is a context-sensitive duty that
  depends on one's stage of life, role, and nature. The same act can be dharma for one person and not for
  another.
- **Bridge:** closest Western analogue = **virtue ethics + vocation/calling**; also overlaps with the Stoic
  notion of living "according to nature."
- **Key verses:** 2.31, 2.33, 3.35, 18.47.

## 2. Karma (कर्म) — *action; and the moral law of cause and effect*

Two distinct but linked senses (must be disentangled for readers):
- **(a) Karma = action.** The Gita's central insight is *how* to act: do your duty **without attachment to
  the fruits (results)** — *niṣkāma karma* (desireless action) / *karma-phala-tyāga* (relinquishing the
  fruit of action).
- **(b) Karma = the moral law.** Every action produces consequences that bind the soul to *saṃsāra* (the
  cycle of rebirth) — *unless* performed in the spirit of detachment and surrender.
- **Bridge:** like "you reap what you sow," but extended across lifetimes. The *detachment* teaching is close
  to Stoic focus-on-what-you-control (Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius).
- **Key verses:** 2.47 (most famous verse in the Gita), 2.48, 3.9, 4.20, 5.10.

## 3. Ātman (आत्मन्) — *the Self, the true immortal soul*

- Not the ego or personality; the **unchanging conscious witness** within all beings.
- **In the Gita:** it is **eternal, unborn, undying, uncuttable, unburnable** (2.20–24). It merely changes
  bodies "as a person discards old clothes and puts on new ones" (2.22).
- **Nuance (schools disagree):**
  - **Advaita (Shankara):** ātman *is* identical with Brahman. The separate self is illusion (māyā).
  - **Viśiṣṭādvaita (Rāmānuja):** ātman is a real, eternal part/attribute of Brahman — related but not
    identical (like spark to fire).
  - **Dvaita (Madhva):** ātman and God are eternally **distinct**.
- **Bridge:** Plato/Plotinus on the immortal soul; the "real self behind the thinking mind" in contemplative
  traditions.
- **Key verses:** 2.13, 2.17, 2.20–22, 2.30.

## 4. Brahman (ब्रह्मन्) — *the Absolute, ultimate impersonal reality*

- The infinite, undivided, conscious ground of all existence — beyond name, form, and attributes.
- **In the Gita:** Brahman is the substrate; Krishna identifies himself with the *personal* aspect
  (**Bhagavān/Puruṣottama**) that includes and transcends the impersonal Brahman (this is the Gita's genius:
  reconciling the *nirguṇa* formless Absolute with a *saguṇa* personal God).
- **Bridge:** the "Being itself" of Western mysticism (Eckhart); Spinoza's "God or Nature"; the Tao.
- **Key verses:** 13.12–18, 14.27, 10.20.

## 5. Mokṣa (मोक्ष) — *liberation, freedom from saṃsāra*

- The supreme goal of life in the Gita: release from the cycle of birth and death, attained through
  **knowledge, selfless action, and/or devotion** — culminating in union with / nearness to the Divine.
- **Key idea:** mokṣa is **not** an escape from the world or from action — it is freedom from *bondage to
  ego and desire*. One can be liberated *while* acting (this is the *jīvanmukta* idea, foreshadowed here).
- **Bridge:** Buddhist nirvāṇa; Christian/Platonic beatific vision; "salvation" but framed as *knowledge*
  and *union* rather than forgiveness of sin.
- **Key verses:** 2.51, 5.24–26, 18.54–55, 18.66.

## 6. Saṃsāra (संसार) — *the cycle of rebirth (wheel of becoming)*

- The endless round of birth, death, and rebirth driven by karma and desire. Liberation = exit from this
  cycle.
- The Gita presupposes reincarnation (2.22, 2.27, 8.19). **Explain this directly** to readers from
  one-life traditions; don't assume they accept it.
- **Bridge:** common to most Indian traditions (Hindu, Buddhist, Jain); parallels in Greek (Pythagoras,
  Plato's myth of Er).

## 7. The three Guṇas (गुण) — *qualities/modes of Prakṛti (Nature)*

From **Sāṃkhya** philosophy, central to chapters 14, 17, 18. All of Nature (mind, body, emotion) is woven
of three strands:
- **Sattva (सत्त्व)** — purity, clarity, light, harmony, goodness.
- **Rajas (रजस्)** — passion, activity, ambition, restlessness.
- **Tamas (तमस्)** — inertia, dullness, ignorance, heaviness.

Every person is a mix; spiritual growth = increasing sattva, becoming **guṇātīta** ("beyond the gunas," ch.
14) — free of their pull.
- **Bridge:** temperament theory (humours); modern psychology's arousal/valence dimensions; Stoic mastery of
  the passions.
- **Key verses:** 14.5–18, 14.19–26, 17.2–22, 18.26–28.

## 8. Prakṛti & Puruṣa (प्रकृति / पुरुष) — *Matter/Nature vs. Spirit/Consciousness*

The Gita adopts the Sāṃkhya dualism of the field:
- **Prakṛti** = the field (*kṣetra*): all matter, body, mind, ego, the gunas — everything that *changes*.
- **Puruṣa** = the knower of the field (*kṣetrajña*): pure consciousness — the unchanging witness.
- Liberation = knowing the difference, and no longer mistaking yourself for your prakṛti (body/mind).
- (Chapter 13 develops this as *kṣetra/kṣetrajña*.)
- **Bridge:** Cartesian mind/body dualism (but note: here *mind* belongs to matter, not spirit — a key
  difference from Descartes).

## 9. Avatāra (अवतार) — *descent/incarnation of the Divine*

- Vishnu "descends" in every age to restore dharma (4.7–8): *"Whenever there is a decline of righteousness
  and a rise of unrighteousness, I manifest myself."*
- Krishna is presented as the **pūrṇa-avatāra** (complete/full incarnation) — the Supreme itself in human
  form.
- **Bridge:** conceptually like Christian incarnation but cyclical (many avatars across ages) rather than
  once-for-all. Be careful not to flatten the analogy.
