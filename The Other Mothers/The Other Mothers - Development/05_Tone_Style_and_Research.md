# THE OTHER MOTHERS — Tone, Style & Research Checklist

---

## PART A — TONE & STYLE GUIDE

### The governing sensation
**A held breath that never fully releases.** The prose should feel like the minutes after a child goes missing — hyper-alert, slightly numb, every sense cranked up, time both crawling and skipping. The reader should feel slightly sick and unable to stop.

### Voice principles
1. **Five distinct first-person voices, not five versions of the author.** Each narrator must be identifiable from a blind paragraph. Give each:
   - **Nora:** precise, controlled, sensory, with a proofreader's exactness and small tells of strain (over-precise when she's lying to herself).
   - **Jen:** breathless, over-accommodating, full of qualifiers ("I just," "sorry, but," "it's probably nothing"), shrinking syntax.
   - **Charlotte:** clipped, managerial, brittle; sentences that organise the world to keep it from collapsing.
   - **Priya:** dry, economical, darkly funny, a nurse's gallows calm; the most grounded voice.
   - **Elsie:** patient, slightly formal, antiquated phrases (she's been in the same job 25 years); the voice of long keeping.
2. **Close third is forbidden — this is a first-person novel.** The intimacy and the unreliability both depend on it.
3. **Nora never lies on the page.** Her unreliability is *omission and emphasis*, never fabrication. This is the fair-twist craft standard and what makes the re-read devastating.

### Sentence-level craft
- **Vary length ruthlessly.** Long, accumulating sentences for the buildup; single-line paragraphs for the gut-punches. The CCTV chapter (22) should go short and staccato; the Elsie 2003 fragment (15) should be long, tidal, dreamlike.
- **End every chapter on a question or a turned knife.** Never give the reader a natural stopping point.
- **The 10-page gasp law:** roughly every 2,500 words, a line that makes the reader inhale or reread.

### Sensory palette (London, N8)
- **Sound:** the specific screech of the Victoria line at Finsbury Park; the 3:30 school bell; a kettle in a too-quiet flat; the WhatsApp *ping* becoming ominous.
- **Smell:** wet wool in the cloakroom; the vegetal stink of the nature pond in late summer; Charlotte's expensive perfume as control; hospital soap on Priya's hands.
- **Touch:** a child's hand slipping from yours (the spine image); a cold phone screen; a spray-painted door under fingertips.
- **Weather:** an unseasonable autumn heat that makes everything feel wrong; the particular grey of a London afternoon at 3pm.

### What to avoid (style)
- No italics for emphasis except sparingly in dialogue.
- No chapter cliffhangers that resolve with "it was nothing" — cheap.
- No info-dumps about 2003; reveal it in fragments, out of order, through texture.
- No omniscient intrusions; stay locked in the narrators.

## PART B — THEMES TO KEEP ALIVE IN EVERY SCENE

1. **Inherited/redistributed guilt** — who carries a dead child's weight, and who is made to carry it.
2. **The violence of truth** — is exhuming a buried harm always justice?
3. **Motherhood as performance and prison** — the school gate as stage and cage.
4. **Class as invisible architecture** — who is suspected, believed, destroyed, by postcode.
5. **The digital mob as the new village square** — grief weaponised into cruelty.

**Craft rule:** every scene should quietly advance at least one theme *and* one plot beat. If it only does one, cut or combine.

## PART C — RESEARCH CHECKLIST (verify before & during drafting)

> Accuracy is the floor that lets the 10% of nightmare land. Everything below must be checked; flag with [VERIFY].

### 1. Missing-child procedure & UK policing
- [ ] **"Missing" vs "Absent"** classification under College of Policing APP (Absence vs Missing framework) — the thresholds that trigger a response. [VERIFY current 2024/25 guidance.]
- [ ] **First 48–72 hours**: standard Met response to a missing child under 10 — Gold/Silver command, Child Rescue Alert criteria, the threshold for public appeal.
- [ ] **Family as first suspect** — the actual investigative protocol (family interviews, phone seizure, CCTV trawl order). Confirm how soon police look at parents.
- [ ] **CCTV in/around London primary schools** — what's typically held, retention periods, how police obtain it. [VERIFY: school CCTV is usually internal; gate footage from neighbours/council.]
- [ ] **DI Royston's unit** — the correct Met command for a missing child that may be abduction (likely Specialist Crime Command, possibly SC&O1 Homicide or a missing-persons unit). Get the terminology right.

### 2. Charging & sentencing (Nora) [VERIFY — UK law, 2024/25]
- [ ] **Child abduction by a parent** — Child Abduction Act 1984 s.1 (taking a child out of/keeping from lawful guardian). Confirm whether a mother taking *her own* child (with father's PR) from school to grandmother's is even an offence, and the realistic charge if the father has Parental Responsibility and objected.
- [ ] **Perverting the course of justice** — the likely lead charge for a staged disappearance; sentencing range (custodial).
- [ ] **Wasting police time** (s.5(2) Criminal Law Act 1967) — minor charge.
- [ ] **Child cruelty** (Children and Young Persons Act 1933 s.1) — whether the psychological harm to Theo/ community qualifies.
- [ ] Realistic **sentencing outcome** for a mother with no prior record, mental-health mitigation, no physical harm to child — likely custodial but possibly suspended; consult recent comparable cases.

### 3. School safeguarding & family court
- [ ] **Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL)** — the school's actual role and legal duties when a child goes missing from premises. Who calls police, when.
- [ ] **Ofsted** reporting obligations for a serious incident at a school.
- [ ] **CAFCASS / family court** — realistic custody proceedings (Priya's ex; Nora's access to Theo post-conviction). Confirm supervised-contact mechanics.
- [ ] **School nature ponds** — health & safety duty of care; how a 2003 drowning would have been investigated (HSWA 1974; inquest; coroner's verdict categories: accident, misadventure, narrative).

### 4. The 2003 cold case (legal & forensic realism)
- [ ] **Coroner's verdict (pre-2009 terminology)** — pre-2009 used "accidental death"; the "narrative verdict" became common after 2009. For a 2003 drowning, "accidental death" is period-correct. [VERIFY.]
- [ ] **Re-opening a historical death** — what triggers it (new evidence); who investigates (coroner vs police); the realistic likelihood and process.
- [ ] **Statute of limitations** — the UK has none for indictable offences, but **death by misadventure / a 10-year-old's dare** would almost certainly not lead to prosecution 22 years on. Confirm Charlotte faces **No Further Action**; Carol negligently but not criminally. The legal *non-resolution* is itself thematically powerful.

### 5. The memory mechanic (the science — handle with care)
- [ ] **Misinformation effect / suggestive memory** (Elizabeth Loftus) — the *real, peer-reviewed* phenomenon of memories being altered by suggestion, especially in children. This is Nora's mechanic. Use it accurately; cite the principle in author's note if useful.
- [ ] **AVOID "recovered memory therapy"** — scientifically discredited and will date/criticise the book. Nora's truth comes from **hard evidence** (the drawing, the photo, Elsie's testimony), *not* from a therapeutic "uncovering." This is non-negotiable for credibility.
- [ ] **Childhood traumatic amnesia vs overwriting** — the difference, stated correctly.

### 6. The digital mob
- [ ] **WhatsApp class-chat culture** — realistic norms, group admin powers, the speed of pile-ons, screenshot culture.
- [ ] **Doxxing legality UK** — Malicious Communications Act 1988, Communications Act 2003 s.127; the realistic (low) likelihood of prosecution. Criminal damage (spray paint) is the only charge that likely sticks for Jen's attackers.
- [ ] **"Reception Mums" WhatsApp groups** — confirm naming conventions, the comically earnest emojis, the performative concern.

### 7. London geography & texture
- [ ] **N8 (Crouch End / Harringay)** — real street names, the Broadway, Priya Park, Parkland Walk, the specific primaries (create a fictional CE primary plausibly sited; do not libel a real school).
- [ ] **The Whittington Hospital** (Priya's workplace) — real, in Archway; confirm nurse shift patterns, agency/bank work for a single mother.
- [ ] **Council flat vs private road** within walking distance — find a real contrast pair (e.g., a Hornsey estate vs a Crouch End private road) to ground the class geography.
- [ ] **Transport** — the W7 bus, the Victoria/Northern line interchanges at Finsbury Park; Tom's Manchester commute (King's Cross → Euston).
- [ ] **A Hertfordshire/Essex village** plausible for Gillian's hide and Carol's care home.

### 8. Misc verisimilitude
- [ ] **Proofreader** working life (Nora) — freelance rates, the isolation, the plausibility of being at every school gate.
- [ ] **Nursery nurse** (Jen) vs **TA** (Elsie) — distinct UK roles, pay bands, realities.
- [ ] **A 5-year-old's Reception year** — the actual first-term rhythms (the "settling-in" period is precisely when a disappearance is most chaotic — verify).
- [ ] **Dementia (Kamala) and alcohol-related dementia (Carol)** — represent accurately and without stigma; consult a sensitivity point.

## PART D — SENSITIVITY & ETHICS NOTES

- **Mental health:** Nora's grief is not pathologised into a convenient disorder; it is grief weaponised by buried trauma. Avoid making her "unhinged" — she is calculating and devastated simultaneously, which is more frightening and more fair.
- **Class:** the council-flat/care-home characters must be written with full dignity, never as pathology. Priya and Jen are the moral centres, not cautionary tales.
- **The accused:** the registered offender (red herring) must be written as a *human being destroyed by the mob*, not a plot device — this is the novel's point about presumption of innocence.
- **Carol's dementia:** portray without mockery; the "cleared too late" beat is the tragedy, not the condition.
- **A real school:** use a fictional institution; never risk libelling an actual primary.

## PART E — DELIVERABLES ROADMAP (next steps after this blueprint)

1. **Sample chapters:** write Ch 1 (Nora, the bell) and Ch 15 (Elsie's 2003 fragment) first — they establish the two poles of the voice and the two timelines. If these sing, the book is alive.
2. **Full chapter-by-chapter beat sheet** (expand each outline line into 4–6 beats) — before drafting, to lock pacing.
3. **A "secret journal" per narrator** (Kimi blueprint technique) — one page each, written in voice, where they confess what they'd never say. Use to calibrate the five voices.
4. **Research pass** on the checklist above (Items 1–6 are legal/procedural and must precede drafting; 7–8 can run alongside).
5. **Draft** to ~95k, then a structural edit focused on one thing only: **does every chapter re-read differently after Inversion #1 and #2?** That single test is the difference between a good thriller and the thriller of the decade.
