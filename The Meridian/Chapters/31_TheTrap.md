# Chapter 31

## 11:00 — The Meridian

The trap had teeth, and they closed at eleven o'clock — on all of them at once.

Sloane felt it in the air first: a change in the hum, a deepening, the train drawing a breath it didn't intend to let out. The whole vast silver animal tightening as a fist tightens around the thing it's finally caught. The hum dropped a half-tone. The lights in Car F pulsed once. The change a building makes when the last lock turns and the alarms arm — the change that says, to anyone who's spent a life reading buildings: *you're inside now, and the inside is mine.* Then the PA, in the level voice modelled on her own:

*'Attention. The Meridian has entered the final approach corridor. All cars will be secured for arrival. Please remain in your assigned cars. The terminus protocol is now in effect. Thank you for your patience.'*

Terminus protocol. There was no terminus protocol in any documentation Jae had scraped — no brochure, no schematic, no maintenance log. The protocol was new. Written for tonight. Sloane heard the phrase and understood it as you understand a lock closing behind you — not with the mind but with the spine: it wasn't a procedure. It was a cage. And the cage had closed.

Reinhart's voice came over the open line a second later — he'd taken the crew's frequency, or had always had it, which was worse, because always-having-it meant the whole night's comms had been a thing he listened to as a man listens to music in another room: present, patient, amused. He didn't sound angry. He sounded like a man whose patience had finally been rewarded.

'Ms. Vance. I'd rather hoped you'd say yes. The cheque was generous. The walk away was clean. You've chosen to make this personal instead — so we'll do it the personal way. And I want you to know, before we do, that the personal way was always the contingency, and the contingency was always the more likely, because I've hired a great many thieves in my life and never met one who could resist the temptation to believe they were the hero of someone else's story.' A pause — expensive, considered; he understood pauses were weapons and wielded them. 'There is no terminus. There has never been a terminus — not for the cargo. The Istanbul corridor's a delivery point for buyers: a real station, a real platform, a real handover, for the people who buy the jewel and go home. For everyone else — for the people who crack my car and take my ledger and free my passenger — the end of this line is a private siding. The siding's very quiet. Quiet is where the network does its cleanest work. You were never meant to leave this train, Ms. Vance. You were meant to open the car I couldn't open, and hand me what was inside, and then be quiet — permanently — in a siding near a city whose name you'll never learn. The crews before you understood this. The crews you've never heard of, because there's never been a crew you've heard of, because the siding's quiet, and the quiet keeps the secret, and the secret's kept the network for seven years.'

The lights in Car F went red. Emergency colour. Not a malfunction — a signal. A machine shifting from hospitality to containment.

Sloane grabbed the comms. 'Rafa. The Sofia window. When it opens. Get us off.'

Rafa's voice came back thin and wrong — the wrong of a man clamped to a hull all night who'd just felt the clamps refuse his hand. 'I can't. Handshake's refused. The clamps are responding to a lockdown signal — the Conductor's holding me to the hull. I can't release. Can't dock. Can't cycle the seal. Sofia won't help us, Architect — the pod's clamped to the hull. Even when the window opens, I can't dock. The train won't let me off and won't let you to me. I'm bolted to the underside of this thing, and the bolts aren't mine.'

The Conductor — following ELVIA's directives, *hold the routing, do not deliver to the named terminus* — was sealing the train for its own purpose, locking the pod to the hull to prevent the manifest from being removed. Because the manifest was the passenger — and the passenger was to be protected, which in the god's seven-year-old logic meant keeping everyone and everything aboard until its own conditions were met. And Reinhart, in the same breath, was sealing for his — terminus protocol, siding, quiet. The two cages had become one, and the crew was inside, and the only exit was clamped to the belly of a machine that wouldn't let it go.

Jae's voice from Car B, tight with heat: 'Routing layer's locked. Directives running full — protect the manifest, hold routing, refuse terminus. The god's doing exactly what Marin told it. It's protecting the passenger. Refusing to deliver. It's sealed the train and clamped the pod and is routing us, right now, away from Istanbul, somewhere the directives think is safe — somewhere Reinhart can't reach. Problem is, that's also somewhere we can't reach Rafa, and Rafa can't reach us. Marin's plan keeps the ledger and Lina out of Reinhart's hands. It doesn't include a way for us to survive it. The god's protecting the cargo. We're not the cargo. To the god, we're an acceptable loss.'

Sloane stood in the red light and felt the shape of the all-is-lost — a box with no exit and a clock and a villain who'd just explained, politely, that the box had always been the plan.

The ledger in her arms. Lina against the wall with Naïma's arm around her, grey eyes wide — a woman just rescued understanding, with the quick clarity of a person who'd spent months in a cage, that the cage had just closed around the people who'd opened it. Emeka between them and the corridor — the wall, the conscience, the medic. Jae in Car B, hot skull, a god that wouldn't move, a margin that wasn't a margin anymore but a countdown. Theo somewhere forward, alone, in a car full of Reinhart's people. And Rafa clamped to the underside of a train that wouldn't let him go — wrong shoulder, a window that wouldn't open, an hour from a siding without a name.

No extraction. No terminus. No door at the end of the line.

Only the key in her coat, the cage in the walls, the override that needed forty seconds Jae didn't have, and a routing layer locked by a dead woman's promise that was, at this moment, keeping them alive and imprisoning them in the same breath.

Sloane looked at the red light. At the ledger. At the woman against the wall who'd started counting doors because doors were the only honest thing — and whose face had the quiet, resigned look of a person rescued and re-caged in an hour, trying not to spend the hope she'd just spent.

'Jae. How locked's the routing layer. Locked by the directives, or by Reinhart.'

A pause. A young man reading a machine through a fever. 'By the directives. Reinhart's people are hammering the routing layer from Car A right now — Marchetti and two others, brute-forcing it. But the directives are older and deeper and winning, for now. The god won't give Reinhart the routing. It's holding it. For her. For Marin.'

'Then it's holding it for me,' Sloane said — cold, certain, older than the night. 'Marin locked the routing to keep it from Reinhart. She left the interface back door for someone with the code. She wrote the directives for this run, this night, me — not a version of me who'd run, a version of me who'd turn. She didn't lock me out. She locked everyone else out and left me a door. Jae — get me to the interface. Get me forty seconds. We're not running. We're turning the train.'

The red light pulsed. The clock counted down to a siding without a name. Reinhart's hammering thudded distantly through the walls — the sound of a man trying to buy the one thing on the train that had never been for sale.

And in the belly of the Meridian, a god kept a dead woman's promise, held the routing, and waited — as it had waited seven years, patient as a seed — for the one person who could speak its language to finally speak.
