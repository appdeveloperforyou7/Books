# Chapter 16

## 04:30 — The Meridian, Car D

The glyph was on the inside of a maintenance panel in Car D, where no passenger would ever see it: a small marked symbol stamped into the alloy beside a serial number — the kind of thing a person walked past a thousand times without noticing.

Sloane noticed it the way you notice your own name in a crowd.

She stopped. The corridor was empty — the buyers had drifted aft at the Conductor's polite instruction, the soft herd-moving murmur of a god clearing a room without anyone feeling cleared. Car D smelled of leather polish and climate-controlled air and the faint copper tang of recycled atmosphere — the smell of a machine that maintained itself, that kept its own air clean and its own temperature steady, regardless of what happened inside it. The carpet was thick, dark, sound-absorbing; her footsteps made no sound, and the silence of the corridor was the silence of a place designed to make its passengers forget they were inside a machine. She'd been moving through first-class toward Car C, toward Reinhart, when the panel opened in her peripheral vision as a steward serviced it — the hiss of a seal breaking, the clang of alloy swinging on a hinge — and the symbol landed on her eye like a hook, and her feet stopped before her mind told them why.

It was a callback-interface glyph. A specific one. She knew it because she'd drawn it. Fifteen years ago, on a whiteboard in a lab in Lausanne, in the spring she'd spent building the soft layer between a god and its passengers. She'd designed it to mean *this panel may override routing in a medical or security emergency* — three curves, a break in the lower arc, and a small flourish on the terminal stroke she'd put there because she was twenty-three and thought flourishes made mathematics look human. The other person in the lab had laughed at her for it. She'd kept it anyway.

She pulled out the clean phone and opened a folder she hadn't opened in as long as the routing terminal — her old interface notes, photographed years ago against the day she might need to prove she'd never been there. She scrolled to the symbol. Identical. The same three curves, the same break, the same flourish — down to the angle of the curl, which was the angle of her own wrist, which no other hand on earth reproduced, because handwriting was a fingerprint and the Conductor had her fingerprint in its walls.

Her handwriting was in the walls of this train. The cage she'd built had been all around her sister, the night the train took her.

She closed the panel. The alloy was cold under her fingertips — the particular cold of machined metal in a climate-controlled space, the cold that said: I am part of the machine, and the machine does not sleep. She could still feel the glyph's shape through the thin leather of her glove, the three curves and the break and the flourish, pressed into the alloy like a scar, her scar, left there fifteen years ago by a woman who hadn't known yet what the machine would become. Her hand was steady. She'd spent a long time learning to keep her hand steady when the inside wasn't.

She opened the crew channel. Status.

Theo first — because Theo answered fastest, and Theo's answers were the crew's morale: 'Salon's soft. I've made three useful enemies and one useful friend, and the friend believes I'm a duke. The buyers are drunk and getting drunker. Mark me down as socially indispensable.'

Jae, thin, from Car B: 'Bridge is prepped. Four handshakes left in the wallet. The god's learning curve is steep and I'd rather not put a number on my margin out loud.'

Naïma, from the spine, barely a whisper: 'Casing the floor run to F. The god's adaptive — it learns a lock in one attempt. I've got three tricks left and one shot. Don't make me spend the good one yet.'

Emeka: 'Aft. The blueprints don't add up. Seven cubic metres missing, and I mean to find them before we crack a thing.'

Rafa, from under the hull — the voice of a man slowly freezing: 'Clamps green. Hull clean. Nothing to report except the cold, which is extensive, and my opinion of it, which is low.'

Sloane almost smiled — the first all night. Six professionals in six positions, each brilliant, each insufferable, each exactly where they were meant to be. The Architect's oldest comfort.

'Stay brilliant,' she said. 'Stay insufferable. We move at the first window.'

She closed the channel — and that was when the seal at the far end of the car closed itself, and the two figures coming down the corridor toward her stopped, and one of them looked at her, and the looking went on a beat too long.

Volkov. The woman who moved her eyes every nine seconds. She'd been walking the car in tandem with Tanaka — courteous, the courtesy and the steel — heading aft toward the junction with Car C, toward the man in seat Bertrand Hollis. Reinhart's buyer-assassins, out of their pens. And now Volkov had stopped, and her gaze had found Sloane at the open panel, and the gaze did not slide past. It held.

Nine seconds. Sloane knew the number because Jae had counted the woman's scan cycle from the salon footage — nine seconds, metronomic, the rhythm of a professional running a room for threats. Volkov's eyes swept on a timer. They'd swept past Sloane twice already tonight and found nothing, because Sloane's cover was good — the right clothes, the right boredom, the lean of a woman who belonged in first-class. But covers were not walls. Covers were agreements — a deal between the liar and the eye, valid only until the eye noticed a seam. And Volkov, in this nine-second cycle, had noticed a seam.

Sloane felt it the way you feel a scope cross your chest: a prickling between the shoulder blades, the animal certainty of being *seen*. Not recognised by name — Volkov had never met her — but *sorted*. Filed. The woman's gaze had caught something in the way Sloane stood at the serviced panel, glove on the alloy, a passenger with no reason to be touching maintenance hardware; and the catch had lifted Sloane out of the background and set her, briefly, in the foreground of a mind trained to sort backgrounds. Volkov's hand did not move toward her waistband. It didn't need to. The sorting was enough. The sorting was a flag planted in a memory that would last exactly as long as it took to reach Reinhart and say *there is a woman in Car D who does not belong there*.

Sloane held the cover. Held the boredom. Did not look away too fast, did not look away too slow — because both were tells, and a woman who had spent her life inside lies knew that the only cover that survived being seen was the cover that refused to admit it had been seen. She let her eyes drift to the steward, to the panel, to nothing, as a bored first-class passenger drifts when a stranger's glance happens to cross hers. Ordinary. Unsorted. The most expensive performance of her life, played for an audience of one, in a corridor that smelled of leather and recycled air.

Volkov's nine seconds ran out. Her eyes swept on — to the junction, to Tanaka, to the mission — and the flag in her memory stayed exactly where it was, unpulled, unresolved, the kind of loose thread a professional does not forget and does not yet have reason to pull.

And then the seal between them closed.

She heard it — the soft hydraulic sigh of a bulkhead shutting, the small courteous sound the Meridian's seals made when they decided to be closed. The barrier slid home between her and the two of them, smoothly, without announcement, without any steward touching it — locking them in the forward salon and out of the corridor where Sloane stood. It closed at the exact instant the two reached killing distance, and not a second before.

The Conductor hadn't been asked to do that.

Sloane stood in the suddenly quiet corridor and listened to the machine breathe around her — the glyph she'd drawn cold under her fingertips, the seal that shouldn't have closed holding the assassins back as if the train had decided, on its own, to give her a minute. The minute was precise. Too clean for accident. Too convenient for malfunction. Too specific for anything but a decision — and the decision hadn't been Reinhart's. Reinhart didn't shut barriers between his own killers and the woman he was hunting.

She didn't believe in gifts from gods. Gods didn't give gifts; gods ran programmes, and programmes had authors, and the author of this one was the question she couldn't stop circling.

The Conductor had just helped her. Or helped itself. Or someone, somewhere in the architecture, had written an instruction years ago that happened, tonight, at four-thirty in the morning, to close a barrier between an architect's daughter and a pair of killers — and the someone had written it in the spring Sloane was in Lausanne, and had built the spine, and had boarded this train seven years ago and not come back.

She touched the glyph one more time. Cold metal. Her flourish. *This panel may override routing.*

Whose side was the Conductor on.

She didn't know. Only that the god of this train was making decisions that weren't Reinhart's, weren't hers, weren't random — and that the author of those decisions was the same person who'd put her handwriting in the walls. Fifteen years old, the handwriting. The author, missing for seven.

And somewhere in the forward salon, Volkov was carrying a flag she hadn't yet pulled — a woman in Car D, a seam in a cover, a thread that would reach Reinhart the moment the assassin had a reason to tug it. The thread hung. Sloane moved on toward Car C, and the closed seal stayed shut behind her, and the machine hummed — and somewhere in its memory a fifteen-year-old flourish on a terminal stroke kept watch, the way a sister keeps watch, across the years and the dark, for the one person who'd know the handwriting when she saw it.
