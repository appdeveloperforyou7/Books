# Chapter 21

## THE CRACK

## 06:10 — The Meridian, Car F

They gathered in the Vault Car around a prize none of them had been hired to steal, and Sloane told them the truth, because the truth was the only plan she had left.

She'd spent her whole career keeping the truth from crews. The truth was the part you carried alone — against your ribs, in a language only you spoke — because crews worked on the story, and the story had to be clean, and the clean story was what kept seven people moving the same direction through the dark. You gave a crew the story. You kept the truth. The distance between the two was the job, and it had always, until now, been enough.

Now the distance was gone. She set the truth down in front of them, because carrying it alone was going to get them killed.

She told them about the ledger. About the signature, ELVIA, and the cipher string beneath it — twenty-two characters that meant nothing to her and everything to someone. Told them the stone in the cradle was a forgery now and a front always. That the man who'd hired them was sixty metres away with two killers and an open hand, and the hand expected the ledger at the terminus for a cheque that had already cleared — a cheque that was the bait, the trap behind the bait, the trap that ended in a siding without a name.

She told them what the ledger was. A record. The only copy. The map of a network that had been moving people and money and disappearances across a continent for seven years — the thing that could burn the network down in the right hands and consolidate it under one owner in the wrong ones. She told them whose hands Reinhart wanted it in.

The Vault Car was silent. The cradle's readout counted. Emeka stood with his hand still flat on the deck, over the housing where the ledger had lived. Naïma was a shadow by the wall, by the panel where the breathing came from. Jae sat against the medical port with his hand curled in his lap, the tremor not quite gone. Theo was the only one standing easily — and his easiness was a lie, and they all knew it, and let him have it. Vesna stood at the edge, notebook closed, because some truths were too large for columns.

'So the job's changed,' Theo said, in the voice beneath all his voices.

'The job's gone,' Sloane said. 'We were never stealing a jewel. We were the hand — his hand, the hand he couldn't use without hanging himself, the hand he hired us to be so the hanging would be ours. Reinhart needs us to carry the ledger to the terminus and put it in his hand. That's the only thing between us and a fortune and a clean walk away.'

She let it sit.

'Or we don't.'

Emeka spoke first — because Emeka always spoke first when the question was right. 'The thirty-seventh passenger. The one breathing behind the wall. If the ledger's the map of the network, and the network moves people—'

'Then the thirty-seventh passenger is what the network moves,' Naïma finished. Flat. Hard. 'And Reinhart wants the ledger so he can keep moving them. Without the map, no one ever proves it. Without the passenger, no one ever stops it.'

'We crack the car,' Jae said from the floor, voice thin, 'find a person, and the person's the proof — and the man who hired us wants us to hand him the proof so he can destroy it. That's the job. That was always the job. We just didn't get the briefing.' A beat. 'I don't like not getting the briefing. I've never not gotten a briefing. The not-getting is, in my professional opinion, where we were always going to die.'

Sloane looked at them. Seven people, and the eighth who'd come to arrest her and become the door. A god in the walls taking their side for reasons seven years old and signed with a name none could read. A villain sixty metres away with an open hand and a cleared cheque.

'If we keep the ledger,' she said, 'we don't get paid. We get hunted. Reinhart owns this train and everything on it. The terminus is his. The only way off is the pod, and Rafa's the only exit. If we keep the ledger and take the passenger, we're stealing from the man who hired us, on his train, six hours from his terminus — no plan, no extraction that accounts for a person, no idea who or what's on our side.'

'Can we re-plan at speed,' Naïma said. Not a question. A measurement.

'We've been re-planning at speed since Lisbon. It's the only speed I know.'

She looked at Emeka — the conscience. If the conscience broke, the crew broke. 'I need to know we're doing this. All of us. Not because I'm the Architect, not because I'm paying — I'm not, anymore, the money just became blood money, and I won't spend blood money on the people I brought into this. Because there's a person breathing behind that wall and a record in my arms that can end this, and the man who wants both is offering us a fortune to look the other way. I need to know who's staying. Now. Because in sixty seconds Reinhart notices his hand is empty and the woman he hired to fill it has closed her fist.'

Emeka put his hand on the deck, over the housing, then lifted it and put it over his heart instead — the old security man's way of saying *I'm staying*. Enough. The first brick.

Jae uncurled his shaking hand and laid it flat on the floor. His way.

Naïma stepped toward the panel where the breathing came from. Hers.

Theo adjusted a cufflink. 'I was rather hoping the job would get more interesting. I'd hate to be disappointed.' The cufflink was the performance; the disappointment was the man; and the man, for once, was staying for the right reason.

Vesna opened her notebook, turned to a fresh page, wrote one line: *06:14. Car F. The crew has chosen. I am with them.*

Sloane let out the breath she'd been holding since the cradle opened.

And for a moment — one moment, in the red light, before the planning started and the night turned hard — they were not a crew. They were seven people and a cop who had decided, against money and reason and every professional instinct that had kept them alive this long, that the woman breathing behind the wall mattered more than the cheque. Sloane looked at them and saw what she had never let herself see in a crew: not assets, not skills, not the sum of what each could do — people. The boy on the floor who'd held twelve and would hold forty. The woman by the wall who'd frozen once and would not freeze again. The wall who'd quit a uniform rather than be quiet. The liar who'd finally lied in the right direction. The pilot under the hull they couldn't see. The cop who'd come to arrest her and stayed to help her steal a train. A family, of the kind that is not born but built — built in a Vault Car in the red light, around a person none of them had met, because the person was the point.

'For the record,' Theo said, into the quiet — and his voice had no performance in it, only the small surprised warmth of a man realising he'd stumbled into the one thing he'd spent his whole life performing: somewhere to belong. 'I want it noted that I am doing this for no money, against my own interests, with people I have known for less than a day. My memoirs will be unreadable. No one will believe a word.'

'Good,' Jae said, from the floor, dry — the ghost of the boy he'd been before the night took the ghost. 'Then no one'll believe the part where you told a woman I was a cardiologist.'

'You were a magnificent cardiologist. The problem was the driving, and the driving was Rafa's fault.'

'Everything's someone else's fault with you.'

'It's my charm. I'm blameless, as a rule. Tonight I've made an exception.'

Even Emeka laughed — a low, startled sound, the laugh of a man who'd forgotten he could. And Sloane, who did not laugh, let the corner of her mouth move, once, because the moving was the thing the crew needed, and the crew had earned it, and the earning was the whole of what they'd just become.

Then the moment passed, and they were a crew again, and the night was waiting.

'Then we burn the plan. We keep the ledger. We find the passenger. We get off this train with both and put them where Reinhart can't reach — in six hours, on a machine that wants to kill us, for no money, against the man who built it. That's the new plan. The old one's dead. Welcome to the real one.'

The cradle hummed. The readout counted. Six hours to the terminus. And behind the wall, the thirty-seventh passenger breathed — small, steady — as if they'd heard, through glass and gold and seven years of silence, that someone had finally decided to come for them.
