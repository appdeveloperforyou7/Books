# Chapter 25

## 07:30 — The Meridian, undercarriage

Six hours under the hull had taught Rafael Souza the language of the Meridian's belly.

Not its voice — the voice was for the passengers above, the calm PA, the weather-reading murmur. Down here the train spoke in vibration: the deep thrum of the guideway coils against his spine, the higher whine of the climate plant cycling every nineteen minutes, the small distinct shudder of each coupling as the articulated spine flexed through a curve. Rafa read the vibration the way a doctor reads a pulse. He could feel, through the alloy pressed to his back, when the train leaned into a bank and when it levelled; could count the cars by the cadence of the couplings; could feel the moment a hatch opened three cars up because the pressure in the boundary layer shifted by a fraction and the leash hummed a half-tone sharper. The belly was its own instrument, and Rafa had six hours of listening in him, and the listening was the only thing keeping the cold out.

The cold was the real opponent. Not Reinhart, not the god, not the other pilot shadowing him two kilometres back in the dark. The cold. It came up through the leash harness and down through the hull and found every joint and stayed. His shoulder had stopped reporting two hours ago and sunk into a numb sulk — the old injury, the one he flew around, the one he'd never fixed because fixing it meant grounding himself and grounding himself meant not flying and not flying was, Rafa had decided somewhere in his forties, worse than the pain. He'd built his whole careful life around that decision: fly with the bad shoulder, check everything twice, never let the shoulder be the reason something went wrong. Tonight the shoulder was going to be the reason something went right — or so he'd decided — and the deciding was the only warmth he had.

He checked the clamps. He checked them every twenty minutes, because checking was the difference between a pilot who flew once and a pilot who flew home. Magnetic, four-point, each one reading green on the tiny suit display he could read by pressing his wrist to his cheek. Green. Green. Green. Green. The pod was married to the hull. The hull was doing six hundred kilometres an hour over a continent he couldn't see. And Rafa was married to the pod, and the marriage was holding, and the holding was the only fact that mattered until the window opened and they needed him again.

The second pod was gone. Detached at half-six, dropped into the dark — and not empty, because Rafa had watched it go and seen the way it went: not the dead fall of an abandoned thing but the controlled vector of a flown thing, a pilot aboard, running dark, choosing a heading. The crew above had been taken; the pilot below had not. He'd escaped the way Rafa would have escaped — by being the one part of the crew the killing couldn't reach, the part that lived under the hull.

And he was still out there.

Rafa had found him an hour ago, at the ragged edge of the passive scanner — a contact so faint it was almost imagination, a single signature matching the Meridian's course two kilometres back and a hundred metres below, holding station the way a shadow holds station with the thing it shadows. Running dark. No transponder, no lights, no signature a less careful eye would ever catch. But Rafa was not a less careful eye, and the contact was there, and the contact was a pod, and the pod was flown, because nothing unflown held that course at that speed for an hour through a mountain corridor.

The other pilot. The only other man alive who could do this. He'd lost his crew to Reinhart's people the way Rafa had nearly lost his, and he'd run, and then he'd stopped running and turned back, and now he was shadowing the train that had killed his crew — two kilometres back, holding steady, watching, with the same patience and the same cold and the same impossible skill Rafa was watching with.

Rafa didn't know whose side he was on. A pilot whose crew had been murdered by the man who owned this train had exactly two sides to choose from, and one of them had already killed everyone he'd worked with. Rafa watched the faint contact at the edge of the scanner and did not reach for the radio — because the radio was a thing the god could hear, and the other pilot, if he was anything like Rafa, would be listening on the same silence two kilometres back, deciding.

So Rafa watched, and waited, and listened to the belly of the train, and did the thing he was best at: nothing, precisely, for as long as nothing was the correct action. Patience was a skill the way flying was a skill — it looked like absence and was in fact the most active thing a man could do, the held breath, the unsent word, the hand kept off the throttle until the throttle was the answer. Rafa had patience the way other men had tempers: it was the weather of him, the thing he lived inside. Tonight the patience was the only thing between him and the fear — the fear that the other pilot would reach the car first, and take the woman breathing behind the wall for his own buyer, his own ending, and the carrying-off was the thing Rafa had decided, somewhere in the cold and the dark, he was not going to allow.

He thought about the woman. He'd never met her — knew her only as the thirty-seventh signature, the breath behind the wall, the reason the job had stopped being a job. But he thought about her the way he thought about everyone he flew: as a weight he was responsible for setting down safe. Every person Rafa had ever carried had been, to him, a weight. The pod was the machine; the people were the weight; the landing was the setting-down; and the setting-down safe was the only prayer he knew. He'd flown strangers, criminals, a head of state once under a false manifest — and every one of them had been the same weight, carried the same way, set down with the same care. The woman behind the wall was the weight tonight. He intended to set her down safe on a platform with a name, in a country with a court, and the intending was what kept his hands steady and his shoulder quiet and his patience intact through the seventh hour of the cold.

The comms bead in his ear — the one frequency the god couldn't hear, the one Naïma had rigged — carried the murmur of the crew above. He didn't speak. He listened. Jae's thin bright counts. Naïma's silences, which were movement. The Architect's low directives. Theo's occasional deflections — which were, Rafa had come to understand, Theo's way of caring for people without admitting it. The crew moved above him like a phrase he was part of but couldn't see, and the being-part was the warmth the cold couldn't reach, and Rafa held it the way he held the throttle: lightly, constantly, with the whole of his attention.

Seven hours. The shoulder. The cold. The other pilot, two kilometres back, patient as he was patient. The woman behind the wall. The window five hours away, then three, then — when the Architect turned the machine — ninety seconds, and the pod would open, and Rafa would do what only he could do, and the weight would be set down safe, or it wouldn't, and the wouldn't was the thing he was not going to think about. So he thought instead about the vibration, and the couplings, and the green of the clamps, and the held breath of a train carrying a woman toward a door he intended to open from the outside.

He checked the clamps. Green. Green. Green. Green. He settled the bad shoulder against the hull, and the hull hummed against the shoulder, and the cold came, and Rafa waited — because waiting was the skill, and the skill was the only thing he had, and the only thing he needed, until the window.
