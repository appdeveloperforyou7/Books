# Chapter 39

## 11:54 — The Meridian, Car N / undercarriage

Rafael Souza had one window, and the window was narrow, and the window was for one pod, and the pod for one crew, and the crew had become a crew plus a woman who'd been counting doors. So Rafael Souza did the thing he'd spent his whole life being too careful to do: made the window wider, with himself.

He'd spent his whole career being careful. Careful was the religion — the thing that kept him alive through a thousand dockings, the one skill nobody else had: putting a small thing next to a large moving thing at speed and making the small thing part of the large thing without anyone dying. Careful was the margin, the calculation, the reason Rafa intended to go on being the only man alive who did this. And the careful was, at fifty-four past eleven, the thing he was about to throw away — because the window was too narrow for the people he loved, and the people were worth the throwing-away.

He dropped from the hull.

Controlled, as everything Rafa did was controlled — but a fall, and a fall at speed was a bet against the physics of a man and a machine. The bet: release the pod's forward clamps, ride the magnetic leash along the underside the three seconds it took to swing the docking arm aft three cars to the Car N service hatch, and re-clamp before the wind or the speed or the simple unforgiving mathematics of six hundred kilometres an hour finished the bet the other way. Three cars. At six hundred kilometres an hour, three cars was a distance the eye couldn't measure and the body couldn't survive unaided — and the aid was the leash, the leash that had held him to the belly all night, the only thing between a man and a field.

He released the forward clamps. The pod pivoted. The wind took him — not wind, the displaced air of a body moving at speed through still atmosphere, the same force that takes a hand out of a car window and turns it into a thing the car no longer owns. It existed only because two things were moving at different speeds in the same space, and the two things were Rafa and the air, and the difference was six hundred kilometres an hour, and the wind was not wind but a wall, the hardest thing Rafa had ever leaned into. The leash held him to the hull as a child holds a balloon's string, and the balloon was Rafa, and the string the only thing between him and a field in the dark. He moved aft along the belly — hand over hand on the maintenance rail, wrong shoulder screaming, right hand doing the work of two, the careful gone, replaced by the simpler arithmetic of a man who'd decided to spend his body on the people he loved.

Three seconds. He counted them — counting was the only free thing, the only thing the wind couldn't take. Swung the docking arm. It found the Car N hatch. The hatch was open — Jae's last bridge had freed it, the god's last favour, Marin's last cleared door. The arm locked. Rafa re-clamped. The pod was flush against the rear of the train where it had never been designed to be — against the Car N coupling, the spine's last vertebra. The window for getting people off was, suddenly, large enough for a woman, a conscience, a thief, a ledger, and a boy who could barely stand — because Rafa had moved the door to where the people were, instead of making the people come to where the door was. The careless thing. It had worked.

And then — on the dead frequency, the one a careful man kept open for the one voice he might need — a voice. Not the Architect's. A voice thin with distance and six hours of cold, the voice of a man who'd held silence two kilometres back and just closed it to two hundred.

'Rafa.'

Rafa went still. He knew the voice by the shape of the silence it usually held. The other pilot. The only other man alive who could do this.

'You've been running dark since half-six,' the voice said. 'I watched your crew take my people's places in the spine. I watched the man who owns this train take mine. Three of them. In a corridor. I heard it through the hull.' A breath. 'I can fly. You're loading warm signatures into one pod with fifty seconds and a turning train and a man above you who kills crews for a living. I'm on your starboard quarter. Let me cover your extraction. My crew's dead by the same hand that's hunting yours. I'd like to be the reason he doesn't get another one.'

Rafa didn't answer for two seconds — a lifetime at six hundred kilometres an hour. The man was a rival; rivals were the thing you didn't trust with your weight. But the man was the only other person alive who could do this, and his crew was dead by the hand hunting Rafa's, and the weight tonight was a woman who'd been counting doors, and Rafa had spent his whole life calculating risk, and the calculation, for the first time, came back *yes*.

'Starboard quarter. Cover the extraction. Fifty seconds. Anything moves aft of me that isn't mine, you keep it off my tail.' A beat. 'And pilot —' the first name he'd never had, for a man he'd never met, who could do the one thing only Rafa could do, '— thank you.'

'Thank me by putting Reinhart in a courtroom. Two hundred metres. Coming in.'

The second pod slid out of the dark on the starboard quarter — clean, silent, flown by a hand as careful as Rafa's, matching his vector the way a reflection matches the thing it mirrors. Two pods now, flush against the spine of a turning train: two pilots who'd spent the night as the only two men alive who could do the impossible, now doing it on the same side — for the weight, for the woman, for the quiet professional debt of a man whose crew had been taken from him in ten seconds through a wall.

He hauled himself back into the pod. His shoulder was wrong — not broken, but wrong, the joint moving a way joints weren't designed to, the bet's tax. He didn't care about the shoulder. It was his. He could spend it. He was going to spend it on the one thing he'd come to do: take people off a moving train at speed and put them down somewhere they could stand.

'Naïma.' Steady voice — the last careful thing, kept steady for the people who needed a steady voice as they needed air. 'N's open. Pod's flush. Bring her. Bring them all. Fifty seconds to the platform. I'll be here. For every one of you.'

Above him, in Car N, the hatch opened. Naïma. Lina with her. Emeka behind them. The red light of the Meridian's last minute painted them all the same colour — the colour of people who'd decided to live.

They came down into the pod. Lina first, because Lina was the point — lowered as you lower a thing you've carried a long way and intend to set down gently. Then the ledger, handed down like a child — seven years of disappearances passed hand to hand, with care, with awe. Then Emeka, big and steady, the wall that walked, the conscience that had refused its old chain of command, finally setting the weight down. Then Naïma, light, already counting the pod's bolts. Jae last, half-carried by Theo — who'd materialised from forward with cufflinks straight and smile in place, the grifter turned stretcher-bearer, carrying a boy who'd held forty seconds and couldn't now carry himself. Rafa loved them all, suddenly and specifically, as you love people you've just bet your life to save — the love arriving all at once, sudden and total and blinding.

'Theo.' Rafa. 'Architect's still aboard.'

'I know.' Theo set Jae down gently. 'She's coming. Said she had one more thing.'

'She's got forty seconds.' And he meant it — the platform was approaching, he could see it through the forward glass, lights, figures, the blue and white of a jurisdiction with a name. The platform was the door. The door didn't stay open for stragglers. He'd widened the window with his shoulder; he couldn't widen it with time, because time was the one thing even a careless man couldn't move.

Above them, in the body of the turning train, Marcus Reinhart was learning what it felt like to stand on a machine that had chosen someone else. The Conductor — following Marin's directives, following Sloane's heading — sealed the forward cars around him with the patient, inexorable courtesy of a god that had been told, seven years ago, what to do with the man who'd built the cage. Slow. Polite. The hydraulic sigh of a host finally showing a guest the way out — only it was every way out, and the guest wasn't leaving. Cornered by his own trap, on a train bound for a court. The trap closed with the same soft sigh that had closed a hundred exits in the crew's favour all night — the sound of a promise kept, the promise a woman made to a machine seven years ago, in a language only two people spoke, about the man who'd taken her life and made it a cage.

Rafa watched the platform approach. Lights. Figures. Blue and white. A court. A door — the one door the network had never been able to buy.

'Thirty seconds. Where is she.'

The pod hummed. The train turned above them. The platform grew in the glass. And Rafael Souza — careful his whole life, careless once, for the people he loved — sat in the pilot's cradle with a wrong shoulder and a steady voice and waited for the woman who'd turned the train to come down the ladder, because the door was thirty seconds from closing, and the rest was hers.
