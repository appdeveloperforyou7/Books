# Chapter 5

## 23:50 — Toledo Staging

The god of the train had a soft spot, and Jae-won Kang was going to put a needle in it.

He sat in the cold back room of a logistics warehouse outside Toledo — aviation fuel, old coffee, the metallic chill of a building that stored things not meant to be looked at closely — and ran the simulation for the forty-third time. On the screen, a simplified Conductor pulsed: sensor mesh, routing layer, and — isolated in red, glowing like a wound — the medical bus, the one channel that could ask the god to open its fist. In the larger dark of the warehouse, Rafa's pod waited under a drop cloth, an insect shape Jae wasn't supposed to touch and didn't need to. The pod was Rafa's religion. Jae had his own.

Forty-three runs. Forty-three successes, because the simulation was kind, and simulations were always kind, and the real machine would not be. Simulations forgave the heat in an implant. They forgave a shaking hand. The real Conductor forgave nothing — it noticed, and adapted, and the noticing was the part the simulation couldn't reproduce.

He touched the implant. The skin was warm today. Warmer than yesterday, and yesterday warmer than the day before. He'd been keeping a small private graph of the warmth in the back of a notebook he'd never show anyone. The line went gently and steadily up. Lines like that were either healing or dying, and he didn't have time to decide which, so he filed it under *fine* and moved on.

The bridge worked like this. His implant spoke the medical bus natively — the surgeons put it in to stop his seizures, and the only way to stop a seizure in real time was to talk to the body's own network faster than the body could misfire. The Conductor's medical bus used the same protocol. Not a coincidence. The Meridian's life-support was built to interrogate exactly the class of device Jae carried in his skull — an implantable medical unit speaking the same short-range medical frequency the train's onboard defibrillators used to talk to their monitors — a frequency no one ever questioned, because no one ever questioned a medical frequency. The same people who built the Conductor built the standard, and standards were locks that forgot to be locks, if you had the right key. Jae had found the seam the way he found every seam: by reading the documentation the engineers were proudest of, and looking for where pride had made them lazy. They'd reused a medical standard because it was safe and nobody questioned medical standards. So the god of the train spoke fluent hospital, and so did Jae, and the conversation between them was going to be very brief and very one-sided.

For twelve seconds — if he pressed the implant to a live port and ran the handshake — the Conductor would treat him as a justified override. A patient in distress. A doctor with authority. For twelve seconds the god would open whichever door he asked, because its first law was older than its suspicion. Then the god would notice the lie, and adapt, and never fall for the same handshake twice. He had four handshakes in him tonight: four lies, four shapes, each spent and gone, each teaching the god a little more about its enemy.

The danger wasn't the god. The danger was his own skull.

The bridge wasn't current — it was signal, an inductive handshake, his implant's coil coupling to the port's field and exchanging packets the way two radios exchange words, nothing flowing through him but information. The danger was what his implant did with the signal. To hold the handshake it had to answer, and answering meant firing its own stimulation — the pulses it normally reserved for the lip of a seizure, only now sustained, continuous, dumped into the nerves that govern the heartbeat and the fear, the threads the surgeons had wired it against. Twelve seconds of that and the body's core began to object: heart rate slipping, the rhythm going wrong — not from electricity across the chest but from the nervous system arguing with itself, the way it does in the rare, documented cases where one of these implants pushes a patient toward cardiac arrest.

Hold it too long and the argument didn't end in a skipped beat. It ended in the heart stopping cold — on orders from an over-cooked brain, the rhythm simply giving up.

He'd done it once before. Osaka, eight months ago. A smaller system, a shorter bridge — six seconds. He came out of it on the floor of a server room, vision white, pulse doing something pulses aren't designed to do, and the woman he was working for stood over him and said, *Again.* So he stood up and did it again, because he would rather die than be the person who said he could do a thing and then couldn't. The woman paid him well and never hired him again — which was, Jae suspected, her way of saying she'd seen the graph in the back of his notebook and decided he was an asset with an expiry date, and the date wasn't her problem.

He was not going to die tonight. He'd told the Architect twelve, and twelve was within the margin, and the margin was real because Jae didn't invent margins. But the margin assumed a clean handshake on the first try, and an implant that wasn't running hot, and a god that didn't change protocol mid-bridge — which a clever god might, if it felt the lie forming. Three assumptions. Three cracks in the floor, and the floor was all that held the house up.

The simulation pulsed. Forty-four runs. Forty-four successes. The medical bus glowed red in the diagram, patient, waiting.

Jae closed the laptop and pressed two fingers hard against the implant until the warmth became a small sharp ache. Ninety minutes to the Madrid window. In ninety minutes he'd step into a pod with six people and an empty seat, ride it across the dark at the side of a train, walk up to a port in a moving machine, and put the needle in.

He believed he could do it. He believed it the way he believed in gravity — not because it was kind, but because the mathematics held.

The mathematics held.

He touched the implant one more time. The skin was warm. The graph was going up. The woman in Osaka had seen it coming. Jae saw it now.

He chose to believe it was fine. It was the only shelf left with room on it.
