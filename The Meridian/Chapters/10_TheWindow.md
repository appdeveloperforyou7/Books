# Chapter 10

## 01:30 — Madrid Window

The window opened in the dark like an eye.

Sloane felt it before she saw it — the moment the Meridian's guideway and the staging grid aligned, ninety seconds in which the train, still doing six hundred kilometres an hour, passed close enough to the ground to touch. Rafa's instruments chirped — a small clean sound, one machine recognising another. The pod's hull went from cold to humming. Somewhere in the black, a silver shape the length of a cathedral was bearing down on them at a speed that would turn a missed grab into a field of debris.

She had ninety seconds to decide whether the man who'd hired her was on it.

The pieces had been arriving all night, and she'd been refusing to assemble them — the way you refuse to assemble a shape you're afraid to see. The verbal fingerprint from Theo, an hour ago: a buyer in the Lisbon lounge speaking the Client's exact sentence, *kindness I save for people who finish the job*, in the Client's exact cadence, with the little pause before *kindness* Theo had called the one human crack in the man's voice. The old man's careful announcement that the Client would be *unreachable* — a word you only deployed to stop a question you couldn't afford. The empty eighth seat. The bracelet. The seven-day anniversary. And the one fact that wouldn't stop pressing: a man who paid in advance and spoke only through proxies didn't, as a rule, board the trains he robbed.

She assembled them now, in the ninety seconds she had left, the way she assembled every plan: coldly, without feeling. The feeling would come later.

The Client had a man aboard who spoke with his mouth. That was the first piece — the Client's voice was on the train even if the body wasn't. But the old man had said *unreachable*, not *not aboard*, and the word choice was the tell. You didn't call a man five thousand kilometres away *unreachable*. You called him that when he was close enough to be reached, and you were afraid someone would try.

Sloane pulled up the bidder manifest Jae had cracked two hours ago. Thirty-six names, most of them lies. She'd read it twice and dismissed it. She read it a third time now, in the cold blue light of the pod, looking not for the lie but for the silence — the name that explained nothing, the bidder with no provenance and no greed, the passenger who'd come not for the jewel but for the job. A buyer who wanted the stone would have a bidding history, a hotel, a flight, the small expensive litter buyers always left. A buyer who'd come to watch would have none of it. A buyer who'd come to watch would be a name with nothing behind it.

There. Car C. Bertrand Hollis, private collector. No public profile. No bidding history. No photograph in any file Jae's machines could crack. A name with nothing behind it — the kind you gave yourself when you wanted to be on a train without being on a train.

Sloane didn't know the face. She didn't need to. She knew the silence. She'd sat across from it in a windowless room, and it had set a dead girl's bracelet on the table and called itself the Client, and the silence around the name had been exactly this silence — careful, expensive, the silence of a man who'd practised being nobody until the practising became the person.

Marcus Reinhart was aboard the Meridian. The man who'd hired them to rob his own train was riding it tonight, under a name that wasn't a name, in the car closest to the front.

The realisation didn't hit her so much as settle — the way a lock settles when the last tumbler turns. Of course he was aboard. You didn't build a machine like the Meridian and trust strangers to run it for you. You sat in Car C under a borrowed name, with a glass of champagne you didn't drink, and you watched the clock, and waited for the door to open — the door you'd paid seven people to open because you couldn't open it yourself without revealing the door existed.

Which meant the job was not the job.

Sloane closed the manifest. The pod hummed. The window was half-spent. Jae had his eyes shut, fingers on his neck. Theo was being no one. Naïma was counting bolts. Emeka carried his worry in his shoulders. None of them knew the man who'd hired them was sixty metres forward, watching the same clock.

She had a choice. Abort — signal Rafa, pull the pod, scrub the window, walk into the Madrid dark with seven people and a notebook and no answers. The safe play. What a careful thief did when the mark turned out to be aboard the train he was robbing: get off before you get on.

Sloane was a careful thief. She was also a woman who'd spent seven years with a gap in a charm bracelet and a notebook she couldn't open, and the man who'd made both was sixty metres away — close enough, for the first time in seven years, to reach.

She didn't signal the abort.

'Window in forty,' Rafa said from the front, hands on the controls, voice as calm as if he did this on Sundays. 'Everybody ready to be very uncomfortable for about three seconds.'

'The Client is on the train,' Sloane said.

Nobody moved. The pod hummed. Jae opened one eye.

'Repeat that,' Theo said — a voice stripped back to plain steel, no Lord anything-or-other in it.

'The Client. Marcus Reinhart. Car C, under the name Bertrand Hollis. He's aboard his own job. Has been since Lisbon.' She looked at each of them in the blue light. 'We're going to board anyway.'

'Why,' Emeka said. Not a protest. A question, the way honest men ask questions — without heat, with the steady weight of a man who needed the answer before he could move.

'Because he put my sister's bracelet on a table three weeks ago and told me it was personal. Because a man who does that wants something from me he isn't going to get by phone. Because he's sixty metres away and for the first time in seven years I'm close enough to ask him what he did — and I'm not wasting a window I've waited seven years for.' She paused. 'And because I don't think he hired us to steal a jewel. I think he hired us to open a door he can't open himself. And I want to know what's behind it before I hand him the key.'

The pod hummed. The window bled away.

'If any of you want off, get off now. There's no second window. Only the dark and a long walk and the rest of your lives — and I won't think less of a single one of you.'

Naïma pulled her hood tighter. Jae closed his eye again. Theo adjusted a cufflink. Emeka put a hand on the bag with the blueprint. Nobody moved toward the exit.

'A rousing speech,' Theo said. 'Genuinely. I'm moved. I'd like it noted that I am choosing, of my own free will, to board a train owned by the man trying to kill us, and that this is the single worst decision of a life not short of bad decisions.' He paused. 'I'm in.'

'Noted,' Sloane said. 'Twenty seconds, Rafa.'

'Twenty,' Rafa said. 'Hold something.'

The pod lurched. The dark outside the glass began to move, then streak, then become a wall of silver light bearing down on them out of the night at a speed that didn't forgive a single mistake.

Sloane braced. The notebook pressed her ribs. The seven-digit code pressed the back of her mind. And somewhere ahead, in a first-class salon she couldn't yet see, a man who spoke through old men and paid in advance watched a clock and waited for the crew he'd hired to step out of the dark and onto his train.

He didn't know — couldn't know — that the woman he'd hired to open his door had spent seven years learning to open doors, and had spent the last ninety seconds deciding to open one he hadn't planned for.

The silver wall filled the glass. Rafa's hands moved. The pod screamed toward the side of the moving train, and Sloane Vance stopped being careful, and committed.

The window closed behind them like an eye.
