# Chapter 11

## THE BOARD

## 01:32 — Madrid Window

Rafael Souza had docked at speed exactly twice in his life. The first time was an accident he'd spent four years learning to repeat on purpose. Tonight he was going to do it a third time with six people and a secret in the back of his pod — and if he was wrong, none of them would ever be wrong about anything again.

*Ninety seconds.*

The number sat in the corner of the glass, small and polite, ticking down. Ninety seconds in which the Meridian, still doing six hundred kilometres an hour, would pass close enough to the staging grid for a human being to touch it. After that the train would slew away from the ground and into the high guideway, and the window would close, and the next one was Belgrade — four countries and a lifetime east, on a train that did not stop.

The Meridian came out of the dark like a wall of white noise.

It didn't arrive the way a train arrives. It was simply not there, and then it was everywhere — a sound first, a pressure in the ears, a low subsonic throb Rafa felt in his teeth before he heard it. Then a shape. Silver. Infinite. Filling the windscreen edge to edge, a moving cliff of metal doing six hundred kilometres an hour in the dark. The pod was a gnat in front of it.

He didn't look at it. Looking at the thing about to kill you was how you flinched, and flinching at six hundred kilometres an hour was how you became a smear. He looked at the instruments. They told him the relative speed — five hundred and twelve and falling — the closing vector, the magnetic handshake between the pod's belly and the guideway. The instruments were Rafa's religion. He trusted them the way other men trusted gods: completely, with the understanding that the trust was earned daily, by precision, and lapsed the moment the precision did.

Behind him, the six of them sat in the webbing. He could feel them in the pod's balance — the small shifts as six bodies tensed at once, the held breath of six people understanding, all at the same instant, that the next ninety seconds were the only kind that mattered.

'Everybody shut up,' Rafa said, in the voice he used for funerals and landings, which was the same voice. 'And hold something that isn't me.'

*Eighty-one seconds.* Relative speed three-eighty and falling.

'Naïma. When I call the lock, you go. You don't wait for the seal to finish. The ladder's outside the hull skirt — eight rungs, bare alloy, no cage. Wind at altitude will try to take your legs out from under you. Keep three points of contact until you're inside the hatch. If you slip, I can't catch you, and the train won't stop, and the slip is the end. Do you understand.'

'Yes.'

'Say it back to me. Three points of contact.'

'Three points of contact.' No fear in her voice. He wished there were. Fear would have meant she understood.

*Sixty-eight seconds.* Relative speed two-forty.

'Jae. You're second. Don't open your eyes on the ladder — your hand knows the rungs, your eyes will lie to you at speed. Count the rungs by feel. Eight, then the lip, then drop. Two seconds inside the hatch or the seal catches your ankle.'

'I'll be inside in one.'

*Fifty-four seconds.* Relative speed one-eighty.

The Meridian's hull filled the right-hand glass now, close enough that Rafa could see the grain of the metal, the rivets, the faint stippling of dust a continent long. Six hundred kilometres an hour, and he could see the dust. The mind refused it. The eye kept reporting. He made himself look at the instruments.

'Emeka. Third. The coat. If the coat catches the wind it'll sail and it'll take you with it — button it, hold the lapels, go fast. You're the biggest thing on the ladder and the wind knows it.'

*Forty-one seconds.* Relative speed one-twenty.

The pod was running with the train now — not matched, not yet, but running alongside, the gap narrowing, the magnetic handshake strengthening. Rafa's hands did the work his eyes wouldn't. Throttle back. Feel the pod's shudder smooth as the two velocities began to marry, the way two gears marry when the teeth finally find each other. Once they matched, the hull would carry its own thin country of slower air — the boundary layer, a cushion of near-still gas clinging to the skin of the train where the six-hundred-kilometre slipstream thinned to something merely savage. The boundary layer was the only reason a human could cross the gap and live. Outside it, the slipstream was a wall that killed. Inside it, against the hull, it was a wind that only wanted to.

*Twenty-nine seconds.* Relative speed sixty. Falling fast.

'Sloane. Fourth. Theo. Fifth. In that order. Theo — no flourish on the ladder. I know you. No wave, no comment, no performance. Two hands, eight rungs, in.'

'You take the joy out of everything.'

'I take the death out of everything. Same thing.'

*Eighteen seconds.* Relative speed twenty-two.

Almost matched. Almost. The gap between the pod and the hull was the width of a man's hand, then the width of a fist, then the width of nothing. The hull slid past the glass so close the rivets were individual, countable, each one a small silver eye watching him back.

*Ten.*

The relative speed read zero and held.

'Lock,' Rafa said — and fired the docking clamps.

The pod kissed the hull with a sound like a door closing in a quiet house. The shudder ran through the whole frame, into his spine, into his teeth. Locked. Married. The pod and the train one body, moving at one speed, six hundred kilometres an hour, the gnat become part of the cliff.

'Door's open,' he said. 'Two seconds up. Move like it matters.'

*Seven seconds of window left.*

Naïma went first. She was out of the webbing and onto the ladder before the seal finished cycling — a ghost in black pulling herself into the body of the train. The wind hit her on the third rung. Rafa saw it — saw her legs swing out sideways as the slipstream took them, her body horizontal for one lurching heartbeat, her hands the only thing keeping her on the alloy. Three points of contact. She'd said it back to him. Three points of contact. She hauled her legs in, found the fourth rung, the fifth, and was through the hatch and gone.

Jae, eyes shut, hands reading the rungs. Faster than Naïma — he trusted the map in his head and let his body run it blind. Eight rungs, the lip, the drop. Inside. One second.

Emeka. The coat. Rafa had been right to warn him — the wind caught the hem the moment he was clear of the pod, a black sail snapping sideways, and for one second the big man was a pendulum, the coat trying to tear him off the ladder, his grip on the alloy the only thing between him and the dark. He didn't let go. He went up the rungs one-handed, the other fist clenched in the lapels, hauling the coat in against his body like a thing he was strangling, and was through.

*Three seconds of window.*

Sloane. She didn't hesitate — out and up and in, clean, the way she did everything, and she didn't look back, and Rafa loved her a little for that, the way you love anyone who trusts you with the hard part and refuses to watch you do it.

Theo last. Two hands. Eight rungs. In. No flourish. Rafa noted it — the closest Theo Harlow had ever come to respect.

The seal closed. The window ended. The Meridian slewed toward the high guideway and the staging grid fell away beneath them and was gone.

Rafa sat in the pilot's cradle and let out the breath he'd been holding for ninety seconds. His hands were steady. He checked them, because Rafa checked things. Steady. Good.

Then — because checking was the difference between a pilot who flew once and a pilot who flew home — he ran the passive scanner across the hull he was clinging to. Read-only. The kind of look that didn't announce itself. The belly of the Meridian was supposed to be empty.

It was not empty.

*Three cars aft.* A second pod. Dark. Clamped to the hull in almost exactly the spot his shouldn't have been. No lights. No transponder. No signature except the one it couldn't hide from a man who knew what a hidden pod looked like — because Rafa had built his career on being the only man who did this, and he'd been told all night he was the only man who did this.

He was not.

Someone else had boarded the Meridian at speed. Someone else had done the impossible thing only Rafa was supposed to be able to do — had done it before him, cleanly enough that the only trace was the shape of a pod on the belly of a train. They were aboard now, somewhere above his head, in a train he'd just put six of his people inside.

Rafa touched the radio. Didn't transmit. If there was a second pod, there was a second crew, and if there was a second crew the Meridian wasn't the private stage Sloane believed it was — and the radio was a thing the Conductor could hear.

He sat in the dark under the belly of a moving train and did the maths the way he always did: calm, certain, alone. The maths told him the plan was a story, and the story had just added a character no one had written in.

Six of his people were inside. So was at least one more crew. So was a god that didn't sleep. And Rafa was outside all of it, clinging to the underside, with nothing but a radio he couldn't use and a window twelve hours away.

He settled in. He was good at waiting. He did it on Sundays.

But he kept one hand on the clamps, and one eye on the second pod, and didn't — for the rest of the long night — quite manage to feel calm again. Because whoever had flown that second pod had flown it the way Rafa flew: clean, silent, and well. And the only thing more dangerous than a man who could do the impossible was a second man who could do it too.
