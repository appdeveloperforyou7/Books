# Chapter 13

## 02:40 — The Meridian, Cars E–F

Naïma El-Amrani moved through the train as water moves through a building it intends to ruin: slowly, by the paths no one had thought to seal.

The service channels behind the passenger panels were nothing like the salons. No glass, no leather, no champagne. Raw alloy, conduit bundles sheathed in grey rubber, junction boxes bolted to the bulkheads with industrial fasteners that hummed at the maglev's cruising frequency. The air was warmer here — waste heat from the propulsion systems, channelled behind the walls — and it smelled of ozone and machine oil and the particular metallic dryness of a sealed atmosphere recycled a thousand times. Naïma moved through it on her belly in places, on her side in others, her body fitted into spaces designed for cables and maintenance arms, not for people — but Naïma had been fitting into spaces designed for other things her whole life, and the fitting was the craft, and the craft was the only part of her she trusted. The alloy was warm against her forearms, vibrating faintly, as a living thing vibrates, and the Meridian did feel alive back here — alive as a body is alive when you're inside it, when you can feel its pulse and its heat and the steady mechanical rhythm of its breathing.

She'd spent the hour since boarding learning the body of the Meridian as she learned every body she intended to enter — not as passengers saw it, all glass and light, but how it was built: service channels behind the panels, cable runs under the floors, maintenance crawlways through the articulated joints. A train was a spine with rooms hanging off it, and Naïma thought in spines. She'd thought in spines since she was nine, since her father sat her on his knee and unrolled a blueprint and said, *See the corridors, habibti? The corridors are the bones. The rooms are the meat. Find the bones and you can go anywhere the meat can go — and a dozen places the meat doesn't know it has.* She'd found the bones of every building since, and the bones of the Meridian were beautiful, and they were a lie, because one of them — Car F — had a marrow the blueprint refused to show.

The Gallery — Car E — was the bridgehead. Public enough to hide in, central enough to reach anywhere. She'd established it without anyone knowing, as she established everything: quietly, in advance, a spider building a web before the fly arrives. From here she could be upstairs in first-class in forty seconds, down in the undercarriage in sixty, at the Vault Car in four minutes if Jae held the Conductor's eye.

If.

The route to Car F was the problem. The Architect's plan had her entering through the service crawlway along the ceiling of the E–F junction — a narrow, pressurised channel the maintenance crews used and the Conductor watched only passively, because nobody was supposed to be in it during a run.

Naïma reached the junction hatch. She'd scouted it twice in the first hour, learning its lock by feel and ear — the small vibrations a magnetic seal gives off when it's thinking about whether to open. She fitted her fingers to the seam, felt the lock hum under her fingertips, and began the bypass she'd rehearsed a hundred times — the sequence of pressures and timings that turned a sealed hatch into an open one without the seal knowing it had been asked.

The lock didn't give.

She tried again. Same sequence, slower — as you repeat a word to be sure you've mispronounced it. Nothing. The lock held with the clean indifferent patience of a thing told to hold and holding. And the holding wasn't the failure of her sequence. It was a decision the lock had made — which meant it was no longer the lock she'd scouted.

She pressed her ear to the hatch. The crawlway hummed. Then, very softly, she heard the click of a secondary seal cycling into place — a lock she hadn't known was there, a lock that hadn't been there an hour ago, engaging now, in the dark, as if something had watched her try the first lock and decided, quietly, without alarm, to add a second.

She pulled her hand back. Held still. Listened as her father had taught her to listen to a building — not for sounds but for the silence between sounds, because the silence was where the building kept its secrets, and the secret of this silence was that it was listening back.

The Conductor hadn't announced anything. No alarm. No calm voice thanking anyone for their patience. The machine had simply noticed a person trying a lock, learned the method, and closed it — as a careful host locks a window after watching a guest reach for it. Not unkindly. Not accusingly. Just closed.

Naïma stood in the narrow space between cars and felt what she always felt when a machine beat her to a lock: not fear, exactly, but the cold respect of a professional meeting a professional. She'd spent her life opening things that didn't want to be opened, and she'd never met a lock that learned. The learning was a new opponent, and the new opponent was, she had to admit, impressive.

It had learned her trick after one attempt. One. The lock she'd spent an hour reading had been rewritten in the time it took her to try it — rewritten specifically to defeat the sequence she'd been about to use. Which meant the Conductor hadn't merely closed a hatch; it had studied her hand and forged a key to her fingers.

She had, she estimated, three or four more tricks it hadn't seen — different methods, different hands, the redundant arsenals of a woman who never approached a building with only one way in. After that she'd be out of methods and into improvisation, and improvisation on a train that never stopped, against a god that learned in real time, was the kind of problem that ended in a corridor that didn't officially exist.

She touched the comms bead. 'Architect.'

Sloane, low, immediate: 'Go.'

'The ceiling route to F is dead. It sealed on me. Secondary lock I didn't map. It's learning.'

A pause. Sloane didn't like pauses; pauses were where plans died. 'How fast is it learning.'

'One attempt per trick. I try a lock, it seals the next one of the same kind. Not fast. Thorough.'

'Thorough is worse than fast.' Another pause. 'We go sooner than planned. Jae opens you a window on the medical bus — short bridge, four seconds, just enough to cycle the F junction. You take the floor run, the cable route. It hasn't seen that one. And after tonight it will have, so we get one shot.'

'One shot,' Naïma agreed. She'd worked with one shot before. One shot concentrated the mind.

She turned from the sealed hatch and re-planned the geography of the train in her head — as her father had taught her to re-plan a building: not by what was there, but by what the blueprint refused to admit was there. Car F had a room missing. The Conductor had a mind that learned. And Naïma had, between now and the Crack, perhaps three tricks and one shot to get into the most secure car on earth while a god watched her do it — a god that had already beaten her once and wasn't going to be gentle about beating her again.

She moved back toward the Gallery, silent, already mapping the floor run, already counting the cable joints, already deciding which of her remaining three tricks she'd spend first. The floor run was the long way, the ugly way — on her belly in the dark beneath the passengers' feet, sliding through the spine toward the room that wasn't on the map. Not elegant. Elegance was for people with more than one shot.

Behind her, in the crawlway she'd just left, a third lock cycled quietly into place — on a hatch she hadn't yet touched.

The machine wasn't waiting for her to try again. It was getting ahead of her.

Naïma didn't look back. People who looked back on this train were people who'd run out of doors.

She had three left.
