# Chapter 4

## 23:07 — Lisbon

After the crew went dark, Sloane opened the Conductor.

Not the train. The footage. Seven years of recordings on a drive she carried close, always — a thing she was afraid to lose and afraid to look at, which was its own kind of loyalty. She plugged it into the office screen and let the god's voice fill the empty room.

*'Car Seven is temporarily restricted for service. The Meridian apologises for any inconvenience. Refreshments are available in the Gallery.'*

A lie. She knew it as well as she knew the Leviathan's flaw map. The second run. She could see it on the screen — the salon footage, grainy, timestamped, the passengers' oblivious faces. The Conductor had sealed Car Seven for forty-three seconds because a courier in Car Six carried a weapon and a heartbeat over the threshold the machine had decided was safe. The service line was politeness. Underneath it, the god had watched a man through the walls until he stopped being a problem, then unsealed the car and apologised. None of the passengers had noticed. The courier had walked off at the terminus and never known how close he'd come to not walking off it at all.

She scrubbed forward. The footage had a texture — the slightly degraded quality of surveillance carried through too many compression cycles, the colours washed, the sound thin and tinny through the laptop's speakers. But the tells were there, the small human stammers she'd written into the interface fifteen years ago, invisible to anyone who hadn't built them.

Third run. A hatch that would not open. The Conductor cycling the lock once, twice, a third time — and on the third cycle, pausing. One-point-two seconds. Then releasing.

She sat forward. The PA voice came through the speakers, flat, patient, cleaned of every inflection — and she heard it. The pause. One-point-two seconds. A machine did not pause. A machine either acted or didn't. One-point-two seconds was a hesitation, and a hesitation was a thought, and the brochure swore the Conductor did not think.

Sloane knew the pause. She had built it.

She'd built all of it — the small human failures that made the voice bearable. The fractional drop in pitch before a lockdown, so the passengers heard warning before they heard the words. The half-second delay before the word *patience*, because patience was the one thing the god had in surplus and the one word it should never sound smug saying. The one-point-two-second hesitation at the edge of a decision — long enough to read as thought, short enough to read as grace. She'd argued for each of them in the Lausanne room, and the funding men had smiled the smile of people who didn't understand and didn't care to, and signed off, and she'd put the tells in anyway — because a god that never hesitated was a god no one would trust, and a god no one trusted was a god that couldn't do its job. The tells were the lie that made the truth usable. The hesitation was the trapdoor she'd left for herself, in case she ever needed to know whether the machine was deciding or merely computing.

Someone had used her tells tonight. Someone who knew them as well as she did.

Lausanne. Fifteen years ago. A white room on the third floor of a building whose name she'd signed an NDA to forget. Server hum. The particular institutional light of a place that did not want to be remembered — flat, even, the colour of bleach. Four of them at the build: Reinhart, who funded it and never understood it and came once a week in an expensive coat to ask when it would be done; a hardware engineer with nervous hands, quiet, precise, who did the spine's physical layer and died later of something the papers called an accident; Sloane, who built the soft layer — the interface, the voice, the tells; and one other, who built the rest. The one who understood the machine not as a face to be worn but as a body to be lived in.

She could still feel the microphone. The booth at the end of the corridor, Tuesday afternoon, the foam pop-shield smelling faintly of someone else's breath. She'd read test sentences into it for three hours — "The temperature in Car Three is twenty-one degrees. The dining car is now serving. Please remain seated" — and the voice they'd cleaned from those recordings was the voice now speaking through her laptop speakers, patient, level, stripped of every human irregularity except the ones she'd deliberately left.

She closed the laptop. Her own voice came back at her out of the speakers a half-second late — the PA voice, the ghost of a twenty-three-year-old reading sentences into a microphone for money she'd needed and a future she hadn't earned. The god spoke in her voice. It always had.

The office was dark except for the screen. Rain moved against the window. The Tagus glinted far below, and across it, the Meridian's running lights lay silver on the water — already leaving, though it hadn't moved. The room smelled of old varnish and instant coffee and the staleness of a building that closed at six. She'd been here since nine. The night guard's keys jingled distantly, two floors down, making rounds. The world was ordinary. The world was outside. Inside the laptop, the god waited in its loop of footage, patient as its voice, ready to be watched again.

She took out the clean phone — the one the Client didn't know about — and opened an application that shouldn't have worked. A routing terminal. The access had been revoked the day she left the project, seven years and one dead sister ago. She checked it every year on the anniversary — the anniversary of Marin's disappearance, April, the spring the first run carried the Leviathan and a girl named Vance and did not bring the girl back. She checked it standing in whatever dark room she was in at midnight on the anniversary, expecting each time to find it cold, the connection severed, the access finally closed.

It was not cold.

The cursor blinked in the dark, patient as the Conductor's voice. The screen's blue glow lit her face — the only light in the room now, the laptop closed, the rain outside, the city asleep. Somewhere inside the Meridian, a legacy routing layer still listened for a seven-digit code that only two people in the world had ever known. One was Sloane. The other had been in the Lausanne room that spring, had built the spine, had boarded this train seven years ago, and had not come back.

The other was dead.

Sloane looked at the blinking cursor and did not, for a long moment, let herself think the name. Names had weight, spoken alone in a dark office at twenty-three minutes past eleven. She did not enter the code. She was not going to enter it tonight, or any night, unless every other option closed. But she let herself remember it was there — a pistol in a drawer, kept not to be used but to make the option hers.

Seven digits. Seven years. Seven runs.

She closed the terminal, powered the phone down, and slid it into the lining of her coat, next to the notebook.

Her hand stayed on the notebook. Seven years she'd carried it and not opened it — not once, not in the worst hours, because opening it was the one door she had sworn she would not walk through until she had earned the right. Tonight her hand wanted to. It wanted it the way a hand wants a wound it knows is there — to touch, to confirm, to make the pain real instead of theoretical. She let herself feel it for three seconds: the weight of seven years, the grief she had filed and controlled and never once let surface, pressing up under her ribs like a thing trying to drown her. Three seconds. Then she put the hand down, and the grief went back into the vault where she kept it — cold, exact, unanswered — and she was the Architect again, and the Architect did not open notebooks she could not bear to read.

The coat was heavy — the notebook, the phone, the weight of both pressing against her ribs like a second heartbeat.

She was not afraid of the train. She was afraid that she knew it too well, and that the knowing was going to get someone killed.

Her watch read twenty-three twenty-one. In two hours and nine minutes she'd step into a pod with six strangers and one empty seat, and Rafa would shoot them across the dark at the side of a moving train, and she'd put her hands on the machine she'd built and pretend she'd never seen it before.

Outside, the Meridian's running lights reflected on the wet Tagus — long, silver, already leaving, though it had not yet moved.

She went down to meet the machine she'd helped build — carrying a dead sister's handwriting and a key she had sworn seven years never to use, toward a night that had been waiting just as long to make her use it.
