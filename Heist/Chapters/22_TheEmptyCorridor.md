# Chapter 22

## 06:40 — The Meridian, aft (Cars K–N)

Naïma went aft to find the rival crew, and the rival crew found her first — by not being there at all.

She moved through the rear of the train by the paths no one watched. Past the private cabins of Cars K and L, where the buyers slept the expensive, medicated sleep of people who'd paid to be unconscious. Past the auction floor in Car M — dark now, bid-boards cold, the whole expensive theatre of the Meridian's true business sitting silent and guilty in red light. Into the staff decks of Car N, where the undercarriage met the coupling for the transfer pods.

She was looking for the second crew — the three Theo had catalogued and dismissed. The woman with the good shoulders, the man with the bad shoes, the tight-jawed third. They'd come aft after the Gallery, frustrated, and Jae's thermal map had tracked them toward the rear. Three warm signatures moving through the spine.

She was halfway through Car M when she heard it.

Through the bulkhead — muffled, but clear enough in the sealed quiet of the aft cars. Voices. Two, maybe three, speaking fast and low. The tense, clipped cadence of professionals negotiating a corridor they shouldn't be in. Then a fourth voice — different, harder, a voice that didn't negotiate. Then a sound that was not a word. Short. Wet. The sound a body makes when it meets a surface it didn't choose to meet, and the surface wins.

Then another.

Then silence.

Naïma froze. Pressed her back to the bulkhead. Counted to ten. The silence held — not the silence of an empty room but the silence of a room that had just been emptied, the held-breath quality of air that had witnessed something and was waiting for permission to forget.

She moved forward. Slowly. The infiltrator's pace, each step placed, each breath shallow, because whatever had made those sounds was either still here or had left tracks she could read.

She found the first sign in the M–N junction: a dropped ear-bead on the deck. Still warm. Ear-beads didn't fall out on their own. An ear-bead was knocked loose — by a hand that came too fast, in a struggle that was over before the bead hit the floor. The warmth said: minutes. Maybe less. She'd heard it. She'd heard the end of it, through a wall, and the end had taken seconds.

The second sign: a smear on a stanchion in the Car N service corridor, wiped quickly by someone who knew how to clean but hadn't been given time to do it well. The hasty professional wipe of a person who understood evidence and was working against a clock — the clock of a train that would reach the next window, the next station, the next pair of eyes. She didn't need the third sign, didn't want it. But the corridor ended at a sealed maintenance hatch, and the hatch was open, and behind it was a space that should have held three people and held none.

The corridor was empty. Three had walked in. None walked out. And Naïma had heard the moment they stopped being three — through a wall, two sounds, then nothing — and the hearing was worse than the finding, because the finding was evidence and the hearing was memory, and memory stayed.

Not the Conductor. The Conductor sealed things; it didn't make sounds like that, didn't knock ear-beads, didn't leave the hasty professional wipe of a human hand. By people. By Reinhart's people, or the buyer-assassins, or someone she hadn't seen — someone the manifest didn't name and the thermal map hadn't flagged, because they were good enough at removal to leave no signature but the absence of the removed. Someone aboard was willing to make three professionals disappear in seconds and leave nothing but a wiped stanchion, an open hatch, and a silence Naïma could still hear the shape of — because she'd heard the sounds that made it, and the sounds were the kind you don't unhear.

The willingness frightened her. Not because it was violent — she'd expected violence on this train. Because it was *efficient*. Willingness of that calibre wasn't hired for a single night. It was kept on retainer. And the retainer meant someone had been removing people from this train for a long time, quietly, and the quiet was the point, and the sounds she'd heard through the wall were the only time the quiet had broken — and it had broken for less than ten seconds, and then it had sealed itself shut again, and the sealing was the professional part, the part that said: we've done this before.

She crouched in the empty corridor and listened. The train breathed. Somewhere aft, beneath her feet, Rafa's pod clung to the hull. Somewhere else aft, the second pod — the rival pod — should have been. She touched her comms.

'Rafa.'

Thin with distance: 'Here.'

'The second pod. Where is it.'

A pause. A bad one. 'Gone. Detached eight minutes ago. But not empty, Naïma — there was someone in it. A pilot. The one who flew it in. He was under the hull when your three were taken, the way I'm under the hull, and he detached the moment he understood what was happening above him — dropped into the dark with his pod and his life and nothing else. Whoever sent the three sent them to die, and him to fly them out — only he flew himself out instead, because he was the one part of their crew Reinhart's people couldn't reach, the way I'm the one part of ours they can't reach. He's out there somewhere, running dark. I don't know whose side he's on. But he's alive, and he's a pilot, and he can do the thing only I'm supposed to be able to do.'

She closed her eyes. Eight minutes. The rival crew removed, then their escape removed behind them — the way you remove a ladder after climbing it, so no one else can climb down. A crew without an escape was a crew that had never been aboard, and a crew that had never been aboard was a crew no one would ever look for. Now there was one way off the train — Rafa's pod, one man, one window at the far end of the night.

'Three people,' she said. 'The woman with the good shoulders and her two. Gone. I heard the end of it, Rafa. Through a wall in Car M. Two sounds, then nothing. Four minutes, maybe less. Reinhart has people on this train who can do that — or someone does. The someone is good. The someone's done this before. The someone is the reason the curse has a shape.'

'Naïma. Get forward. Get back to the crew. Whatever this train is, the back of it isn't safe anymore, and whoever cleaned that corridor is between you and us. I can't come get you. The moment I release the clamps I lose the only door we have left.'

She was already moving. She didn't look back at the open hatch — the open hatch was a door that had been used, and doors used that way were doors that watched. Her father had taught her the first rule of a building that turned against you: never give it your back twice.

She slid forward through the spine, faster now, the careful pace of the infiltrator replaced by the honest speed of a woman who'd just learned — who'd just *heard* — that the rear of the Meridian was a place where people stopped existing, and the stopping took less than ten seconds, and the silence afterward was permanent.

She reached Car F and sealed the junction behind her, stood a moment with her back to the door and her hand on her racing pulse, and made herself a promise she intended to build into the next thing she did: whatever was breathing behind the wall of this car, she was going to get it off this train alive. Three people had already been taken off it who weren't. She'd heard them go. She wasn't going to let the count rise.

The corridor behind her stayed empty. It stayed empty in a way that felt watched.

She didn't look back.
