# Chapter 34

## 11:07 — The Meridian, Car E

Vesna Janković had spent twenty years building cases for courts that were slow and careful and bound by rules, and tonight she was going to break every one of them to deliver a case to a court that was faster — because the slow court wouldn't reach them in time and the fast one was the only door left.

She'd built her whole life on the rules. The rules were the columns — time, place, detail — and the columns were the method, and the method the only thing that had ever put anyone away. The putting-away was the reason she'd gotten up every morning for twenty years, and the reason was the line she'd told Sloane she lived by: *I don't arrest people who are trying to stop something worse.* The line was why she was here now, in a red-lit Gallery, about to break the rules she'd built her life on — because the rules were made for a world that moved at the speed of courts, and the world inside this train moved at the speed of a god, and the two speeds didn't meet, and the not-meeting was going to kill everyone she'd spent the night coming to respect if she didn't break the rules faster than the god could seal the doors.

She found Sloane in the Gallery, between movements. Didn't waste the minute.

'I have a channel. Europol. Secure. Outside the network, outside Reinhart, outside anything this train can reach or buy or seal. It's the channel I was going to use to arrest you at the terminus — carried all night, the way you carry your notebook, against the day it might be the thing that matters. It still works. The Conductor hasn't sealed it, because the Conductor doesn't know it exists — it runs on a satellite frequency the Meridian's Faraday cage wasn't built to block, because the cage was built to block the frequencies the network knew about, and the network didn't know about Europol's emergency band, because the network doesn't believe the law is fast enough to matter. Right now it's the single clean line off this machine.'

Sloane stopped mid-stride — and not only because of the offer. Satellite frequency. The device. The blue glow she'd seen in Vesna's hand behind the service curtain in the Gallery, the fast pocketing, the cop talking to someone the thief hadn't known about. Not Reinhart. Not a mole. Her task force. The suspicion she'd carried since that corner dissolved in a single breath — and what replaced it was something she hadn't felt for a cop in her criminal life: relief so sharp it was almost anger, because the knife she'd been carrying in the alliance's back had been imaginary all along, and the cop had been doing the one thing a good cop does: calling for backup.

'You're offering to take the ledger.'

'The ledger, and the witness, and everything your crew's about to die for — put it into a system that'll do with it what courts are supposed to: evidence, protection, names. The whole of it, filed, witnessed, entered into a record that doesn't disappear, doesn't ride a train, can't be sealed. The network doesn't survive the ledger and a living witness in the same hands as a European task force — and my channel runs to the EU's edge, Ms. Vance, which on this line means Bulgaria. We don't make Istanbul; Istanbul is Turkey, and Turkey is outside everything I carry. We make Bulgaria: their national police, their warrant, and Europol carrying the file the rest of the way. That's the only soil between here and the border where the law still bites. Reinhart doesn't survive it. The seven years of disappearances don't survive it. The siding doesn't survive it. That's the door, Ms. Vance. Not a siding. Not a terminus. A court — the one door Marcus Reinhart has never been able to buy, because you can't bribe a record once it's entered, can't silence a witness in protective custody, can't seal a courtroom the way you seal a car.'

Sloane looked at her — at the woman who'd boarded to arrest her and was now, in a red-lit Gallery, offering the one thing no thief ever expected from a cop: a way to make the stolen thing count, to turn theft into verdict, to convert the ledger from weapon into judgment. The looking had changed between them over the night — stopped being the measuring of an adversary and become the measuring of an ally.

'If we turn this train,' Sloane said, 'we can route it. Briefly. Before the network responds, before Reinhart's people brute-force the routing layer back. Ninety seconds, maybe less, of holding a heading the network doesn't expect. Where does your channel need us to be.'

Vesna named a place. A rail hub. Neutral jurisdiction — the one scrap of Europe where the network's money hadn't reached the right pockets, the one platform where a task force could meet a train without the meeting being reported first. A siding with a name, and a name that meant law. Not on the published route. Ninety seconds of hard routing from the Istanbul corridor — a detour the god could make if someone with the code told it to. A maintenance spur that existed, in the routing layer, for disasters — the kind every long-route rail system keeps, the kind that had never been used for a heist, because no heist had ever had a key to the routing layer.

'Ninety seconds,' Sloane said. 'That's the window. Ninety seconds of a heading the network doesn't expect, to reach a platform your people are standing on — and they're the most expensive ninety seconds any of us have ever spent.'

'Ninety seconds is enough. My people are already moving — I sent the word the moment you told me what the ledger was, the moment I understood the case I came to build was a smaller case inside a larger one, and the larger one was the one that mattered.' Steady, the steadiness of a woman who'd decided the case she'd come for was smaller than the case she'd found. 'I came to arrest an architect. I'm leaving having helped her steal a train. I'm aware of the irony. I don't care. There's a woman behind a wall in Car F who deserves a court more than she deserves a train, and you can't give her a court, and I can — and the giving's the reason I'm here, and the reason's enough.'

Sloane put out her hand. Not a thing she did — thieves didn't shake on deals; deals were for people who trusted paper. But she put out her hand, and Vesna took it, and the shake was brief and firm and meant. The alliance that turned an inspector into a door.

'The law becomes the door,' Sloane said.

'The law becomes the door. Turn the train. Aim it at my platform. I'll make sure someone's standing on it when you arrive — and the someone'll have a badge, and the badge'll be the last door Marcus Reinhart walks through as a free man.'

They parted. Sloane forward, toward Car A, Jae, the code. Vesna to her secure channel, to summon the people who'd stand on a platform in ninety seconds of borrowed heading and take the truth off a train that wasn't supposed to stop.

Vesna found the service alcove behind the Gallery bar and opened the device — the flat sat-phone she'd carried against her spine all night, the one frequency the Meridian's cage couldn't choke. She'd carried it to arrest Sloane Vance. She was about to use it to save her. The irony was the kind only cops and thieves understood: the same channel, the same law, pointed the other way.

She keyed the Europol liaison line. It rang twice — the longest two seconds of the night, because if the cage had learned to block it, everything was over. It connected. A voice she'd worked with for nine years, half-asleep, three time zones away in a Hague office.

'It's Janković. Priority. I'm on the Meridian. The case is live, and it's bigger than the architect.'

The voice on the line went very awake, very fast. She gave it to him the way she gave a court a case — in columns, time, place, detail, the method that had never failed her, only faster: a trafficking network, an annual run, a captive passenger, a ledger, an architect who'd turned, a train about to be diverted. She named Reinhart. She named the buyers' auction. And she mapped, in three sentences, the shape of a thing that had moved people across a continent for seven years behind the front of a cursed jewel — the buyers in the salon bidding millions on a lid, the silent second sale of a human being at every terminus, the disappearances the papers had called accidents. Seven years of it. One ledger. One witness, breathing, alive, behind a wall.

'I need a Bulgarian intercept at the Svilengrad rail yard. National police, a prosecutor on the night desk, an arrest warrant for Marcus Reinhart drawn from the ledger's contents — I'll transmit the index the moment I have it — and protective custody for the witness. The train diverts onto a maintenance spur near the border at approximately eleven-forty-eight local. I need your people on that platform when it does. Europol carries the file; Bulgaria makes the arrest. I have no power to arrest, and neither do you, but Bulgaria does — and a European Arrest Warrant executed on Bulgarian soil by Bulgarian officers is a door Reinhart cannot buy, cannot seal, and cannot run from.'

A pause. The voice on the line was doing the arithmetic every senior officer did when a field agent called in something this large at this hour: the risk, the paperwork, the political weight, the thing that would end careers if it went wrong and make them if it went right. Then: 'You're asking me to green-light an unsanctioned cross-border intercept on the strength of a stolen ledger and a living witness, coordinated in forty minutes, on a train that doesn't officially stop.'

'I'm asking you to green-light the arrest of a man who has sold human beings from a train for seven years. The ledger's the evidence. The witness is the proof. The train is the crime scene. Forty minutes is the window. After that it reaches Turkey, and the law ends, and the network keeps running. Decide.'

The voice decided. She heard it decide — the small shift in breathing that meant a career was being risked on the strength of a woman's word, and the word was being trusted, because the woman was Vesna Janković and the trust was the thing she'd spent twenty years earning. 'Svilengrad yard. Bulgarian national police, night-desk prosecutor, EAW on your index. Forty minutes. Janković — if this is wrong, it ends both of us.'

'I know.' She closed the line. Opened it once more, to a night-desk prosecutor in Sofia, and began the second call — the one that would put real officers on a real platform in a country she'd never worked, to meet a train that had never stopped, to take a truth off it that seven years of quiet had buried. The machinery of the law was slow. Tonight it was going to move at the speed of a god, because Vesna was going to make it move — and the making was the line she'd crossed, and the line was the reason she'd gotten up every morning for twenty years: to be the door, when the door was the only thing left.

The Gallery pulsed red. The law, which had been hunting Sloane all night, had become the thing that would save her — and the irony wasn't lost on either of them, and they didn't have time to enjoy it, because the clock was counting and the door at the end of the line was, for the first time all night, a real door, with a name, and the name was justice.
