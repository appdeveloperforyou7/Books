# Chapter 17

## 05:15 — The Meridian, Car B

The Meridian carried thirty-six passengers. Jae-won Kang knew this because he'd counted them, because the manifest counted them, because the brochure counted them — thirty-six was the number the train was built around, the way a theatre is built around its seats.

The Conductor's life-support system counted thirty-seven.

Jae sat in the service bay of Car B and stared at the readout he hadn't been looking for and had found anyway — by tripping over it in the dark. Life-support tracked respiration and thermal signature across the train, car by car, because the god's first law was that passengers arrived alive, and you couldn't keep them alive if you didn't know how many were breathing. It was the most honest system on the train — it measured bodies, not names, and bodies didn't lie.

Thirty-seven breaths. Thirty-six names.

He ran it again. Cold, in columns, without feeling. The array wasn't wrong; it didn't care about names or covers or lies, only about warm lungs and the carbon dioxide they left in the air. The array said thirty-seven. The manifest said thirty-six. Somewhere on the Meridian, a person was breathing who wasn't on the list.

He did what he always did with a number that wouldn't sit still: he broke it apart. The life-support log was a feed — respiration rate, exhaled CO2, thermal return — sampled per car every four seconds and held for the whole run. It tracked passengers, not crew — the twelve crew were badged, logged, known to the god on a separate channel; the god trusted its own staff. Passengers were the variable. Thirty-six passengers was the honest number. The array said thirty-seven. So he went looking for the thirty-seventh not by reading the total but by reading the *deltas* — the small second-to-second deviations a clean system never produced. A clean car's CO2 curve was a flat line with a pulse. A car with an extra lung in it had a curve that crept.

He isolated the cars one by one. A through E: clean, flat, matched to manifest. K, L, M: clean. N: empty, clean. Each curve held its shape. Then Car F — and the curve didn't hold. It crept. A slow, steady upward drift in carbon dioxide, two parts per million over the hour — not the signature of a jewel in a sealed atmosphere but of a metabolism at work: a lung taking in oxygen, putting out carbon, the quiet arithmetic of a living body spending air. The drift had begun at Lisbon. At Lisbon the curve had been flat; by five-fifteen it had a slope; and the slope was the slope of one adult human, breathing shallow, breathing still, in the one car on the train that held no people.

He cross-checked it three ways, because Jae didn't trust a number he'd only counted twice. Respiration rate: eleven breaths a minute — slow, the rate of someone asleep, or trained, or kept very still. Thermal return: a faint warm spot behind the cradle's housing, three-tenths of a degree above ambient, exactly the signature of a warm body in a cold box. CO2 slope: consistent, unbroken since Lisbon, ninety-four hours of it. Three measurements, one conclusion, no other reading that fit. The thirty-seventh passenger was real, had been real since the run began, and the god had been logging every breath of it and telling no one.

Car F. The Vault Car. The sealed car that held a jewel that didn't need to breathe.

Jae touched the implant. Hot now — genuinely hot, the kind that was no longer *fine* and edging toward *not fine*. He had a graph for it, in the back of a notebook he'd never show anyone: the warmth plotted against the days, a line that had been climbing for weeks, gentle and steady and irreversible, the shape of a component running out of the lifetimes it had been rated for. He filed the new data point under *later*, because *later* was the only shelf with room — and because the line was going to do what the line was going to do whether he watched it or not, and watching was a luxury he couldn't afford at eleven breaths a minute in a car that was supposed to hold a stone.

A person was breathing in the Vault Car.

He thought about the Cat, sliding through the spine toward Car F even now, the floor run he'd opened for her four seconds at a time. About the Hitter, who'd spent an hour staring at a blueprint with seven cubic metres missing. About the Leviathan — a jewel, and jewels didn't respire.

He opened a line to the Architect.

'There's a thirty-seventh passenger,' he said. 'Life-support counts thirty-seven. Manifest counts thirty-six. The extra's in or near Car F. It's breathing.'

Quiet on the line. Then Sloane, very controlled: 'A stowaway.'

'No. A stowaway hides in a hold. This signature's inside the secure car — behind the cradle. The Conductor knows it's there. Life-support's been logging it since Lisbon. The god's been keeping it alive and not telling anyone it exists.'

Another silence. Jae could hear, behind it, a woman revising the shape of her night in real time.

'The jewel,' Sloane said slowly, 'has been breathing since Lisbon.'

'Yes.'

'And the Conductor's been feeding it air.'

'Yes.'

'Then the Vault Car isn't a vault.' Jae heard the moment the word changed meaning in her mouth — *vault* becoming *cage*, *jewel* becoming *lid*. 'It's a cage. Jae — the thing we were sent to steal is alive. Or someone is. And the man who sent us didn't tell us, which means he already knew, which means—'

She didn't finish. She didn't have to. Jae could finish it himself: the Client had sent them to crack a car that held a person, and hadn't told them it held a person, and the only reason to send thieves into a cage without telling them what was caged was if the thieves weren't meant to leave with the truth.

Jae looked at the readout. Thirty-seven. The thirty-seventh was small — respiration shallow and slow, thermal signature faint. The signature, he thought with a cold certainty he didn't want, of someone kept very still and very quiet for a very long time.

Someone was aboard the Meridian who wasn't on the manifest, and the god was keeping them alive, and the man who'd hired the crew hadn't mentioned it.

'Keep it to yourself,' Sloane said. 'For now. Naïma's at the cradle. We find out what's breathing before we decide what to do about it.'

'Understood.' A beat. Then, because the heat was getting worse and the shelf was getting smaller and Jae was, despite everything, twenty-four and not ready: 'Architect. If the bridge runs longer than twelve tonight — the line in my head, it's—'

'I know,' Sloane said. And the *I know* was the thing he'd needed — not a number, not a plan, just the acknowledgment that she saw the line climbing and was going to factor it in. Factoring-in was, from Sloane, a kind of care he'd never been shown by an employer and didn't know how to repay. 'I'll factor it. You hold what you can hold. Nothing more.'

Jae closed the line. He looked again at the thirty-seventh signature — small, steady, breathing in a car that was supposed to hold a stone.

The god knew it was there. The god was feeding it air. And Jae-won Kang, who didn't believe in gifts from machines, sat in the humming dark of Car B and began, unwillingly, to suspect that the god wasn't the only one keeping secrets on the Meridian tonight.

He touched the hot skin behind his ear.

*Later,* he told himself. *Later.*

The thirty-seventh passenger breathed on.
