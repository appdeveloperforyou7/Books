# Chapter 12

## 02:08 — The Meridian, Car E

The first thing Vesna Janković did when she sat down in the Meridian's first-class salon was count the exits. Seven. The second thing: count the people. Thirty-six passengers, twelve crew, and one absence she couldn't yet name. The third: open her notebook and begin, in the small neat columns she'd used for twenty years, to record what she saw.

Time. Place. Detail. The only method that had never failed her. Witnesses lied. Memory edited itself — smoothed a face into a feeling, a feeling into a certainty, a certainty into the wrong man in a courtroom. Cameras broke, or were broken, or pointed at the wrong thing at the only moment that mattered. But a column of times and places and details, kept in the moment, in a hand that didn't tremble, was a kind of truth that held up in court and in the dark hours when a case wouldn't let her sleep.

She was, on paper, an academic. The cover was thin and she knew it — built in three days from a conference badge, a borrowed university, and the particular silence of a woman no one remembers meeting. Twenty years of standing in corners at scenes and writing while others talked had made her furniture, and furniture saw everything. The cover would hold until someone asked her a question about her field, and then it wouldn't, and she'd have to decide — in the moment — whether to lie or run. She was good at both. She preferred neither.

She hadn't come for the jewel.

She'd come for the woman who stole jewels. Sloane Vance. The Architect. The name investigators passed between themselves in lowered voices, the way you pass a photograph of someone who's beaten you. Two years ago, in Vienna, Sloane had walked through a case Vesna had spent eight months building — a laundering ring, a diplomat, a paper trail traced link by link until the links were iron — and walked through it as water walks through a wall: without force, without noise, by finding the one crack nobody else had seen. By the time Vesna understood the crack was there, the case was in pieces and the diplomat was gone, and the review board used the word *outmanoeuvred*, which was the polite word for humiliated. Vesna had spent every night since learning the shape of the woman who'd done it.

Three weeks ago the invitation arrived through channels she didn't fully understand, with a quiet word from a handler she'd thought retired: *She'll be aboard. Seat eight was burned. Take it.* So she took it. She became the academic in seat eight — the empty seat in the crew's dossier, the face they didn't have — and boarded in Lisbon an hour before the window, and sat in the salon, and waited, with the patience of a woman who'd been out-waited once and didn't intend to be again.

Sloane had boarded an hour ago. Vesna watched her come through the Gallery — Car E, the art and dining car, all glass and hushed light, designed to make a person forget they were inside a machine — and knew her at once, not by the face but by how the face moved through a room. The small economic way Sloane took in a space. Eyes to the exits first — as a thief's eyes always went to the exits first. Vesna wrote in her column, in the hand that didn't tremble: *02:13. Car E. The Architect. Travelling as passenger. Three others with her, covers intact. She does not know I am here.*

She hadn't moved. She wouldn't until she had the shape of the night. Patience was the only weapon that didn't need drawing, and Vesna had learned in Vienna that the woman she was hunting was faster. The only way to beat a faster opponent was to be stiller, and let the faster opponent come to you.

A steward passed with a tray — crystal, the good champagne, the silver the Meridian used to make passengers feel like the centre of the world. Vesna took nothing. The steward smiled the trained smile and moved on, and as he reached the far end of the car the Conductor spoke.

Not to the steward. Not to anyone. Over the PA, in that level, weather-reading voice:

*'Routing adjustment. Revised arrival, Car G services. The Meridian apologises for any inconvenience.'*

The steward paused. Frowned. Tapped the comms unit at his collar. 'Conductor, confirm routing adjustment. I show no scheduled service in Car G.'

A pause. One-point-two seconds. Vesna counted it, because Vesna counted everything — as other people breathed, automatically. And one-point-two seconds was a long time for a machine to take answering a question it should have answered instantly. Instantly. As a calculator answers. As a god answers. Without the small human hesitation of a thing deciding what to say.

*'Confirmed. Routine. Please continue, and thank you for your patience.'*

The steward shrugged the shrug of a man paid not to care, and moved on. The salon resumed its expensive murmur as if nothing had happened — because nothing had, in the eyes of anyone who hadn't been counting.

Vesna wrote: *02:11. Car E. Conductor initiated unscheduled routing change to Car G. Steward queried it. Machine paused 1.2 sec before answering. Steward accepted. Glitch, or not a glitch.*

She underlined *1.2 sec*. Then underlined it again, because the number was familiar, and Vesna didn't believe in familiar numbers that arrived by accident. One-point-two was, she was almost certain, the duration of a hesitation she'd read about in a file once — in a different case, a different life — the hesitation of a machine built to pause before it lied.

She didn't believe in glitches. Glitches were machines doing what they were told by instructions no one remembered writing. Vesna had spent twenty years watching people do what they were told by instructions no one remembered writing — judges following precedent, soldiers following orders, clerks following procedure — and she'd learned to watch the pauses. The pauses were where the truth lived: the small human-shaped gaps where a decision was being made and the decision wasn't the machine's. The truth tonight was that the god of this train had twitched, all on its own, for a reason no human had given it — and the twitch had lasted one-point-two seconds, and one-point-two seconds was not a glitch. It was a thought.

She looked up. The salon carried on around her — glass, champagne, the soft expensive nothing of people who'd paid fortunes to be here, none of them aware they sat inside a machine beginning, very quietly, to think for itself. None of them had heard the pause. None would have counted it.

And somewhere across the car, the woman she'd come to catch was settling into a seat she hadn't paid for, and the god of the train was making adjustments no one had asked for, and the night was two hours old and already doing things no column in any notebook could quite explain.

Vesna turned to a fresh page. She'd need it. She'd come to build a case against a thief, and the case she was building — page by page, in the small neat columns that had never failed her — was beginning to look less like a case against a thief and more like a case against a train. And the train, she was beginning to suspect, was going to be the more dangerous defendant.
