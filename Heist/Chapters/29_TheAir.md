# Chapter 29

## 10:00 — The Meridian, Car F (inner wall)

The air told the secret before the wall did.

Naïma had been working the hidden compartment for an hour, mapping the seams as she mapped every seam — by touch, by ear, by the small vibrations a sealed panel gives off when it's pretending not to be a door. The seam she couldn't find was the one that mattered, so she stopped looking for seams and started listening to the air — because air, unlike walls, couldn't be told to lie.

The Vault Car was sealed. Had been since Lisbon. Its atmosphere was a closed loop, scrubbed and recycled — the inert cold breath of a room built to hold a stone. A stone didn't change the air. Consumed nothing, added nothing, left the loop exactly as it found it.

The loop in Car F was not as it had been found.

Naïma pressed her palm flat against the inner wall, below the cradle, where the breathing came from — and felt the faint difference: the air warmer here, wetter, carrying beneath the sterile nothing of the scrubbers the small unmistakable signature of a living lung. Carbon dioxide. Rising and falling. The faintest tide of exhalation against her palm, as you feel a baby's breath against a hand held near a sleeping face. Someone on the other side of the panel was using the air, and the scrubbers were working harder to hide it — past their stone-calibration, the recycle running a half-cycle faster, the machinery of a closed loop compensating for a variable it wasn't built to hold. The working-harder was the tell. A stone didn't make a scrubber work.

She traced the airflow with her fingers, as her father had taught her to trace airflow in a building: not by the walls but by the systems that served them, because the systems were the honest map and the walls were the lie. The vent that fed this wall didn't feed a stone. It fed a space pressurised for a person, the airflow calibrated to adult-human respiration at rest — and the person was breathing at a rate that said they'd been breathing it a long time. Shallow. Slow. The respiration of someone who'd learned to use very little air, because for a long time very little air was all they'd been given.

'Emeka.'

He was there, at her shoulder — inspector, medic, conscience. He put his hand on the wall beside hers and felt it too: the warmth, the wetness, the faint tidal breath. She watched his face change — the slow heavy settling of a man whose blueprint had finally been answered, and the answer was worse than the question.

'It's a person,' he said. Not a question.

'Since Lisbon. The scrubbers are calibrated for two — one for the cradle, one for the wall. They built the air system for a passenger. They built the whole car for a passenger. The jewel was always the lie. The person was always the point. And the person's been breathing small a long time, Emeka. The respiration's trained. Someone's taught this person to use almost no air — which means someone's kept them in a small space before, and the keeping's been going on long enough that the body learned it as a way of life.'

Emeka closed his eyes. She watched him do what he did when the cost of a job became clear: carry it, visibly, as some men carry an injury, because Emeka didn't hide the weight of what he saw. The weight now was a person sealed behind a wall, fed scrubbed air, breathing shallow for a long time — because a network of people had decided this person was a thing to be moved.

'We open it,' he said.

'We open it. But carefully. Whoever's in there has been in the dark a long time, and the dark has taught them doors don't open from the outside — because the doors they've known only opened to move them from one cage to another. We open it slow, and kind, because the first thing a person in a wall sees is the person who let them out, and that person has to be worth seeing, worth trusting, worth the hope — because the hope is the thing they've stopped spending, and we're asking them to spend it again. The asking has to be gentle.'

She found the seam — not with her eyes (invisible, the work of people who built walls to not look like walls) but with the airflow, water finding the crack in a dam. *Follow the air, habibti. The air knows where the space is.* The vent narrowed to a slit, ran to a join. The join was the door. The door had a manual release on the inside — because even cage-builders build a way out, for themselves, in case they ever need to pretend the cage was a kindness.

She couldn't reach the inside. But Jae could.

'Jae. I need a bridge. Short. Two seconds. Cycle the manual release on the inner wall of Car F. The scrubbers let me find the join; I need the lock to let go.'

'Two seconds,' Jae said — the voice of a man spending the last clean bills in a wallet almost empty. 'Stand by.'

She pressed her fingers to the join. Felt the lock hold — the small stubborn resistance of a thing told to hold and holding. Then felt it let go: the small mechanical surrender of a thing told, for two seconds, that letting go was medicine. Instant. The god didn't suspect — two seconds wasn't long enough to suspect, and Jae had learned, by now, exactly how short a lie could be.

The panel moved. Slowly, as she'd promised — an inch of dark becoming an inch of light. The air changed, the closed scrubbed nothing of the Vault Car rushing against the warmer, wetter, lived-in air of the compartment. And the smell hit Naïma like a fist — stale skin, fear-sweat gone sour and then gone dry, the chemical tang of scrubbers working past their calibration, and beneath all of it something private and devastating: the smell of a person who had been kept. Not the smell of a room, or a space, or a cell. The smell of a body that had been breathing the same recycled air for weeks, that had been warm and afraid and alive in a space no bigger than a wardrobe, and the air had taken everything that body gave it and given nothing back — and the giving-back-nothing was the cruelty, because a body needs new air as a body needs light, and the compartment had given neither. Naïma's gorge rose. She swallowed it. The breathing on the other side changed — faster now, afraid — the respiration of someone who'd heard the lock move and didn't know if the moving was rescue or the next part of the cage.

Naïma put her face to the opening. Didn't reach in. Didn't grab. Made her voice as quiet and certain as she knew how — because the first words mattered, and she'd thought about them for an hour, as she thought about every lock: by understanding what the lock was afraid of, and becoming the thing it wasn't afraid of.

'My name's Naïma. I'm here to take you off this train. We're going to go slowly, and together, and you're not going to be behind a wall anymore. Do you hear me. You're not going to be behind a wall anymore.'

The dark breathed. The long slow trained breathing of a person who'd learned to use almost no air — then the faster afraid breathing of a person who'd heard a voice through a panel, the two fighting each other as hope fights habit. Naïma waited, because waiting was the only key that fit this lock.

Then, from the dark, a voice — thin, unused, the voice of someone not spoken to in a long time who'd almost forgotten how it worked:

'I stopped counting days. I started counting doors.'

A pause. The dark breathed. Then, quieter — the small amazed fragility of a person testing a thing they'd stopped believing in:

'You're the first one that opened from the outside.'
