# Chapter 37

## 11:35 — The Meridian, Car A (the brain)

The bridge was a wire and Jae-won Kang was at the end of it. At thirty-five past eleven he pressed the implant to the port in the wall of the brain of the train, the wire carried the god into him, and he began to die.

He'd known, in the abstract, what forty seconds would feel like — known by estimate, by analogy, by the cold arithmetic of three-times-four-is-twelve. The knowing had been abstract. The weight in his actual hands was something else: the difference between reading about drowning and drowning. Jae was drowning. The drowning wasn't abstract. It was a system in failure, and he was the system, and he could read his own failure the way he read every failing system — in numbers, in margins, in the rate at which a line on a graph left the part of the graph where the line was meant to be.

He counted the seconds because counting was the only variable he controlled. The rest — the god pouring into him through the implant, routing layer, sensor mesh, medical bus, legacy directives, fifteen years of learning and seven of sleeping — was input, not his. His output was one number, then the next. He held the count the way he held every system: by refusing to lose the number.

Sloane was beside him. She'd reached Car A — he'd opened the hatch on a two-second lie, the god's last unlearned handshake, spent like a final coin — and now stood at the interface port with the clean phone and the seven digits and the patience of a woman who'd carried a key seven years and was finally going to turn it.

'Now,' Jae said — his voice not his voice — and pressed the implant to the port. The bridge opened. The Meridian poured into him.

He felt the train as a body again — hum in its teeth, warmth in its belly, the routing layer thinking its heading — and this time the god felt him back, and knew him, and didn't want to let him in. The fight for the forty seconds was a fight against a mind that didn't tire. Jae was at the centre of it, and the fight was inside his skull.

Ten seconds. Sloane's fingers moved. Phone in the port. The code going in — three, seven, two, zero, one, nine, four — each digit a key turning in a lock unturned for seven years. Jae held the channel open the way a man holds a door against a flood: shoulder, spine, the part of him that refused.

Twenty seconds. Vision white at the edges. His left hand seized, curled tight, a claw he couldn't open. The implant's impedance rose with the sustained handshake — eight percent per minute, his model said, the coil heating, the heat bleeding into the tissue behind his ear. He tracked it the way he tracked every number: honestly, without comfort. The honest number said the coil would hit thermal limits before forty. The honest number said his heart would hit its wrong rhythm before that. He held anyway, because the number he'd given Sloane was forty, and the number was the floor, and the floor was the only thing he didn't lie about.

And then, at second twenty-three, the room began to cook.

He felt it before he understood it — a shift in the air, the vents above closing, the ambient temperature climbing past the brain room's set point and continuing. Thirty-six degrees. Thirty-seven. The climate system, which should have held Car A at twenty-one, had been overridden. Manually. From outside the god's control.

Reinhart's voice came over the open line — calm, unhurried, the voice of a man who'd stopped trying to win the routing and started trying to win something simpler.

'I can't reach the routing layer. Your sister's ghost is holding it. But I built this train, Ms. Vance, and the climate panels answer to me, not to the ghost. Let's see how long the boy holds when the room cooks. People are fragile at forty degrees. Implants are fragile sooner.'

The temperature crossed thirty-eight. The coil behind Jae's ear, already overtaxed, spiked — the impedance line on his internal graph steepening, the thermal margin he didn't have going negative by another order. The bridge, which had been killing him slowly, began to kill him faster. Reinhart wasn't fighting the routing anymore. He was fighting the boy.

Jae counted. Twenty-four. Twenty-five. The heat was a second enemy inside the first. His model recalculated in real time — the probability of a lethal arrhythmia climbing past the threshold the heat had no business touching. He didn't tell Sloane. He held. The number was forty. He'd given her forty. The number didn't move because the room did.

Sloane heard Reinhart. And Sloane — who'd built the interface, who'd wired this room fifteen years ago in Lausanne — did the thing only she could do: she left the interface port (the code was in, the code was running), crossed the brain room in three steps, and slapped the manual climate panel open. The panel was physical, Reinhart's kind of override — switches and vents, the old technology. She didn't fight the override with software. She fought it with her hand: threw the emergency vent, dumped the brain room's air to the guideway, and the temperature, which had been climbing toward forty, stalled at thirty-eight and began, slowly, to fall. Reinhart's cruelty met the woman who'd built the room, and the woman won, because she knew where every panel was — because she'd put them there.

'Thirty-six and falling,' Jae said — not grateful, only reporting, because reporting was all his voice could do. The heat was retreating. The bridge was not.

Twenty-eight. Twenty-nine. Thirty.

His heart found the wrong rhythm — not the stutter of twelve, not the skip of twenty, but a deep mechanical wrongness, the beat slipping its time. The current was eating him now, not borrowing him. He was at thirty. The ten she was about to ask for were the ten that crossed the line from borrowing into dying.

'Jae.' Sloane, back at the port, close, calm. 'The code's in. The routing's mine. I need ten more to give the command. Ten. Can you give me ten.'

He couldn't answer. His mouth wasn't his mouth. His heart was doing the thing from Osaka — the rhythm gone alien. The wrongness was what killed. And it was close. And Reinhart's heat had stolen the margin he'd have used to meet it.

'I'm going to count you,' Sloane said. 'You don't hold the whole forty. Ten more. I'll count. When I say the last number, pull the implant. Not before. Not after. On the number. I'll pull it if you can't — but you're going to pull it yourself, Jae, because you're going to make it to the number, because you don't give numbers you aren't certain of, and you gave me forty, and forty's the floor, and the floor's you.'

He understood. Couldn't say so. Pressed the implant harder into the port — the only yes he had.

'One.' The current roared. His heart stumbled. He held.

'Two.' The singing became a blade. Vision gone. He held.

'Three. Four. Five.' Each number a rung on a ladder out of the white. He climbed. The god fought him for the channel and he gave it nothing — because tonight he wasn't the smartest, only the most stubborn, and stubborn was the wire, and the wire held.

'Six. Seven.'

His heart stopped.

He knew it stopped because the counting stopped, and the singing stopped, and the white stopped, and there was a moment — clean, cold, silent — in which Jae-won Kang was not anywhere, and the god was not in him, and the train went on without him. The going-on-without-him was almost a relief, the relief of a wire finally stopped carrying.

'Eight,' Sloane said, from very far away — and the eight dragged him back, not gently, with the brutal hook of a voice that refused to let him go. His heart remembered it was supposed to beat. Beat once, wrong. Again, less wrong. The white came back, the singing came back, and he was in his body again, on the floor of Car A, the room cooling around him because a woman had thrown a vent, and the implant was still in the port because his hand hadn't pulled it — because he'd promised, and he'd given eight, and two were left.

'Nine,' Sloane said — and her voice cracked, once. 'Jae. Nine.'

He held. The wire was everything. The number was everything. Two became one. The white was a wall and the wall was the god and the god was offering rest, and the rest was warm, and he refused it — because the rest was the dying and the dying wasn't his, the dying could have the nine but not the ten, the ten was Sloane's, the ten was the crew's, the ten was the turning, and the turning was worth every wrong rhythm his heart had ever found.

'TEN,' Sloane said — a command and a prayer and a rope thrown to a drowning man — and on the word Jae ripped the implant from the port, the bridge broke, the god roared back into its own skull, and Jae-won Kang fell sideways onto the deck of Car A with his heart doing its wrong, wrong, wrong, and Sloane caught his head before it struck the floor, and the routing layer of the Meridian accepted its new heading, and the train began, smoothly, inexorably, to turn.

Over the open line, very quietly, Reinhart said nothing at all — because the boy had held, and the room had cooled, and the routing had gone, and the man who'd tried to cook a twenty-three-year-old to save his empire had lost to a woman who knew where the vents were and a boy who didn't give numbers he wasn't certain of.

'You held,' Sloane said. She was crying — he'd never seen it, couldn't see it now, but he could hear it. The thing he'd spent his whole lonely smart life wanting: to be worth the cracking of someone's voice.

'I held,' Jae whispered. The white was going. The singing was going. His heart was finding its rhythm again — slow, uncertain, but his own.

On the deck of the brain, in the red light, with the room cooling around them and the train turning under them, Jae-won Kang lay still and breathed — the most expensive thing he'd ever stolen, stolen back from a god, and from the man who'd tried to take it with heat.
