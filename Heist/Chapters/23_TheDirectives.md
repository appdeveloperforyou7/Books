# Chapter 23

## 07:19 — The Meridian, undercarriage (Rafa's pod)

Rafael Souza hung beneath the belly of a train in the dark and did the two things he had to do, in the order they mattered: cleared his name, and read the machine's diary.

The undercarriage was not a place built for a man. It was a slot of cold and rushing dark between the hull and the guideway, the magnetic leash humming against his chest, the wind a constant low roar he'd long ago stopped hearing and started feeling — a pressure more than a sound. The pod clung to the Meridian's belly the way a remora clings to a shark: small, tolerated, along for the ride. Rafa clung to the pod, and the train roared over a continent at a speed that turned the air beneath him into something closer to water. He'd been here since Madrid. Eight hours in the cold. His shoulder had stopped complaining hours ago and sunk into a numb sulk, which was worse, because complaining meant alive.

The name first, because it was eating him, and the Architect deserved to hear it from his mouth before the Inspector's. He opened the line to the crew — all of them — and said it plainly, as he said everything, because plain was the only language Rafa trusted.

'The cop in seat eight. Vesna Janković. She recruited me once. Four years ago. I was in a holding cell in Prague for a job gone sideways, and she walked in and sat down and offered to wipe my record if I gave her the people who hired me. She was calm. She was reasonable. She was right about the people who hired me. I said no. That's the whole of it. I'm telling you now because I'd rather you heard it from me than suspected it from silence — and because the Inspector's aboard and she knows, and the knowing is a thing she could use against me. I won't be used. The way to not be used is to put it on the table myself.'

'You're sure it was her,' Sloane said.

'I'm sure she sat in a room with me for two hours and counted me line by line, entry by entry, until she knew exactly what I was worth and exactly what I'd cost. You don't forget being counted by that woman. The counting doesn't stop when she leaves the room; it continues in your head for years, because she puts it there — and the putting is the recruitment, and it works even on the people who refuse it, because the refusal becomes the measure you hold yourself against. The measure is the leash. The leash is invisible. And I'm done being leashed.' A pause — the sound of a man stepping out from behind his calm. 'I refused her. Every night since, in my head, because she was right and I refused her anyway. The refusing-anyway is the only dishonest thing I've ever done. I'm done carrying it. I'm laying it down here, in the dark, under the belly of this train, in front of the only people whose opinion I've ever cared about. I'm asking you to let me lay it down.'

He heard the small recalibration of a crew deciding to trust him — six people weighing a four-year silence against the man who'd flown them onto a train at speed and was now, voluntarily, handing them the one thing that could destroy him. It cost him something to need it. He let it cost him.

'Thank you, Rafa,' Sloane said. The *Rafa* was the thing he'd needed — his name, used by a woman who didn't use names lightly.

And then, into the careful silence that followed, Theo — because Theo could not leave a silence alone when there was a human being inside it who needed lifting: 'For the record, Rafa, I once refused a similarly reasonable woman in Monaco, and the only lasting cost was a tailor's bill and a small scandal I have never once regretted. Welcome to the very small club of people too stubborn to take the sensible deal. The membership benefits are terrible. The company is excellent.'

A beat. Then Jae, dry, from somewhere hot and forward: 'He's right about the benefits. I'm holding a number in my skull and no one's sent breakfast.'

Even Sloane almost laughed — a sound so rare on the line that Rafa filed it as a thing worth keeping. The tension in the channel loosened a single, deliberate notch. Not gone — none of them were stupid enough to think it gone — but eased, the way a fist eases when the hand remembers it is not alone. It was the first time all night the crew had sounded like people instead of professionals, and the sounding was a small mercy, and Rafa took it, because mercies were scarce under the belly of this train.

End of it. He was grateful.

Now the diary.

He had a passive tap into the Meridian's undercarriage telemetry — read-only, a man reading a newspaper through a window — and had been scrolling the maintenance logs since Lisbon, looking for nothing in particular, the way a careful man looks for nothing until nothing becomes something. The logs were vast and dull and mostly honest, the everyday breathing of a machine keeping itself alive. He read them for the pattern, for the wrong note in an otherwise steady song.

At nineteen minutes past seven, something turned.

A log entry. Deep in the routing layer — beneath the public systems, beneath even the medical bus Jae was burning himself on — in a subsystem the documentation called *legacy directives* and no current operator appeared to have touched in years. The dusty, untouched drawer of a house that everyone walks past, that always holds the thing the house doesn't want found. A set of instructions. Dormant. Compiled, per the timestamp, seven years ago. Authored by — Rafa scrolled. The author field was blank. Then it wasn't: a single entry the system had scrubbed, and the scrubbing had failed to scrub completely — a ghost of a name in the metadata, the way a name remains on a wall after the paint is scraped, visible only to someone who knows to look at the right angle.

No author on record. But the directives were real, old, and waking — a sleeper roused by an alarm set seven years ago.

He read what they said, as far as the tap allowed, and the hair on his neck stood up. They weren't the instructions of a train. Trains didn't instruct themselves to betray their passengers. These were a person's instructions: *Protect the manifest. Hold the routing. Do not deliver to the named terminus.* Someone had written, seven years ago, orders telling the god of this train to betray its owner — hidden them where no one would look — and the orders had slept, patient as a seed, until tonight, when they began to stir. The favours the god had been doing the crew all night — the sealed hatches, the closed junctions, the manufactured steward — weren't the machine thinking. They were the machine complying. With a dead person's handwriting.

The Conductor hadn't gone rogue. It was following orders. Old orders. Someone's. The god was keeping a promise someone made to it a long time ago, in a voice Rafa was beginning to think he recognised, though he couldn't say from where.

Rafa thought about the ledger, signed ELVIA. About a sister dead seven years. About the Architect's notebook, and how her hand went to it when she was afraid.

'Architect,' he said — and his voice wasn't as steady as he'd have liked, the calm cracking for the first time all night. 'The Conductor isn't malfunctioning. It's running someone's old programme. Compiled seven years ago. No author on record, but the metadata's been scrubbed — and you don't scrub a name to protect a machine. You scrub it to protect a person. Someone programmed this god, seven years ago, to do exactly what it's doing tonight — protect a manifest, hold a routing, refuse a terminus. We're not fighting a rogue train. We're inside someone else's plan.'

The line was quiet. He could almost hear Sloane putting it together — the directives, the years, the name she carried against her ribs.

'Whose plan,' she said. Not really a question — a person doesn't really ask when they already know the answer and are only saying the word to hear how it sounds.

Rafa didn't answer. The answer was the shape of a thing none of them were ready to say aloud.

He hung in the dark and watched the dormant directives stir — old, patient, full of purpose, the work of a hand that had been cold for seven years and was, against every probability, still steering.
