# Chapter 30

## 10:30 — The Meridian, Car F (Belgrade Window)

They brought her out the way you bring a person out of a long winter: slowly, light behind you so it didn't strike her eyes, a coat over her shoulders because the cold of the Vault Car was the only temperature her body still trusted, hands that didn't grab and a voice that didn't hurry.

She was smaller than she should have been. The compartment had taken her dimensions — weeks in a space the size of a wardrobe had compressed her, not just physically (the legs folded, the shoulders curled, the spine curved into the shape of the wall it had leaned against) but in some deeper way that showed in the way she held her arms against her chest, close, protective, as if still fitting inside a space that was no longer there. Her skin was the colour of paper — the translucent, blue-veined pallor of a body that had not seen light in months, through which Emeka could trace the veins of her forearms like rivers on a map. Her eyes, when they adjusted to even the dim blue of the cradle's readout, were the eyes of an animal pulled from a hole: huge, dark, the pupils blown wide from weeks of dark and now contracting painfully against the glow, flinching, watering. She made a sound when they moved her legs — not a word, not a cry, just a small involuntary vocalization, the body's protest at being unfolded after months of folding, the joints remembering what range of motion felt like and rejecting it.

Her name was Lina Provst. She told them between breaths, in a voice that kept forgetting how loud to be — because no one had spoken to her in weeks, and the volume of speech was a thing you lost when you weren't practised in it. Twenty-eight. Danish. A journalist in Copenhagen — a good one, she said, with the small fierce pride of a person asserting a self she wasn't sure had survived the cage. Then a person who asked the wrong question about the wrong train. Then a name on a manifest that didn't exist, behind a wall that didn't exist, breathing air a jewel didn't need. She said it in the flat careful tone of a person reciting a report — because the report was the thing she'd held onto, the journalist's habit, the last professional muscle the cage couldn't take, and the holding was the reason she was still sane, and the sanity was the reason she was still alive.

Emeka knelt beside her and did the work he was built for — not hitting, not inspecting, but the careful human arithmetic of a medic reading a body that had been badly used. Dehydration. Muscle atrophy, the legs especially — the legs that hadn't been allowed to walk. The particular flatness of the eyes that came from long dark and longer silence. Old marks on the wrists where restraints had been, faded — which meant the cage had shifted, at some point, from chains to habit, and habit was the deeper chain. Nothing that would kill her in the next hour. Everything that had been killing her for the months before. He wrapped the coat closer. Gave her water in sips, not gulps — because a body starved of water would reject a flood the way a dry field rejects a storm, and the rejection could kill her faster than the thirst.

'You're off the wall,' he said, in the low steady voice he used for people who were frightened — the same voice he used for everything, because Emeka didn't believe a frightened person deserved a different voice than a calm one. The consistency was the kindness; the same voice whether you were calm or not meant the same person, and the same person meant safety. 'You're among people taking you off this train. No one's going to put you back behind a wall. I need you to believe that, even if you can't believe it yet. Tell me if anything hurts — I'm a medic as well as a wall, and the medic is the part of me that's for you tonight, and the medic isn't leaving your side until you're off this train and on solid ground.'

She looked at him. Grey eyes, very clear, very tired — searching his face the way a person searches when they've learned that faces lie. Then they stopped searching, because his face was the kind that didn't, and the not-lying was a thing the eyes could read even when the brain had stopped believing. She let herself, very slightly, begin to believe.

'They move people,' she said. 'On this train. Once a year. The auction's for the jewel in front of the buyers — the champagne, the bids, the catalogues, the curse, all of it, the show the buyers think they're here for. Behind the buyers, in this car, there's a person. And the person's sold to a buyer the buyers never meet — a buyer in a different city, paying in a different currency — and the person's delivered at the terminus and the train moves on and the buyers go home with their jewels and the person's gone and no one asks, because no one knows the person was ever aboard. The person was never on the manifest.' She said it the way she'd practised it in the dark, for weeks — the report she'd written in her head because they'd taken her notebook, her phone, her name, but couldn't take the part of her that filed the story. 'I was the person. I was the proof. I found the ledger — I was a journalist, I was good, I followed the money, I followed it to this train — and they found me, and put me here, because a witness behind a wall is a witness who doesn't testify. And a witness who doesn't testify is a witness who doesn't exist. And a witness who doesn't exist is the network's favourite kind.'

Emeka didn't let his face change. The medic's discipline held. Inside, the weight of every blueprint that had ever asked him a question settled into the shape of an answer — and the answer was this woman, worse than the question, and also the reason he was here, and had always been the reason, since the first moment the seven cubic metres refused to add up.

'We have the ledger,' he said. 'We have you. We're going to get both of you off this train and into the hands of people who can use them. You're the proof, Lina. You're the part the network couldn't afford to let walk. And you're walking. Out of this car, off this train, into a place the network can't reach — and the walking's what we're doing tonight, for you, because you're the reason the heist stopped being a heist and became what it was always supposed to be.'

The Belgrade window slid past beneath them — felt more than known, the ninety-second shudder of the train passing close to the ground. Rafa's voice on the comms, asking if they wanted the window. Sloane answered no — not yet, not with Reinhart's coverage unknown and the turn-the-train plan unmade. The window passed, and with it one exit. Exchanges down to Sofia, then the terminus, then nothing.

Naïma helped Lina stand. The journalist swayed and steadied, the legs that hadn't walked in weeks remembering, slowly, how to hold. Naïma held her elbow the way you hold a thing you intend to carry a long way. Lina looked at the cradle where the Leviathan had been — the empty nest of white silk — then at the wall where she'd been, the open panel, the dark behind it — then at the small crew of strangers who'd cracked a vault, found a person, and decided, against all reason and all money, that the person was the prize.

'Why,' Lina said. 'You were hired for the stone. Why would you—'

'Because there's a man on this train who was willing to sell you to whoever bought you,' Emeka said, 'and there are people on this train who weren't willing to let him. That's the whole of it. The stone was a story. You're the truth. And the truth is what we're stealing tonight.'

Lina looked at him, then at Naïma, then at the dark porthole where the continent streamed past at a speed that didn't forgive — and did something none of them expected. She laughed. Once. Small, cracked, amazed — the laugh of a person who'd stopped believing anyone was coming and had just been proven wrong, spending, for the first time in months, the hope she'd stopped spending.

'You're all completely insane,' she said.

'Yes,' Naïma said — and almost smiled, the first she'd allowed herself all night. 'Stay close to me. We're going to climb.'

The heist, which had begun as a theft of a jewel and become a theft of a weapon, became, in the Vault Car at half past ten, what it had probably always been: a rescue. And the crew, who'd assembled in Lisbon around a plan that wasn't the plan, found themselves, finally, around the plan that was — a woman, a ledger, a god that kept a promise, and a train that was going to have to be turned.
