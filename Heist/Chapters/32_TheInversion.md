# Chapter 32

## THE SWAP

## 11:02 — The Meridian, Car F

There is a calm that comes after the worst thing. Sloane found it at two minutes past eleven, in a red-lit Vault Car, with no exit and a clock and a woman who had just learned to count doors.

She'd spent her career planning for the chapter where the plan died — the exit gone, the money forfeit, nothing left but the thing you were actually made of. Most thieves ran. Sloane stood still, and counted what she had.

The ledger, in her arms — heavier than a stone, lighter than a sister. Lina, against the wall, grey-eyed, alive in a manner the Leviathan had never been. A crew that had chosen truth over the cheque. A god holding the routing against Reinhart on a dead woman's seven-year-old orders. A key in her coat. A boy in Car B who could reach the interface if she could find him seconds he didn't have. An inspector in first-class with a conditional alliance and a device Sloane didn't fully trust.

No exit. No time. No door at the end of the line.

She didn't need a door at the end of the line. She needed to move the line.

The Vault Car was cold. The cradle's readout cast a blue glow that mixed with the emergency red — purple, the colour of a bruise, the colour of a thing that had been hit. The air smelled of ozone and the metallic nothing of sealed atmosphere, and beneath it the faint recycled warmth of the compartment where Lina had been kept — the air still carrying the ghost of her breath, the body heat of someone who'd lived behind a wall for months. The hum of the maglev had dropped a quarter-tone since the terminus protocol sealed them in; the whole train sounded lower, older, like an animal settling its weight before it lay down. The Leviathan sat in the cradle, lit, beautiful, the most expensive lie in the world, and Sloane looked at it now the way you look at a tool you've outgrown: with respect, and without need.

'We're not running,' she said. The calm was in her voice, and it spread — because calm in a red light is courage, and courage is the only currency that spends in a box with no way out. 'Reinhart plans for thieves. Thieves run. We're about to stop being thieves and become something he has no model for.'

'What,' Emeka said.

'Engineers. We're going to steal this train.'

The word landed in the red light and nobody moved. Emeka, who had spent a career reading the moment a plan stopped being a plan, went very still — the stillness of a man recognising, behind the madness, a structure he could audit. Naïma, at the open panel, lifted her head. Lina, against the wall, watched with the quiet intensity of a woman who had months ago stopped expecting anything and was now, against her own policy, expecting something.

She laid it out in movements, in order, in the old names — the five phases she'd given them in Lisbon. But inverted now, because the plan you were shown was never the plan.

'The Wire first. Not last. We need a destination Reinhart can't reach before we move the routing — because a turned train with nowhere to go is a weapon without a target, and it kills the people holding it. The Wire is the inspector. The Wire is the court.'

She paused. Let them hear it. Outside the car, distantly, the thud of Reinhart's people hammering the routing layer — brute force against seven-year-old code, patient and losing.

'The Swap. The forgery's in the cradle; that swap is done. The real swap is control. The moment my code takes the routing layer, I become the Conductor's new author, and Marin's directives hand the train to me as they held it against Reinhart.'

'The Crack. The interface. Car A. The seven-digit code. Jae's seconds.'

'The Board. Rafa's pod, unclamped, at the rear — widened to carry a crew, a passenger, and a ledger the world is about to read.'

'The Mark. Last. Because the real mark was never the Leviathan, or the ledger, or even Lina. The real mark is Marcus Reinhart. The heist was always, from the bracelet onward, about putting him where the truth can reach him.'

Silence. The cradle hummed its blue count. Lina sat against the wall with her arms around her knees, watching, the journalist cataloguing everything because cataloguing was the only muscle the cage hadn't taken. Naïma stood by the panel she'd opened — the compartment, still dark, still warm with the ghost of Lina's imprisonment. Jae was on the deck, hand curled, the tremor not quite gone, his skin grey in the mixed light. Theo leaned against the far wall, arms crossed, the red catching his cufflinks.

'Five movements,' Sloane said. 'Inverted. Wire first, because we need a destination before we move. Then Swap, Crack, Board, Mark. We leave Reinhart aboard a train that's no longer going where he told it, carrying a ledger about to be read by everyone he spent his life hiding from.'

'How do you win a heist with no exit,' Naïma said. Not a question — the shape of the thing they were about to attempt, said aloud so they could all carry it.

'You stop trying to exit. You change where the exit is.'

'Genuinely, sincerely,' Theo said, stepping forward, uncrossing his arms — and for once his voice had no performance in it, only the plain exhausted admiration of a man who'd spent his life inside cons and had just been shown one he couldn't have imagined. 'You want to steal a train. Turn it. Deliver it to a court. For no money. On a night I was promised a jewel.' He shook his head. 'I want it noted for the record that this is the single worst plan I have ever been inside, and I was once inside a plan that involved a fake zeppelin. I am, against every instinct I own, in.'

'Noted,' Sloane said.

'Also noted: if we survive this, I'm writing the memoirs. Chapters through "Fields."'

'You're not writing memoirs.'

'I am absolutely writing memoirs. The title is "The Time We Stole a Train." It will be a bestseller. You'll hate it.'

Jae, from the deck, surprised them all — a sound that took a second to recognise as a laugh, dry and short, bought at a cost his lungs could barely afford. 'Title's taken,' he said. 'I read it. Terrible ending. Everyone dies holding a number.'

'Nobody's dying,' Sloane said, and meant it, and the meaning was a promise she had no right to make and made anyway — because the crew needed it, and because she had decided, somewhere in the red light, that the boy who'd held forty and the woman who'd counted doors and the wall and the cat and the liar were all going to walk off this train, and the deciding was the floor she built the rest on.

Emeka spoke, slow and careful, in the voice of a man auditing a structure for the first time instead of a plan. 'The inspector. You're trusting the device I watched her pocket. You're trusting the cop who came aboard to arrest you.'

'I'm trusting the cop who came aboard to arrest me and stayed to help steal a train,' Sloane said. 'The alliance is conditional. So is every exit we have left. Conditional's the only kind on offer tonight — so we take it, and we watch it, and we keep a fallback in case the cop's court turns out to be a cage with different bars.'

'Fallback being?'

'We improvise. Like thieves. One last time.'

Rafa's voice came through the comms from beneath the hull — thin, tired, the voice of a man clamped to the belly of a machine that wouldn't let him go. 'Architect. Pod's still bolted. The moment your routing takes and the god lets me loose, I need ninety seconds' warning. I can't widen a window I haven't got.'

'You'll have it.'

'I'll widen it anyway,' Rafa said, and the calm in his voice was the calm of a man who had already decided what he was willing to spend, and Sloane heard the spending inside the calm and did not like it and filed it among the costs she would settle later, if there was a later.

'Go to your movements,' she said. 'We have less than an hour. We move at speed.'

She'd always moved at speed. Tonight she moved at the speed of a woman who had finally stopped running from the thing she'd built, and was going to use it — for the first time — for the purpose it was meant for.

The crew scattered into the body of the machine to do the five things that would end the night, in the order no heist had ever done them. Naïma went first, sliding back into the floor run toward Car A, the Cat disappearing into the spine of the animal she'd spent all night learning. Emeka followed, the wall walking, bracing the route behind her. Jae pulled himself up with a hand that shook and a spine that didn't, and went to find the interface that would cost him whatever it cost. Theo straightened his cuffs in the red light — the last ritual of a man about to walk toward the one true thing he owned — and went forward, toward Car C, toward the mark.

Sloane stood a moment longer in the cold purple light, beside the woman who counted doors and the ledger that named the dead. Lina looked up at her.

'You're really going to turn it,' Lina said. Quiet. Not a question.

'We're really going to turn it.'

'He'll kill you for it. If it doesn't work.'

'He'll try,' Sloane said. 'But he planned for people who run. I'm done running.'

Lina nodded — the small, definite nod of a woman who had spent months behind a wall deciding exactly what she would do with freedom if she ever got it. 'Then take me with you when you go. I have things to finish too.'

The plan you were shown was never the plan. The real plan was about to turn — and at its centre, no longer a thief but an architect: the woman who'd built the cage and was finally going to break it open from the inside, with a crew she hadn't foreseen and a key she'd carried seven years and a dead sister's handwriting against her ribs.
