# Chapter 28

## 09:20 — The Meridian, Car F

There is a kind of confession that is also a key. Sloane made it at twenty past nine, in the Vault Car, because the truth had outgrown the vault she kept it in.

'I built the interface.' She said it flat, the way she said everything that cost her. 'Fifteen years ago. Lausanne. The soft layer between the Conductor and its passengers — the voice, the glyphs, the tells. The part of the god that talks. I built it alongside my sister. Marin was nineteen — she'd built the spine the year before, a girl who understood machines as living bodies, who'd walked out of university at sixteen because the mathematics bored her and the machines didn't. We were young. We built a god between us, and neither of us yet knew what a god was for. She used the same terminals, the same season, to write the directives waking in these walls.'

She let that land. The crew was quiet around her — Emeka by the door, Naïma at the wall, Jae on the deck with his hand curled in his lap, Theo forward where the red light caught his cufflinks.

'I helped build the cage. Marin walked into it. I spent seven years pretending those were two separate griefs instead of one.'

Emeka, by the door, spoke — not to judge but to match her. 'I audited the security layer. Five years ago. The seals, the biometrics, the car-to-car protocol. Signed off on most of it. Walked away when I saw what it was for, and told myself the walking was enough.' He looked at her. 'It wasn't. We built the same cage, Architect. Different parts. Same cage.'

She looked at him — and something passed between them that was not forgiveness and not absolution but recognition: two people who'd helped make the machine, and who knew it, and who were going to use that knowledge tonight to break it open.

She touched the inside of her coat. 'The interface has a back door. I put it there. I was twenty-three, I didn't trust the people funding the project, and I wanted a way to override the routing if the machine ever did something I hadn't told it to. A seven-digit code. It should have been revoked when I left. It wasn't. I check it every year. It's still live.'

She touched the coat again — not the code this time; the other thing she carried, the notebook. 'I made the code out of her bracelet. Marin's. Seven charms. The fish third from the clasp — her favourite, the one I gave her for her fifteenth birthday because she was afraid of the dark and I'd told her fish could see in it. At twenty-three I wanted a key I could never forget and never write down, so I hid it inside the one person I couldn't lose. The override that turns this train is my sister's wrist. I've been carrying her seven years. I didn't know I was carrying the way back in.'

Emeka, by the door, spoke — not to doubt her but to indict himself. 'I audited the security layer. Five years of it. Seals, biometrics, car-to-car protocol. I never once touched the speech layer. Nobody did. You don't audit what a god says in its own voice.' He looked at the deck. 'The override lived in the one part of the machine we were trained to leave alone — and that's how it lasted fifteen years. I signed off on every lock around the room and never once checked the room's mouth.'

Jae looked up. 'A routing override. In the interface layer.'

'Legacy. Listens for the code, hands over the steering.'

'And the medical bridge reaches the interface layer.' His voice had changed — the thin bright tone it took when maths became meaning. 'Architect — the bridge doesn't only let me lie to the god. Held long enough, with the code entered at the right moment, it lets *you* talk to the god in the layer you built. It lets you reach the routing. It lets you tell the train where to go.'

The Vault Car was silent except for the cradle's count and the breathing in the wall.

Sloane watched the shape assemble. She had spent the whole night looking for a door at the end of the line. The door had been the line itself.

'We can't get off this train,' she said, slowly. 'The pod is one window. Reinhart owns the terminus. The exits are closing. But if I can reach the routing layer — if Jae holds the bridge long enough for me to enter the code and speak to the machine in the language I built — then we don't have to leave the train to win.'

'We turn the train,' Naïma said.

'We turn the train. We don't steal the ledger off the Meridian. We aim the Meridian. We route it — and the ledger, and the passenger — wherever we choose, and we leave Reinhart aboard a machine that is no longer going where he told it.'

'Lovely,' Theo said, from the red light. 'Genuinely lovely. A small technical question, by which I mean the only question that matters.' He stepped forward. 'How many seconds, Jae.'

Jae did not flinch. 'Forty.'

'You promised twelve.'

'I promised twelve for a handshake. This is a sustained hold on the interface layer itself. The deepest channel. The one the current runs hardest through. Forty.'

'And the cost of forty.'

'The wire is rated for twelve. I am the wire. Forty stops my heart.'

Theo looked at Sloane. For once there was nothing performed in his face. 'Split it,' he said. 'Break the forty into holds with rests between. Naïma and I buy the time. Jae rests. Sloane codes in segments. Nobody dies holding a number.'

Jae shook his head. 'The routing command is atomic. Once I open the interface, the code has to go in and the heading has to be spoken in one unbroken hold. The god seals the channel the instant the bridge breaks. There is no splitting it.'

'Forty,' Theo said.

'Forty.'

Sloane looked at Jae — the shaking hand, the heat behind his ear, the stubborn pride of a boy who had promised twelve and was now being asked for forty and was not refusing, only pricing it.

'We will find a way to buy you rest before the hold,' she said. 'And when you say stop, the bridge comes out. Whatever I have, whatever I have not entered. On the word. I promise.'

'I'll give you what I can,' Jae said. 'When I say stop, you pull the implant yourself. I won't be able to.'

'I will.'

He did not believe her. She did not quite believe herself. But the key was in her coat and the cage was in the walls and a terminus waited ahead that they were going to turn into a trap of their own.

She had built the cage. Tonight she was going to break it open from the inside, with the key she had carried for seven years, in the language she had invented, on a night that had stopped being a heist and become a promise kept across death.
