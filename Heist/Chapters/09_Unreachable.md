# Chapter 9

## 01:20 — Madrid Staging

The staging hangar was a converted cargo warehouse on the edge of the Madrid field — corrugated steel walls, concrete floor stained with oil marks older than the pod, strip lighting that buzzed at a frequency designed by someone who hated concentration. The cold came up through the floor in waves, the kind of cold that found the soles of your boots and climbed. Rafa's pod sat under a drop cloth at the far end, its magnetic clamps humming a low pre-flight note that was more felt than heard, the sound of a machine preparing to do the thing it was built for. The crew's gear was stacked in crates along the walls — the forgery in its shock case, the comms beads in their charger, the tools Naïma had requested in a canvas roll she'd tie to her thigh. The hangar smelled of aviation fuel and cold metal and the particular staleness of a space that was used for things that didn't want to be looked at closely.

The old man's voice came through the line like cloth through a closed hand: quiet, careful, and not quite telling the truth.

'The Client will be unreachable for the next two hours,' Gianni Brunelli said. 'He sends his confidence. He asks only that you finish what you started.'

Naïma sat in the cold of the staging hangar with the line pressed to her ear and heard the pause the old man didn't mean to leave. *Unreachable.* The word sat wrong. The Client had been unreachable for the entire job — that was the nature of the Client. Announcing it now was like announcing that water was wet. You didn't announce a thing unless the announcing was the point, and the point of *unreachable* was to stop anyone asking the next question — which was *unreachable from where.*

She didn't ask it. Not yet. She filed it alongside the empty eighth seat and the verbal fingerprint Theo had quietly passed up the line an hour ago: things that didn't fit, and that she'd make fit later, with her hands if she had to.

'Thank you, Gianni. Tell him we'll finish it.'

'Be careful, child.' The voice he used when he was frightened and didn't want her to know — worn smooth by decades of careful grief. 'The forgery's ready. I finished the inclusion this morning — the birthmark in the lower-left. It'll hold under any glass the buyer's appraiser owns. The dead-drop schedule is set to the windows. If the lines go dark, the schedule is your only friend. Trust the schedule.'

'I always trust the schedule.'

'You trust your hands. There's a difference.' A pause. 'Naïma. Why are you really on this train.'

The hangar was loud with the pod's pre-flight. The question sat between them like a stone.

'For the stone.'

'You're a poor liar for a woman who lives by it. I knew your father, you know. Before. He was a good man who worked for bad men and called it engineering. The Meridian was his, once — the security layer, the seals, the way the cars talk to each other. He was proud of it. He shouldn't have been. Look where it got him.'

She closed her eyes. Her father. The word still had weight — the way a word has weight when the thing it names has been taken from you and given to strangers and called a scandal. Omar El-Amrani had helped design the Meridian's security layer, then been made to carry the blame for everything it let happen, then erased — professionally, the way the network erased people. Not with a bullet. With a story, a ruin, a name the newspapers used as a synonym for shame. He died believing the network had broken him on purpose, because he'd started asking questions about a room in Car F that didn't appear on his own blueprints.

Naïma had his hands. That was the thing she'd kept, when the network took everything else — his hands, and his way of reading a building, and his habit of trusting the spaces between things. She'd learned to pick locks the way he'd learned to design them: by understanding what a lock was afraid of. And a lock was afraid of the person who knew it better than the person who'd built it.

She hadn't told the Architect any of this. She'd said she was in it for the money and the craft, and the Architect had looked at her the way she looked at everyone — measuring the distance between what you said and what you were — and said, *Fine. Don't die,* and moved on. Naïma had been grateful for the not-pressing. Not-pressing was, in her experience, the rarest form of kindness.

She wasn't on this train for the Leviathan. She was on it because the Meridian had eaten her father one question at a time, and she intended to ask the last question herself, in the place where the answers were kept, with her hands — the only part of her she'd ever fully trusted.

But that was her truth, and truths were private, and everyone on this crew was carrying a private war inside the public one — the Architect with her notebook, Jae with the heat behind his ear, Theo with his warm pocket — and that was how crews worked. It was also how they broke.

'Be careful,' Gianni said again, and the line went dead, and Naïma was left in the cold with the word *unreachable* turning over like a key without a lock.

From where. Why say it now. Why say it like a man covering a door he didn't want her behind.

She thought about the Client — who paid in advance, spoke through cloth-voiced old men, and used phrases his own buyers repeated in first-class lounges a continent away, as if his voice could be in two rooms at once. Voices didn't occupy two rooms. Voices had bodies. And a body that was *unreachable* was a body close enough to be reached, and being kept from reaching.

The pod's ramp lowered. Inside, Jae sat with his eyes shut and his fingers on his neck. Theo sat being nobody, cufflinks straight, pocket warm. Emeka sat with his worry. The empty seat at the far end waited like a question the Architect hadn't finished asking.

Naïma pulled her hood up and went to take her place.

In ten minutes the window would open. In ten minutes the train would come out of the dark at a speed that didn't forgive, and they'd have ninety seconds to become part of it, and she'd put her hands on the machine that had eaten her father, and find the room that wasn't on the map.

She intended to open it. Whatever was inside.

The old man had asked why she was really on this train, and she'd lied, and the lie was the smallest betrayal of the night — and not, she was beginning to think, the last.
