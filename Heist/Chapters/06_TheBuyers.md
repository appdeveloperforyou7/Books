# Chapter 6

## 00:15 — Madrid

Theodore Harlow had been a duke, a fund manager, a minor royal, and a man with a cancer he didn't have. He'd enjoyed all of them. None had been him. That was the joke of Theodore Harlow's life: excellent at being other people, only adequate at being himself.

Tonight he was a buyer. The charter read Lord something-or-other — he'd picked the name three days ago and already forgotten the second half, which he felt was the truest possible preparation, because real lords forgot their own names constantly and only the impersonators remembered them. The money was real. The interest in cursed jewels was performed with exactly the right bored enthusiasm — never too eager, eagerness was the death of the con. The suit cost more than most people's cars, because Theo believed, with the conviction of a man who had nothing else to believe in, that a good suit was the first lie a convincing man told.

He sat in a private lounge at the edge of the Madrid staging field — dark wood, low light, the hush of a place built to make rich men feel richer — and read the dossier on the people he was about to share a metal tube with for fourteen hours. He didn't like what he read.

The Architect's intelligence was good. It was always good. Sloane didn't send people in blind; she sent them in well-dressed. The passenger list had been scraped from a dozen half-secure sources and stitched into faces, names, and — where the names were lies — the shapes of the lies underneath. Theo admired the work as one craftsman admires another: professionally, and with a small private jealousy, because her stitching was better than his.

Three of the thirty-six buyers were not buyers.

The first: a woman the file called Irina Volkov, though Volkov was almost certainly not her name and Irina was a guess. First-class from Lisbon. She hadn't touched the champagne. Hadn't spoken to the steward. Hands flat on her knees, eyes moving once every nine seconds — as a person moves their eyes when they're counting exits. Theo had met women like Irina. They didn't collect jewels. They collected problems, and solved them quietly, and the quiet was the warning.

The second: Hiroshi Tanaka. Might have been a buyer, except buyers didn't usually travel with the particular folded steel that left a print on the back of a trouser waistband, and Mr Tanaka's tailor hadn't quite hidden it. He was courteous in the intercepted footage — almost excessively so — as a man is courteous when courtesy is the lid he keeps on something less courteous. Theo, who'd built a career on the gap between what people showed and what they were, recognised the gap at once: the wider the courtesy, the deeper the thing underneath.

The third was a brute. Olek Marchetti, half-Italian, half-something harder. Took up space as a wardrobe takes up space. Already frightened a steward, and the run hadn't properly begun. Brutes, in Theo's experience, were the easiest to read and the most dangerous to be wrong about — because brutes didn't bother with lids. What you saw was what you got, and what you got, if you misjudged the distance, was a great deal of force delivered without art.

Three killers in a carriage of thirty-six. Theo made a note to be very charming, very forgettable, and to stand near none of them. He adjusted his cufflinks — the thing Theo did when he was thinking, as other people touched a rosary.

He was about to close the file when the audio caught.

A clip from the Lisbon boarding lounge, an hour old — ambient noise, two men in eveningwear the camera hadn't fully caught. One laughing about the auction. The other, low and amused:

*—kindness I save for people who finish the job.*

Theo rewound it. Played it again.

*Kindness I save for people who finish the job.*

He sat very still. The lounge carried on around him — dark wood, money, the murmur of it — and none of it touched him, because he was listening to a sentence he'd heard before, in a room he remembered exactly, spoken by a man whose mouth he could see in the dark.

The Client had said it to him, three weeks ago. Theo had asked for more money. The Client had refused, smiled, and said: *I pay well because I can. Don't mistake the cheque for kindness — kindness I save for people who finish the job.* The exact sentence. The exact cadence. The little pause before *kindness*, as if the word amused him — the one human crack in his voice, the one thing Theo had liked about him.

The Client was supposed to be off the train. A voice on a line that didn't exist. A signature on a transfer. Hands that never touched the job. The crew had been told, in so many words, that the man paying them would be nowhere near the Meridian tonight.

And yet, an hour ago in Lisbon, someone had walked onto that train speaking with the Client's exact mouth.

Theo played it a third time. The voice was half-covered by laughter and clinking glass; the camera had missed the face. It was *possible* — just possible — that the sentence was coincidence, a phrase two unconnected men had arrived at independently. Phrases floated free of their owners. It happened.

It did not happen to Theodore Harlow. Theo didn't believe in coincidence any more than he believed in dukes — he'd been too many of them to trust the real thing. Coincidence was what lesser con artists blamed when they'd been outplayed, and Theo hadn't been outplayed, and he wasn't going to start blaming coincidence now.

He reached for the secure line to the Architect. Then he stopped.

Because there was one other person who used that sentence. Theo had heard it from him too — a different room, a different night, attached to a different offer entirely: the Client's standing invitation to flip. The offer Theo had never accepted and never refused, the one he kept in the lining of his jacket as Sloane kept her notebook — against the day it might be useful, or the day it might be the thing that broke him, and Theo had never been entirely sure which.

If the Client had a man aboard who spoke with his voice, then the Client wasn't off the train. And if the Client wasn't off the train, the job Sloane had sold them in Lisbon had a shape none of them had been shown.

Theo sat in his expensive suit and didn't call the Architect, because calling would mean explaining the standing offer, and explaining the offer would mean admitting he'd been keeping a secret from the only person whose good opinion he wanted — and Theodore Harlow would rather board a train full of killers than do that. He'd rather carry the secret, warm and private against his ribs, and find out, in his own charming time, which of the thirty-six faces in first class was the one that wrote the cheques.

He closed the file. Adjusted his cufflinks. Practised his smile in the dark glass — three reflections of a buyer who didn't exist smiled back, all convincing, none true.

In ninety minutes he'd walk onto a train beside a man who spoke with his employer's voice, and find the face the charming way — by being so thoroughly someone else that the real people forgot to guard themselves, and by listening, in the gap between the performance and the person, for the one sentence that didn't fit.

He rather thought he was going to enjoy it. He usually did.

And the ugly phone, in the lining of his jacket, stayed warm against his ribs, and waited — patient, as offers wait — for the moment it became useful, or the moment it became true.
