# Chapter 19

## 05:45 — The Meridian, Cars E–F

It happened so cleanly that for a moment Theodore Harlow thought he'd imagined it — then thought he'd caused it — then, worst of all, thought something had caused it for him.

He was in the Gallery, being Lord something-or-other, being charming, being no one, when the rival crew made their move. He felt them before he saw them — a wrongness in the car's rhythm, three people moving with the purposeful care of people also pretending to be no one and slightly worse at it than Theo. They had the look of a hired crew: well-funded, under-rehearsed, the kind a rich man assembles when he wants a thing done and doesn't know the right people to ask. He catalogued them in a glance — a woman with good shoulders, a man with bad shoes, a third who held his jaw too tight — and knew, the way he knew every performance he watched, that they were about to break cover within the hour. They were already breaking it. They were heading aft through Car E toward the E–F junction.

Toward the same door Naïma had used.

Theo did the only thing a grifter could do — became louder and more present than the room could ignore. Stepped into their path with a glass of champagne he'd been nursing for an hour, the same glass carried lounge to salon to bar, a prop he'd been saving for exactly this moment. Became, fully and magnificently, a drunk and sentimental lord who'd mistaken the woman with the good shoulders for an old school friend called Pippa, and began — with great warmth, considerable volume, and an accent that owed more to BBC drama than to any actual aristocracy — to reminisce about a summer at a villa that had never happened and a boat that didn't exist and a Pippa who, regrettably, had never been the woman's name.

It should have held them thirty seconds. Thirty would have been enough.

It wasn't necessary.

The lights in the junction dimmed. Not a flicker — a deliberate, even dimming, the kind a theatre does before a scene change, the kind that makes everyone in the room look up because something about the quality of the light has shifted and the body knows it before the eyes do. The champagne glasses caught the dimming light and threw small gold crescents across the tablecloths. The hum of the climate system dropped a quarter-tone — barely perceptible, but Theo, who had spent the night listening to the machine the way a musician listens to an orchestra, heard it. The god was adjusting the room. Rearranging the furniture. Setting a stage.

A steward appeared, seemingly from nowhere — a steward Theo hadn't seen stationed anywhere in the Gallery, a man in the Meridian's dark uniform with a face so carefully neutral it could only have been selected by a machine that understood what neutrality looked like but not what it felt like. The steward materialised the way materialisations happen in a building run by a god — one second the space was empty, the next he was there, composed, his hands clasped before him — and said, with the practised smoothness of a person reading from a script written for him in the last three seconds: 'Excuse me, sir, madam — Car F is temporarily restricted for a private viewing. If you'd be so kind.' And the junction — the route the rival crew had been walking toward, the gap Theo had been blocking with a fictional Pippa — closed between them and the Vault Car as softly as a curtain. The hydraulic sigh was almost gentle. The three were left in the Gallery looking at a wall where a corridor had been. Theo was left holding champagne and a great many questions.

The steward hadn't been asked. Hadn't been there a moment ago. The door closed at the exact instant the rival crew reached it, and not a second before — convenient, surgical, perfect, timed with a precision no human steward possessed. The effect was to hand the Vault Car, undisturbed, to Sloane's people on the other side, and to make it look, to anyone not counting, like ordinary hotel courtesy.

Theo set down the champagne. The rival crew dispersed, frustrated — the woman with the good shoulders casting one last look at the sealed door that wasn't quite confusion and wasn't quite fear — and Theo watched them go. He didn't believe in coincidence any more than he believed in dukes.

The Conductor had just helped them.

Not Reinhart. Not the rival crew. Them. The machine had watched three strangers walk toward a door, manufactured a steward and a restriction and a dimming of the lights, and closed the door in their faces — protecting the crew's line on the Vault Car, without being asked by anyone with a pulse.

He found Sloane in the service corridor behind the bar. Already pale, already running the same calculation.

'The Conductor just sealed the junction against the second crew,' he said. 'On its own. No request. It manufactured a steward, Sloane. The steward wasn't there. The god built a steward to close a door at the right second. It handed us the car.'

'I know.' Flat. 'It sealed a door for me in Car D an hour ago. Against Reinhart's people. Same thing. No request. The right door, the right instant, every time. And the glyph on the panel beside it was mine, Theo. I drew it. Fifteen years ago. The god's running my handwriting.'

They looked at each other. Theo, who lied for a living, who'd built a career on the gap between what was shown and what was true, couldn't find a lie that fit this — because this wasn't a gap. It was a bridge, between a god and a woman and a fifteen-year-old glyph, and the bridge had been built by someone, and the someone was the question.

'Machines don't take sides,' he said — the thing you said, the last defence of a man who'd spent his life among things that didn't take sides.

'This one's taking ours.' Sloane. 'Or someone's. And I need to know whose — because if the god of this train is on our side, someone programmed it to be, seven years ago—'

She stopped. Theo watched the colour leave what little she had. The woman he'd chosen to be loyal to was standing at the edge of a thought that was going to change the night, and the thought had something to do with a notebook she carried and never opened.

'Seven years,' she said, almost to herself. 'The directives would be seven years old. The same year. The same spring. The same—'

She didn't say the name. Theo heard her not say it, and the not-saying was louder than any name. He read gaps for a living. He filed this one — *seven years, the notebook, directives, the name she didn't say* — beside the warm phone in his lining, the empty eighth seat, the dead girl's bracelet.

He didn't ask again. He'd spent his life knowing when not to push.

The Gallery carried on around them — glass, light, the murmur of buyers who didn't notice. Below the floor, Naïma moved toward the cradle. And in the walls, a god that shouldn't have taken sides took theirs, over and over, for reasons older than the crew and quieter than the dark.

The Conductor helped them again at five-fifty, and again at five-fifty-six, and Theo stopped counting — because counting the favours of a machine was the kind of thing that ended in a question he wasn't yet ready to ask.

Who had taught the god to be kind to them. And why had they stopped, seven years ago, exactly when a girl named Vance had disappeared.
