# Chapter 36

## 11:20 — The Meridian, aft (the ascent)

Naïma El-Amrani carried a woman up the spine of a sealed train at speed, and the train did not want to let her, and she did it anyway — because carrying people out of buildings was the thing her father had taught her, and the thing she'd frozen on once, and the thing she was never going to freeze on again.

She'd frozen once. Two years ago. Rotterdam. A building site in the dark, a job gone wrong. A crewmate — a man whose name she still didn't say — had been on the wrong side of a hatch when it closed, and Naïma had been the one holding it, and she'd frozen, for two seconds, and the two seconds had been the difference between the seal opening and not opening. The man had been on the wrong side. The two seconds were the thing she carried now instead of him — a stone in the chest, unmoving, around which everything else flowed. She hadn't frozen since. Tonight, carrying a woman up the spine of a sealed train with hunters in the corridors behind them, she was going to carry the two seconds out too.

They moved aft. *Up*, the crew called it, though the train was level — aft toward Rafa's pod, toward the only hatch that would open when the Architect turned the machine. Lina walked between Naïma and Emeka, unsteady, blinking in the red emergency light. The woman could barely run. Three months behind a wall had taken her legs — the muscles that remembered walking had forgotten running, and every stride was a negotiation between what the body intended and what the body would give. Naïma set the pace at the edge of what Lina could hold, which was half of what the hunters behind them could hold, and the mathematics of the next five minutes did not favour them.

She read the train as her father had taught her to read a building — not by what was there, but by the pressures underneath. The deck-plate's vibration under her boots told her the maglev was still at cruise; the airflow through the service channel told her which junctions were open and which were sealed (sealed channels drew, open ones breathed); and the faint change in pressure at every threshold told her, two seconds before she reached it, whether the next car was clear. She'd been reading buildings this way since she was nine. Tonight the building was doing six hundred kilometres an hour, and she read it anyway, because the bones were the bones.

Behind them — distant, but closing — the sound of a seal that should have held failing to hold. A lock cycling, failing, cycling again. The god was trying to hold every seal at once and couldn't. It was clearing their road by pouring its attention aft, and the concentration left gaps forward, and Reinhart's remaining people were pouring through the gaps like water through cracks in a dam.

'They're in Car L,' Jae said, thin and hot, over the comms — reading the thermal map from Car B with a skull running out of margin. 'Two of them. Moving aft. They'll reach the M junction in ninety seconds. Maybe less.'

'How fast can Lina go,' Naïma said. Not to Jae. To Lina.

'I can go,' Lina said. She was breathing in short, shallow pulls — the trained breathing of the cage, which had become, against all intention, a kind of stamina, the lungs efficient with almost nothing. 'Don't slow for me. If I fall, keep going.'

'If you fall, I've already fallen,' Naïma said. 'We go together or we don't go. Move.'

They reached Car K — private cabins, buyers fled forward, the cabins open and dark, champagne glasses half-full on side tables, the whole expensive pretence of the evening abandoned. Naïma pulled them into the narrow service channel behind the cabin row, where the carpet gave way to deck-plate and the air smelled of ozone and recycled linen. Faster here, out of the main corridor, but the channel was narrow — single file — and Emeka, at the rear, filled it shoulder to shoulder. Emeka was the wall now.

'Seventy seconds,' Jae said. 'They're moving faster than you.'

The K-to-L junction opened for them before Naïma's hand reached it — the ghost, the lock cycling a half-second ahead of their approach. Emeka and Lina went through. Naïma, covering the rear, was a step behind —

And the junction sealed.

Not behind her. On her. The lock slammed home a half-second early, the hydraulics biting shut a metre in front of her face, and Naïma's hand hit alloy where a second ago there had been a gap. The god had meant to seal it behind them. It had sealed it on her instead — the timing off by the half-second she'd spent checking the channel. A favour becoming a trap. She heard Emeka's shout, muffled, on the far side, and Lina's voice, and then the barrier was a wall and she was alone in Car K with the boots coming.

She didn't freeze. The two seconds stayed where they were — a stone she'd long since learned to step around. Naïma dropped.

The floor-run. The ugly way, the last resort, the cable channel beneath the deck-plates, forty centimetres high, pressurised for the conduit, never meant for a person. She'd mapped it in the first hour, as she mapped every building's bones. She found the access plate by feel — a recessed handle at the baseboard — torqued it, and slid into the dark under the floor in a single motion, pulling the plate over her as the first hunter rounded the corner into Car K above her head.

She lay in the channel. Forty centimetres of clearance. The deck-plate cold against her cheek, the conduit bundles hot against her back, the maglev's vibration a deep hum in her sternum. Above her, boots — two sets, moving aft along the corridor, passing over her. She could hear them, low, professional, the discipline of people who hunted in pairs. She waited. Counted the boots past her. When the second pair had crossed the L junction and moved on, she slid forward — elbow, knee, elbow, the crawl she'd done a thousand times in a thousand ceilings and floors, reading the airflow through the conduit gaps: the air knew where the space was.

The airflow thinned at the L-M boundary — a sealed junction above her, the hunters paused there, deciding. She slid under it. The channel narrowed at the M bulkhead, the conduit bundling tight, and she had to exhale entirely to fit through, ribs compressed, the pressure in her ears telling her the seal above was holding. She came through. Crawled on. The channel opened under Car M, narrowed again toward N, and the air ahead changed — warmer, thinner, the signature of the rear engineering bay.

She came up behind them. The access plate at the base of Car M's medical bay — she shouldered it, rolled out, and was on her feet in the service corridor fifteen metres behind Emeka and Lina, who'd reached Car N. Emeka turned at the sound, saw her, and the relief on his face was the kind men don't show. She'd crossed three sealed cars beneath the floor in under two minutes, alone, and come up behind her own people. The god's favour had tried to trap her. She'd gone under it.

'I'm here,' she said. 'Don't ask.'

But the boots had crossed the L junction too, and now the two hunters were in Car M behind her, and the distance was thirty metres and closing.

Car N. The door.

Sealed. Not by the ghost. By Reinhart — a manual bolt, physical, on a circuit the god's directives didn't touch. The one lock the ghost couldn't open, because the ghost lived in the machine and the manual bolt lived outside it, thrown by a hand.

Naïma slammed her palm against it. The bolt didn't move. Behind her, the boots — twenty seconds, the length of the M corridor at a run.

'Architect,' Naïma said into the comms, breathing hard. 'N's sealed. Reinhart's lock — manual, separate circuit, the god can't reach it. We're one car from Rafa and the hatch won't open. This one needs a hand. And there are two of Reinhart's people in the corridor behind me. Now.'

Sloane's voice, from forward, from the threshold of the brain: 'Jae. When I give the code — can you free Car N. The manual bolt.'

Jae, thin, hot: 'I can free anything for the seconds I can hold. The bridge reaches the interface, the interface reaches the manual overrides, the overrides reach the bolt. Get me to the bridge. I'll open every lock between here and the sky.'

Behind her, fifteen seconds. Naïma pulled Lina against the wall, pressed her down behind Emeka, who turned at the throat of the corridor and filled it — the wall, the conscience, the only thing now between the hunters and the woman who'd counted doors.

The two came around the corner at a run and read the geometry: the corridor ending here, a sealed hatch, a very large man. One of them — the courtesy-and-steel one — slowed. The other didn't. The brute came on, and the thing in his hand was short and dark and not a conversation.

Emeka didn't move out of the way. He did what seven years of security work and one ruined career had trained the body to remember even when the mind had forgotten why: he stepped into the swing, took the arm at the wrist, and used the brute's own speed to put the man into the bulkhead. It wasn't pretty. It was the applied physics of a man who'd decided the woman behind him was getting off this train, and the decision was final. The brute hit the wall, and didn't get up quickly. The courteous one read the corridor again — his man down, the wall unmoved, the sealed hatch — and made the professional's calculation: not tonight. He backed, slow, hands where Emeka could see them, and melted aft toward the junction. Two hunters, dealt with. Not by a god. By a man, and the floor under a woman.

'One door away,' Naïma said to Lina.

'You said that about the wall,' Lina said. The joke of a woman who'd learned to count doors because doors were the only honest thing. Brave. Terrified.

'This one opens from the outside too. I promise. And this time the outside's me.'

She pressed her hand to the seal and waited for the boy at the brain to give her the second she needed.

And then the bolt behind Naïma's back let go — the small mechanical surrender of a thing told, for two seconds, that letting go was medicine — and the hatch of Car N gave, and the cold air of the rear engineering bay rushed in, and beyond it the coupling, and beyond the coupling the hull, and beyond the hull the pod.

'Go,' Naïma said. 'Now. Both of you. Through. I'm right behind you.'

She'd come up through the floor to do it. The god had tried to seal her out; she'd gone under. Reinhart's man had come for them; Emeka had put him down. And the last lock — the one no god could touch — a boy at the brain had bought with forty seconds of his own heart. Three people, three skills, one door opened from the outside. The way rescues were supposed to work — the way her father had said they did, in buildings, when the people inside were worth the floor you crawled through to reach them.
