# Chapter 8

## 01:00 — Madrid Staging

An hour before the window, Theodore Harlow did the thing he'd sworn he wouldn't do — took out the phone and looked at the offer.

The phone was old and ugly and lived in the lining of his jacket, where the good phone couldn't see it. One number. The number belonged to a man who spoke with the Client's voice, or who was the Client, or who was something adjacent Theo had never quite mapped — a voice without a clear body, an offer without a clear consequence. Three weeks ago that number had sent him a message: *turn, at any point, and be paid twice, and walk away clean, and never see any of these people again.*

He hadn't shown it to Sloane.

He hadn't shown her because showing it would cost him the only thing he cared about — not the money, not the clean exit, but the small private embarrassing fact that Sloane Vance trusted him. Being trusted by Sloane Vance was the closest Theodore Harlow had ever come to being real. He'd been real exactly once in his adult life: the moment Sloane looked at him as if he were a person rather than a costume, and he decided, right then, to guard that looking with everything he had. True things were rare. Rare things were worth lying for — which was the paradox at the centre of Theodore Harlow: he lied so that one person might, eventually, see the truth.

The hangar was cold and smelled of fuel and burnt coffee and the metallic chill of a building that housed machines. Rafa's pod sat under the stripped lights, a black insect shape humming its pre-flight, and around it the crew assembled out of the Madrid dark as a phrase assembles out of separate words — each arriving in their own way, each already becoming the person they'd be aboard.

Jae was already in the pod, cross-legged, eyes shut, fingers at the side of his neck, running whatever private arithmetic the boy ran before a job. Emeka had landed ten minutes ago — inspector's coat, the worry in his face, the big man carrying his conscience as other men carried luggage. Naïma materialised out of nothing, hooded, silent, already counting the pod's bolts. And Rafa stood at the open hatch of his insect, running the pre-flight with his hands, checking everything twice, because checking was the difference between a pilot who flew once and a pilot who flew home.

'Do you ever sleep,' Theo said, by way of greeting, leaning on the strut. 'Before a thing like this. Or do you just sit up all night counting the ways it can go wrong.'

'I sleep fine,' Rafa said, not looking up. 'The ways it can go wrong are the same nine they were yesterday. I checked them before I slept. They're still nine.'

'Nine is a lot of ways to die.'

'Nine is a small number, once you've eliminated the rest. Go bother someone else. I'm counting.'

The Architect was last, or would be; Sloane was never early and never late, simply there at the moment she intended, as if edited into the scene.

Theo's thumb found the power button of the ugly phone. Pressed it halfway.

The offer was real. That was the trouble. The Client didn't make offers that weren't real. If Theo turned tonight, he'd be paid twice and walk away, and Sloane would never know it was him, because Sloane didn't believe he was capable of it, and her disbelief was the lock the ugly phone's offer was the key to. The Client had seen that — with the cold reader's eye Theo recognised because it was his own eye turned back on him — that Theodore Harlow was a man who could be bought, and had priced him accordingly. The pricing was an insult and a compliment at once, and Theo had felt both when he read the number.

He could do it. That was the other trouble. He could do it so easily. A word to the man with the voice, a nod at the empty eighth seat, a single small betrayal timed to the Crack, and the job folded and Theo was rich and free and unmarked. He'd been a duke, a fund manager, a dying man. Being a traitor would be no harder than any of them.

He powered the phone off and put it back in his lining. He'd keep it for a reason, he told himself. Strategy. Strategy wasn't temptation.

It was. He knew it was. But knowing wasn't doing, and Theodore Harlow had built an entire life in the space between the two.

'You're smiling at your pocket,' Naïma said, from near his elbow. She had a gift for arriving without warning. 'Either you're in love or you're planning something. Both make me nervous.'

'I'm always planning something.' He gave her the smile he gave everyone — exactly warm enough, not a degree more. 'It's my charm.'

'Your charm is a weapon. So is a grenade. I prefer the grenade. I know which way it's pointing.'

'You wound me. My pointing is impeccable.'

'Your pointing is the reason I count the bolts.' She moved away, silent.

Jae, without opening his eyes, from inside the pod: 'She's right. Your pointing is terrible. I've worked with you four times. Twice I was almost arrested. Once I was almost married. The common factor was you.'

'The marriage was your own doing. She was lovely. You hesitated. Hesitation is not pointing, it's cowardice.'

'I hesitated because you'd told her I was a cardiologist.'

'A cardiologist is a respectable man. You should have thanked me.'

'I'm twenty-four. I can't drive. I have a scar I can't explain. I was not a convincing cardiologist.'

'You were a magnificent cardiologist. The problem was the driving, and the driving was Rafa's fault, and Rafa is—' Theo gestured. Rafa, still not looking up: 'Counting.' '—counting. So everything, you see, is someone else's fault, and I am blameless, as always.'

Naïma, from somewhere in the dark, quiet enough that only the crew heard: 'Blameless men don't smile at their pockets, Theo.'

He let the smile drop. It was work, and work was tiring, and he was tired, and the ugly phone was warm against his ribs.

The truth — the one he'd never say to Sloane, the one he barely admitted to the dark — was that he wasn't going to turn. He'd known it since the briefing. Since Lisbon. Since the first time Sloane had looked at him as if he were a person rather than a performance. He'd decided, privately and forever, that he'd rather be a real thing for her once than a successful lie for anyone else a thousand times. The money was real. The exit was clean. The betrayal was easy. None of it was worth the looking.

But the offer was still in his pocket, and the man with the voice was on that train, and the space between knowing and doing was where Theodore Harlow had always lived. Tonight the space was narrow, and the window was close.

He checked his watch. Fifty-eight minutes.

He climbed into the pod, sat across from Jae, and began — cheerfully, thoroughly — to be Lord something-or-other, buyer of cursed jewels, and nobody's traitor, and to keep the phone warm against his ribs like a small black secret he'd decided, for now, not to use.

To anyone watching, the team had a crack in it. The grifter was always the crack. That was the job. That was the point — you pointed the audience at the grifter, let them watch him, while the rest of the crew did the work they weren't meant to see.

Tonight the crack pointed the other way. And only Theo knew which way that was.
