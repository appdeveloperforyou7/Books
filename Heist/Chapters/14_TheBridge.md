# Chapter 14

## 03:20 — The Meridian, Car B

Rafa's voice came through the pod's relay, scratchy with distance, calm with the particular calm of a man who'd just discovered he wasn't the only magician in the building.

'There's something you should know,' he said, in the tone Jae had filed under *Rafa is being fine about something that is not fine*. 'I turned down a job once. Years ago. A European cop wanted me as an informant. Said she'd make my record go away if I fed her the people I worked for.'

Jae, crouched in the service bay of Car B with his sleeve rolled up and a medical port open in the wall at chest height — a small dark port the size of a paperback, the one door the Conductor wasn't allowed to ignore — said, 'And you're telling me this now because.'

'Because she's aboard tonight.'

Jae's hand went to the implant. Warm. It had crossed, sometime in the last hour, from the kind of warm you filed under *fine* to the kind you filed under *watch*. 'How do you know.'

'I don't know. I think. There's a passenger in first-class who moves like her. I only saw her for a second on the hull cams — I've been running the passive scan all night, read-only, the kind the god can't hear — but the way she sat. The way she counted the room.' A pause. 'I've been counted by that woman before, Jae. Four years ago, in a room, for two hours. You don't forget being counted by her. She counts you as an auditor counts a ledger — line by line, entry by entry — and when she's done she knows exactly what you're worth and exactly what you'll cost. I've been inside that count once. I didn't enjoy it.'

'The Architect said seat eight was an asset.'

'The Architect said a lot of things. I'm telling you what I saw.' Rafa's voice went quieter. 'I never took her offer, Jae. I want that on the record before it matters. She asked. She offered me a clean record and a salary and a way out. I said no. That's the whole of it. I said no because she was right about the people I worked for, and I refused her anyway — and the refusing is the only dishonest thing I've ever done. I'm tired of carrying it. I'd rather you heard it from me than from her.'

Jae filed it. He filed everything — the cop, the refused offer, the four-year-old room, the count, the possibility that the empty eighth seat wasn't empty but occupied by a woman who hunted thieves for a living and had hunted this crew's pilot once before. He filed it the way a system logs an error — timestamped, unfeeling, queued for the pattern that would give it meaning. The meaning would come. Facts accumulated; meaning followed. He didn't know what this one meant yet. He would.

But he had a port open in front of him and a Cat waiting three cars away and a god that was learning, and the facts would have to accumulate without his full attention, because the bridge was now.

'Thanks,' he said. 'Hold something.'

He pressed the implant to the medical port.

The handshake hit him like a bell struck inside his skull.

Not pain, exactly. Presence. For four seconds — he counted them, because counting was the only thing keeping him anchored to the side of the current that didn't kill — the Conductor's medical bus ran through him, and he was no longer Jae-won Kang in the service bay; he was a wire, and the wire was live, and the live wire was plugged into the body of a god. He felt the train. Not as data, not as a screen, but as a body: the hum of the guideway in its teeth, the warmth of the passengers in its belly, the slow deep pulse of its routing layer thinking about where it was going. For four seconds he was part of the machine and the machine was part of him, and the two of them were, briefly, one living thing.

And for four seconds the god believed he was a patient in distress in the E–F junction, and opened the floor-run hatch for Naïma — because its first law was that passengers arrived alive, and a patient in distress was a passenger, and a patient needed a door. The god opened it as a body opens a lung: automatically, without thought, because its mercy was older than its suspicion and the suspicion hadn't arrived yet.

Four seconds.

On the third his vision went white at the edges. On the fourth his left hand began to shake — a fine fast tremor he couldn't stop, his own implant firing its stimulation down the vagal threads the surgeons had wired, the handshake answering itself inside his skull. He pulled the implant from the port on the count of four, exactly four, not a tenth of a second more — because Jae-won Kang didn't give numbers he wasn't certain of, and four was the number, and four was what he'd hold. The bell stopped. The train stopped being a body and became a wall of humming metal again. Jae sat on the floor of Car B with his back against the panel and his hand shaking and his heartbeat doing a thing heartbeats weren't designed to do — a stutter, a skip, a wrong rhythm — the nervous system over-firing, the way it does when one of these implants pushes a patient toward cardiac arrest. The wire's tax, coming due — not through his chest but through his nervous system, which was worse, because the nervous system was the thing the implant had been installed to protect.

He waited for it to settle. It had in Osaka, slowly, a bell going thin and high and then gone. It settled now, slowly. The rhythm found itself again. His. The shaking slowed. Stopped. Mostly.

'Window's open,' he said into the comms, and his voice sounded like someone else's — someone who'd been running, which he hadn't, which was worse, because the body that had been running was his own without his permission. 'Four seconds. Go, Naïma. Go now.'

In his ear, Naïma's silence — which was movement — and then, barely, a word that wasn't about the job: 'Breathe.'

Just that. One word. She'd heard his voice on the comms and known, as she knew every lock, that he hadn't been breathing. He started.

He looked at his left hand. Stopped shaking. Mostly. He curled it into a fist and opened it again — testing, as you test a bridge you've just crossed to be sure it'll hold the next person. The hand opened. Closed. His again. Mostly. And *mostly* was the word he was going to have to live with tonight, because *entirely* was a word the wire had taken and wasn't giving back.

Four seconds. He'd given the Architect four.

The critical bridge would be twelve.

He didn't do the maths on what twelve would feel like. He didn't have to. Three times four was twelve, and three times a bell struck inside his skull was a bell he wasn't certain his skull was built to survive.

He touched the implant. Hot now, not warm. He filed it under *fine*. The floor-run was open. The Cat was moving. The god had been lied to once and wouldn't be lied to the same way again — the handshake was spent, the four seconds spent, the medical bus had learned the shape of his lie and would recognise it next time.

Jae stood, slowly, and went to find a place to sit very still and wait for his hand to stop pretending it belonged to someone else.
