# Chapter 18

## 05:25 — The Meridian, Car F (outer cradle)

Emeka Okafor had expected a safe. Safes he understood — mass and metal, the simple honest physics of keeping a heavy thing inside a heavier thing. He'd cracked safes, audited safes, testified in court about a safe. Safes were his country. He knew their language.

Car F did not speak the language of safes.

He stood in the narrow service space beside the Vault Car's outer cradle — Naïma had got him in through the floor run, against every protocol his old life held sacred — and read the sensor array the way he'd read the blueprint: slowly, with the growing suspicion that the people who'd built it had been lying to everyone, including themselves. The space was cold and dark, smelled of ozone and the metallic nothing of sealed atmosphere. The cradle's readout glowed the only light, a cold blue that turned his big hands the colour of deep water.

A jewel sat in a sealed atmosphere. Around it, the cradle's sensors watched. That much was honest. What wasn't honest was what they watched for.

Emeka had expected mass sensors, vibration sensors — the blunt instruments you wrapped around a stone to know if it moved. The honest, stupid hardware of containment, asking *is the thing still here* and nothing else. Instead the cradle was ringed with biometric authentication nodes — the hardware you used to verify an identity, confirm a key, log a transaction. The hardware of permission, not containment. The cradle wasn't asking *is the jewel still here*. It was asking *who is authorised to read what is under the jewel*. The wrong question for a stone. The right question for a lock — and a lock was a door, and a door was a thing you opened to reach what was behind it.

There was nothing under a jewel. Unless there was.

He crouched and let his fingers follow the conduit lines — the way he'd been trained, the way he'd taught juniors. *Follow the power,* he'd told them, *because the power always goes to the thing the machine cares about.* The conduits ran two directions: outward to the seal — the honest direction, powering the locks and sensors. And inward, down, beneath the cradle, to a secondary housing he could feel but not see — a housing that drank more power than any stone had ever required, that hummed under his fingertips the way a heart hums under a hand.

Power didn't go to a jewel. Power went to a lock. And the thing behind this lock wasn't the sapphire, because the sapphire was up here — lit, beautiful, performing the part of a prize while the real prize sat in the dark beneath it, drawing current and asking, over and over, in the silent language of biometrics, *who are you, who are you, who are you.*

The nodes pulsed as he watched — a slow, even rhythm, blue-white, like a heartbeat made of light, each pulse asking its question and receiving no answer and asking again, patient, tireless, the patience of a machine that could wait forever because it had been designed to wait forever. The pulse reflected off the walls of Car F in small moving crescents of light that slid across the alloy like living things, and the cold of the car was absolute now — not the recycled warmth of the passenger spaces but the deep sealed cold of a room that had been closed since Lisbon, closed and waiting, and the cold came up through Emeka's knees where he crouched on the deck plate, through his trousers, into his joints, the cold of a place that was not meant for people and did not welcome them.

'The cradle's not guarding a stone,' Emeka said into the comms. Steady, the way it always was. Inside he wasn't steady. 'The biometrics are keyed to authentication, not containment. There's a secondary housing under the cradle — drawing power, more than a lock should need. It's a lock for something that isn't the jewel. Something under it. Something the god's asking permission to open.'

Naïma's voice, close, from the floor run beneath him: 'A ledger.'

'A what.'

'The old man said the Meridian kept a record. Gianni. The night of the briefing, in the pause he didn't mean to leave. I thought he meant the auction bids. I didn't think—' A pause. 'A ledger. A record of everything this train has ever moved. If it's under the stone, the stone's a lid. The prize was never the jewel. The jewel's the door.'

Emeka put his hand flat on the deck, over the conduit, and felt the faint vibration of the housing beneath — drawing current, holding its lock, asking its question in the dark below the most beautiful lie in the world.

He'd spent the night afraid of seven cubic metres. Now he was afraid of something worse: the certainty that the man who'd hired them had pointed them at a door and not told them what was behind it — and that the only way to find out was to open it, and opening it was exactly what the man who'd hired them wanted. They weren't thieves cracking a safe. They were keys being inserted into a lock by a hand they couldn't see.

'The Architect needs to know,' he said. 'Now. Before we crack anything. If we crack this cradle, we're not stealing a jewel. We're handing someone the key to whatever this is. And I don't think the someone we're handing it to is someone we want to have it. I think the someone is the someone who locked it away — and he locked it away because it could hang him, and he hired us to open it because he can't open it himself without hanging himself. We're the rope.'

The cradle hummed beneath his hand. The biometric nodes pulsed — patient, asking their question in the dark.

*Who are you.*

Emeka didn't have an answer. But he had, finally, the right question — the one his blueprints had been asking all night, the one his whole ruined career had been circling:

What was the Meridian really built to carry, and who built it to carry it, and what happened to the people who found out?

The stone sat above — blue-black, cursed, beautiful. Beneath it, in the dark, something waited to be read.
