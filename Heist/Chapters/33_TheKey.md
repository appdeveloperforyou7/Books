# Chapter 33

## 11:05 — The Meridian, Car A approach

Jae-won Kang ran toward the brain of the god with a fever in his skull and a number in his mouth. The number was seven.

Seven digits. The legacy code. Not a hack — Jae had checked, twice, against the build documentation Rafa pulled from the undercarriage tap. The routing override was a legitimate maintenance function of the interface layer: every long-route maglev kept one, a sanctioned emergency-reroute credential for the engineers who commissioned the line, so a faulty spur could be quarantined without bricking the network. The interface layer owned routing because the interface was the only subsystem the god trusted to speak for a human. Reinhart's people had never removed the override, because removing it meant admitting, on paper, that it existed — and admitting it existed meant admitting a single credential could turn their train. The medical bus reached it because medical emergencies were authorised to reroute — a patient in distress could be diverted to the nearest trauma bay, and the god's first law let medicine overrule everything. Jae's implant spoke the medical bus. Sloane's code spoke the interface. The two of them together weren't a trick. They were the train's own safety architecture, used in the one order of operations its designers never imagined: a thief, and a patient, and a doctor — all the same boy, all at once. And it never touched the safety core — the fail-safe brain whose only job was to keep the train from ever crashing, sealed away behind three independent locks, untouchable, the one part of the machine nothing and no one could reach. They didn't break the machine's sense of what was safe. They only reached up, above it, to the layer that chose which safe road to take — and chose one the owners hadn't prescribed.

He had the number now. The Architect had given it to him in two-second fragments over the medical bus, between bridges, the way you pass a grenade: carefully, with the pin still in. Seven digits. He repeated them in the head the way he repeated everything — in a vault that kept things cold and exact: three, seven, two, zero, one, nine, four. He would not forget. Could not forget. Forgetting was the one failure Jae-won Kang had never allowed himself, and he wasn't going to allow it now, with the heat behind his ear pulsing in a rhythm no longer *later* but *soon*.

The Conductor was hunting.

He felt it in the walls as he moved forward through the articulated joints — the god sealing cars behind him the way a careful host locks a house against a burglar, one seal at a time, methodical, learning. It had learned three of his handshakes — three lies, spent, each teaching the god the shape of his dishonesty. Two of Naïma's routes — two methods, catalogued, the locks rewritten to defeat the specific geometry of her fingers. Not fast — the Architect had been right, and Jae had confirmed it — but thorough, and thorough in a train with a finite number of cars was the same as inevitable. Every seal it shut was one the crew couldn't use, and the routes were running out, and the god was patient the way only machines were patient — the patience of a thing that didn't need to breathe, didn't tire, didn't stop.

He reached the junction to Car A. The brain. The sealed core where the routing layer lived, where Marin's directives held the heading against Reinhart's hammering — he could feel the hammering through the wall, the distant thud of brute force against seven-year-old code. The interface port waited on the other side of the sealed door — a port listening for a seven-digit code and a forty-second bridge, waiting the way the directives waited, the way the whole train waited, seven years, for the one person who could speak its language.

The door to Car A was sealed. Of course it was sealed — the most sealed door on the most sealed train on earth, the god on the other side, and the god had learned to hate Jae-won Kang specifically, because he'd lied to it four times and the god didn't enjoy being lied to. It had revised its protocols four times to close the lies he'd used, and the fifth lie — the one that would open this door — was the one he'd been saving. The last handshake. The one he hadn't named, because the one he hadn't named was the one he might need to spend on himself.

He put his hand on the lock. Touched the implant. Very hot now — not warm, not the warm of the early night, but the hot of a thing used past its design, the hot of a wire carrying current it wasn't rated for, the graph in the back of his notebook climbing past the line into the blank space where the labels ran out, and the space beyond the labels was where the body stopped cooperating and the mind went on without it. He'd filed it under *later* all night. *Later* had come. *Later* was the door in front of him, and the door required forty seconds, and forty seconds of the medical bus through a fevered skull was forty seconds he'd already decided he might not have — and the deciding was the thing he wasn't going to tell the Architect, because the Architect had promised to stop when he said stop, and the promise was the rope, and the rope was the only thing between him and the water.

He'd promised the Architect twelve. Promised himself he wouldn't promise more. And here he was, at the door of the brain, about to attempt forty — because the Architect had asked, and because the alternative was a nameless siding and a quiet the network did its cleanest work in, and Jae-won Kang hadn't come this far to be quiet in a siding.

He keyed the comms. 'Architect. I'm at Car A. Door's sealed. God's on the other side and it knows me — knows me well, has closed four of my lies, waiting for the fifth. When you get here, I bridge, you code, and we have — what we have. I won't count it in advance. I'll hold until I can't, and then one more second, because I've never given a number I wasn't certain of, and I'm not certain, so I'll give you the only honest thing I have left: I won't stop until the number stops me. And when it stops me you pull the implant yourself, because I won't be able to — and the pulling's what saves me, and the saving's your job, Architect, because you brought me onto this train, and you don't break the people you bring.'

Sloane, from somewhere aft, moving toward him through the sealed cars: 'Jae. That's not a plan.'

'It's not a plan. It's a promise. The plan's your code. Get here.'

He pressed his forehead to the sealed door. Cool metal. The heat behind his ear was not. Between the two temperatures — the cool of the god's door and the heat of his skull — Jae-won Kang waited for the woman with the key, rehearsed the seven digits, and didn't let himself think about the forty seconds, because thinking about them was the kind of thought that ended in a hand too shaky to press the implant to the port.

Three. Seven. Two. Zero. One. Nine. Four.

The key that freed them was the current that would run through the boy at the lock — and the boy knew it, and was going to do it anyway, because some numbers were worth more than the skull that held them, and seven was, tonight, the most expensive number in the world.
