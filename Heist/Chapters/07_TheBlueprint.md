# Chapter 7

## 00:40 — Over the Mediterranean

Emeka Okafor read blueprints the way some men read scripture: slowly, respectfully, and with the growing suspicion that the people who wrote them had something to hide.

The Meridian's security schemata filled his tablet in clean white lines, blue against the dark cabin of the red-eye. The tablet's glow was the only light in his row — the cabin was blacked out for the overnight crossing, the windows dark, the overhead panels dimmed to a deep blue that was meant to suggest sleep and instead made everything look underwater. The man beside him slept with his mouth open, a faint whistle on each exhale, his head tilted toward Emeka's shoulder in a way Emeka had been deflecting for two hours. The engines droned — a steady, vibratory hum that came up through the seat frame and into the bones, the kind of sound that became inaudible after the first hour and then suddenly, when you noticed it again, deafening. The cabin smelled of recycled air and the faint chemical sweetness of the blankets they handed out on overnight flights, and beneath it the particular staleness of forty-three bodies in a sealed tube at altitude — which was, Emeka thought, exactly what the Meridian was, scaled up, and the thought sat in his chest like a cold stone.

He'd been staring at Car F for the better part of an hour. Car F did not add up. Emeka sat in the middle of the night with a blueprint of a lie on his screen.

He'd told the Architect he knew the train's layout from his old life. True. Also the smallest part of the truth. The truth was he'd left rail security because the layout had started asking him questions he couldn't answer without becoming a man he didn't want to be. Five years ago he'd been an inspector — a good one, thorough, the kind who read schematics with the slow patient suspicion of a man who knew every drawing was a confession if you read it closely. Then the schematics started asking the wrong kind of questions, and the answer to them was *be quiet*, and Emeka couldn't be quiet, so he left. It cost him the uniform, the pension, and the certainty that he was a man who did the right thing. The certainty was the heaviest loss.

Car F was the Vault Car. Sealed, biometric, Faraday-caged — the most over-engineered room on the most over-engineered train on earth. It held the Leviathan, the cradle, the sensors, the seals. That was what the blueprint said. The blueprint was lying.

Emeka had been an inspector long enough to read volume the way a doctor reads a pulse. Every car had a stated internal volume and a stated use, and the difference between the two was the car's honesty. An honest car used what it had. A dishonest car had space it didn't admit to. You found the dishonesty the way you found any lie: measure the outside, measure the inside, look at the gap. The gap was where the truth lived, and the truth was always bigger than the lie wanted you to believe.

Car F had a lie in it. The blueprint said nineteen cubic metres of cradle and corridor and sensor housing. The mathematics — external dimensions, wall thickness, maglev couplings, the service channel — said closer to twenty-six.

Seven cubic metres the blueprint didn't account for. A room the size of a large wardrobe, or a small cell, hidden inside the most secure car on the train, behind the most valuable jewel in the world, on no official diagram Emeka had ever found. He'd looked. For years. The way a man looks for a thing he's afraid to find — because the looking was safe, and the finding was not.

He zoomed in. Out. Rotated the schematic, measured the couplings, cross-referenced the service channel. The seven cubic metres stayed missing — with the patient stubbornness of a thing hidden on purpose, by professionals who'd hidden rooms before.

A jewel didn't need seven cubic metres. A jewel needed a cradle the size of a shoebox. Whatever sat in that space, it wasn't the Leviathan.

He thought about the disappearances. The same stories Sloane had read — an owner who vanished, a courier who walked off and was never seen, a seat found empty, a door that shouldn't have been open. He'd read them the way a security man reads them: not as ghost stories, but as incident reports with the incident removed. People didn't vanish. People were removed. And the cleanest place to remove a person, on a train that never stopped, was a room that didn't officially exist — because a room that didn't exist was a room no investigator would search, and a body in an unsearched room was a body that stayed missing, and a missing body was a file that closed itself.

He had not told the Architect about the seven cubic metres. Twice, in the briefing, the sentence had died in his throat — because the sentence was *I think there's a hidden compartment in the Vault Car and I think it's for people*, and you didn't say that over an encrypted line to a woman you respected unless you were certain. Emeka wasn't certain. He was only afraid. And afraid was not the same as certain. He'd been trained never to act on the one without the other.

His phone buzzed. A line from the Architect, routed clean: *Rival interest confirmed. Second team may be running tonight. Heads up.*

He read it twice. A second crew. Hitting the same train, the same night, for the same prize or a different one. A train with a hidden room and a cursed jewel was dangerous enough. A train with a hidden room, a cursed jewel, and a second crew was the kind of problem that ended in corridors that didn't officially exist, with two sets of professionals bumping into each other in the dark. He'd seen the after-action of crews that had crossed. Never clean.

He closed the blueprint and looked out at the dark water far below. In an hour he'd land and become someone else — a security inspector auditing the Meridian's protocols, the one cover aboard with real authority. He'd walk onto that train with the blueprint of a lie in his bag and a question in his chest he couldn't yet ask aloud.

Whatever was in those seven cubic metres, he was going to find it. He'd left one job because the blueprints started asking questions. He wasn't going to leave this one until he had an answer.

The plane banked toward Madrid. Below, the world was dark and moving, and somewhere in it a silver train carried a secret the size of a wardrobe toward a city at the end of a line — and Emeka Okafor could not, for the life of him, make the numbers add up.

Seven cubic metres. Seven runs. Seven years since a girl named Vance had disappeared on this train, and the papers had called it an accident, and the file had closed itself, and no one had thought to search the room that didn't exist.
