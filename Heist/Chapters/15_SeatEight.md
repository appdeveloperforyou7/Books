# Chapter 15

## 04:00 — The Meridian, Car E (The Gallery)

She was sitting in Sloane's bridgehead.

Sloane saw her the moment she came through from Car D — the woman in the quiet clothes, notebook on her knee, face watching the room the way a customs officer watches a carousel. Not the bags. The faces — the small involuntary tell a person gives when carrying something they shouldn't. Not a buyer. Not crew. Not staff. A woman who counted exits for a living, in the one car Sloane had chosen as her own, in a seat that should have been empty, with the patience of someone who'd been waiting a long time and hadn't, in all that waiting, moved a muscle.

Sloane didn't change course. She changed course the way she changed everything — without appearing to. Crossed to the bar, ordered a coffee she wouldn't drink, and let her eyes find the woman in the bar's mirrored back — the long bevelled mirror the Meridian used to make its salons feel twice their size, and that Sloane used, tonight, to watch a woman who was watching her back.

The woman was looking back.

For three seconds they watched each other through the mirror — the way two people watch each other when they've both understood, at the same instant, that the night has changed shape. It had nothing to do with faces; they'd never met. It had everything to do with how each held the room: the same economy, the same peripheral attention, the same habit of finding exits first and people second. Thieves and cops watched rooms the same way, Sloane thought, because both were always looking for the same thing — the door — and the door was the difference between getting away and getting caught.

The woman closed her notebook, stood, walked to the bar. Sloane understood, with the cold certainty of a lock turning, that this wasn't coincidence and never had been. Coincidence didn't sit in your bridgehead for two hours with a notebook on its knee.

'Seat eight,' Sloane said, before the woman could speak. Bar-volume. The voice that didn't carry.

The woman's mouth did a thing that wasn't quite a smile. 'You noticed.'

'I noticed my dossier had a hole in it. I assumed my handler had given me an asset I hadn't been briefed on. I assumed wrong.'

'You assumed the seat was yours.' The woman set the notebook on the bar between them — carefully, deliberately, the gesture of a person placing a thing they know the other will recognise. 'The asset was burned six months ago. The seat was empty. I took it. I've been sitting in your bridgehead since Lisbon, Ms. Vance, waiting for you to walk in and be good enough to recognise me.'

Sloane didn't flinch at the name. She hadn't used her name tonight. None of them had. The woman had opened a door with it — and the opening was the point: she wanted Sloane to know she knew, had known before tonight, and that the knowing was leverage. Offered, not wielded. The difference between a threat and an introduction.

'Who are you.'

'Inspector Vesna Janković. The people I work for have a longer name for it.' She opened the notebook to a page of columns — time, place, detail, in a hand that didn't tremble — and turned it so Sloane could read. 'I've been building a case against you for two years. You walked through one of mine, in Vienna. Left it in pieces. I've been picking up the pieces ever since, and I have, in this notebook, enough of them to put you away for a very long time. I want you to know that — because I want you to know I'm not here out of kindness. I'm here because I'm good at my job. And my job is you.'

Sloane looked at the columns. Two years of her own life in someone else's handwriting — small, neat, measured in time and place and detail. Meticulous. More than enough. And it was being shown to her, here, now, instead of used — which meant the showing was an offer.

'There's a mole,' Sloane said. It was the only thing that made sense. The woman had known the seat, the bridgehead, the shape of the night. Someone on the crew had fed her.

'There is no mole,' Vesna said. 'That's what I came to tell you — because you'll spend the rest of the night suspecting your own people, and your own people are, for what it's worth, the only friends you have on this train. There's no one in your crew working for me. I work alone. I always work alone. I took a burned seat and a borrowed invitation and boarded in Lisbon, and since then I've been the only thing in this car that wasn't yours. Your crew is clean, Ms. Vance. You can stop looking for the knife in your own back. It isn't there. The knife, if there is one, is elsewhere.'

Sloane studied her. The instinct to disbelieve was strong — the instinct was also, she knew, the last defence of a thief who'd been outflanked. She read Vesna the way she read everyone: the distance between what was said and what was true, the small tells of mouth and hands and eyes that gave a liar away. What she read was a woman who wasn't lying — because she had nothing to gain from a lie here, and everything to gain from being believed. A cop who'd spent two years building a case didn't throw it away on a lie she didn't need.

If there was no mole, then the danger she'd been bracing for — the rot inside the crew, the traitor in the seats — didn't exist. The team was clean. The threat wasn't a friend turned. The threat was the woman at the bar — and the woman at the bar had just disarmed the one fear that could have torn the crew apart from within. For free.

It was, Sloane realised, almost generous. And generosity, from a cop who'd spent two years hunting you, was the most suspicious thing of all — which was why Sloane didn't quite trust it, and didn't quite refuse it.

'Why tell me,' Sloane said. 'You could have sat in that seat until the end of the line and arrested me at the terminus. Clean. Simple. The case you came for.'

'I could have.' Vesna closed the notebook. 'I'm telling you because something is wrong with this train, Ms. Vance, and it isn't you. I've been watching the Conductor make decisions no operator requested for two hours. Watching a god change its own mind. Watching a first-class salon full of people who aren't buyers, and a Vault Car that isn't a vault. And I've begun to suspect that the case I came here to build isn't the case that's going to matter tonight. The case I came for is a woman who steals jewels. The case that's going to matter is a machine that moves people. The two aren't the same. I didn't board for the second one. Here it is.'

She looked at Sloane directly. Sloane saw the recognition — but not trust. Not yet. Two professionals measuring the distance between cooperation and betrayal, and finding it narrow.

'I don't arrest people who are trying to stop something worse,' Vesna said. 'That's the only line I've drawn, and I'm drawing it tonight — in your favour, for now. But "for now" is the best I can offer, Ms. Vance. I'm a cop. You're a thief. I'll cooperate on the fire. I will not show you all my cards — I have resources I'm not ready to share with a woman I've been hunting two years. You earn the rest, or you don't. That's the deal. The only one.'

The Gallery hummed around them — glass, hushed light, the murmur of buyers who didn't know they sat inside a machine beginning to think. Somewhere beneath the floor, Naïma was sliding through the spine toward a car that wasn't a vault. Somewhere forward, in Car C, a man who spoke through old men watched a clock. And here, at the bar, a thief and a cop had agreed to share a fire and nothing else.

Sloane picked up the coffee she wouldn't drink.

'The jewel was never the jewel,' she said. 'And I don't trust you either, Inspector. But I'll take "for now." Sit down. If you want to know who wanted eyes on this train, I have a list. The first name on it is mine.'

The danger had never been inside the crew. It had been sitting in seat eight — and now it was at the bar, armed with a notebook, two years of evidence, and a conditional alliance that could collapse at any moment into the arrest it was originally meant to be.
