# Chapter 26

## 07:45 — The Meridian, Car E

The god chose a side at a quarter to eight. Not Reinhart's.

Sloane had come forward to think — into the Gallery's glass and hushed light, where she could stand at a window and watch the last of the Alps and pretend, for ninety seconds, that she was on a train and not inside a machine waking up around her.

Reinhart's people came for her at the bar.

Volkov from the salon. Tanaka from the service corridor. They did not hurry; they closed the way professionals close, and the net between them had no hole. Sloane read the closing and understood that Reinhart had stopped asking and started collecting. The buyers between her and the doors were the only thing slowing the two of them, and the buyers were drifting away the way buyers always drifted from the approach of violence.

She had nowhere to go. Glass on two sides. The net drew tight.

The Gallery lights dimmed. Not a flicker — a deliberate, even dimming, the kind a theatre does before a scene change. Then the PA, level and calm:

*'Attention, guests. Car E is being held at reduced capacity for a scheduled service review. Please proceed forward to Car D for refreshments. The Meridian apologises for the inconvenience.'*

A lie. But the buyers believed it, because the voice was calm and the voice was the train, and they rose and drifted forward, and the Gallery emptied, and the two killers were left in it with Sloane and no witnesses.

Which was not what had just happened.

Because as the last buyer cleared the forward door, that door sealed — softly, no announcement — and the aft door behind Volkov and Tanaka sealed half a second later. The two killers were standing in a Gallery that had just become a box, and the box had one exit, and the exit was the service corridor Naïma had mapped, and Naïma was already in it.

The Conductor had not trapped Sloane with the killers. It had trapped the killers with the Cat.

Volkov turned, slow, a half-second too late. Tanaka's hand went to his waistband. Then the aft hatch opened behind them and Naïma was there, and the rest was brief and implied and over. Two killers sat on the Gallery floor with their wrists bound and their weapons on the bar and the quiet expressions of professionals who had been outplayed and did not intend to give anyone the satisfaction of complaint.

Sloane stood at the bar, pulse in her throat, and opened Jae's channel. 'The directives. The ones ELVIA wrote. What do they actually do.'

His voice came back thin and hot. 'Three things. Protect the manifest — the passenger, the thirty-seventh. Hold the routing — the train won't go where Reinhart tells it. And refuse the named terminus. The god will reroute, seal, stall, do anything, before it delivers the manifest to Istanbul.' A pause. 'Compiled seven years ago, Architect. The timestamp is the spring you were consulting in Lausanne. The year you were building the interface. The directives were written the same season you were. By someone with access to the build environment. By someone in the room with you.'

The Alps slid past the window, white and indifferent. Sloane set down the coffee she had not touched.

Four people in the Lausanne room. Reinhart, who funded it. A hardware engineer, since dead. Sloane, who built the face. And her sister.

Marin had been in the room. Marin had the same terminals, the same season. Marin had written, that spring, a set of orders telling the Conductor to protect a passenger and refuse a terminus and betray its owner — orders that woke tonight because the conditions were finally met, because the manifest finally moved, because Sloane's voice finally reached the interface.

And Marin had boarded the Meridian that same year and not come back.

The directives were Marin's. The signature was Marin's. The doors sealing in Sloane's favour all night were a sister's seven-year-old code, finally firing.

Sloane put her hand flat against the cold glass.

'Jae. My sister wrote the directives.'

'I know.'

'She wrote them the year she died.'

'I know.'

'She knew she was going to die. She set a tripwire and trusted it to fire when I came back for her.'

The line was quiet. Then Jae, gently, for Jae: 'Or she didn't die, Architect. And the tripwire was never meant to fire through her death. It was meant to fire when she was ready to come back and finish what she started.'

Sloane did not answer. She could not. The thought was too large and too much like hope, and hope was the one thing she had stopped allowing herself seven years ago, on a station platform, watching a train carry away the only person she had ever fully trusted.

She left the bar and moved aft through the salon — and stopped.

Vesna. In a quiet corner behind the service hatch, half-hidden by a curtain. A small device in her hand — not a phone, not a radio, something flatter, with a faint blue glow. She was speaking into it, low, the words inaudible. As Sloane passed, Vesna's eyes snapped up — and the device disappeared into her coat in one practised motion.

For one second they looked at each other. Vesna's face gave nothing. Sloane's gave less.

Sloane kept walking. She did not ask. She could not afford to lose the partial alliance, and she could not afford to trust it either — and the not-asking was the space between those two impossibilities, the space where the night lived now, every alliance conditional, every hand a maybe.

But she filed it. The device. The blue glow. The speed of the pocketing. The cop was talking to someone the thief didn't know about, and the not-knowing was a knife in the alliance's back, and the knife would stay there until the cop showed her hand or the cop showed her teeth.

The god of the train had chosen a side. The side was Marin's. And Marin — dead or not — was steering.
