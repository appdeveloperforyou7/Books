# Chapter 27

## 08:50 — The Meridian, Car C

Theodore Harlow had carried the ugly phone against his ribs for three weeks — three weeks of a betrayal waiting for a buyer — and tonight, at ten to nine, he sold it to the man it was meant to betray him to. The sale was the most loyal thing he'd ever done.

He sent the message himself. Powered on the ugly phone in the first-class salon of Car C and typed, with the careful warmth of a man accepting an offer he'd been pretending to refuse:

*I'm in. The ledger's with the Architect. They plan to extract at the Belgrade window — she's moved it up from Sofia, she's spooked, she thinks the Client's aboard. I can get it to you before they run. Name your price. I want out, and paid, tonight.*

End to end, a lie. Belgrade was a real window — the next one, ten-oh-five — and that was what made the lie land. A window that hadn't passed, a corridor Reinhart could reach, a story sharp enough that a sharp man would believe it: Reinhart would pull his people toward Belgrade to intercept a ledger that would never be there, because the crew weren't running to Belgrade. They were going to turn the train.

Eleven seconds. The reply: *Car C. Now. Come alone.*

Not "bring it." *Come.* Reinhart wanted to see him. In person. Face to face. Theo's stomach dropped three floors. A text was a performance with a curtain between you and the audience. In person was a performance without a net — and the audience was a man who read people the way Sloane read machines.

Theo straightened his cufflinks, checked his smile in the dark glass — three reflections smiled back, all convincing, none true — and walked forward through the salon toward the private alcove at the aft end of Car C where the buyers drank champagne and pretended they were here for jewels.

He was halfway across when he saw Reinhart.

Not the voice on a line. Not a name on a manifest. The man. Marcus Reinhart sat in a corner banquette, a glass of something amber untouched before him, reading nothing, watching everything. He was younger than Theo had imagined — mid-fifties, silver at the temples, a face that had been handsome and was now merely expensive, the kind of face that aged into authority the way some wine aged into price. He wore no watch, no ring, no visible mark of wealth; the wealth was in the stillness, the way a heavily armed man is quiet because he doesn't need to be loud. Two buyer-assassins flanked the alcove — Volkov, recovered from the Gallery, her eyes moving every nine seconds; Tanaka, courteous, the courtesy and the steel.

Reinhart looked up as Theo approached. The eyes were the thing. Grey-blue, steady, warmed by no feeling Theo could read — the eyes of a man who had looked at a great many people and decided what each was worth, and was now, with the same unhurried attention, deciding what Theo was worth.

'Lord — forgive me, I've forgotten the second half,' Reinhart said, and smiled, and the smile was perfect — warm enough to flatter, shallow enough to warn. He gestured to the seat across. 'Sit. You look terrible. Drink?'

'No.' Theo sat. The banquette was leather, cold through his coat. He could feel the ugly phone against his ribs like a coal.

'You're shaking.' Reinhart tilted his head — a bird watching a worm. 'Is it the job, or is it the decision? They feel the same, don't they. The adrenaline is identical. The body can't tell the difference between courage and treachery. I've always found that... poetic.'

'You wanted to see me,' Theo said. Keep it clipped. Keep it greedy. The greedy man is curt because the greedy man is frightened.

'I wanted to look at you.' Reinhart picked up his glass, swirled the amber, didn't drink. 'I've been watching your crew all night, Lord-whatever-your-name-is. Your hacker has been busy in Car B — running bridges on the medical bus, if I'm not mistaken, which I'm not. Your Cat has been trying doors she shouldn't, and the Conductor has been sealing them behind her, which is interesting, because the Conductor doesn't seal doors without a reason, and the reason is usually a person. Your Hitter has been reading blueprints of a car he has no authorised reason to audit. And your Architect — well. Your Architect has been in Car F, which is where the prize is, and which is where I told her to go, and which is where she is now doing the thing I hired her to do.' He set the glass down. 'I hired all of you because you're the best. I've been watching the best at work. It's been... instructive.'

Theo kept his face still. His whole career was in that stillness — twenty years of performances, a thousand lies, every one of them a wall between the truth and the eye. But this was different. This was lying to a man who lied for a living, who was looking at him with the same gap-reading eye Theo used himself, and the gap was closing, and the ugly phone was burning his ribs.

'The ledger,' Theo said. 'At Belgrade. I can hand it to your people at the window. Ninety seconds. She won't know it's gone until we're off.'

'I know.' Reinhart studied him for a long, unhurried moment — the cold reader reading the cold reader, two professionals at the same trade, separated by the table and by the fact that one of them was telling the truth and one of them wasn't, and both of them knew the other was doing the math. 'You're sure she suspects I'm aboard.'

'She's spooked. She thinks the Client — you — hired us to open a door you couldn't. She thinks the job isn't the job.' All true. That was the beauty of the best lies — they were built on true foundations. Sloane DID think all of that. Theo was only lying about what he intended to do about it.

Reinhart nodded. Slowly. And then — the thing that nearly broke Theo's composure — he leaned forward, conspiratorial, warm, the expensive carpet of his voice dropping to a murmur only Theo could hear:

'I'm going to tell you something, Lord-whatever, because you've made the right choice, and people who make the right choice deserve to know what they've chosen. The terminus is not Istanbul. There is no terminus for the cargo — not for the people who open my car and take my ledger. The end of this line is a private siding, and the siding is quiet. I tell you this not as a threat — you've made the right choice, the siding is not for you — but as a courtesy. A gesture of respect between professionals. You've earned the courtesy. Don't make me regret extending it.'

The words landed in Theo's chest like cold water. The siding. The quiet. The thing Sloane had suspected — Reinhart confirming it, casually, as a GIFT to a man he believed had just flipped. Theo held the greedy man's face — grateful, slightly afraid, greedy — and none of it was hard, because he WAS grateful (for the confirmation), and he WAS afraid (of this man), and the greed was the only part that was a lie, and it was the smallest part.

'Belgrade,' Reinhart said. 'My people will be at the N-car service hatch when the window opens. Bring the ledger. You'll be paid. You'll walk. And we'll never speak again.' He leaned back. The smile returned — warm, shallow, final. 'Go. Tell your Architect nothing. And Theo — ' the first name, deliberate, intimate, the cold reader's parting touch — 'I know about the phone in your pocket. Keep it. I gave you the number. It would be rude not to use it.'

Theo stood. His knees were steady — twenty years of performances had trained the knees even when the stomach was gone. He nodded, turned, and walked back through the salon without looking back, because looking back was the thing the guilty man did, and Theodore Harlow was not, tonight, the guilty man. He was the loyal man, playing the guilty man, for the only people whose good opinion he wanted.

He didn't breathe until he was two cars aft, in the service corridor, where Sloane was waiting.

'Well?' she said.

'He confirmed the siding.' Theo's voice was flat. 'The terminus is a kill-box. He told me to my face — as a "courtesy." He's been watching us all night. He knows about Jae in Car B, Naïma at the doors, Emeka with the blueprints. He's not passive. He's been tracking us move for move.'

Sloane's jaw tightened. 'Did he believe the feint?'

'He believes I flipped. He's too smart not to have doubts — but he can't afford to doubt, because the ledger is the one thing he can't lose, and the possibility that I'm lying is smaller than the possibility that I'm telling the truth and he misses the Belgrade window. His self-interest overrides his suspicion.' Theo paused. 'He's formidable, Sloane. He's the real thing. And he knows about the phone — he gave me the number, he expected me to use it, and he told me to keep it. The feint works because he thinks the flip is real, but he's watching now. We have until Belgrade before he realises the ledger isn't coming.'

'Belgrade is ninety minutes.' Sloane was already re-planning — the Architect reassembling the night around the hour and a half Theo had bought. 'He'll pull his people toward the N-car hatch for the Belgrade window. That thins his coverage aft. Buys us time at the compartment. But Theo — if he's tracking us the way you say, he'll notice when we don't go to Belgrade. The window closes the moment he realises.'

'Then we'd better be ready to turn the train before he does.'

She almost smiled. He'd remember the almost-smile longer than anything else about the night.

'Theo — that was the best performance of your life. And I'm including the dying man.'

'It was a lie. It's what I do.' He set his back against the corridor wall and let out the breath he'd been holding since the banquette. 'I just finally did it in the right direction.'

He told her everything — the offer, the three weeks, the ugly phone, the in-person meeting, Reinhart's eyes, the siding, the phone-in-the-pocket tell. All of it. Because he'd decided, somewhere between Car C and this corridor, that he was done carrying private wars inside the public one. The only way to be the real thing he wanted to be for her was to stop being the lie he'd always been for everyone else.

The ugly phone stayed warm in his pocket. Spent. The most honest object he owned.

He'd been the crack in the team. The crack always pointed the other way. And tonight, at ten to nine, the grifter sat across from the villain and lied to his face — and the lie held — and the hour it bought was going to matter more than any of them knew.
