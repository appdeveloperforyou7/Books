# Chapter 71

Director Shah's confiscated plates lasted forty-one minutes.

The standoff in the liaison office at two in the morning had been quiet, which was worse than if it had been loud. Shah had demanded the copper-plate impressions. Rathore had refused. Shah had cited national security. Rathore had cited the committee charter — the same charter Shah's own office had drafted, with the same paragraph seven that had been written to let the government classify anything it wanted. The irony was not lost on anyone. Rathore had, in the end, called not his superior in Delhi but the cabinet secretary, at three in the morning, on a line he would not have used for anything less than the end of his career, and said five sentences that Meera would not learn the content of for another six months.

Whatever those five sentences were, they worked. At 2:41 AM, Shah received a call on his secure phone, listened for ninety seconds without speaking, and left the room. He did not take the plates.

Meera watched him go. Her hands were steady. They had not been steady an hour ago, when the alignment beam had struck the Sri Yantra and the first copper plate had emerged from the wall slot with a sound like the earth clearing its throat. They were steady now because the worst thing had already happened and she had survived it, and the second-worst thing — Shah taking the record — had not.

Rathore closed the door behind Shah and leaned against it. His face was the color of wet concrete.

"What did you say to the cabinet secretary?"

"I told him the truth." Rathore's voice was hoarse. He had been shouting, in the passage, an hour ago, when the government's team had arrived thirty minutes early and he had put himself between them and the mechanism. "I told him that if the Indian government confiscated a record that had just been authenticated in front of an international committee, every one of those twelve committee members would walk out of Thiruvananthapuram tomorrow and hold a press conference in Geneva by Wednesday. I told him the 2008 Nizam's jewels case was a parking ticket next to what this would do to India's cultural-heritage credibility. I told him the algorithm was already photographed and distributed — which it isn't, but he doesn't know that — and that confiscating the plates would change nothing except the government's reputation." He paused. "I told him my father did not die at Kargil so that a conference room in South Block could decide what the world gets to know about its own history."

Meera stared at him. She had heard him say the Kargil line before, to her, in a guarded moment. She had not heard him say it to the cabinet secretary of India, at three in the morning, as leverage.

"That was either very brave or very stupid."

"It was both. They are not exclusive." He straightened. "The midnight committee session is in four hours. The publication vote. You should sleep."

"I can't sleep."

"Then you should pretend to sleep, because in four hours Rothwell is going to walk into that room and you are going to need to be sharper than he is."

Meera almost smiled. It was, she realized, the first time in three days she had come close. "Rothwell isn't the enemy tonight."

"Rothwell is always the enemy. He is simply sometimes the enemy who is currently voting your way." Rathore opened the door. "Sleep, Meera. The plates aren't going anywhere."

Left alone, Meera sat for a long time with the forty-seven copper plates spread on the table before her — the authenticated record, the inventory of the repository, the thing Ashoka's engineers had designed the Reveal configuration to produce. Forty-seven plates. Each one a bronze impression of a manuscript, a tablet, a folio. Each one proof.

She had done what her father could not. She had opened the door without breaking it.

She had not yet understood that the door had been a test, and that she had just passed it.

She went upstairs, lay down on the cot in her borrowed room with the rain drumming on the window, and closed her eyes. Sleep did not come. But for the first time in days, the want of it felt like something other than dread.

# Chapter 72

The committee convened at 6 AM in the administrative building, the monsoon hammering the windows, the twelve members taking their seats around the long table with the slow, careful movements of people who understood that what they did in the next hour would be studied for a long time.

Shah was not present. His seat — the government's security seat — was empty, which was its own kind of statement. Mrs. Kapoor, the Prime Minister's invisible hand, sat in his place, her face arranged in the expression Meera had come to think of as *diplomatic neutral*. She had, Meera would learn later, been on the phone with Delhi until five in the morning. The result of that phone call was the empty seat and the neutral expression and the single sheet of paper in front of her that she did not look at and did not need to.

Dr. Krishnan, chairing, called the session to order. The motion was on the table: full publication of the forty-seven authenticated plates to the committee's six member institutions, with a six-month embargo to allow for preliminary conservation assessment. Mueller had drafted it. Bennett had seconded it. The Vatican's Kurien — quiet, watchful, the man who had found the Portuguese document that had completed the puzzle — had signaled his support.

Meera, as secretary, sat at the side and took minutes. She watched the room.

Krishnan called the roll. India's two government loyalists voted no, as expected. Mueller, Bennett, Kurien, and the JNU Sanskritist voted yes. Tanaka — Rothwell's hidden piece, the woman no one but Meera and Rathore knew was Rothwell's dealer — voted yes, which surprised no one; Tanaka always voted in whatever direction preserved her access. That was four to two in favor, with five votes remaining: the conservation specialist, the External Affairs appointee, Rathore (in his security-liaison capacity), Menon — and Rothwell.

The conservation specialist voted yes. External Affairs voted no. Rathore, when his name was called, said, "Yes," in a voice that cost him something.

Six to three. One vote remaining.

Menon — the compromised director-general, the leak, the man whose consulting fees and quiet conferences with Tanaka Meera had documented and held without using — sat with his hands flat on the table. He had not spoken all morning. His eyes were on the copper plates at the room's center. He had spent his career in the ASI, a career built on the patient, unglamorous work of cataloguing the past, and the plates in front of him were the thing his entire career had been a footnote to. Meera watched him weigh it: the fees, the access, the comfortable retirement Rothwell's patronage represented, against the weight of forty-seven bronze plates that would outlast every government and every patron and every comfortable retirement on earth.

Menon voted yes.

Seven to three. The motion carried.

Krishnan called the final name anyway, because the record mattered. "Mr. Rothwell."

Rothwell, who had been sitting with his leather portfolio and his pale eyes and the stillness of a man who had calculated every outcome before entering the room, raised his hand.

"Yes," he said.

The room did not visibly react. Meera, watching from the side, saw Mrs. Kapoor's pen stop moving for one beat — the only acknowledgement, from the Prime Minister's hand, that the most powerful private collector in the heritage trade had just voted to give away the leverage he had spent the entire operation building.

Rothwell caught Meera's eye as the meeting broke. He did not smile. He gave her the faintest nod — the nod of a chess player who has just sacrificed a queen because he sees a mate three moves ahead that no one else in the room has found.

After the meeting, in the corridor, Meera fell into step beside him. "Why?"

"Because I will get more from being inside the committee than I would ever get from fighting it." He did not look at her. "And because, Ms. Desai, you and I are going to be doing this for a very long time, and there is no percentage in being the man who tried to bury knowledge that the entire committee had just authenticated. The world is watching. One does not profit from being the villain of a story everyone is reading."

"You're already the villain of this story."

He did look at her then, and his pale eyes held something she had not seen in them before — not warmth, Rothwell did not do warmth, but a kind of recognition, the look of a man who has met the person he will be measured against for the rest of his life.

"Not yet," he said. "But I might be. We'll see."

He walked away into the monsoon. Meera watched him go, and she understood, with the certainty that came from having watched him operate for three weeks, that Rothwell's concession was not a defeat. It was a repositioning. The man had not stopped. He had simply changed the game.

She went to find Rathore. There was one more thing she needed to do before she could let herself believe it was over, and she needed his help to do it.

She did not yet know that it was not over. She would learn.

# Chapter 73

Two days after the committee vote, Priya found it.

She had been working through the enhanced-Reveal copper plates — the forty-seven impressions the mechanism had produced — cross-referencing them against the repository's original manuscripts in the now-permanently-lit chamber beneath the temple. The work was painstaking. The light was good now, the air circulation improved by the ASI's climate system, and the repository had begun to feel less like a sealed vault and more like a library that was open for business. Scholars came and went in pairs. Priya was the only one who came alone and stayed all day.

She called Meera at four in the afternoon. Her voice had the particular flatness of someone who was holding something too large to hold.

"You need to come down here."

Meera was in the liaison office, drafting the committee's first quarterly report. She set down her pen. "What is it?"

"I can't — I can't explain it on the phone. Bring Rathore. Bring my father's notebook."

Twenty minutes later, the three of them stood in front of the repository's central pedestal, where the Ashoka Chronicle lay open to its final plate — the one Meera had read on the first night, Ashoka's instruction: *Let this knowledge sleep until a generation arises that seeks truth more than power.*

Priya had the enhanced-Reveal plate forty-seven in her hands. It was the last plate the mechanism had produced, the one pressed from the surface of the Chronicle's reverse — the side that had lain face-down on the pedestal for twenty-three centuries and that no one, until the Reveal configuration had lifted it, had ever seen.

"I didn't read this on the first night because I couldn't," Priya said. "The notation is Bakhshali-variant, but it's encrypted. It's a substitution cipher keyed to the Reveal sequence itself — the twelve rotation positions, in order, are the key. The plate can only be read by someone who has completed the Reveal. Which means Ashoka's engineers designed it to be read only by someone who chose sharing over hoarding or exploitation."

She set the plate down on the pedestal. Her hands were shaking. Meera looked at the plate. The Bakhshali-variant numerals, when read through the Reveal key, resolved into Sanskrit. Priya had already done the translation. She'd written it in her notebook in the cramped handwriting that Meera had come to know as well as her own.

Priya read it aloud. Her voice shook on the second sentence.

*"To the one who has chosen the Reveal: you have proven worthy. What you have opened is not the only repository. The engineers of Ashoka, by the king's command, built seven. This chamber is the first. It was the test. Only a generation that chose to share, rather than to take, would be entrusted with the locations of the others.*

*"The second repository lies beneath the mountain the Greeks called Kōphēn, where the Kushan kings later carved their Buddhas. The third lies beneath the lake the Dravidians call Pālai, now dry. The fourth beneath the temple the Tamils raised to the storm god. The fifth beneath the city the Persians burned. The sixth beneath the island the Romans called Taprobane. The seventh lies where the river that is also a goddess meets the sea that has no memory.*

*"Each chamber holds a portion of what Ashoka preserved. Together they hold the whole. They were separated so that no single conqueror could claim them all, and so that the knowledge could survive the fall of any one. They were keyed to the Reveal so that only those who sought to share could find them.*

*"You have begun. The others wait, as this one waited, for a generation worthy of what they hold."*

The repository was silent. The LED panels hummed. Somewhere far above them, the monsoon was hammering the temple, and the temple was holding, as it had held for centuries, the way the mountain and the lake and the island would also be holding, in places Meera had never been, holding chambers she had never imagined, holding knowledge that had been waiting twenty-three centuries for someone to choose sharing over taking.

Rathore spoke first. His voice was very quiet. "Seven."

"Seven," Priya said.

Meera did not speak. She was looking at the plate, and at the Sanskrit Priya had transcribed, and at the names of places she did not yet know — *Kōphēn, Pālai, Taprobane* — and she was thinking about Ashoka's engineers, who had built seven repositories across an empire and keyed them all to the same test, so that the knowledge would survive anything, anything at all, except the wrong hands, and that the only hands the knowledge wanted were the ones that would give it away.

She had passed. The first test, the test that had stood for twenty-three centuries, she had passed. And in passing it she had learned that the test had been only the beginning.

"There's one more thing," Priya said. She had gone pale. "The plate. It's not just an inscription. It's also a warning. The last line."

She turned to the final words of the translation.

*"But know this: the test cuts both ways. He who chose the Reveal may find the others. He who chose the Open, or the Seal, may not. Yet the test does not prevent them from trying. Guard the locations as the knowledge itself was guarded. For the same hunger that drives men to take will drive them to follow you to the others. And there will be those who, learning of the seven, will not rest until they hold all seven in their hands."*

The three of them stood in the repository, in the light, in the silence, and Meera understood — fully, finally, with the weight of twenty-three centuries pressing on her shoulders — what Ashoka had done. He had not hidden a treasure. He had set a quest. And the quest was not for the knowledge. The quest was to find six people, six generations, six keepers, who would choose to share what they found. The knowledge was the prize. The sharing was the point.

And the moment anyone learned there were seven, the race would begin.

Rathore's phone buzzed. He looked at it. His face, which had been the color of old stone, went the color of ash.

"Meera."

"What."

"The Geneva server. Tanaka's. It went dark three hours ago. The backup node in Zürich is dark too." He looked up. "Rothwell's people are scrubbing. All of it. Every transaction, every intercept, every file."

Meera felt the floor tilt, the way it had tilted in Oxford when the block-seven numbers didn't match.

"Why would they scrub now? The committee voted. He won."

Rathore's jaw worked. "He won the committee. He didn't win the algorithm. The algorithm is under committee oversight now. Restricted access. Five-year review. Rothwell voted for it himself." He paused. "Unless Rothwell already has a copy. Unless the scanning equipment Tanaka brought into the repository on the second descent — the equipment we never confirmed she used, because we were watching the wrong thing — already got what it needed. Unless the Geneva server is being scrubbed because the files have been moved somewhere we can't touch."

Meera stared at him.

"The algorithm isn't under committee oversight," she said slowly. "A *copy* of the algorithm is. The original is in this chamber. But if Tanaka scanned it—"

"Then Rothwell has it. And the server that held the proof is gone. And the seven repositories—"

She finished the thought for him. "He's heard about the seven. Maybe not the locations. But he's heard. And he's erasing his tracks because he's about to need them."

They looked at each other across the pedestal, across the copper plate that had just doubled the size of the world, across the warning that Ashoka's engineers had carved for exactly this moment.

Rathore said, very quietly: "It's not over."

Meera picked up the copper plate. Forty-seven. The last one. The one that had been lying face-down for twenty-three centuries, waiting for someone to choose sharing, and that had just told her there were six more chambers and a man in England who was already erasing the evidence of what he'd stolen and a quest that would take her to mountains and lakes and islands she had never seen.

"No," she said. "It's not over. It's just started."

She looked up at the Sri Yantra pattern carved into the chamber's ceiling — the same geometry her father had decoded, the same nine interlocking triangles, the same forty-three intersections — and she thought about the other ceilings, in the other chambers, in the places whose ancient names she was only beginning to learn. Six more. Six more ceilings. Six more mechanisms. Six more tests.

She turned to Priya. "I need you to translate the location names. All six. Tonight. Kōphēn, Pālai, Taprobane, the others. I need to know where they are before Rothwell does."

Priya was already reaching for her notebook. "I'll need the Bakhshali key and the Reveal sequence and—"

"You have them. You have everything. Go."

Priya went. Rathore stayed.

"Meera."

"I know."

"You can't do this alone. Six repositories. Rothwell. Whatever government decides it wants them. You can't—"

"I know." She looked at him. "I'm not going to do it alone. That's the point. Ashoka didn't build one chamber and one guardian. He built seven, because he knew one wasn't enough. One breaks. Seven hold." She paused. "I need a team. I need you. I need Priya. I need the committee, the real committee, the ones who voted yes because they meant it. And I need my father."

"Your father is in Pune."

"My father has been waiting thirty years for someone to ask him the right question." She picked up the plate. "The right question isn't about this chamber. It's about the other six. And he's the only person alive who has spent thirty years thinking about what Ashoka's engineers built."

Rathore was quiet for a moment. Then: "There's a flight to Pune at six in the morning."

"I know. I booked it an hour ago. I booked yours too. To Delhi. You need to be in RAW when this breaks, not in Kerala."

"And if Rothwell moves first?"

Meera looked at the copper plate in her hands. Forty-seven. The first of seven. The test she had passed, and the warning that had come with it, and the quest that was now hers whether she had asked for it or not.

"Then we move faster."

She walked out of the repository, up the ancient staircase, into the monsoon morning, with the copper plate in her cloth bag and the names of six places she had never been pressing against her ribs like a second heartbeat.

It was not over. It would not be over for a very long time. And somewhere in the rain, in a hotel in Thiruvananthapuram, Edmund Rothwell was on a secure line to London, and Reinhardt Kessler was already packing, and the race for the seven repositories had begun.

# Chapter 74

Pune. Three days later.

Her father was waiting at the door of the flat, and the flat was no longer the wreckage the prologue had left — the bookshelves had been rebuilt, the Brahmagupta reproduction re-framed, the desk restored to its place by the window. Only the small things were different: a new lock on the back door, a chain, and a small camera Ravi did not know Meera had had installed from Arlington by a contractor who owed her a favor. The Krishna family's kitchen door, through which he had fled thirteen days and a lifetime ago, now had a deadbolt.

He had not asked about the camera or the deadbolt. He understood why they were there. He had understood, in the way of a man who had carried a secret for thirty years, that the secret's release would not make him safer. It would make him a target of a different kind.

Ravi Desai stood in the doorway and looked at his daughter, and Meera looked at her father, and for a long moment neither of them spoke. He was thinner than she remembered. The tremor in his left hand was visible even at the threshold. But his eyes — the eyes that had seen the Sri Yantra's proportions for what they were, in 1996, in a temple in Kerala, when he was forty-one and the world had not yet asked him to keep a secret that would outlive his marriage and nearly outlive his relationship with his daughter — his eyes were clear.

"You found it," he said.

"I found it."

"You found all of it."

Meera reached into her cloth bag and drew out the copper plate. Forty-seven. She held it out to him.

He did not take it. He looked at it, and his face went through a sequence of expressions that Meera, who had spent twenty years learning not to read him, finally let herself read: recognition, then wonder, then a grief so old it had become geological, then — at the last, in the lines around his eyes — something that was not quite joy but was the closest thing his face could make to it.

"The reverse plate," he whispered. "I never saw the reverse. I never knew there was a reverse."

"There was." Meera's voice was steady. She had rehearsed this conversation on the plane and abandoned the rehearsal somewhere over the Arabian Sea, because the conversation she had rehearsed was not the one she needed to have. "The Reveal configuration lifted it. The reverse contains an inscription that can only be read using the Reveal sequence as a key. Father —" she stopped. Started again. "Papa. There are seven."

He did not understand. She watched him not understand, and she watched the understanding arrive, and she watched it arrive the way she had watched the alignment beam strike the Sri Yantra — slowly, then all at once, the light finding every line.

"Seven," he said.

"Seven repositories. Ashoka's engineers built seven. This one — Padmanabhaswamy — was the first. It was the test. Only a generation that chose to share would be shown the locations of the others." She held the plate out again. This time he took it. His trembling hands held it the way they had once held equations — carefully, reverently, as if the thing might break or might break him. "The plate names them. Six more. Kōphēn. Pālai. Taprobane. Places I've never been. Places no one has looked, because no one knew."

Ravi read the inscription. He read it standing in the doorway of his flat in Pune, in the morning light, with the tremor in his hands and the camera Meera had installed watching from the eaves and the sound of Bhandarkar Road waking up around them. He read it, and Meera saw his lips move over the ancient names, and she saw him understand, finally, completely, what he had spent thirty years touching the edges of without ever grasping the whole.

"I knew there was more," he said. His voice broke on *more*. "I knew, when I saw the Sri Yantra, that one chamber was not — that a civilization does not preserve everything it knows in one place. That is not how preservation works. You build redundancy. You build seven. I could not prove it. I did not have the Reveal. I could not read the reverse." He looked up at her, and his eyes were wet, and Meera realized she had never, in her adult life, seen her father cry. "You read it. You read what I could not."

"I had your notebook."

"You had your own eyes." He set the plate down on the desk — the desk he had swept the notebook off, thirteen days ago, in the dark, while two men broke down his back door. "Meera. The others. Rothwell will—"

"I know. His network scrubbed its Geneva server the night of the committee vote. He has something. Maybe the algorithm. Maybe just the knowledge that there are seven. Either way he's moving." She sat down across from him, in the chair she had sat in as a child, watching him fill pages with numbers she did not understand. "I need your help. Not the notebook. You. Thirty years of thinking about Ashoka's engineers. You know things about the geometry, the notation, the design philosophy, that aren't written down anywhere. I need that. The committee needs that. The seven—"

"The seven need a guardian each," Ravi said. "That is what Ashoka intended. Not one guardian for seven chambers. Seven guardians. One for each. A braid, not a thread."

Meera looked at her father. He was sixty-seven, and his hands shook, and the secret he had carried had cost him his wife's trust and his daughter's childhood and nearly his life. And he was sitting up straighter than she had seen him sit in years, and his eyes were the eyes of the forty-one-year-old who had walked into a temple in Kerala and found a code, and she understood — with the clarity that comes once in a life, if it comes at all — that the thirty years of silence had not been wasted. They had been preparation. For this. For the moment his daughter walked through his door with a copper plate and six names and a quest, and he was, finally, the right man in the right place.

"Papa."

"Yes."

"Finish the proof. The one in the back of the notebook. The theorem you couldn't close." She pulled the notebook from her bag and set it on the desk between them. "I worked on it on the plane. I got most of the way. But the last step is yours. It's always been yours. And I think—" she stopped, because this was the part she had not rehearsed, the part that mattered, the part she had been afraid of for twenty years. "I think the proof isn't just mathematics. I think it's the key to the others. The Reveal sequence for Padmanabhaswamy was derived from the Sri Yantra here. The other chambers will have their own geometries, their own Reveal sequences. The theorem — the relationship between the geometry and the Reveal — is the master key. Finish it, and we can open the others. Finish it, and Rothwell can't."

Ravi looked at the notebook. He looked at his daughter. He looked at his hands, trembling in his lap, the hands that had once written the most beautiful Brahmi numerals in Pune and now could barely hold a pen.

"You finish it," he said. "Your hands are steady. Mine are not. But I will tell you what to write." He reached across the desk and took her hand — the hand that had traced the Brahmi on the Ashoka Pillar at Sarnath when she was eight, the hand that had rotated the twelfth stone block into position in a passage beneath a temple in Kerala, the hand that was, now, the steadiest thing in the room. "You see patterns the way I see numbers. Differently, but just as clearly. I have been waiting thirty years for someone to tell me that and mean it."

They worked through the night. The window faced the blank wall it had always faced. The desk lamp burned. The Brahmagupta reproduction watched from its frame. Father and daughter, mathematician and pattern-seer, talked the theorem into being — his voice low and precise, her pen moving across the notebook's final page, the proof assembling itself line by line the way the copper plates had assembled themselves in the chamber, pressed out of the past by the weight of the present.

At four in the morning, Meera wrote the final line. She set down the pen. They looked at it together — the theorem complete, the master key, the relationship between sacred geometry and the Reveal that would let them open the other six without the years of trial that had preceded this one.

Beneath the proof, in her own hand, she wrote: *Completed by Meera Desai, with gratitude to her father, who saw the numbers in the stones.*

Ravi read it. His trembling hand found hers again.

"I should have said it every day," he said. "I'm proud of you, Mai. I should have said it every day since you were eight."

Meera did not say *I forgive you*. She did not need to. She had finished his theorem. That was her forgiveness, in the only language they had ever truly shared.

Outside, the Pune dawn came up red over Bhandarkar Road. The camera Meera had installed blinked its small red eye. Somewhere, a flight was being loaded for Thiruvananthapuram. Somewhere in England, a man was studying a fragment of sandstone. Somewhere beneath six places whose ancient names Meera was only beginning to learn, six chambers waited in the dark, the way this one had waited, for a generation that would choose to share.

Meera closed the notebook. She had a plane to catch. She had a quest to begin. She had a father to come back to, and a team to build, and six ceilings to look up at, and a villain to outrun.

She was ready.

# Chapter 75

Six months later.

Arlington, Virginia. November. The first cold rain of the season on the window of the apartment that smelled, still, faintly of stale coffee, but that now also smelled of sandalwood — the small block Kesavan sent from Thrissur every month, in his brief precise letters that always ended *the knowledge protects itself*.

Meera sat at her desk with the priest's log on one side, the committee's quarterly report on the other, and the framed photograph from Sarnath on the shelf above — the photograph whose back now bore, in her own hand, six ancient names and the modern coordinates Priya had translated them to.

*Kōphēn* — Kabul. Beneath the Bamiyan valley, where the Buddhas had stood.
*Pālai* — a dry lakebed in Tamil Nadu, unremarked.
*Taprobane* — Sri Lanka. A temple the Sinhalese kings had built over a cave.
And the others. Each one a place. Each one a chamber. Each one a test.

The committee had published the first batch of translations. Mueller's Heidelberg team had the mathematical manuscripts. Bennett's consortium had the medical texts. Priya's Indus paper was in review at *Nature* — the proto-Dravidian hybrid, the woman engineer's voice speaking across forty-five centuries. The algorithm was under restricted review, five-year embargo, a committee of cryptographers in four countries studying the defensive applications before anyone studied the offensive ones. The copper-plate impressions were in five institutions on three continents. The knowledge was moving.

And the knowledge about the knowledge — the seven, the master theorem, the locations — was known to four people. Meera. Ravi. Rathore. Priya. Held the way the guardianship had always been held: not by an institution but by a choice, repeated, in each generation, by people who decided that what they knew was more important than what they wanted.

Rathore called at seven, Delhi time. His voice was tired and tight. He had built, in six months, the cultural-property crime division he had wanted — twelve officers, a mandate, a budget, and three open cases, all of them connected, in ways only he could see, to the network Rothwell had spent thirty years building.

"The Kabul field office flagged something," he said. "A survey team near Bamiyan. Private funding. The paperwork traces to a foundation in Lichtenstein."

"Rothwell."

"Rothwell. He's moving on Kōphēn."

Meera looked at the photograph on her shelf. At the six names on its back. At the proof in her father's notebook — the master key, the theorem that would let them open the others, the thing Rothwell did not have because Rothwell had chosen the Open and would never be able to read the reverse plate.

"We're not ready," she said.

"No."

"He's going to get there first."

"Maybe. Maybe not. Bamiyan is not Kerala. The geometry will be different. The Reveal sequence will be different. Without the master theorem—"

"He has the algorithm. He has money. He has Kessler. He doesn't need the Reveal if he's willing to blast his way in."

Rathore was quiet. Then: "Meera. The failsafe. If Rothwell tries to force the Bamiyan chamber—"

"I know." She did know. The same engineers who had built the Reveal had built the Seal into every chamber. Force the door and the door sealed forever. Rothwell could destroy the second repository trying to open it. "How long do we have?"

"His survey team is three weeks from the site. If they're fast."

"Then I need to be there in two."

There was a pause. She heard him breathe. She had grown, in six months, to know the cadences of his breathing the way she knew her father's pen on paper — when he was thinking, when he had decided, when he was about to say something that would cost him.

"I'll have the Kabul station chief meet you," he said. "Officially you're there as an FBI liaison on a stolen-artifacts case. Unofficially—"

"Unofficially I'm the eighty-second guardian of a chain that started twenty-three centuries ago, and I'm about to walk into a mountain in Afghanistan to stop a billionaire from blowing up the second of seven chambers full of the oldest knowledge on earth."

"That about covers it."

Meera almost laughed. It was, she realized, absurd. It was also, she realized, exactly what she had been trained for — not the FBI training, not the art-crime expertise, not the Brahmi literacy, but the older training, the one from the kitchen table in Mumbai, the one from the photograph at Sarnath, the one from a father who had taught her to read the numbers in the stones because he had understood, even then, that the numbers were a door and that the door was meant to be opened by someone who would share what was behind it.

She closed the quarterly report. She opened the drawer of her nightstand and took out the cloth bag — the same cloth bag, the one that had held her father's notebook across the Arabian Sea, that had held the copper plate forty-seven from Thiruvananthapuram to Pune to Arlington. The bag was beginning to wear soft at the corners, the way the notebook had. She put the notebook inside it, and the plate, and the photograph, and the six names.

"Book the flight," she said. "And tell Priya. She'll want to come."

"Priya is already in Kabul. She flew yesterday. She didn't tell you?"

Meera closed her eyes. Of course Priya had flown yesterday. Priya, who had wept over the Indus translation, who had read the reverse plate, who understood — as only someone who had spent her career learning to read the dead could understand — that the six chambers were not a burden but an invitation, and that the invitation was not to her alone.

"Tell her I'm coming," Meera said. "Tell her to wait for me. Tell her—"

She stopped. Outside, the November rain was coming down on Arlington, the way the monsoon came down on Thiruvananthapuram, the way the rain came down on Pune and on the dry lakebed in Tamil Nadu and on the mountain in Afghanistan where the second chamber waited. Rain on the world that held, beneath its skin, seven gardens of knowledge, planted twenty-three centuries ago by engineers who had understood that the hardest thing was not to hide the truth but to share it.

"Tell her the guardianship has only started."

She hung up. She picked up the cloth bag. She looked one more time at the photograph on the shelf — her father's hand pointing at the Ashoka Pillar, her eight-year-old face squinting up at the inscription, the morning light on the sandstone — and she thought about the other photographs she did not yet have. The mountain in Bamiyan. The dry lake. The island. The six places, and the six tests, and the six gardens waiting for their gardeners.

Then she turned off the lamp, picked up her bag, and walked out into the rain, toward the airport, toward Kabul, toward the second of seven, toward the rest of her life.

The fight for heritage never ended. The guardians didn't stop. There were seven gardens in the world, and only one had a gardener, and the other six were waiting in the dark.

Meera Desai was going to find them.

*The first repository was open. Six waited. Somewhere in England, a man held a fragment of sandstone and planned his next move. Somewhere beneath a mountain in Afghanistan, the second chamber slept, as it had slept since the year 264 before Christ, dreaming of the generation that would prove itself worthy of what it held.*

*And somewhere, always, the knowledge protected itself — patient, ancient, and ready, the way it had been ready for twenty-three centuries, for the ones who would choose to share it.*
