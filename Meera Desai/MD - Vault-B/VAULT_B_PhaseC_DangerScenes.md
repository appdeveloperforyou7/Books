### WITH this:

Her phone buzzed. A text from Rathore: *Motion sensors. North drainage channel. Meet me there. Now.*

Not *will update*. Not a briefing. A summons, at twelve minutes past midnight, in the monsoon.

Meera was out of the hotel room in ninety seconds — trousers, shirt, the ankle boots she could run in, the cloth bag with the notebook locked in the room safe first. She did not call for an Embassy car. The temple was three kilometers. She ran.

The monsoon hit her at the door. Warm rain, horizontal, the kind that did not fall so much as inhabit the air. She ran the service road behind the hotel, turned north on the main avenue, and cut through the temple quarter's narrow lanes where the streetlamps had given up against the wet and the dark was total. Her glasses fogged. She pulled them off and ran half-blind, the pavement slick under her boots, her breath loud in her own ears.

She smelled the temple before she saw it — wet granite, lamp oil, jasmine from the garlands the late devotees had left at the gate. She came in through the east gate, the one the devotees used, and a guard in plain clothes caught her arm.

"Agent Desai? Joint Secretary Rathore said to bring you. This way. Quietly."

He led her along the inner face of the north wall, past the godown where the ASI stored their equipment, to a service yard she had not seen before. A drainage grate — iron, square, three feet on a side — had been pulled up and set against the wall. A portable LED work-light stood at the mouth of the shaft below, throwing a hard white cone into the dark. Two of Rathore's men were in the channel, ankle-deep in black water, sweeping the beam of their torches along the stone.

Rathore was at the grate's edge, on his phone. He looked up when she came in and his face did something she had not seen it do: it went flat with a controlled anger that was worse than any expression.

"Two sets of prints," he said. "One set is Mehra's size — we have his boot profile from the Geneva file. The other set is larger. Much larger. A man who weighs, I would estimate, a hundred and ninety pounds. Size thirteen. Western boot tread. Not ASI. Not Mehra."

Meera's art-crime instinct — the thing she had spent nine years training — went cold and still. A second man. Larger. Western.

"What did they do down there?"

"Look."

He handed her a clear evidence bag. Inside: a crowbar, a portable cordless drill, a laminated card. She crouched under the work-light and studied the card through the plastic.

It was a map. Hand-drawn, in ink, on plastic-laminated paper — the kind of waterproof field document a professional would make. The temple's underground passages, rendered from above. The known vaults were marked correctly. The anomaly area — the void beneath Vault B — was marked too, with a small rectangle and a question mark. The map was wrong in three places: a passage drawn where there was none, a junction misplaced by ten meters, and the entrance between Vaults B and D drawn as if it were inside Vault B itself.

Wrong, but close. Close enough that the man who had drawn it knew the rough geometry and was refining it on the ground.

"Papa," she said, before she could stop herself.

Rathore heard. His eyes went to her face and stayed. He did not ask. He filed it.

"They came in through the north channel at eleven-forty," Rathore said. "The motion sensors caught them. My team was here in six minutes. They were gone in four. They knew the response window. They were timed to it." He paused. "They left the tools on purpose. The map too. They wanted us to know how close they are."

Meera looked at the map in the bag. At the rectangle. At the question mark.

"This isn't reconnaissance," she said. "This is a calling card. They're telling us they can map us faster than we can change the locks."

She straightened. And that was when she saw him.

Not at the grate. Not in the channel. Across the service yard, in the dark gap between the godown wall and the temple's north colonnade — a shape. Tall. Standing still in the rain the way a man stands still when he has been taught that stillness is harder to see than motion. The rain was coming down through the colonnade's gaps and he was in it and he was not moving and he was watching her.

She saw him for perhaps two seconds before he turned and was gone — not running, walking, with the unhurried economy she would learn to recognize — into the dark of the colonnade and then around the corner and then nothing, only the rain and the work-light and the two men in the channel sweeping their torches.

She did not shout. She did not point. She stood very still and filed the shape of him — the height, the stillness, the pale blur that might have been hair or might have been rain — in the place where an FBI agent keeps the things she will need to identify a man later.

"Rathore."

He was already looking at her. He had seen her go still.

"There was someone in the colonnade. Tall. Western. He was watching."

Rathore spoke rapidly into his phone. Within thirty seconds four of his men were moving into the colonnade. They found nothing. Of course they found nothing. The man had been timed to the response window, the same way the drainage probe had been timed to the response window. He had wanted her to see him. The seeing was the point.

Meera stood at the grate's edge in the rain and understood, with the cold clarity that came after the adrenaline, that the man across the yard had not been a lookout. He had been a message. *I am here. I am in your temple. I am watching you watch me. And I am not afraid of you.*

She did not go back to the notebook when she returned to the hotel. She could not. She sat on the edge of the bed with the lights off and the rain on the window and the shape of the tall man in the colonnade burned into the back of her eyes, and she understood — for the first time since she had arrived in Kerala — that the people who wanted what was beneath this temple were not going to wait for her to finish decoding it.

They were going to take it. And the man in the colonnade was the one who was going to do the taking.

Her phone buzzed. Rathore: *Patrols doubled. Drainage sealed. North colonnade swept, nothing. Whoever he is, he's gone. But Meera — they're inside our perimeter. They've been inside it. We are not ahead of them.*

She stared at the message. Then she opened the room safe, took out the notebook, and went back to page one.

If they were going to take it, she was going to have to open it first.

### WITH this:

She left before he could argue. In the corridor, she passed a young man in an ASI uniform who nodded at her — polite, unremarkable. She nodded back and kept walking.

She made it thirty feet before the thing that had been bothering her about the young man resolved into a single clear fact: his boots. ASI field staff wore sandals or soft-soled shoes in the temple complex. The young man had been wearing boots. Western boots. Size thirteen.

Meera did not turn around. She kept walking, her pace unchanged, her hand steady on the cloth bag's strap. At the end of the corridor she turned left, toward the operations center, as if heading back to work. She passed the operations center. She passed the stairwell. She pushed through the fire exit at the end of the hall and found herself in the service lane behind the liaison building, the same lane the Embassy sedans used, wet from the afternoon rain, empty.

She flattened herself against the wall beside the fire door and counted to fifteen.

The fire door opened, slowly. A hand came through first — checking the frame, the way a professional checks a frame — and then the young man in the ASI uniform stepped into the lane. He was alone. He stopped two paces past the door, looking the wrong way (down the lane, away from her), and she had a half-second to see him clearly: the ASI tunic hung wrong on him, the shoulders too broad, the fabric straining; the boots; the earpiece and the wire running down his neck into his collar, which no ASI field staffer on the planet wore.

Then he saw her reflection in the puddle at his feet. He turned.

Meera was already moving. She went through the fire door, slammed it, and ran.

Three flights of stairs. She took them in bounds, one hand on the rail, the cloth bag slamming her hip. Behind her, below her, the fire door banged open. He was fast. She had known he would be fast; she had seen the economy of him in the corridor. But she had grown up in Mumbai, and she had been running in the monsoon since she was nine, and the liaison building was a building she had memorized in her first two days because memorizing your exits was the thing you did when you were an FBI agent who had read enough after-action reports to know that the people who died were the ones who had not memorized their exits.

Second floor. The monitoring suite. She burst through its door and into a room full of CCTV screens and two startled RAW analysts.

"Lock this door," she said. "Now. Someone is coming up the east stairwell. Do not open it for anyone except Joint Secretary Rathore or me. Call Rathore. Tell him I am in the monitoring suite and there is a hostile in the building."

The analysts — both young, both desk-trained, neither of them prepared for a breathing FBI agent to crash their room with this specific sentence — stared at her for one beat too long. She pulled her FBI credentials from her pocket and held them up.

"Now."

The older of the two moved. The door locked. The younger one was already on the phone.

Meera stood with her back to the CCTV wall and her hand on her hip where her service weapon would have been if she had been allowed to carry one in India, which she was not, which meant her hip was empty, which meant that if the man in the stairwell reached this door before Rathore's team did, she was going to have to stop him with a chair and whatever was in reach.

The door did not open. She heard footsteps in the stairwell — fast, then slowing, then stopping. Then nothing. She watched the CCTV feed from the east stairwell: empty. By the time she thought to check the lobby feed, the man was already crossing the lobby in his ASI tunic, unhurried, nodding to the guard at the desk, walking out the front door into the afternoon.

She did not catch him. She had not expected to. What she had done was confirm what she had suspected since the colonnade three nights ago: the man in the tall boots was not a lookout and not a thief. He was an operator. He was inside the liaison building. He had followed her to Oxford's flight line. And he was, almost certainly, the same man whose boot prints Rathore's team had found in the drainage channel.

She stood in the monitoring suite with the two RAW analysts and the humming screens and the rain coming down outside, and she understood that the soft phase — the phase in which the people who wanted the knowledge were content to watch and wait and map — was already over, whether anyone had announced it or not.

When Rathore arrived, eight minutes later, with four armed officers and a face like thunder, she told him. He listened. He did not interrupt.

"We need to accelerate," she said. "Whatever the timeline was, it just halved."

Rathore looked at the lobby feed, frozen now on the empty street outside. "His face. We have his face."

"No," Meera said. "We have the face of an ASI tunic and a man who knew where every camera in this building was. We have nothing. And he knows we have nothing. That's why he walked out the front door."

She left Rathore in the monitoring suite and went to pack for Oxford. The flight was in four hours. She would not miss it. But she understood, as she had not understood that morning, that going to Oxford was no longer a research trip. It was a race. And the man in the ASI tunic was already running it.

The mechanism completed its first cycle. The standard Reveal — the controlled opening, the documented access, the fourteen-minute window. The wall slid inward. The cold air rushed out.

But the enhanced Reveal wasn't finished. Deep inside the repository, something was happening — a grinding, a pressing, the unmistakable noise of metal being worked by ancient machinery. The copper-plate impression system, triggered by the three additional block rotations, was producing the authenticated record.

And then, from above, from the passage junction where the priest's tunnel met the vault corridor: voices.

Meera's head snapped up. Rathore was already moving. He had heard it too — footsteps, more than one set, moving fast, the hard sound of regulation boots on stone.

"They're early." Rathore's voice was flat. "Thirty minutes early."

He drew his weapon — the one he wasn't supposed to be carrying, the one his father had carried before him — and put himself between Meera and the junction.

The first operative came around the corner at a jog. Then a second. Then a third. They were not Shah's delegation staff. They were RAW's tactical unit, the one Meera had seen drilling at the perimeter her first week, in black kit and with the focused economy of men who had been told there was a job and not told what the job was. Behind them, in the corridor, in a suit that had no business being underground, Director Shah.

"Stand down, Rathore," Shah called. His voice echoed strangely in the stone. "The mechanism is to be secured. Agent Desai is to step away. Now."

Rathore did not stand down. He shifted his weight, settled his stance, and aimed his weapon at the junction in a way that was not theatrical but was absolutely clear.

"Director Shah. The mechanism is mid-cycle. The astronomical alignment is in progress. If your men enter the chamber now, they will disrupt the Reveal configuration and trigger the failsafe. The passage collapses. The repository seals forever. That is not a guess. That is engineering."

Shah stopped. The three tactical officers stopped behind him. In the flashlight beams, Meera could see the calculation moving behind Shah's eyes — the same calculation she had watched move behind every bureaucrat's eyes in this operation: *how much can I take, and what will it cost.*

"You have no authority to—"

"I have the authority of the man standing between you and a chamber that will collapse if you take one more step." Rathore's voice did not rise. "The alignment completes at 6:47. It is now 6:39. You will wait eight minutes, Director, or you will be the man who destroyed the greatest archaeological find in human history by being thirty minutes early. Choose."

Eight minutes. Meera turned back to the mechanism. The grinding from inside the chamber was still going — the plates, being pressed. She could not stop it. She could not speed it. All she could do was stand in front of it and pray the eight minutes held.

The third tactical officer — the one in the rear, the biggest of the three — shifted his weight. It was a small movement. Meera, who had been watching all three of them the way a woman watches three men with guns when she has none, saw it. She opened her mouth.

"Rathore—"

The officer moved. He was fast — the same economy she had learned to recognize, the boot-tread economy, the one that did not waste a motion. He came off the junction wall like something released and went straight for the mechanism, straight for the chamber mouth, straight for the wall slot where the first copper plate was even now beginning to emerge.

Rathore hit him.

It was not a clean takedown. It was the collision of two large men in a narrow stone passage, and it made a sound like a door slamming. They went into the wall together. Stone dust came down. The officer was younger and heavier and he had been trained to take a man apart in a corridor and he was very good at it. Rathore was forty-four with a bad knee and he had not been in a fistfight since Kashmir and he was better.

Meera did not see the second and third officers move because she was already moving herself. She grabbed Priya — who had been frozen at the chamber mouth, notebook clutched to her chest — and shoved her bodily into the repository, behind the pedestal, out of the line of the passage. "Stay down. Do not move."

She came back out with the lever they had used on the stone blocks — three feet of bronze, older than Rome, weighing what a small child weighs. The second officer was in the passage, weapon up, and Meera understood in the half-second she had that he was going to fire and that the only thing between his weapon and the mechanism was her.

She did not think about it. She threw the bronze lever. It was not a good throw. It was the throw of a woman who had not thrown anything heavier than a softball since the Quantico gym, and it was the only throw she had. The lever caught the officer's raised arm, not his head, and the deflection was enough — his shot went into the ceiling, a flat crack that filled the passage and kept filling it, and a chunk of stone the size of a fist came down three feet from where Meera stood.

The third officer had Rathore. He had gotten behind him in the scramble and had his arm around Rathore's throat and Rathore was going down, slowly, the way a man goes down when the blood to his brain has been cut, and his weapon was skittering across the stone floor.

Meera dove for the weapon. Her hand closed on it. She came up on one knee, the grip wrong for her hand — too large, configured for Rathore's grip — and she aimed it at the third officer from six feet.

"Off. Him. Now."

The officer looked at her. He looked at the weapon. He was a professional. He understood that an FBI agent with a service weapon she did not know how to shoot was more dangerous than one who did, because the agent who did not know might pull the trigger by accident, and the bullet would go somewhere, and in a stone passage the somewhere might be him.

He let Rathore go. Rathore went to his knees, his hand at his throat, his breath a rasp that filled the passage.

The second officer was on the ground, holding his arm where the bronze lever had caught it. The first — the one Rathore had put into the wall — was sitting against the stones with the stunned expression of a man whose concussions were arriving separately.

In the sudden, ringing silence, Meera became aware of the grinding. It was still going. The plates were still emerging. The mechanism had not stopped. It did not care about the men or the gun or the blood. It cared about the alignment, and the alignment was completing, and at 6:47 PM, on the second, the beam of the setting sun reached the Sri Yantra through the aperture above and the Brahmi numerals glowed and the mechanism's luminescence peaked and the last copper plate slid from the slot and came to rest, warm, on the stone.

The grinding stopped. The luminescence faded. The wall began to close.

Meera lowered the weapon. Her hands were shaking. She had not known they were shaking.

"Rathore."

He was still on his knees. He looked up. There was blood on his face — from his nose, from a cut above his eye, she could not tell. He was breathing. He was breathing and that was what mattered.

"It's done," she said.

She looked at Shah, who had not moved from the junction, who had watched his three officers get taken apart by a woman with a bronze lever and a man with a bad knee, and whose face in the flashlight beam had gone the particular gray of a man recalculating the cost of everything.

"The plates," she said. "Are the authenticated record of the repository. Forty-seven items. Stamped with the astronomical alignment any astronomer can verify. Witnessed by three people. You can have the chamber, Director. You can classify the chamber. You cannot have these."

She picked up the cloth bag and began, methodically, to load the plates.

Behind her, the wall sealed. The repository closed. The mechanism slept.

Rathore got to his feet. He leaned on the wall. He was going to have a headache for a week and a scar on his eyebrow to match the one he already had, and he was going to be reassigned or worse, and he did not care.

"Let's go," he said. "Before they send more."
