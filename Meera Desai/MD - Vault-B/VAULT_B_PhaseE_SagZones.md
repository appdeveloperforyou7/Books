### INSERT into the Pune sequence (during current Ch 23, the all-night equation-solving session), after the current passage where they begin working through the night:

They were three hours into the work — Meera at her father's desk, Ravi pacing behind her, his voice low and precise as he talked her through the projective geometry of the Sri Yantra — when the new deadbolt on the back door stopped them both.

Not a sound. The absence of one. Ravi had stopped pacing. Meera, mid-equation, looked up. Her father was standing very still, his head turned toward the kitchen, toward the Krishna family's door, toward the service lane.

"Papa."

"Be quiet." His voice was not afraid. It was the voice of a man who had rehearsed this twice and now, on the third time, did not have to think about what came next. He crossed the study in three steps, pulled the desk lamp's plug from the wall, and the room went dark.

Meera was already moving. Not toward the door — toward the window. The window faced the blank wall it had always faced. She edged the curtain a centimeter and looked down at the service lane.

A man. Standing in the lane beneath the Krishna family's kitchen window, looking up at Ravi's study. He was not the tall one — not Kessler; she knew Kessler's shape now the way she knew her own hands. This one was smaller, Indian, in a dark shirt, and he was on a phone, speaking low, his free hand gesturing toward the window she was hiding behind.

She let the curtain fall. "There's someone in the lane. On a phone. Pointing at your window."

"Yes." Ravi's voice, in the dark, was terribly calm. "They came to the door this afternoon while you were at the airport. A man asking if Professor Desai still lived here. Mrs. Krishna sent him away. She is a good woman." A pause. "They are confirming I am here. In twenty minutes, or thirty, someone will come."

Meera's mind went cold and clear — the art-crime mind, the one that had taken apart the Subhash Kapoor network, the one that did not panic because panic was a luxury she had never been able to afford. Twenty minutes. Maybe thirty. Ravi's flat was on the third floor. The service lane was the only quiet exit. The front stairs opened onto Bhandarkar Road, which at 3 AM would be empty but watched.

"Your phone," she said.

"On the desk."

"Is it clean?"

"I do not know what clean means."

"It means is anyone listening."

"Ah. Then no. It is not clean. It has not been clean since I mailed the notebook."

She took his phone. She dialed the only number in India she trusted — Rathore's burn phone, the one he had given her for emergencies, the one that was not connected to his name or his office or his official life.

He answered on the first ring. She spoke for twelve seconds. Location. Number of hostiles. The deadbolt. The twenty-minute window.

Rathore said: "The Pune station chief is twenty minutes from you. I am calling him now. Do not engage. Do not open the door. If they come before the chief arrives, get your father out through the Krishna kitchen and the back stairs. The same way he went before."

"The same way he went before" — meaning the night of the prologue. Meaning the route Ravi had timed at seven minutes, eight with the tremor. Meaning the route that had worked once, in the dark, against two men, and that might not work a second time against men who had now confirmed the target's location.

Meera set down the phone. In the dark, her father's hand found her wrist. His fingers — the trembling ones, the ones that could no longer hold a pen steady — closed around it with a strength that surprised her.

"The equation, Mai."

"Papa—"

"The equation. We are three hours from solving it. You heard what I said about the projective geometry. You see it. I can hear you seeing it. If they take me tonight — and they may, we both know they may — you need to finish it. You need to be able to open the chamber without me."

"I'm not leaving you."

"You are not leaving me. You are finishing the equation while we wait for the cavalry, because the equation is the only thing that matters and because—" his voice broke, just once, in the dark, and she felt it in her wrist where his fingers held her, "—because if you can finish it in the next twenty minutes, then everything I did for thirty years was worth it, and if you cannot, then it was not, and either way we will know."

Meera pulled the notebook toward her in the dark. She could not see the equations. She did not need to. She had been staring at them for three hours, and her father's voice had been talking her through them, and the projective geometry — the thing he had missed for thirty years, the thing his Euclidean eyes could not see — was now as clear in her mind as the Brahmi on the Ashoka Pillar.

She picked up her pen. She wrote in the dark, by feel, the way her father had taught her to write Brahmi when she was six — the muscle memory older than sight.

Ravi talked. Softly, steadily, his hand on her wrist, his voice naming the angles and the projections and the relationships between the flat Sri Yantra and the three-dimensional form it described. Meera wrote. The pen moved. The equation — the one that had circled itself for thirty years without closing — began, in the dark, in the room where they might both be taken in twenty minutes, to close.

Outside, in the service lane, the man on the phone finished his call and did not leave.

Inside, in the dark, a father and a daughter finished the work that a civilization had buried beneath a temple twenty-three centuries ago.

At 3:47 AM, Meera wrote the final line. She set down the pen. She could not read what she had written. She did not need to. She knew.

"Papa."

"Yes."

"It's done."

Ravi's hand tightened on her wrist once, and released. In the dark she heard him breathe — the long, shaking breath of a man who had been holding something for thirty years and had just, in a dark room in Pune with men gathering in the lane below, set it down.

Then the knocking started. At the front door. Hard. Professional. Three men, she thought, from the rhythm. Maybe four.

Meera stood. She took the notebook. She took her father's arm. They went, together, through the kitchen, past the door to the Krishna family's apartment (dark now, the good woman asleep, the smell of the 2 AM cooking lingering), and down the back stairs into the service lane — the lane where, twenty minutes ago, the man on the phone had been standing.

The lane was empty. The man had gone to the front door with the others. The back stairs, the route Ravi had timed twice, had held for a third time.

They came out on Bhandarkar Road. The Pune station chief's car — unmarked, driven by a woman with the focused face of someone who did this for a living — was pulling up to the curb as they reached it. Forty seconds. They were in the back seat. The car moved.

Ravi, in the dark of the back seat, held his daughter's hand and said nothing. The notebook, with the completed equation written in the dark by a woman who could not see what she was writing, lay between them.

Behind them, in the flat, four men broke down the deadbolt Ravi had installed and found an empty study and a lamp that had been unplugged and a desk where, twenty minutes earlier, the most important mathematical breakthrough of the century had been completed by candlelight in the dark.

They found nothing else. The notebook was already on its way to the airport, in the back seat of a RAW vehicle, in the hands of the eighty-second guardian, who was leaving for Thiruvananthapuram at dawn.

## ZONE 2 — THE DELEGATION/BRIEFING SEQUENCE (Ch 32–34)

### The fix: dramatize Menon's exposure as a confrontation, not paperwork.

Currently (Ch 32–34), Menon's status as the leak is established through Rathore's research — consulting fees, conference attendance, an email — all delivered as a briefing. The Deepseek review flagged this: *"the government conspiracy is too neat."* The fix: convert the reveal into an on-page confrontation where Meera, alone with Menon in the repository, catches him in the act of transmitting.

### INSERT/REPLACE into the delegation sequence (current Ch 33, during the delegation's descent into the repository), replace the current passage where Meera watches Menon's "calculating gaze" with this dramatized scene:

Meera had been assigned to monitor the delegation's descent from the passage junction — not allowed inside the chamber (her consultancy was concluded), but allowed to watch. She stood at the junction with Rathore's second, a young officer named Iyer, and watched on a handheld monitor as the delegation moved through the lit repository.

She watched the RAW director examine the manuscripts. She watched Mrs. Kapoor's face go carefully blank. She watched Dr. Krishnan, the ASI lead, weep quietly at the Ashoka Chronicle. She watched Father Kurien cross himself again.

She watched Dr. Menon, director-general of the ASI, the man whose name had been on every official document authorizing this operation, stop at the metallurgical tablets shelf and, with the practiced casualness of a man who had done this before, slip a small device from his sleeve and hold it against the face of a tablet for three seconds.

A scanner. High-resolution. Pocket-sized. The kind of device that did not exist in any ASI inventory because the ASI did not use them, because the ASI catalogued with cameras and notebooks and the slow patient work of scholarship, not with pocket scanners made in a Geneva lab.

Meera did not move. She did not speak. She watched Menon move to the next tablet and scan it, and the next, and the next — four tablets in ninety seconds, each one held for exactly the duration required to capture the surface at the resolution that would let a forger reproduce it. She watched him pocket the device and rejoin the delegation with the unhurried composure of a man who had been doing this, she now understood, for years.

"Iyer," she said, without turning her head.

"Ma'am."

"Did your monitor catch the director-general at the metallurgical shelf?"

Iyer, who was twenty-six and had been trained by Rathore and was therefore paying attention, nodded once. "Full video. Time-stamped."

"Send it to Joint Secretary Rathore's secure server. Now. Encrypted."

Iyer sent it.

Meera stood at the junction and watched the delegation complete its tour. She watched Menon smile at Dr. Krishnan. She watched him shake Father Kurien's hand. She watched him, at the chamber's exit, pause and look back at the repository with an expression she recognized — she had seen it on the faces of every trafficker she had ever put in federal prison. The expression of a man inventorying what he had not yet stolen.

She waited until the delegation had ascended. She waited until Menon was alone in the passage, walking toward the junction, toward her, the smile still on his face.

She stepped out in front of him.

"Dr. Menon."

He stopped. The smile did not waver. It was a good smile — warm, avuncular, the smile of a senior academic greeting a junior colleague. Meera had seen that smile in a hundred interrogation rooms.

"Agent Desai. I understood your consultancy had concluded."

"It has. I have one professional courtesy to extend before I go." She held up her phone. On the screen, paused: a frame of Dr. Menon, director-general of the Archaeological Survey of India, holding a pocket scanner to the face of a 2,300-year-old metallurgical tablet. "I'm going to give you one opportunity to tell me how many you've scanned, who you've sent them to, and how long this has been going on. And then I'm going to give the recording to Joint Secretary Rathore, who is, at this moment, in his office, waiting for it."

The smile left Menon's face. It did not leave quickly. It drained, the way color drains from a face — slowly, unevenly, leaving the mask of the man beneath.

"Agent Desai, I don't know what you think you—"

"I think you've been selling access to the repository to Edmund Rothwell for at least three years. I think the consulting fees from the Rothwell Centre were not for consulting. I think the conferences at Rothwell House were not academic. And I think the email you sent two months ago — before this operation began — referencing 'the Kerala matter' was not a reference to a holiday." She lowered the phone. "I also think you're going to tell me everything, right now, in this passage, because the alternative is that this recording goes to the press instead of to Rathore, and the director-general of the ASI does not survive that."

Menon's face had gone gray. He looked, for the first time since Meera had met him, like a man who understood that the game was over — not the larger game, not the one Rothwell was playing, but his small, comfortable, well-compensated piece of it.

He talked.

He talked for eleven minutes, in the stone passage, while Iyer's monitor recorded every word. He talked about the fees and the conferences and Tanaka and the email and the moment, three years ago, when his gambling debts had become unsustainable and the offer from the Rothwell Centre had arrived like an answer to a prayer he had not known he was sending. He talked about the scanning. About the manuscript fragments. About the tablet coordinates he had texted to a Geneva number the night Meera had opened the chamber.

He talked, and Meera listened, and when he was done she said, very quietly: "You are going to continue going to work tomorrow. You are going to attend the committee meetings. You are going to smile at everyone you have always smiled at. You are not going to contact Rothwell, Tanaka, or anyone in that network. Because if you do, this recording releases, and your career — such as it is — ends on the evening news."

She turned and walked up the passage toward the light, leaving Menon standing in the dark, and she understood — as she had not understood when she had been a junior agent, as she had learned in the years of taking apart men like Menon one by one — that the way to defeat a conspiracy was not to expose it. It was to turn it.
