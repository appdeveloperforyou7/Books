# Prologue

Pune, India — thirteen hours before

They came for the notebook at 2:14 in the morning.

Professor Ravi Desai heard them first as a sound that did not belong — not the rickshaw horns of Bhandarkar Road, not the street dogs, not the 2 AM train three streets away that he had stopped hearing years ago. A click. Metal on metal. At the back door of the flat, the one that opened onto the service lane.

He was sixty-seven years old, and his hands had begun to betray him in ways he could no longer hide. The tremor had started in the left ring finger eighteen months ago and spread, finger by finger, the way a stain spreads through paper. His neurologist had a name for it. Ravi had not yet told his daughter.

But his hands remembered the motions of a life lived in a body that had once been reliable, and they were already moving before his mind had finished deciding. The desk lamp. Off. The notebook — the leather one, R.D. tooled in faded gold — swept off the desk and into the cloth bag that hung on the chair. The cloth bag had lived on that chair for twenty-six years, against exactly this night. He had rehearsed this. He had never believed it would come.

The back door opened.

There were two of them. The first was young, Indian, moving with the trained economy of someone paid to move fast and silently through places he was not meant to be. The second was not Indian, and he did not move silently. He moved the way large predatory animals move — with an economy that was its own announcement. Ravi could not see his face in the dark, but he could see the shape of him in the ambient glow of the service-lane lamp: very tall, very still, fair hair cropped to nothing, a scar that pulled the skin white across one cheekbone. He stood in the doorway and let the young one work.

The young one had a flashlight. He played it across the study — the bookshelves to the ceiling, the framed Brahmagupta reproduction, the desk by the window facing its blank wall. The beam found the desk. Found it empty.

"Professor," the young one said, in Hindi. His voice was almost gentle. "We only want to talk."

Ravi was in the bedroom corridor, the cloth bag against his chest, his bare feet silent on the marble floor that his wife had chosen twenty-eight years ago and that he had never changed after she died because changing it would have meant admitting she was gone. He did not breathe.

He had known, the way a man who has carried a secret for thirty years knows, that this night was coming. He had known since the Supreme Court order was published in the papers six weeks ago. The order had cracked the seal on something the whole world wanted, and the people who wanted it most were not the ones who would knock on the front door.

The service lane. There was a way out through the service lane — through the Krishna household's kitchen, down the back stairs, out onto Bhandarkar Road, and three hundred meters east to the all-night post office on Prabhat Road. He had walked it in the dark twice, timing himself, in the weeks since the court order. Seven minutes. Eight with the tremor.

He was at the corridor's end when the flashlight beam swung into the bedroom behind him.

"There."

Ravi ran.

It was not running, really. It was the lurching, graceless forward motion of a sixty-seven-year-old man whose legs remembered running and could no longer quite do it. He took the Krishna family's kitchen door in two stumbling steps, said nothing to the startled woman at the stove — it was 2 AM, he had no idea why she was cooking, he would think about that later — and took the back stairs down into the service lane.

The tall one was faster than Ravi had expected. He heard the man's feet on the stairs above him, unhurried, a man who knew he would win any race that mattered and was not concerned about the ones that didn't. The young one was somewhere behind, on a phone, speaking in low clipped Hindi that Ravi could not make out.

Bhandarkar Road was empty and wet. It had rained in the evening and the asphalt shone under the sodium lamps like the skin of something alive. Ravi turned east. His left knee, the one he had damaged on the temple steps in '98, buckled and caught and buckled again. The cloth bag hit his hip with every step. The notebook inside was the only thing in the world that mattered, and it was the only thing in the world that he could not let them have.

Prabhat Road. The post office. It sat behind a low gate, a single bulb burning over the night counter, the clerk half-asleep under a calendar of Ganesh. Ravi had prepaid the package three days ago — the brown paper, the string, the international air-mail labels, all of it ready in his desk drawer for exactly this. All he had lacked was the notebook itself, because he had not been able to let go of it, because letting go of it was the thing he had been unable to do for thirty years.

He wrote the address in the shaking hand that had once written the most beautiful Brahmi numerals in Pune. Meera. Arlington, Virginia. The address he had written on a hundred birthday cards and academic reprints and the silent letters he had never sent.

The clerk took the package, weighed it, dropped it into the night sack. "Tomorrow's collection, saheb. Six AM."

"That's fine." Ravi's voice did not sound like his own. "That's fine."

He stepped back out onto Prabhat Road. The tall man was standing across the street, under a neem tree, watching him. He made no move to approach. The young one was nowhere to be seen — on the phone still, Ravi guessed, reporting.

The tall man raised one hand, slowly, and pointed at Ravi. Then he turned the hand and pointed at himself. The gesture needed no translation. *I know who you are. I know where she is. And I will be there.*

Ravi walked home. The flat was destroyed — bookshelves emptied, the Brahmagupta reproduction smashed on the floor, the desk drawers pulled out and upended, the floor a snowstorm of paper. They had been thorough. They had been looking for one thing, and they had not found it.

He sat on the floor among the wreckage of his study, his hands trembling too badly to stop, and he thought about his daughter, nine thousand miles away in a city he had never visited, sleeping in a life he had never been part of. He thought about the word he had written on the back of a photograph twenty-six years ago, the word he had whispered to her when she was small enough to ride his shoulders through the monsoon. *Padmanabha.* He thought about the package crossing the world toward her, slower than the men who were already coming.

He picked up the desk phone from where it had fallen, dialed the international operator, and asked for the number of the Federal Bureau of Investigation, New York Field Office. Then he set the phone down again. Meera was FBI. If he called them, they would ask questions he could not answer without explaining things he had sworn, on his word, in a temple in Kerala in 1996, never to explain.

Instead he sat in the wreckage and watched the dawn come up red over Pune, and he listened to the city wake around him, and he understood — with the calm clarity that sometimes comes to people in the hour before their life changes forever — that the thirty years of silence were over, and that the thing he had been most afraid of was no longer that someone would find the knowledge.

It was that someone would find his daughter first.

# Chapter

The American woman landed at 14:47, four minutes early, and the man watching the arrivals hall noted it the way he noted everything — without expression, without moving, the way a man who has done this work on three continents notes the small deviations from a schedule that other people call luck and he calls intelligence.

Reinhardt Kessler did not like India. The heat was a physical insult. The crowds defeated the geometry his mind required. And the temple — the great stone thing they had driven him past on the way in from the airport, its tower crawling with carved figures — offended him in a way he could not have explained to anyone who had not grown up, as he had, in a house where the only permitted images were the cross and the family photograph on the mantel. He was not a religious man. He had stopped being one in a farmhouse outside Lubango in 2011, in a room where he had learned what men were. But the disciplines of a Calvinist childhood did not leave a man. They only went quiet.

He was forty-one. Six-four. A hundred and ninety pounds, maintained with the same joyless precision he applied to everything else. The scar across his right cheekbone had been a gift from a man in Kinshasa who had been quicker than expected; the man was no longer quick, or anything else. Kessler's hair was the pale almost-white of lowland Dutch stock, cropped to stubble. His eyes were the pale blue that does not warm in any light. He wore a cheap linen shirt and cheaper trousers bought in Mumbai two days ago, and he carried nothing that could not be abandoned in three seconds, because he had learned, on the second job he had ever done, that the difference between a professional and a corpse was the willingness to leave everything behind.

The woman came through the gate with a single carry-on and a checked bag. She was smaller than her file photograph had suggested — compact, dark-haired, reading glasses pushed up on her forehead in a way that made her look like a distracted graduate student. She moved through the immigration queue with the slight impatience of someone who has done this a hundred times. The immigration officer studied her FBI credentials too long. Kessler noted that the woman's jaw tightened — a micro-expression, gone in a half-second, but there. She did not like being examined. She liked being the one who examined. Kessler filed it.

Meera Desai. FBI Art Crime Team. The file Mr. Rothwell's people had assembled in Geneva was two hundred pages and contradicted itself in three places, which meant it was honest. She had been born in Mumbai. She had been educated in America since the age of fifteen. She read the ancient script — Brahmi — which her father had taught her. She had not spoken to her father in four months. She did not know that her father had mailed her a package seventeen hours ago, or that the package was, at this moment, somewhere over the Arabian Sea in the hold of a cargo aircraft.

She did not know her father's flat had been turned inside out, or that the men who had done it were now in this city.

Kessler followed her at the professional distance — sixty meters in the terminal, a hundred in the parking structure, never in her mirror. The Embassy sedan was waiting where the file said it would be. The driver was RAW-supplied, which meant competent and surveilled, which meant Kessler would not go near the car or the driver. He had his own vehicle — a rented white sedan so nondescript it was its own camouflage — and he followed the Embassy car at four vehicles' distance through the monsoon green of the airport road toward Thiruvananthapuram.

His phone buzzed. Sanjay Mehra.

"She's here."

"I know. I'm behind her."

A pause. Mehra did not like being told things Kessler assumed he already knew. Mehra was a former ASI man, clever, well-connected, and he made the mistake of thinking his local knowledge outweighed Kessler's professional one. "Mr. Rothwell wants the approach kept soft for now. Surveillance only. No contact."

"I heard him the first time."

"Kessler." Mehra's voice hardened. "Soft."

Kessler did not answer. He had been hired for the soft phase. He understood the soft phase. But he also understood, as Mehra perhaps did not, that soft phases ended, and that when they ended the people who had hired you expected you to be the reason they ended cleanly. Mr. Rothwell was a man who paid very well for the soft phase and paid better for the one that came after. The professor in Pune had gotten away with the notebook. Kessler had let him go, because Mr. Rothwell had said let him go — let him send it, let the daughter receive it, let the Americans do the work of decoding what the old man's hands could no longer hold.

But the daughter was here now. And the notebook was in the air behind her. And somewhere beneath the great carved tower of the temple that offended his eye, there was a door that the old man's notebook was going to open.

Kessler's phone buzzed again. He ignored it. He watched the Embassy sedan's brake lights flare red in the rain three cars ahead, and he let the distance between them settle to the professional four, and he followed the American woman into the city that was holding its breath.

She did not check her mirror once.

That would change.

# VILLAIN POV CHAPTER 2 — THE FRAGMENT

# Chapter

Edmund Rothwell III held the fragment the way he had held it every morning for thirty-two years — between the thumb and forefinger of his left hand, the oils of his skin forbidden to touch the carved face, the edges resting on a square of acid-free linen on the corner of his desk in the east wing of Alderbury Park.

It was the size of a playing card. Pale sandstone, the color of bone. On its face, in a script most scholars could not read and the few who could dismissed as decorative: a sequence of Brahmi numerals, and beneath them, in a hand older than the temple it described, a single line that Dr. Yuki Tanaka had translated for him in 2003, in a reading room in Geneva, with the particular care of a woman who understood she was handing a man the rest of his life.

*"Beneath the great temple of the serpent lord, the engineers hid the chamber of all knowledge. He who can read the numbers may open it."*

His father had bought the fragment in 1952, in a bazaar in Madras, from a dealer who did not know what it was. Edmund Edward Rothwell II had been twenty-six, a junior diplomat at the tail end of the Raj's afterglow, already married to the inheritance, already married to the certainty that the Rothwells were a family meant to own important things. He had paid eight pounds for it. He had spent the next forty-two years trying to understand what he had bought.

He died in 1994, in the master bedroom at Alderbury, the fragment on the table beside him, his son Edmund holding the hand that had once held it. The cancer had taken eleven months. In the last week, when the morphine made the walls soft and the dying man's words came loose from their meanings, he had said, to no one and to the room: *I wasn't clever enough. You'll have to be.*

Edmund had been twenty-four. He had not wept at the funeral, because Rothwells did not, and he had not wept since, because he had discovered in the year of his father's dying that grief was a thing one could choose to convert. He had converted it. Into the Foundation. Into the collection. Into thirty years of methodical, patient, escalating acquisition that had made the Rothwell name the most quietly powerful one in the private heritage trade.

And now, in the east wing of Alderbury Park, with the rain coming down on the Cotswolds the way it had been coming down for six hundred years, Edmund Rothwell read the morning's intelligence packet and understood that his life's work was about to resolve.

The Indian Supreme Court had ordered Vault B opened. That alone was the earthquake. But the aftershock — the one his network had spent six weeks confirming — was the thing that mattered. Ground-penetrating radar had detected voids beneath the known vaults. Rectangular voids. The ASI was calling them natural formations. Edmund Rothwell knew, with the certainty of a man who had stared at a fragment of sandstone every morning for thirty-two years, that they were not.

His phone was on the desk beside the fragment. He picked it up and dialed the Geneva number from memory.

Yuki Tanaka answered on the second ring. She did not say hello. She had not said hello to him in nineteen years.

"The radar confirms it," she said.

"I've read the packet."

"The Indian government will seal it the moment they understand what they have. You have a window, Edmund. It is not large."

"How large."

"Weeks. Perhaps less. The Prime Minister's office has already classified the radar findings at cabinet level. The moment the vault opens and the chamber is confirmed, the repository becomes a state secret."

Rothwell set down the fragment, on its linen, in its place. He looked out the window at the rain on the park. His father had looked out this same window at this same rain and seen, in the last year of his life, only the thing he had failed to solve.

"I am not going to let them seal it," Rothwell said.

"No."

"I want copies. Everything. Every manuscript, every plate, every folio. The originals can stay in India — I do not need the originals, I have never needed the originals, the originals are a liability. I need the knowledge. Documented, preserved, and held in enough copies that no government and no court and no priest can ever bury it again."

"There is a complication."

Rothwell waited. Tanaka did not make him wait long.

"The Americans are sending someone. An FBI art-crime specialist. She reads the Brahmi script. Her father is a mathematician in Pune." A pause. "His name is Ravi Desai."

Rothwell's hand, reaching for the fragment, stopped.

He did not know the name. But his father had. He remembered, suddenly and completely, a conversation in the library at Alderbury in 1987, when he was seventeen and his father was fifty-seven and the cancer was still seven years away. His father had been reading a mathematics journal, and he had looked up with the startled expression he wore when the world surprised him, and he had said: *There is a man in India who understands. A professor. He published a paper on the Sri Yantra that no one will read. But I read it. And he is right.*

*What did he get right, Father?*

His father had turned the journal to face him. *The geometry. The proportions. He proved that the Sri Yantra at Padmanabhaswamy is not decoration. It is a code.* And then, in a voice his son would remember thirty-seven years later in a rain-soaked study in the Cotswolds: *He is the one who will find it. Not me. Him.*

Rothwell had forgotten. Or chosen not to remember. He had spent thirty years assuming his father's obsessions had died with his father.

"Find out everything about Desai," he said. "Today."

"It is done. I sent the file to your secure server an hour ago. There is one more thing."

"Tell me."

"Desai has a daughter. The FBI agent. She reads Brahmi because he taught her. She is thirty-four. She is unmarried. She has not spoken to her father in four months." Tanaka's voice did not change. "She is the one they are sending to the temple, Edmund. The professor's daughter. The one he taught to read the numbers."

Rothwell stood at the window and watched the rain come down on the six-hundred-year-old park, and he thought about his own father's hand going cold in his, and the words *you'll have to be*, and the thirty years he had spent being clever enough.

"Kessler is already in country," he said.

"I know."

"Tell him the soft phase is over." Rothwell paused. He looked at the fragment on its square of linen — the Brahmi numerals, the line about the chamber of all knowledge, the eight pounds his father had paid in a Madras bazaar in 1952. "Tell him I want the notebook. The professor has a notebook. Thirty years of work. I want it before the daughter can use it."

"Edmund." Tanaka's voice, for the first time, carried a weight that was not professional. "The professor is an old man."

"I am aware of what he is. I am not asking Kessler to hurt him. I am asking Kessler to take what he has. If the professor gives it willingly, no one is hurt. If he does not—" Rothwell stopped. He did not enjoy this part. He had never enjoyed it. He had simply decided, at twenty-four, in a dying man's bedroom, that he would do whatever the work required. "Kessler knows the difference."

"And the daughter?"

Rothwell looked at the fragment one more time. *He is the one who will find it. Not me. Him.* But the professor was old, and old men's hands shook, and the work had passed — the way all work passes, the way his father's work had passed to him — to a new generation. To a woman in an FBI windbreaker who read the script his father had spent forty years failing to read.

"The daughter," Rothwell said, "is the key. Watch her. Let her do the work. When she opens the door, we will be standing behind her."

He hung up. He placed the fragment back in its case, locked the case, and returned it to the wall safe behind the Gainsborough. Then he poured himself the single whisky he allowed himself before noon, and he stood at the window and drank it, and he thought about his father, and the rain, and the chamber that his father had not lived to see opened, and the daughter of the man his father had said would be the one to find it.

The soft phase was over. Edmund Rothwell had waited thirty-two years. He would not wait much longer.

# VILLAIN POV CHAPTER 3 — THE ORDER

# Chapter

The rain had not stopped for three days. Reinhardt Kessler did not mind rain — he had worked the eastern Congo in the wet season, where the rain came down like a punishment — but the Kerala rain had a quality that burrowed. It found the gaps. It got into the cheap rental sedan and stayed there, fogging the windows, fogging his thoughts, making the city a place without edges.

He had been watching the American woman for five days.

She was better than her file. The file had described an art-crime specialist — academic, procedural, a woman who worked cases from desks and courtrooms. The woman Kessler had been watching moved through the temple and the liaison office with something the file had not mentioned: instinct. She noticed things. She checked corners. Twice she had looked up at a CCTV camera in a way that suggested she was thinking about where the camera did not reach. On the third day she had walked the temple's outer wall at dusk, and Kessler, watching from a tea stall across the street, had seen her stop at the drainage grate on the north side and study it for ninety seconds. She had not been told about the drainage grate. She had found it herself.

The professor's daughter. The one who read the numbers.

His phone buzzed. Mehra. Kessler let it ring twice before answering, because Mehra needed, periodically, to be reminded that he did not work for Mehra.

"The old man sent the notebook," Mehra said. "Geneva intercepted the tracking. It is in the air. It will arrive in Thiruvananthapuram the day after tomorrow, by diplomatic pouch — the Indians routed it through their embassy in Washington. Clever. We cannot touch it now."

"I know."

"Mr. Rothwell wants the drainage probe tonight. A test of the temple's response time. Soft extraction. I need your man on the north channel."

"I don't have a man for the north channel. I have me."

A silence. Mehra did not like working with principals in the field; it suggested the principal did not trust the organization. The principal did not trust the organization. The principal was correct not to.

"I will take the channel," Kessler said. "Have your men at the secondary exit. If the temple guards respond in under six minutes, we learn their deployment. If they respond in under four, the drainage route is dead and we move to the well. Either way I need to be inside long enough to confirm the passage geometry."

"The geometry is in the notebook."

"The geometry is in the notebook the old man wrote. I want to confirm the old man was not a fool. Mr. Rothwell has spent thirty years trusting a man he has never met. I do not share that trust."

Another silence. Then Mehra, with the careful flatness of a man recording a disagreement for later use: "Eleven tonight. North grate. You will have eight minutes."

Kessler hung up. He did not need eight minutes. He needed three. But he had learned, long ago, that the men who hired him felt safer when he asked for more time than he required; it made them feel that the margin was theirs to give.

He sat in the sedan and watched the rain come down on the temple's east gate, two hundred meters away, where the devotees lined up in the mornings. The tower — the gopuram, they called it — was a dark shape against the wet sky, its carved figures invisible at this distance but present, the way a thing is present even when you cannot see it. He did not like it. He had not liked it since the first night, when he had driven past it and felt, in the Calvinist marrow of him, the low vibration of something that did not belong to him and did not want him there.

He thought about the American woman, asleep now, three kilometers east, in a hotel room he had entered and searched on the second night while she was at the temple. (Her room was clean. She kept no notebook. She had photographed something on a ceiling — he had found the photographs on her laptop, geometric patterns, meaningless to him, and left them.) He thought about the old professor, who had mailed the notebook and then, according to Geneva, walked back into his wrecked flat and sat down among the wreckage and done nothing. The old man had given up the thing he had carried for thirty years and then sat in the ruins of it. Kessler did not understand this. He filed it as a fact about a kind of person he did not need to understand, only to predict.

He thought, finally, about Mr. Rothwell, in his house in England, staring at his fragment of sandstone, waiting for the door to open. Kessler had worked for Rothwell for nine years. He understood the man in the way a good weapon understands the hand that aims it. Rothwell was not cruel. Rothwell was not greedy in the way the men in Lubango had been greedy — the grasping, sweating greed that left fingerprints. Rothwell's greed was dry and patient and had the shape of a cathedral. It wanted one thing, completely, for a long time, and it would pay any price and cross any line to get it, and it would be — this was the part that made Kessler, in the dark sedan, in the Kerala rain, occasionally uneasy — perfectly polite while doing so.

The soft phase was over. Mr. Rothwell had said so. And the soft phase ending meant that Kessler would, in the coming days, be asked to do the things that the soft phase existed to postpone.

He looked at the temple gate. He looked at the rain. He started the sedan and drove back to the safe house to prepare for the eleven o'clock probe, and on the way he thought about the American woman who had noticed the drainage grate on her own, and who checked her corners, and who was, as of tonight, exactly one tunnel away from the thing he had been hired to take from her.

She did not know it yet.

She would.

# ENCRYPTION THREAT SCENE

*(To be inserted within current Chapter 50 [the factorization algorithm reveal], immediately after Priya identifies the algorithm that breaks RSA. Converts the abstract MacGuffin into visceral dread. ~1,000 words.)*

---

The manuscript sat on the table between them, the palm-leaf pages weighted at their edges with glass beads, the Brahmi numerals catching the LED light like a language that had been waiting twenty-three centuries to be heard. Priya's hands were still trembling. Meera's were not — she had gone past trembling into a stillness that felt like the inside of a bell.

"We need to understand what this actually does," Meera said. "Not the mathematics. The mathematics is yours. I need to know what happens to the world if this works."

Rathore, who had been standing at the door, turned. "I have someone. Dr. Anjali Verma, DRDO, seconded to the National Cyber Coordination Centre. She's been my cryptanalysis contact for the ICJ filings. She's in Delhi. She can be on a plane in an hour."

"Do it," Meera said. "And tell her to bring whatever she needs to explain it to a federal agent who last studied mathematics in 2013."

Dr. Anjali Verma arrived at the liaison office at nine the next morning, a small woman with sharp eyes and a government tablet she did not let out of her sight. She did not look like the kind of person who thought about the end of the world. She looked like the kind of person who had thought about it so much that it had stopped being interesting.

Rathore had arranged a secure room. Meera spread Priya's photographs of the palm-leaf manuscript across the table. Verma studied them for eleven minutes without speaking. Then she sat down, folded her hands, and looked at Meera with an expression that was not quite fear and not quite respect.

"You understand what you have here."

"Not fully. That's why you're here."

"What you have here," Verma said, "is a method for factoring the product of two large primes in polynomial time. I need you to hear those words, Agent Desai, even though they will not mean to you what they mean to me. A method for factoring the product of two large primes in polynomial time. Do you know what that is?"

"The basis of RSA encryption."

"The basis of RSA encryption. The basis of the encryption that protects every bank transfer on earth. Every credit-card transaction. Every military communication that is not quantum-secured, which is ninety-nine percent of them. Every hospital record. Every power-grid authentication handshake. Every diplomatic cable. Every cryptocurrency wallet. Every single thing that the modern world does that requires one computer to prove to another computer that it is what it says it is. All of it. All of it rests on the assumption that factoring the product of two large primes is hard. That it takes, with current algorithms, longer than the age of the universe. You have in front of you a method, written in a script that predates Christ, that does it in —" she looked at the manuscript again — "in something that looks, from the structure, like a tractable number of steps."

The room was quiet. The air conditioning hummed.

"Tell me what it means," Meera said. "Not the math. The world."

Verma nodded, as if she had been hoping for exactly this question. "It means that if this method works the way it appears to work, and if a working implementation of it is built — and a competent team could build one in months, not years — then every encrypted communication that has ever been made, or will ever be made, using RSA, becomes readable. Retroactively. Everything already sent. Everything already stored. Everything in every archive, every server, every cloud. All of it. Open."

She let that land.

"The banking system fails first. Not because the math is hard — because the math is fast. Every interbank transfer in the world is RSA-authenticated. If the authentication breaks, the transfers break. The transfers break, the banks cannot settle. The banks cannot settle, the ATMs go dark within hours. The ATMs go dark, you have runs. Not the slow kind. The kind where people die in the lobbies. This happens everywhere, simultaneously, because the vulnerability is not in any one bank. It is in the assumption underneath all of them."

"Hospitals. Patient records are encrypted. So are drug-supply chain authentications — the system that confirms a vial of insulin is insulin and not saline. Break the authentication and you break the supply chain. Hospitals run out of authenticated medication inside a week. That is not my estimate. That is from a DRDO war-game exercise we ran in 2019, and the only reason the scenario did not get worse is that we stopped the clock at seven days."

"Military. Command and control is authenticated. NATO, the Pacific Command, the Indian armed forces, the PLA. Every order, every deployment, every missile-authorization handshake assumes RSA or its cousins. Break it and the authentication does not just fail — it fails in a way the systems cannot detect, because a broken signature looks identical to a valid one. Orders can be forged. Orders can be denied. A commander cannot tell, in the moment, whether the launch order he just received is real. Do you understand what I am telling you?"

Meera understood. She had understood by the second sentence. But she let Verma say it, because Verma had clearly carried this particular thought for a long time and needed, today, to set it down in front of someone who could do something about it.

"The 2008 financial crisis," Verma said, "was a liquidity problem. A failure of trust. The banks stopped lending to each other because they could not be sure who was solvent. The world came within hours, by some accounts, of the entire global payment system locking. We came that close —" she held up her thumb and forefinger, a centimeter apart — "to the machines stopping. Not the markets. The machines. The thing that moves the money from the place that has it to the place that needs it. That crisis was caused by a rumor. A *rumor*. What you have on this table is not a rumor. It is the certainty. It is the thing itself. And if it leaks, or if it is built, or if a single government decides it is worth the risk to build it first and use it before anyone else can —"

She stopped. She put her hands flat on the table, the way Meera put her hands flat on her thighs, and Meera saw, with a small shock, that she had finally met someone who carried the gesture too.

"Agent Desai. The reason no one has ever launched the cyberattack that ends civilization is that the encryption holds. That is the single thread. Every nuclear deterrent, every financial system, every critical infrastructure — they are all, at the bottom, secured by the assumption that the math is hard. You have in front of you a twenty-three-century-old manuscript that says the math is not hard. It was never hard. Someone, a very long time ago, knew this, and they put it on a leaf, and they sealed it under a temple, and they waited."

Verma looked at the manuscript. Her voice, when she spoke again, was almost gentle.

"I have spent my career thinking about the weapons that might end us. I never thought I would hold one. This is it. This is the one. And the question is not whether it works — Priya's preliminary analysis says it works. The question is what you do with it. Because the thing about this particular weapon is that the only way to be safe from it is for no one to have it. And the thing about people is that someone always will."

The room was very quiet. The air conditioning hummed. Outside, the monsoon hammered the liaison office, and somewhere beneath a temple in Kerala, in the dark, the manuscript's twin — the original, the one Ashoka's engineers had sealed with their own hands — lay on its shelf and waited, as it had waited since the year 264 before Christ, for the world to decide whether it was ready.

Meera looked at the manuscript. She looked at Verma. She looked at Rathore, who had not moved from the door and whose face had gone the color of old stone.

"Now I understand," she said, "why Ashoka sealed it."

Rathore's jaw worked once. "Now we have to decide what to do with it."

"Yes," Meera said. "We do."

And for the first time since she had arrived in Kerala, she understood that the hardest part of this assignment was not going to be the mathematics, or the politics, or the men who were coming for the knowledge.

It was going to be the decision about what the knowledge deserved.
