## 1. VATICAN SECRET ARCHIVES (Now Vatican Apostolic Archive)

### What It Actually Contains
- **85 km (53 mi) of shelving** with 35,000 volumes in the selective catalogue alone
- **16 million pages** from Pope Pius XII's pontificate alone (1939–1958)
- Papal registers from Pope Innocent III (1198–1216) onward; scattered registers from earlier popes including John VIII (872–882) and Gregory VII (1073–1085)
- Notable documents: Henry VIII's annulment request, trial of Galileo, Michelangelo's complaint about unpaid Sistine Chapel work, 1521 bull excommunicating Martin Luther
- Renamed from "Vatican Secret Archive" to "Vatican Apostolic Archive" by Pope Francis on 28 October 2019
- The word "secret" originally meant "private" (the Pope's personal property), not "classified"
- Materials after 1958 remain restricted; personal records of church figures after 1922 are sealed

### Documents Related to India
- **MS Vatican Syriac 22**: The oldest known Syriac manuscript copied in India, a lectionary of Pauline Epistles copied in 1301 AD at Kodungallur (Cranganore), Kerala, at the Church dedicated to Mar Quriaqos. It references the "Catholicos-Patriarch of the East" and the "Metropolitan of India" — direct evidence of the Church of the East's Indian hierarchy.
- **Papal correspondence with Indian kings**: Pope Nicholas V issued the Papal bull *Romanus Pontifex* after Vasco da Gama's return, granting Portugal the *padroado* (patronage) for propagation of Catholic faith in Asia — this is the foundational document of Portuguese ecclesiastical authority in India.
- **Records of the Synod of Diamper (1599)**: Detailed decrees, correspondence between Archbishop Menezes and Rome, and the subsequent submission of the St. Thomas Christians.
- **Letters from Francis Xavier**: Extensive correspondence from the Jesuit missionary to Rome and to the King of Portugal regarding Indian missions (1542–1552).
- **Goa Inquisition records**: Most were **deliberately destroyed by Portuguese officials** when the Inquisition ended in 1812, making reconstruction of its full scope difficult.
- **Pope Alexander III's letter to Prester John** (1177): Sent via his physician Philip — related to the legendary Christian king believed to rule India.

### References to Indian Christians (St. Thomas Christians of Kerala)
- The Archives contain correspondence regarding the 1498 encounter between St. Thomas Christians and Vasco da Gama
- Records of the Portuguese efforts to bring the St. Thomas Christians under Latin authority
- Documentation of the Coonan Cross Oath (1653) and subsequent schisms
- The Vatican Library holds Syriac manuscripts from Indian Christian communities

### Vatican Library's Indian Holdings
- The Vatican Library has **a large collection of texts related to Hinduism**, with the oldest editions dating to 1819
- **75,000 codices** and **1.1 million printed books** total
- Pope Clement XI sent scholars to the Orient to acquire manuscripts and is regarded as the founder of the library's Oriental section
- The Vatican Library has a digitization project underway (DigiVatLib) making manuscripts available online

## 2. KNIGHTS TEMPLAR TRADE ROUTES & INDIA

### The Templar Fleet
- The Templars owned **fleets of ships** and at one point owned the entire island of Cyprus
- The **Commander of the Vault of Acre** also served as the Templar fleet's admiral, though he was subordinated to the Order's preceptor, indicating the Templars considered their ships primarily for **commerce rather than military purposes**
- The Templars developed **letters of credit** (an early form of banking/cheques) for pilgrims — depositing valuables at one preceptory and claiming equivalent value at another, possibly the first use of bank cheques

### Did They Sail East?
- **No direct evidence** of Templar ships reaching India
- The Templars' commercial network was concentrated in the Mediterranean, Europe, and the Holy Land
- They operated nearly **1,000 commanderies and fortifications** across Europe and the Holy Land
- The order was arguably "the world's first multinational corporation" but their operations were bounded by the Mediterranean and Levant
- However, **indirect connections** existed: Mediterranean trade goods from India (spices, textiles, gems) flowed through Templar commercial networks via Venetian, Genoese, and other merchant intermediaries

### Portuguese Order of Christ — The Key Connection
- After the Templars were dissolved in 1312, **Portugal was the first country where they had settled** (only 2–3 years after the order's Jerusalem founding)
- King Denis I of Portugal **refused to persecute** the former knights
- Between 1317–1319, Templar knights, properties, and assets were absorbed into the **Portuguese Order of Christ**
- The Order of Christ's headquarters was the **Convent of Christ Castle in Tomar** (a former Templar castle, now a UNESCO World Heritage Site)
- **Prince Henry the Navigator**, the driving force behind Portuguese exploration, was **Grand Master of the Order of Christ** (1415–1460)
- The **caravels that sailed to India** under Vasco da Gama (1498) flew the **Cross of the Order of Christ** — the same red cross pattée that the Templars wore
- The Templar financial and maritime infrastructure was thus **indirectly inherited** by the Portuguese explorers who reached India
- The Portuguese brought the Order of Christ with them to **Kongo and Brazil**

### Templar Symbols in India
- No confirmed pre-modern Templar symbols found archaeologically in India
- However, the **Cross of the Order of Christ** (evolved from the Templar cross) was erected across Portuguese India — on churches, forts, ships, and official buildings from 1510 onward
- The **Basilica of Bom Jesus** in Goa (containing St. Francis Xavier's body) and numerous other Portuguese-era churches bear crosses directly descended from Templar iconography
- The **Convent of Christ at Tomar** contains the famous Chapter House window (Janela do Capítulo) with its elaborate maritime motifs, directly linking Templar-Order of Christ symbolism to the Age of Exploration

## 3. PRESTER JOHN LEGEND

### Origin and Connection to India
- The legend began in the 12th century with reports of visits from an **Archbishop of India to Constantinople** and a **Patriarch of India to Rome** during the time of Pope Callixtus II — apparently from the **St. Thomas Christians of India**
- The **Acts of Thomas** (3rd century), describing Thomas the Apostle's travels and evangelism in India, planted the earliest seeds of the narrative
- In 1145, Bishop Hugh of Jabala told Otto of Freising about a **Nestorian Christian priest-king** named Prester John who had defeated the Persian monarchs — this was the legend's first written record
- The **Letter of Prester John** (c. 1165), supposedly written to Byzantine Emperor Manuel I Komnenos, described Prester John as "King of India," a descendant of the Three Magi, ruling a kingdom of riches, marvels, and strange creatures
- The Letter circulated in ever-more embellished form for centuries and was **translated into numerous languages including Hebrew**

### How It Influenced European Exploration
- Pope Alexander III sent a letter to Prester John via his physician Philip on **27 September 1177** — Philip never returned
- The prospect of finding Prester John **directly fueled Portuguese exploration**:
  - On **7 May 1487**, Pêro da Covilhã and Afonso de Paiva were sent overland specifically to inquire about Prester John while gathering intelligence on sea routes to India
  - Covilhã reached **Ethiopia** and was received but forbidden to leave
  - Francisco Álvares's book *Verdadeira Informação das Terras do Preste João das Indias* (A True Relation of the Lands of Prester John of the Indies, c. 1520) was the first direct European account of Ethiopia, presented to the Pope
- By the time Portuguese explorers arrived in India and Ethiopia, **Prester John had become identified with the Emperor of Ethiopia** — though the Ethiopians themselves had never used the title
- The legend gradually shifted Prester John's location: **India → Central Asia → Ethiopia**, following European geographic knowledge
- The search for Prester John **encouraged European exploration and colonialism** for several hundred years

### Connection to Portuguese Arrival in India
- Vasco da Gama's 1498 voyage to Calicut was partly motivated by the desire to find the Christian king of the East and establish an alliance
- When da Gama arrived in Kerala, the **St. Thomas Christians** — the very community that had helped inspire the Prester John legend — were among the first to greet the Portuguese as fellow Christians
- The Portuguese saw themselves as fulfilling the Prester John prophecy: reconnecting with lost Eastern Christians

## 4. PORTUGUESE INQUISITION IN INDIA (Goa Inquisition, 1560–1812)

### Scope and Scale
- Established 1560, briefly stopped 1774–1778, finally abolished **1812**
- Between 1561 and 1623: **3,800 cases** brought before the tribunal
- Total population of Goa at the time: approximately **60,000** (with ~20,000 Hindus)
- **71 autos-da-fé** (public acts of penance) recorded
- In the first few years alone, **over 4,000 people arrested**
- Over its 249-year history: **57 people burned at the stake**, **64 burned in effigy** (convicted in absentia or after dying in custody); **4,046 others sentenced to various punishments**
- **74% of those sentenced** were charged with Crypto-Hinduism

### What They Destroyed
- **Books in Sanskrit, Dutch, English, Konkani, and Marathi** were seized and burned — any book suspected of containing teachings deviating from Catholic doctrine
- **Hindu temples**: ~160 temples razed on Goa island by 1566; another **300 temples destroyed in Bardez** (North Goa) by Franciscan missionaries in 1566–1567; approximately **300 more in Salcete** (South Goa)
- A **1569 royal letter** records that all Hindu temples in Portuguese colonies in India had been "burnt and razed to the ground" (*desfeitos e queimados*)
- Laws prohibited repairing damaged temples or building new ones (1566–1576)
- **Hindu books in Sanskrit and Marathi** were burned
- Hindu wedding ceremonies were banned; owning images of Hindu gods/goddesses was criminalized
- The Inquisition seized and destroyed books that promoted **Protestant, Hindu, or Muslim ideas**

### What Manuscripts Were Burned
- The surviving Synod of Diamper records list **18 specific books** ordered destroyed (see Section 6 below)
- Beyond these, countless Hindu religious texts, Ayurvedic medical manuscripts, and literary works in Konkani, Sanskrit, and Marathi were destroyed
- **Most Goa Inquisition records were deliberately destroyed** by Portuguese officials after 1812, making the full scope unknowable
- The suppression of **Konkani language** was systematic: decreed forbidden in 1684, banned in schools in 1869, effectively destroying centuries of Konkani literature and oral tradition

### What Secrets Were Suppressed
- The ancient **Syriac Christian texts** of the St. Thomas Christians — a unique repository of early Eastern Christianity
- Hindu philosophical and scientific knowledge that contradicted Catholic doctrine
- Evidence of the **pre-Portuguese Christian traditions** of India that were independent of Roman authority
- The Inquisition suppressed the knowledge that **India had an ancient, thriving Christian community** dating back potentially to the 1st century AD, with its own liturgy, hierarchy, and traditions completely independent of Rome

## 5. JESUIT MISSIONS TO INDIA

### Francis Xavier (1506–1552)
- Arrived in **Goa on 6 May 1542**, thirteen months after leaving Lisbon
- His primary mission was to **restore Christianity among the Portuguese settlers** in India, many of whom had adopted Indian culture
- He established headquarters at **Saint Paul's College, Goa** — the first Jesuit headquarters in Asia
- Preached along the **Pearl Fishery Coast** (Cape Comorin to Mannar), converting the Paravar community
- Built **~40 churches** along the south Indian coast
- Visited the **tomb of Thomas the Apostle** in Mylapore (now Chennai)
- In **1546**, wrote to King John III of Portugal requesting the establishment of the Goan Inquisition (the Inquisition was established 14 years after he left Goa)
- His **body** (largely incorrupt) is enshrined in a silver casket at the **Basilica of Bom Jesus, Goa** — a major pilgrimage site
- His **right forearm** was sent to Rome and is displayed at the Jesuit church Il Gesù
- Modern scholars estimate he converted approximately **30,000 people**
- Known as the **"Apostle of the Indies," "Apostle of the Far East," "Apostle of China," and "Apostle of Japan"**
- Died on **Shangchuan Island, China** on 3 December 1552

### Roberto de Nobili (1577–1656)
- Arrived in Goa and Bombay on **20 May 1605**
- Established in **Madurai, Tamil Nadu** from November 1606
- Called himself a **"teacher of wisdom" (தத்துவ போதகர்)** and dressed as a **Hindu Sannyasi** (ascetic) — white dhoti, wooden sandals, shaved head with a tuft
- Wore a **three-stringed thread** across his chest representing the Holy Trinity (adopting the Brahmin *upanayana*)
- Claimed noble parentage to approach **high-caste people as an equal**
- **Mastered Sanskrit, Telugu, and Tamil** — Max Müller called him **"the First European Sanskrit scholar"**
- Appropriated Hindu terminology for Christian concepts:
  - *kovil* = place of worship
  - *arul* and *prasadam* = grace
  - *guru* = priest/teacher
  - *Vedam* = the Bible
  - *poosai* = Mass
- Composed **Catechisms and philosophic discourses in Tamil**, contributing to the development of modern Tamil prose
- His methods were **highly controversial** with fellow Jesuits and the Archbishop of Goa
- Pope Gregory XV approved his methods in the apostolic constitution *Romanæ Sedis Antistes* (31 January 1623), allowing the thread, tuft, sandalwood paste, and baths as social (not religious) customs
- Died in **Mylapore** near Chennai on 16 January 1656

### Did They Send Indian Manuscripts to the Vatican?
- **Yes.** Jesuit missionaries were systematic collectors of local knowledge and texts:
  - Roberto de Nobili studied Sanskrit texts extensively and wrote analyses of Hindu philosophy
  - The **Ezourvedam** — a text once attributed to de Nobili (now believed to be by Jean Calmette, 1692–1740) — was a spurious "Veda" that influenced Voltaire and other Enlightenment thinkers
  - Pope Clement XI (reigned 1700–1721) **specifically sent scholars to the Orient to bring back manuscripts**, founding the Vatican Library's Oriental section
  - Jesuits sent reports, translations, and original manuscripts back to Rome throughout the 16th–18th centuries
  - The Vatican Library contains texts on Hinduism dating from 1819 onward, but many earlier Jesuit-acquired materials would be in the manuscript collections
  - Thomas Stephens (1549–1619), an English Jesuit in Goa, composed the **Khristapurana** — an epic poem using Hindu literary forms to tell Christ's story in Marathi

### What Knowledge Did They Acquire?
- **Sanskrit language and Hindu philosophy** — the Jesuits were among the first Europeans to systematically study these
- **Ayurvedic medicine** and Indian scientific knowledge
- **Tamil, Telugu, Konkani, and Marathi** languages and literature
- **Indian social structures** (caste system) — which they adapted to for conversion purposes
- Geographical and commercial intelligence about Indian Ocean trade routes, kingdoms, and resources
- Knowledge of **other Christian communities** (St. Thomas Christians, Ethiopian Christians) that informed Vatican strategy

## 6. THE SYNOD OF DIAMPER (1599)

### What Happened
- Held at **Udayamperoor (Diamper)**, near Kochi, Kerala, **20–26 June 1599**
- Convened by **Archbishop Aleixo de Menezes of Goa**
- Attended by **153 local priests and 660 lay representatives**
- The St. Thomas Christians were **forced to swear** they would not accept any bishop except one nominated by Rome
- The **Patriarch of Babylon** was condemned as a heretic and schismatic
- Issued **200 decrees** in nine sessions

### What Was Lost — Prohibited and Destroyed Books
The Synod explicitly prohibited and ordered the destruction of these texts:

1. *The Infancy of our Saviour* (Syriac)
2. *Book of John Barialdan* (Syriac)
3. *The Procession of the Holy Spirit* (Persian)
4. *Margarita Fidei* (The Jewel)
5. *Fathers* (unknown language)
6. *Life of Abed Isho* (Arabic)
7. *Book of Synods* (Syriac)
8. *Book of Timothy the Patriarch* (Persian)
9. *Domingo or Letter of the Lord's-day* (Malayalam)
10. *Maclamatas* (Syriac)
11. *Uguarda or Rose* (Greek)
12. *Camiz* (Syriac)
13. *Menra* (Hebrew)
14. *Book of Orders* (Tamil)
15. *Book of Homilies* (Arabic)
16. *An Exposition of the Gospels* (Syriac)
17. *Book of Hormisda Raban* (Greek)
18. *Book of Lots* (Aramaic)
19. A Nestorian version of *Flos Sanctorum* (Syriac)
20. *Parisman or Persian Medicine* (Persian)

### Additional Losses
- **Decree XVI** ordered all Syriac manuscripts handed over to the Archbishop — only Qurbana (liturgy) manuscripts were exempted
- The **Anaphorae of Nestorius, Theodore, and Diodore** were abolished and their manuscripts ordered destroyed
- After the Synod, Menezes **toured Kerala until November 1599**, personally examining books in local churches and **burning those deemed heretical**
- The **Peshitta** (Syriac Bible) was among the texts condemned
- **Only very few Syriac manuscripts** survived the destruction — Dr. Istvan Perczel, a Hungarian scholar, found that certain texts did survive
- Liturgical changes: multiple alterations to the Holy Qurbana of Addai and Mari — changes in litanies, hymns, creed, and prayers
- The **Archbishopric of Angamaly** was downgraded to a bishopric under Goa on 4 August 1600

### The Catholic Encyclopedia's Own Assessment
> "The only case in which an ancient Eastern rite has been wilfully romanized is that of the Uniat Malabar Christians, where it was not Roman authority but the **misguided zeal of Alexius de Menezes**, Archbishop of Goa, and his Portuguese advisers at the Synod of Diamper (1599) which spoiled the old Malabar Rite."

### The Aftermath
- The oppressive rule provoked the **Coonan Cross Oath** (1653) — thousands of St. Thomas Christians swore to never obey the Portuguese again
- This led to a **permanent schism** dividing the community into:
  - **Pazhayakūr** (Old Allegiance): Stayed Catholic → Syro-Malabar Church, Chaldean Syrian Church
  - **Puthenkoor** (New Allegiance): Rejected Portuguese → Jacobite/Orthodox, Mar Thoma, and other churches
- The community remains divided to this day into **7+ denominations**

## 7. VATICAN'S CURRENT INDIAN ARTIFACT HOLDINGS

### Vatican Museums
- **70,000 works** in the collection, 20,000 on display; **6.8 million visitors** in 2024 (2nd most-visited museum in the world)
- **24 galleries/rooms** including the Sistine Chapel, Raphael Rooms, Gallery of Maps
- Specific Indian artifacts are not prominently catalogued in publicly available sources, but the Vatican's **Ethnological Museum** (Museo Etnologico) contains artifacts from mission territories worldwide, including India
- The **Anima Mundi** collection (a newer initiative) includes indigenous art from around the world

### Vatican Library Indian Holdings
- Large collection of **Hindu texts**, oldest editions dating to 1819
- **MS Vatican Syriac 22** — the oldest Syriac manuscript from India (1301 AD), a Pauline Epistles lectionary from Kodungallur, Kerala
- Syriac manuscripts from the St. Thomas Christian tradition
- Over **100 Quran manuscripts** — suggesting similar Islamic manuscript holdings from India may exist
- The Vatican Library's **Oriental section** (founded by Pope Clement XI) contains manuscripts from across Asia including India

### Vatican Apostolic Archive
- Contains all papal correspondence related to India from 1198 onward
- Records of the Portuguese *padroado* system in India
- Documentation of the Synod of Diamper and its aftermath
- Jesuit mission reports from India (1540s onward)
- Records of the Vatican's relations with various Indian kingdoms and rulers

### Known Specific Treasures
- **Relics of St. Francis Xavier**: His right forearm is in Rome at Il Gesù; his body remains in Goa
- **Silver casket** in Goa's Basilica of Bom Jesus: crafted by Goan silversmiths (1636–1637), blending Italian and Indian aesthetics, with 32 silver plates depicting Xavier's life — this represents a major Indian-Christian artwork under Vatican/Portuguese patronage
- **Marble pedestal** by Giovanni Battista Foggini (1690s), commissioned by Cosimo III de' Medici, shipped from Florence to Goa via Italy — combining European and Indian artistic traditions

## KEY NARRATIVE THREADS FOR A THRILLER

### The Lost Library of the St. Thomas Christians
The Synod of Diamper destroyed a vast collection of ancient Syriac texts — including potential records of 1st-century Christianity in India, texts predating the canonical Bible, and connections between early Christianity and Indian philosophy. Some manuscripts may have survived and been hidden.

### The Templar-India Connection Through Portugal
The Knights Templar → Order of Christ → Portuguese exploration pipeline means that Templar wealth, knowledge, and maritime technology directly enabled the Portuguese arrival in India. The Templar archive may have contained maps or documents about the East that were passed to the Order of Christ.

### What the Vatican Knows But Hasn't Released
With 85 km of shelving and millions of documents, the Vatican Archive certainly holds unreleased correspondence about:
- The full scope of the Goa Inquisition (before records were destroyed)
- Jesuit intelligence reports from Mughal courts and Hindu kingdoms
- Ancient manuscripts copied or seized from Indian Christian communities
- Prester John correspondence — 800 years of European speculation about Eastern Christian kingdoms
- The private archives of church figures after 1922 remain sealed

### The Prester John Secret
The Prester John legend was based on real St. Thomas Christians in India. The Vatican has known since the 12th century that an independent Christian tradition existed in India — one that predates the Roman Catholic Church's own institutional history. What was the Vatican trying to hide or suppress by "correcting" the St. Thomas Christians at the Synod of Diamper?

### TheDestroyed Knowledge
Between the Goa Inquisition's book-burning and the Synod of Diamper's manuscript destruction, an enormous amount of Indian knowledge was lost:
- Ancient Syriac Christian texts with potential alternate histories of early Christianity
- Hindu scientific and medical manuscripts
- Konkani literature (the language was suppressed for centuries)
- Records of the pre-Portuguese Christian church in India
- Potentially: documentation of contact between ancient India and the Near East that could rewrite historical understanding
