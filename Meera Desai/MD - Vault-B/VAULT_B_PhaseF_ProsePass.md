# VAULT B — Phase F Prose Pass (DVC Edit)

This file is the **tic-elimination and texture guide** applied at fold-in. It contains: (1) the exact occurrence counts of every flagged tic, (2) the replacement palette for each, and (3) the sensory-texture additions for the underground/tunnel scenes.

The pass is **mechanical** — it does not change plot, character, or structure. It changes words. Every replacement preserves meaning; it varies expression.

---

## 1. TIC REPLACEMENT TABLE

### A. "2,300 years" / "2,300-year" (80 occurrences combined) — the book's most overused significance-marker

**Rule:** reduce to ~20 total uses. Replace the other ~60 with varied equivalents. Never use "2,300 years" more than once per chapter; prefer zero.

**Replacement palette (rotate, never repeat the same one twice in a chapter):**
- "twenty-three centuries"
- "older than Rome"
- "before the Common Era"
- "two and a half millennia"
- "since the century after Alexander"
- "since Ashoka's own lifetime"
- "since the Kalinga War" (contextual — ties to the historical anchor)
- "for more than two thousand years"
- "across two dozen centuries"
- "from an age that had no written record in Europe"
- "from before the birth of the alphabet the West uses"
- "older than every government on earth"
- "older than any institution that would now claim it"
- (sometimes): cut the time reference entirely and let the object carry its own age. The reader knows the repository is old. Trust them.

### B. "particular" (39 occurrences) — the Hemingway-crutch qualifier ("the particular expression of," "the particular loneliness of")

**Rule:** cut to ~9 uses. The word "particular" appears in constructions like "the particular weight of a man who..." Replace ~30 of the 39 with the specific quality being described.

**Examples of the fix:**
- "the particular expression of people who were authorized to classify things" → "the expression of people who were authorized to classify things" (just cut it)
- "the particular loneliness of a man who was right and wrong at the same time" → "the loneliness of a man who was right and wrong at the same time" (cut)
- "the particular care of a woman who understood..." → "the care of a woman who understood..." (cut)
- "with the particular stillness of a man trained to listen" → "with the stillness of a man trained to listen" (cut)
- Keep "particular" only where it genuinely adds specificity: "a particular kind of silence — the silence of stone that has not been disturbed" (here it earns its place).

**Rule of thumb:** if deleting "particular" changes nothing, delete it. That's ~30 of the 39.

### C. "did not sleep" / "didn't sleep" / "had not slept" (5 occurrences) — chapter-ending tic

**Rule:** eliminate as a chapter-closer (all 5 instances flagged were closers or near-closers). Replace with a forward-pulling beat.

**Replacements (per instance):**
- Ch 2 end: "She didn't sleep for a long time." → "She lay in the dark and listened to the city, and at 4 AM she stopped pretending and got up to pack." (forward motion)
- Ch 5 end: "She did not sleep that night." → CUT entirely; the prior line ("She would read every word. She would decode every equation.") is already the chapter's true close. Let it stand.
- Ch 6 end (post-Phase C rewrite): the "didn't sleep" beat is already gone — replaced by the Kessler sighting. ✓
- Ch 25: "slept for the first time" → vary: "and for the first time in days, her body gave up before her mind did."
- Ch 31 opening (post-Phase D rewrite): the "didn't sleep" tic is already gone — replaced by the Rothwell-call timestamp. ✓
- Ch 36: "had not slept" → "Kesavan Nambudiri's nights had grown short." (varied)

### D. "for a long time" / "for a long moment" (16 occurrences) — the vague-duration tic

**Rule:** cut ~10 of 16. Replace with specific duration, action, or cut entirely.

**Examples:**
- "She stared at it for a long time." → "She stared at it until the rain on the window changed pitch." (specific)
- "He was silent for a long moment." → "He said nothing. The pen turned once in his fingers." (action)
- "for a long moment, neither spoke" → "Neither spoke." (just cut — the silence is implied by the line break)
- Keep "for a long time" only where duration is the point: "She had not called him for a long time — four months, she'd counted." (here duration is load-bearing).

### E. "hands pressed flat against her thighs" / "pressed her palms" (5 occurrences) — Meera's tell

**Rule:** keep 2 (Ch 1 and one later chapter where it's explicitly flagged as "her tell"). Vary the other 3:
- "She pressed her hands flat against the desk." (surface varies)
- "Her fingers found the seam of her trousers and held on." (different gesture, same emotional beat)
- "She made her hands still by making them useful — she picked up the pen." (action replacing gesture)

### F. "carved from granite/stone" (1 occurrence) — low count, but kill it

- "his face was carved from granite" → "his face had the set immobility of a man who had decided and would not undecide" (or any fresh metaphor).

### G. "held its breath" (3 occurrences) — city/weather tic

**Rule:** keep 1 (Ch 3, the arrival — it's the best use). Vary the other 2:
- "the city held its breath" → "the city waited, the way a room waits before someone speaks."
- "the tunnel held its breath" → "the tunnel went still."

### H. "the weight of" (9 occurrences) — significance-tic ("the weight of 2,300 years," "the weight of it," etc.)

**Rule:** cut ~5. The "weight" metaphor for significance is overused. Replace with:
- specific physical sensation: "the manuscript was heavier than it looked"
- emotional naming: "the responsibility of it" (once)
- cut entirely and let context carry it.

### I. "was quiet for a moment" / "the room was quiet" (6 occurrences) — pause-tic

**Rule:** cut 4 of 6. Replace the pause with action:
- "The room was quiet for a moment." → "No one spoke. The air conditioning hummed." (if the hum matters)
- "was quiet for a long moment" → CUT (the line break does the work).

### J. "without preamble" (4 occurrences)

**Rule:** cut all 4. "Without preamble" is itself preamble. Just have the character speak.

## 2. SENSORY-TEXTURE ADDITIONS (the Gemini review's note, applied)

The underground/tunnel scenes need more sensory specificity to create the "descending into history" feeling. Add these textures at the noted points (small insertions, 1–2 sentences each, woven into existing paragraphs):

### At the first descent (Ch 30 / the priest's tunnel):
The air changed as they descended. Above, the monsoon — ninety percent humidity, warm and wet and oppressive, Meera's cotton kurta stuck to her back, her hair frizzed and tangled, her skin tacky with the sweat that never evaporated. Below, the air dried. The sweat on her neck went cold. The smell shifted — from petrichor and jasmine and diesel to mineral, to chalk, to the dry mineral breath of stone that had not been breathed on in living memory. The temperature dropped two degrees, then four. By the time they reached the mechanism passage, her skin was prickling with the unfamiliar sensation of being cold in Kerala.

### At the second descent (Ch 48 / the delegation):
After three weeks of Thiruvananthapuram's monsoon humidity, where every breath felt like drinking and every surface was slick with moisture, the dry underground air was a shock. Meera's skin prickled as the temperature dropped, the sweat drying on her neck for the first time since she'd arrived. Somewhere far above them — hundreds of feet of granite and laterite and temple stone — the monsoon was hammering the city, filling the streets, turning the Karamana River into a brown torrent, but down here the air was still and ancient and untouched by weather.

### At the climax (Ch 69 / the mechanism):
The bronze pivot frames, when she touched them, were cold — colder than the air, colder than the stone, the cold of metal that has conducted the temperature of the deep earth for two dozen centuries. Her fingers, wrapped around the lever, left faint damp prints on the surface that evaporated in seconds as the metal drank the warmth from her skin.

### The repository (Ch 48 / the chamber itself):
The chamber smelled, faintly, of copper. Not the copper of blood — the copper of old bronze, of the alloy Ashoka's engineers had used for the pivot frames and the plate presses and the cylinder casings. It was a clean, mineral smell, the smell of a metal that did not corrode, that had been engineered not to corrode, that had held its integrity against two thousand years of underground moisture through a process modern metallurgy was only beginning to guess at.

5. **Fifth pass:** vary the remaining minor tics (hands, weight, quiet, held-its-breath, without-preamble).
6. **Sixth pass:** insert the sensory-texture additions at the four noted points.
7. **Final read:** confirm no tic appears more than twice in any chapter.
