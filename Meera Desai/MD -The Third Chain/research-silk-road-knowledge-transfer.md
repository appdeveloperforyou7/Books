## 1. ASHOKA'S MISSIONARY PROGRAM (260 BCE ONWARDS)

### Key Facts

**Emperor Ashoka** (r. 268–232 BCE) of the Maurya Empire launched the first state-sponsored missionary program in recorded history after converting to Buddhism following the bloody Kalinga War (c. 261 BCE). His Rock Edict XIII describes his remorse; his subsequent edicts detail an organized program to spread Buddhist teachings — and with them, Indian scientific and medical knowledge.

### Documented Missions

The Sri Lankan chronicle **Mahavamsa** (5th century CE, drawing on earlier oral traditions) and Ashoka's own **Edicts of the XII Rock** list specific missions:

| Missionary | Destination | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Mahinda** (Ashoka's son) | **Sri Lanka** (Anuradhapura) | Mahavamsa; archaeology of Mihintale |
| **Sanghamitta** (Ashoka's daughter) | **Sri Lanka** | Mahavamsa; brought a Bodhi tree sapling |
| **Majjhantika** | **Kashmir & Gandhara** | Mahavamsa; Ashoka's Rock Edicts at Mansehra & Shahbazgarhi |
| **Mahadeva** | **Mahisa** (central India) | Mahavamsa |
| **Rakkhita** | **Vanavasa** (south India) | Mahavamsa |
| **Yona Dhammarakkhita** | **Aparantaka** (western India/Gujarat) | Mahavamsa |
| **Mahadhammarakkhita** | **Maharatta** | Mahavamsa |
| **Maharakkhita** | **Yona country** (Greco-Bactrian lands) | Mahavamsa — note: a Greek-named monk sent to Greek lands |
| **Majjhima** | **Himalaya region** | Mahavamsa |
| **Sona & Uttara** | **Suvannabhumi** (Southeast Asia/Myanmar) | Mahavamsa |

### Hard Archaeological Evidence

- **Edicts of Ashoka** at **Mansehra** and **Shahbazgarhi** (Pakistan) — written in **Kharosthi** script, proving Buddhist dharma was being propagated in Gandhara region
- **Edicts found at** Kandahar (Afghanistan) — bilingual Greek-Aramaic edicts proving the message reached the Hellenistic world
- **Lumbini pillar inscription** — Ashoka's personal pilgrimage to the Buddha's birthplace
- The **Sanchi Stupa** (Madhya Pradesh) — commissioned by Ashoka; relic caskets found during excavation bear inscriptions of early missionaries including "Kasapagota" and "Majjhima"

### Knowledge Carried Besides Religion

Buddhist monks did not carry only religion. Monasteries were the universities of the ancient world. Monks carried:

- **Medical knowledge**: The Buddhist monastic code (Vinaya) explicitly permits monks to practice medicine. The **Bhesajjakkhandhaka** section of the Vinaya details treatments for snakebite, fever, wounds, and eye diseases. Ashokan missions carried ayurvedic medical practices.
- **Botanical/pharmacological knowledge**: Monastic gardens grew medicinal plants; Nalanda's curriculum included Ayurveda.
- **Mathematical/astronomical knowledge**: The Jyotisha (astronomy) Vedanga was one of the six essential disciplines. Monks needed astronomy for calculating the lunar calendar to determine monsoon retreats and festival dates.
- **Grammar and linguistics**: Panini's Sanskrit grammar (c. 4th century BCE) was transmitted alongside Buddhist texts. The development of Pali and its spread to Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia is itself a knowledge-transfer event.
- **Architecture and engineering**: Stupa construction required geometric and engineering knowledge. The dome of the Sanchi Stupa demonstrates sophisticated structural understanding.

## 2. SILK ROAD KNOWLEDGE TRANSMISSION (200 BCE – 1400 CE)

### The Kushan Empire: Gateway of Knowledge

The **Kushan Empire** (c. 30–375 CE), centered in modern Afghanistan/Pakistan/northern India, was the crucial intermediary. Under **Kanishka** (r. c. 127–150 CE), Buddhism was actively propagated along the Silk Road into China.

- The **Kushans** controlled the key trade routes through the **Tarim Basin** and issued bilingual coins (Greek on one side, Kharosthi/Brahmi on the other) — physical evidence of multicultural exchange.
- The Kushan city of **Bactra** (modern Balkh, Afghanistan) was a major center where Indian, Persian, Greek, and Chinese knowledge converged.
- **Lokaksema** (c. 2nd century CE), a Kushan Buddhist monk of Yuezhi (Central Asian) origin, traveled to the Chinese capital Luoyang and became the first documented translator of Mahayana Buddhist texts into Chinese (c. 178–189 CE). His translations include the *Pratyutpanna Samadhi Sutra* and the *Astaghasrika Prajnaparamita Sutra*.

### Al-Khwarizmi's Acknowledged Indian Sources

**Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi** (c. 780–850 CE), working at the **House of Wisdom** (Bayt al-Hikma) in Baghdad, explicitly drew on Indian sources:

- His work on arithmetic, *On the Calculation with Hindu Numerals* (c. 825 CE), was translated into Latin in the 12th century as *Algoritmi de numero Indorum* ("Al-Khwarizmi on the Hindu art of reckoning"). This single work introduced the **Hindu-Arabic numeral system** (digits 0-9 with place-value notation) to the Islamic world and then to Europe.
- His astronomical tables, the ***Zij as-Sindhind*** (c. 820 CE), were based directly on the Indian **Sindhind** astronomical system (from Sanskrit *Siddhanta*). The *Zij* was brought to Baghdad from **Sindh** (modern Pakistan) in the early 770s CE when an Indian delegation arrived at the court of Caliph **al-Mansur**. The Caliph ordered it translated from Sanskrit into Arabic by **al-Fazari** and al-Khwarizmi.
- The word **"algorithm"** derives from the Latinization of al-Khwarizmi's name. The word **"algebra"** comes from his book *Al-Jabr* (820 CE).
- Al-Khwarizmi's work also contained a **table of sine values**, directly derived from Indian trigonometry. The modern concepts of **sine** and **cosine** were developed in India and transmitted to the Islamic world.

### The Barmakid Family: Buddhist Viziers in Abbasid Baghdad

This is one of the most extraordinary stories of knowledge transfer — and perfect thriller material:

The **Barmakid** family (Persian: برمکیان) were originally **hereditary administrators of the Nava Vihara** (Nawbahar) **Buddhist monastery** in **Balkh** (northern Afghanistan). This was one of the most important Buddhist monasteries in Central Asia, described by **Xuanzang** in the 7th century.

- The family's name may derive from Sanskrit **Pramukha** ("leader" — the title of the monastery's chief administrator), proposed by Harold Bailey.
- **Khalid ibn Barmak** (705–782 CE) converted to Islam and rose to become **vizier** (chief minister) to the first Abbasid Caliph **al-Saffah** (r. 750–754). He was so trusted that his daughter was nursed by the Caliph's wife and vice versa.
- His son **Yahya ibn Khalid** (d. 806 CE) became vizier and tutor to the future Caliph **Harun al-Rashid** (of *One Thousand and One Nights* fame).
- **The Barmakids translated Sanskrit texts on astronomy, medicine, and mathematics into Arabic.** They patronized scholars and physicians and established Baghdad's first **paper mill** (paper technology having come from China via Central Asia).
- Yahya ibn Khalid al-Barmaki is documented as a patron of the translation of **Hindu medical works** into Arabic and Persian.
- The Barmakid court became a center of patronage for scholars, poets, and scientists.
- In **803 CE**, the family was suddenly destroyed. Ja'far ibn Yahya was beheaded by Harun al-Rashid, and the rest of the family imprisoned and their property confiscated. The reasons are debated: their wealth outshone the Caliph's, they controlled a private army of 50,000 men, and they were suspected of treason. The story features in *One Thousand and One Nights*.

**Key Source**: Kevin van Bladel, "The Bactrian Background of the Barmakids" (2011), in *Islam and Tibet: Interactions along the Musk Routes*, pp. 43–88.

### How Astronomical Tables (Zij) Traveled

The transmission of Indian astronomy to the Islamic world is one of the best-documented knowledge transfers in history:

1. **c. 770s CE**: An Indian delegation from Sindh arrives at the court of Caliph al-Mansur in Baghdad, bringing the *Sindhind* astronomical tables (based on the *Brahmasphutasiddhanta* of Brahmagupta, 628 CE).
2. **Al-Fazari** translates the *Sindhind* into Arabic — this is the first Arabic work on Indian astronomy.
3. **Al-Khwarizmi** (c. 820 CE) creates his *Zij as-Sindhind* based on these Indian tables. It contains 37 chapters and 116 tables covering calendar calculations, planetary movements, and a table of sine values.
4. The *Sindhind* system divides time into cyclic **kalpas** (cosmic creation-destruction cycles), an entirely Indian cosmological concept that entered Islamic astronomy.
5. **Ibrahim al-Fazari** and his son **Muhammad al-Fazari** were the key translators. The elder Fazari is sometimes called the first Arab astronomer.
6. The Indian **Brahmagupta** (598–668 CE) had formulated rules for working with zero, negative numbers, and quadratic equations. His *Brahmasphutasiddhanta* was translated into Arabic as *Zij al-Sindhind al-Kabir*.
7. The **Khandakhadyaka** of Brahmagupta was also translated into Arabic, influencing Islamic astronomical calculations for centuries.

### Other Documented Cases of Indian Mathematical Knowledge Reaching the Islamic World

- **Aryabhata** (476–550 CE): His *Aryabhatiya* (499 CE) calculated pi to four decimal places (3.1416), described the rotation of Earth on its axis, and gave rules for solving quadratic equations. His works were known in the Abbasid world.
- **Brahmagupta** (598–668 CE): His *Brahmasphutasiddhanta* (628 CE) was the first text to treat zero as a number in its own right and to define rules for arithmetic with zero and negative numbers. Translated into Arabic during the Abbasid era.
- **The Kerala School** (14th–16th century): Madhava of Sangamagrama (c. 1340–1425) developed infinite series for sine and cosine — essentially discovering calculus two centuries before Newton and Leibniz. However, there is no evidence these specific results were transmitted outside Kerala.

## 3. NALANDA UNIVERSITY AND INTERNATIONAL SCHOLARS

### Overview

**Nalanda Mahavihara** was founded by Emperor **Kumaragupta I** (r. 415–455 CE) of the Gupta dynasty around **427 CE** near Rajgir, Bihar. It operated for nearly **800 years** until its destruction c. 1200 CE.

- **Location**: 25°08'12"N 85°26'33"E — near modern Patna, Bihar
- **UNESCO World Heritage Site** since 2016
- Excavated 1915–1937 by the Archaeological Survey of India
- **Area**: 12 hectares (30 acres); the site measures 240m × 490m

### International Students — Countries of Origin

Nalanda attracted scholars from across the known world:

- **China**: Xuanzang (630–643 CE) and Yijing (673–695 CE) are the best documented. In the 30 years after Xuanzang's return, at least **11 travelers from China and Korea** are known to have visited Nalanda.
- **Korea**: The Silla monk **Hyon-jo** visited and stayed for **three years** at Nalanda. Korean monks **Kyom-ik** (mid-6th century) and others traveled to study and returned with texts, producing 72 *chuan* of translated works. Monks **Hye-ryun** (known by his Indian name **Prajnavarman**) and **Hyon-gak** (who died at Nalanda) are documented.
- **Tibet**: **Thonmi Sambhota** traveled to Nalanda to study Sanskrit, grammar, and other subjects. He is credited with creating the **Tibetan script** based on principles of Sanskrit grammar. Tibetan monks continued visiting through the 14th century.
- **Japan**: Indirectly via China; many Chinese monks who studied at Nalanda later transmitted knowledge to Japan.
- **Southeast Asia**: Students from Sumatra (the king of which donated villages for Nalanda's endowment), Java, and the Srivijaya Empire.
- **Central Asia**: Monks from the Kushan territories, Sogdia, and the Tarim Basin kingdoms.

### Curriculum — Subjects Taught

Nalanda's curriculum was remarkably broad:

- **Buddhist philosophy**: Madhyamaka, Yogachara, Sarvastivada
- **Vedas** (Hindu scriptures)
- **Grammar** (*Vyakarana* — Sanskrit linguistics)
- **Medicine** (*Ayurveda*)
- **Logic** (*Pramana*)
- **Mathematics** (*Ganita*)
- **Astronomy** (*Jyotisha*)
- **Alchemy**
- **Philosophy and debate**

### The Library

Nalanda's library, called **Dharmaganja** ("Treasury of Truth"), was housed in three buildings:

- **Ratnasagara** ("Ocean of Gems") — nine stories tall
- **Ratnodadhi** ("Sea of Gems")
- **Ratnaranjaka** ("Gem-Adorned")

When it was burned, the library was so vast that the manuscripts reportedly burned for **three months** (though this may be legendary exaggeration).

### Xuanzang at Nalanda (630–643 CE)

The Chinese pilgrim **Xuanzang** (602–664 CE) is our most important source. He:

- Traveled to India via the Silk Road through the Tarim Basin, crossing the Pamirs
- Spent approximately **2 years** at Nalanda (637 and 642 CE)
- Studied under **Shilabhadra**, the venerable head of the institution
- Received the Indian name **Mokshadeva**
- Studied Yogachara philosophy, grammar, logic, and Sanskrit
- Later **lectured** at the Mahavihara himself
- Returned to China in 645 CE with **657 Sanskrit texts** and **150 relics**, carried by **20 horses** in **520 cases**
- Personally translated **74 texts** from Sanskrit into Chinese

### Yijing at Nalanda (673–695 CE)

The monk **Yijing** (635–713 CE):

- Traveled via the **sea route** around Southeast Asia and Sri Lanka (unlike Xuanzang's overland route)
- Stayed **10 years** at Nalanda (673–683 CE)
- Returned to China in 695 CE with **400 Sanskrit texts** and **300 grains of Buddha relics**
- His account focuses on Buddhist monastic practices, rules, and daily procedures

### Documented Knowledge Transfer

- **To China**: Xuanzang's 657 texts and 74 translations; Yijing's 400 texts. These included works on logic, medicine, astronomy, and mathematics, not just Buddhist sutras.
- **To Korea**: Korean monks translated Indian texts brought from Nalanda into Korean, creating 72 *chuan* of translated works.
- **To Tibet**: Thonmi Sambhota created the Tibetan script based on Sanskrit principles learned at Nalanda. The Tibetan king adopted Buddhism as the state religion after Sambhota's return.
- **To Japan**: Via the Chinese Buddhist transmission — Japanese monks who studied in China were learning material that ultimately traced back to Nalanda.

### Notable Faculty

- **Nagarjuna** (c. 150–250 CE) — founder of Madhyamaka philosophy
- **Aryadeva** — Nagarjuna's student
- **Asanga** (4th century) — founder of Yogachara
- **Vasubandhu** (4th–5th century) — co-founder of Yogachara
- **Dignaga** (5th century) — founder of Buddhist logic
- **Dharmapala** — renowned scholar
- **Dharmakirti** (7th century) — logician
- **Shantideva** (8th century) — author of *Bodhicaryavatara*
- **Shilabhadra** — Xuanzang's teacher, head of Nalanda

## 4. BUDDHIST TRANSMISSION TO EAST ASIA

### Specific Monks Who Traveled

#### Bodhidharma (c. 5th–6th century CE)

- Semi-legendary Indian (or Persian/Central Asian) monk traditionally described as the **28th patriarch of Buddhism** and the **founder of Chan (Zen) Buddhism** in China
- Known in Chinese as **Putidamo** (菩提达摩), in Japanese as **Daruma** (達磨)
- The *Anthology of the Patriarchal Hall* (952 CE) states he was the third son of a great **Pallava king** of southern India
- Arrived in China during the **Liang dynasty** — the *Record of the Buddhist Monasteries of Luoyang* (547 CE) by Yang Xuanzhi describes a "Persian" (Bosi) monk named Bodhidharma
- According to later traditions (7th–10th century sources), he had a famous encounter with **Emperor Wu of Liang** that ended in mutual incomprehension
- Said to have spent **9 years in wall-gazing meditation** in a cave at **Shaolin Monastery** on Mount Song
- Credited (legendarily) with introducing **martial arts** to Shaolin monks — modern scholarship disputes this
- His teaching, the *Two Entrances and Four Practices*, was recorded by his disciple **Tanlin** (early 6th century)

#### Xuanzang (602–664 CE)

The greatest knowledge-transfer agent in pre-modern history:

- Left China secretly in **629 CE** (the Tang emperor had forbidden foreign travel)
- Traveled overland via the Silk Road through the **Gobi Desert**, **Turpan**, **Samarkand**, and the **Pamirs** to India
- Studied at Nalanda for 2 years
- Returned in 645 CE with **657 Sanskrit texts**
- Founded the **Faxiang** (Weishi) school of Chinese Buddhism
- His travelogue, *Great Tang Records on the Western Regions* (*Da Tang Xi Yu Ji*), is an invaluable historical document

#### Faxian (337–c. 422 CE)

- Chinese monk who traveled to India **399–412 CE**
- Visited major Buddhist sites but notably makes **no mention of Nalanda**, suggesting it had not yet been founded or was very small during his visit
- Returned to China via the **sea route** through Southeast Asia

#### Yijing (635–713 CE)

- Chinese monk who traveled to India **671–695 CE** via the sea route
- Studied at Nalanda for 10 years
- His *Nanhai Jigui Neifa Zhuan* is a detailed account of Buddhist practices in India

#### Other Key Figures

- **An Shigao** (2nd century CE) — Parthian prince who became a Buddhist monk and translated texts into Chinese in Luoyang. One of the earliest translators.
- **Kumarajiva** (344–413 CE) — Kuchan (Central Asian) monk of Indian parentage, one of the greatest Buddhist translators into Chinese
- **Amoghavajra** (705–774 CE) — born in Samarkand of Indian parents, became one of the most powerful Buddhist figures in Tang China

### The Translation Movement in Chinese Buddhism

The translation of Sanskrit texts into Chinese was a massive, state-sponsored enterprise:

- **148 CE**: An Shigao arrives in Luoyang, begins translating
- **2nd century CE**: Lokaksema translates Mahayana texts
- **401–413 CE**: Kumarajiva's translation bureau in Chang'an
- **645–664 CE**: Xuanzang's translation project — supported by Emperor Taizong, he personally translated 74 works
- By the Tang dynasty, the **Chinese Buddhist Canon** had grown to over **5,000 volumes**

### Evidence of Mathematical/Medical Knowledge in Buddhist Monasteries in China and Japan

- **Yi Xing** (683–727 CE) — a Chinese Buddhist monk who was also one of China's greatest astronomers and mathematicians. He calculated the length of a degree of meridian, improved the calendar, and was skilled in Indian mathematical methods.
- **Dunhuang manuscripts** (discovered 1900) include mathematical texts, medical treatises, and astronomical documents found alongside Buddhist sutras in monastery caves — proving monasteries were centers of secular knowledge.
- Japanese Buddhist monasteries preserved and transmitted knowledge from China (originally from India) — including medical traditions, calendrical science, and mathematical techniques.
- The **Shōsōin** treasury in Nara, Japan (8th century) contains Indian-derived medicinal materials and scientific instruments.

## 5. MARITIME TRADE ROUTES

### Indian Ocean Trade: Overview

The Indian Ocean was the "cradle of global trade" connecting the western and eastern worlds since antiquity. Maritime trade between the **Harappan civilization** (2600–1900 BCE) and **Mesopotamia** is archaeologically documented, with goods handled by "middlemen merchants from **Dilmun**" (modern Bahrain).

### Key Maritime Trade Connections

- **Harappan–Mesopotamian trade** (2600–1900 BCE): Evidence includes Harappan seals found in Mesopotamia and Mesopotamian texts referencing **Meluhha** (likely the Indus Valley). Coastal trading outposts at **Lothal** (Gujarat), **Sotkagen-dor**, **Sokhta Koh**, and **Balakot**.
- **Roman–Indian trade** (1st century BCE – 3rd century CE): The *Periplus of the Erythraean Sea* (c. 50 CE) documents 120 Roman ships sailing annually from **Myos Hormos** (Egypt) to India. Roman gold flowed to India in such quantities that **Pliny the Elder** complained about the drain of specie. Major Indian ports: **Muziris** (Kerala), **Arikamedu** (Tamil Nadu), **Barigaza** (Gujarat), **Barbaricum** (modern Karachi).
- **Indo-Southeast Asian trade**: From at least 3000 BCE, Austronesian trade networks connected India to Southeast Asia. The **Srivijaya Empire** (7th–13th century) controlled maritime trade between India and China from its base in Sumatra.

### Knowledge Transfer via Sea Routes

- **To Southeast Asia**: Indian Brahmi-derived scripts spread to Southeast Asia, where they became the basis for scripts in **Indonesia**, **Cambodia**, **Thailand**, **Myanmar**, and the **Philippines**. Sanskrit inscriptions found at **Vo Canh** (Vietnam, c. 3rd century CE) are among the earliest Sanskrit inscriptions outside India.
- **To Arabia**: The **Barmakid family** facilitated translation of Indian medical and astronomical texts into Arabic. The **Zij as-Sindhind** arrived from **Sindh** (Pakistan), likely via maritime or coastal routes.
- **To East Africa**: Indian trade goods, and with them cultural knowledge, reached the **Swahili coast**. The **Periplus of the Erythraean Sea** documents Indian traders at **Rhapta** (likely in modern Tanzania).

### Indian Medical Texts Influencing Arabic Medicine

- **Ibn Sina** (Avicenna, 980–1037 CE): His *Canon of Medicine* (*Al-Qanun fil-Tibb*) drew on Indian sources transmitted through the Abbasid translation movement. The Barmakid family's patronage of Indian medical translations in the 8th century made Indian medical knowledge available in Arabic.
- **Al-Razi** (Rhazes, 854–925 CE): His medical works show awareness of Indian pharmacology.
- Indian concepts of **tridosha** (three humors), pulse diagnosis, and the use of specific drugs (such as **nux vomica** for paralysis) appear in Arabic medical texts.
- The Indian surgical text **Sushruta Samhita** (c. 6th century BCE in its earliest form) described procedures including cataract surgery, rhinoplasty, and the use of over 700 medicinal plants. Elements of this knowledge reached the Islamic world.

## 6. KHAROSTHI SCRIPT AND CENTRAL ASIAN BUDDHISM

### What is Kharosthi?

**Kharosthi** is an ancient script developed in the **Gandhara** region (modern northern Pakistan/eastern Afghanistan) between the 5th and 3rd century BCE. Key facts:

- **Written right-to-left** (unlike Brahmi, which is left-to-right)
- Derived from the **Aramaic alphabet** (brought during the Achaemenid conquest of the Indus Valley, c. 500 BCE)
- Used primarily for **Gandhari Prakrit**, but also for Sanskrit, Pali, and other languages
- Named after its presumed inventor or from Sanskrit *kharoshtha* ("donkey's lip" — possibly a joke by Brahmi users)
- Also known as **Gandhari script**
- **Deciphered** independently by **James Prinsep** (1835) and **Carl Ludwig Grotefend** (1836) using bilingual Indo-Greek coins (Greek on obverse, Kharosthi on reverse)
- Added to **Unicode** in 2005 (U+10A00–U+10A5F)
- Died out in its homeland around the **5th century CE**, but survived possibly until the 7th century in **Khotan** and **Niya** in the Tarim Basin

### Where Kharosthi Was Used

- **Gandhara** (northern Pakistan)
- **Bactria** (northern Afghanistan)
- **Kushan Empire** (Afghanistan, Pakistan, northern India)
- **Sogdia** (Uzbekistan/Tajikistan)
- **Tarim Basin** (Xinjiang, China) — at Khotan, Niya, Loulan
- Along the entire **Silk Road** from India to China

### Buddhist Manuscripts Found in Central Asia (Specific Archaeological Discoveries)

#### 1. The Gandharan Buddhist Texts (British Library Collection)

- **Discovered**: Near **Hadda**, Afghanistan (just west of the Khyber Pass), donated to the British Library in **1994**
- **Date**: 1st century CE (oldest Buddhist manuscripts yet discovered)
- **Material**: **Birch bark** manuscripts written in Kharosthi script in Gandhari Prakrit
- **Contents**: Buddhist sutras including previously unknown texts
- **Significance**: Pushes back the date of written Buddhist texts by centuries; previously, the earliest known Buddhist manuscripts were from the 2nd–3rd century CE

#### 2. The Bamiyan Manuscripts

- Fragments of Buddhist manuscripts found in caves near the **Bamiyan** valley in Afghanistan (where the giant Buddhas stood before their destruction by the Taliban in 2001)
- Written in multiple scripts including Kharosthi, Brahmi, and Sanskrit
- Date from the 1st to 7th century CE

#### 3. The Niya Documents

- Wooden tablets with Kharosthi writing found at **Niya** in the Tarim Basin (Xinjiang, China)
- **Date**: 3rd century CE
- **Contents**: Administrative documents, legal contracts, and Buddhist texts in Gandhari Prakrit
- **Significance**: Proves that Indian administrative and legal systems, along with Buddhism, were in use deep in Central Asia
- Now in the **National Museum of India**, New Delhi

#### 4. The Dunhuang Manuscripts

- **Discovered**: **June 25, 1900** by Daoist monk **Wang Yuanlu** in the **Library Cave** (Cave 17) of the **Mogao Caves** near Dunhuang, Gansu Province, China
- **Contents**: Approximately **50,000 manuscripts** dating from the late 4th to early 11th centuries
- **Languages**: Chinese, Tibetan, Sanskrit, Khotanese, Sogdian, Old Uyghur, Prakrit, Hebrew, and others
- **Subjects**: Buddhist sutras (majority), but also medicine, mathematics, astronomy, history, literature, contracts, and Nestorian Christian, Manichaean, and Jewish texts
- **Acquired by**: **Aurel Stein** (British — ~7,000 manuscripts + 6,000 fragments, for £130) and **Paul Pelliot** (French — ~10,000 documents, for £90) in 1907–1908
- Now scattered across the **British Library**, **Bibliothèque nationale de France**, **National Library of China** (Beijing), and other institutions
- The cave was sealed sometime in the early **11th century**, possibly to protect manuscripts from an invasion that never came

#### 5. The Spli Pa-ts'ag Manuscript

- Discovered at the **Tabo Monastery** in Himachal Pradesh, India
- Contains early Tibetan Buddhist texts translating Indian originals

### The Silk Road Buddhist Kingdoms

| Kingdom | Period | Location | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Kushan Empire** | 30–375 CE | Afghanistan/Pakistan/northern India | Primary conduit of Buddhism to China |
| **Khotan** | c. 56–1006 CE | Tarim Basin (Xinjiang) | Major Buddhist kingdom; center of Sanskrit-to-Khotanese translation |
| **Kucha** | 1st–8th century CE | Tarim Basin | Home of **Kumarajiva**; important translation center |
| **Turfan** | 1st–14th century CE | Tarim Basin | Manichaean, Buddhist, and Nestorian texts found |
| **Sogdia** | Antiquity–8th century CE | Uzbekistan/Tajikistan | Sogdian merchants were the primary Silk Road traders; Sogdian language was the lingua franca of the Silk Road |
| **Bactria** | Antiquity–Islamic conquest | Northern Afghanistan | Greek-Buddhist synthesis (Greco-Buddhist art); the Nava Vihara monastery (home of the Barmakids) |

## 7. SPECIFIC ARCHAEOLOGICAL EVIDENCE

### Indian Inscriptions Found Outside India (Along the Silk Road)

1. **Kharosthi inscriptions at Niya** (Tarim Basin, Xinjiang, China) — 3rd century CE, administrative documents in Gandhari Prakrit
2. **Kharosthi inscriptions at **Loulan** (Tarim Basin) — proving Indian administrative influence deep in Central Asia
3. **Kharosthi coin legends** — Indo-Greek, Indo-Scythian, and Kushan coins bear bilingual inscriptions (Greek + Kharosthi)
4. **Sanskrit inscription at Vo Canh** (Vietnam) — c. 3rd century CE, earliest Sanskrit inscription in Southeast Asia
5. **Ashokan edicts at Kandahar** (Afghanistan) — bilingual Greek-Aramaic
6. **Ashokan edicts at Mansehra and Shahbazgarhi** (Pakistan) — in Kharosthi script
7. **Sanskrit inscriptions at Borobudur** (Java, Indonesia) — 8th–9th century CE
8. **Sanskrit inscriptions at Angkor Wat** (Cambodia) — showing Indian cultural influence

### Buddhist Cave Temples and Monasteries Along Trade Routes

| Site | Location | Period | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bamiyan Caves** | Afghanistan | 3rd–7th century CE | Giant Buddha statues (destroyed 2001); cave paintings show Indian, Persian, Greek influences; Buddhist manuscripts found |
| **Mogao Caves (Dunhuang)** | Gansu, China | 4th–14th century CE | 735 caves; 45,000 sq m of murals; Library Cave (Cave 17) with ~50,000 manuscripts; UNESCO World Heritage Site |
| **Ajanta Caves** | Maharashtra, India | 2nd century BCE – 5th century CE | 29 Buddhist cave temples; paintings show Persian and Greek influence; UNESCO World Heritage Site |
| **Yungang Grottoes** | Shanxi, China | 5th–6th century CE | 252 caves with 51,000 statues; shows strong Indian and Central Asian artistic influence |
| **Longmen Grottoes** | Henan, China | 493–late 7th century CE | 100,000+ statues in 2,345 caves |
| **Maijishan Grottoes** | Gansu, China | 384–417 CE onwards | "Oriental Sculpture Museum"; 7,800+ Buddhist sculptures |

### Key Manuscripts Discovered at Silk Road Sites

1. **Gandharan Buddhist Texts** (British Library, 1994) — 1st century CE birch bark manuscripts in Kharosthi
2. **Dunhuang Library Cave** (1900) — ~50,000 manuscripts in 17 languages
3. **Diamond Sutra** (Dunhuang, Cave 17) — **868 CE**, the world's oldest dated printed book (now in the British Library)
4. **Bakhshali Manuscript** (1881, near Peshawar, Pakistan) — birch bark mathematical text, likely 7th century CE, contains early use of zero as a dot
5. **Niya wooden tablets** — Kharosthi administrative documents from the Tarim Basin
6. **Tabo Monastery manuscripts** — early Tibetan Buddhist translations of Indian texts
7. **Tibetan Dunhuang manuscripts** — earliest examples of Tibetan writing (8th–10th century), including texts of early Tibetan tantra

## 8. HISTORICAL KNOWLEDGE DESTRUCTION EVENTS

### The Burning of Nalanda (c. 1193–1200 CE)

- **Perpetrator**: **Muhammad Bakhtiyar Khilji**, a Turkic-Afghan general of the Ghurid Sultanate
- **Date**: Traditional accounts say 1193 CE, but recent scholarship suggests c. 1200 CE or possibly that the attack was less destructive than legend claims
- **What happened**: According to the Tibetan historian **Taranatha** (1575–1634) and Persian sources, Khilji's forces attacked Nalanda. The library was set on fire. Legend says it burned for **three months**.
- **Reality**: Recent scholarship by **William Dalrymple** and others suggests Nalanda may have remained operational for decades or even centuries after the raids. Archaeological evidence shows some structures were rebuilt. But the destruction marked the beginning of the end.
- **What was lost**: The Dharmaganja library contained thousands of manuscripts on Buddhism, Hinduism, logic, mathematics, astronomy, medicine, and grammar. The vast majority of these texts exist today only in Chinese and Tibetan translations — the Sanskrit originals were destroyed.
- **The last abbot**: **Dharmasvamin** visited Nalanda in **1234 CE** and found it still functioning in a reduced state, with a small group of monks continuing their studies under threat.

### Destruction of Buddhist Monasteries in Central Asia After Islamic Conquest

The advance of Islam into Central Asia (7th–10th centuries) led to the gradual destruction of Buddhist institutions:

- **Balkh** (Afghanistan): The **Nava Vihara** monastery, home of the Barmakids, was eventually destroyed. The Barmakids had already converted to Islam by the mid-8th century, but the monastery persisted until later Islamic campaigns.
- **Bamiyan** (Afghanistan): The Buddhist complex survived in reduced form until the Islamic period. The giant Buddha statues (55m and 38m tall, dating from the 3rd–6th century CE) survived until **March 2001**, when the **Taliban** dynamited them.
- **Khotan** (Tarim Basin): The Buddhist kingdom of Khotan fell to the **Karakhanid** Turkic Muslims in **1006 CE** after a long struggle. This event is one of the reasons the Dunhuang Library Cave may have been sealed.
- **Kucha** (Tarim Basin): The Buddhist kingdom gradually converted to Islam after the 10th century.
- **Sogdia** (Uzbekistan): Buddhist, Zoroastrian, and Manichaean temples were destroyed during the Arab conquest (early 8th century).

### Loss of the Library of Alexandria as Parallel

- The **Library of Alexandria** (Egypt) suffered multiple destructive events: Julius Caesar's fire (48 BCE), Aurelian's sack (270 CE), Theophilus's decree (391 CE), and the Arab conquest (642 CE).
- Like Nalanda, the loss of Alexandria represents the destruction of accumulated knowledge that set back human intellectual progress by centuries.
- **Key parallel**: In both cases, the destruction wasn't a single event but a process of decline punctuated by catastrophic episodes. Both involved the loss of irreplaceable manuscripts whose contents we can only glimpse through later translations.

### Other Knowledge Destruction Events

- **4th century CE**: Hun invasion under **Mihirakula** damaged Nalanda
- **8th century CE**: Gauda king of Bengal attacked Nalanda
- **1193–1200 CE**: Bakhtiyar Khilji's raid on Nalanda
- **1193 CE**: Vikramashila University (another great Buddhist center, founded by Dharmapala c. 770 CE) was also destroyed by Khilji
- **1197 CE**: Odantapura University was destroyed
- **1200s**: Jagaddala University destroyed by Islamic invaders
- **1006 CE**: Fall of Buddhist Khotan to Karakhanids
- **2001 CE**: Taliban destruction of Bamiyan Buddhas
- **2016–2018**: IS/Daesh destruction of Palmyra and other ancient sites in Syria and Iraq

## THRILLER NOVEL "FACT" PAGE — HIGHLIGHTS

For a thriller's opening "Fact" page, the most compelling items:

1. **The Barmakid family** — Buddhist monks who became the most powerful family in the Islamic world (viziers to Harun al-Rashid) and translated Indian science into Arabic before being suddenly destroyed in 803 CE. Their story has espionage, betrayal, and hidden knowledge.

2. **Nalanda's library** burned for months, destroying manuscripts that represented centuries of mathematical, medical, and astronomical knowledge — some of which we know existed only because Chinese and Tibetan translations survived.

3. **The Dunhuang Library Cave** was sealed around 1006 CE and not opened until 1900 — preserving 50,000 manuscripts in 17 languages. The reason for its sealing is still debated: was it fear of invasion, or simply running out of room?

4. **Al-Khwarizmi** built his revolutionary works on algebra and arithmetic explicitly on Indian foundations — the Sanskrit texts brought from Sindh to Baghdad. The words "algorithm" and "algebra" both derive from this one man working in the House of Wisdom.

5. **Xuanzang** carried 657 Sanskrit texts back to China on 20 horses — a 17-year journey across deserts and mountains, defying an imperial ban on foreign travel.

6. **The Gandharan Buddhist texts** (1st century CE) are the oldest Buddhist manuscripts ever discovered — found near the Khyber Pass and donated to the British Library only in 1994.

7. **Kharosthi script** — written right-to-left, derived from Aramaic, used across the Silk Road — was completely forgotten for over a thousand years until deciphered in 1835–36.

8. **The zero** — arguably the most important mathematical invention in history — was first used as a number in its own right by the Indian mathematician **Brahmagupta** in 628 CE. His work was translated into Arabic, and from there it eventually reached Europe.
