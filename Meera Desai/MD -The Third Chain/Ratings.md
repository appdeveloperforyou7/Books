### Verdict
**TTC already surpasses DVC on ~7 of 12 dimensions** — prose, research, character, antagonist depth, theme, climax, set-piece consequence. A discerning reader will rate it *above* DVC.

**DVC still leads on raw page-turn RPM, hook virality, and accessibility** — Brown's *engine*, which is what sold 80M copies. TTC's engine now fires (antagonist in parallel ✅, set-piece ✅, sharpened hook ✅, resisting antagonist ✅); the last gap is **a few more chapter hooks + the final voice pass**.

### Recommended positioning
- **Logline:** *The cure has existed for two thousand three hundred years. So has the reason no one ever received it.*
- **Back-cover hook:** *What if the greatest library in history isn't lost — it's just been locked, by people who decided the world wasn't ready?*
- **Pitch line:** *For readers of Dan Brown — a thriller about the secret Ashoka scattered across the ancient world, and the woman who must decide whether the world has waited long enough.*
- **Don't** sell as "Da Vinci Code but Indian" — that undersells the research and the secrecy-vs-liberation debate, which are *better* than DVC's. Sell it as **smarter**.

## The three diseases (and their status)

Every weakness traced to one of three roots:

**Disease 1 — Narrated subtext** (the text explains gestures/tones the instant it produces them). *Status: ✅ substantially fixed* — the textbook instances cut (Priya's glasses-habit explained in-narration, the static "that spoke to the distance," the "voice of a man who…" decoding in lead scenes); a handful of gesture-decodings remain.

**Disease 2 — Passive protagonist; plot arrives by phone.** *Status: ◐ largely fixed* — Meera now traces Prometheus, cracks the Sarnath & Kurien clues herself, and is paralleled by an active, resisting antagonist; 1–2 phone-reveal chapters remain.

**Disease 3 — The climax is a non-event; the real climax buried.** *Status: ✅ FIXED* — the irreversible partial leak is the dramatized climax; the lock contrivance is gone; speeches fragmented under real pressure.
