# 1. THE VATICAN APOSTOLIC ARCHIVE

## 1.1 What It Is

The **Vatican Apostolic Archive** (Latin: *Archivum Apostolicum Vaticanum*; Italian: *Archivio Apostolico Vaticano*) was known as the **Vatican Secret Archive** until **October 28, 2019**, when Pope Francis issued a *motu proprio* renaming it. The word "secret" (*secretum*) did not mean "classified" in the modern sense — it was closer to "private," indicating the archive was the Pope's personal property rather than that of any Roman Curia department. As one 1969 study noted, the archive was "an extensive buried city, a Herculaneum inundated by the lava of time... secret as an archaeological dig is secret."

**Key dates:**
- **1612**: Pope Paul V ordered all Church records assembled in one place, separating the archive from the Vatican Library
- **1881**: Pope Leo XIII opened the archive to researchers (January 1)
- **2019**: Pope Francis renamed it from "Secret" to "Apostolic" Archive

## 1.2 Size and Scale

- **85 kilometers (53 miles) of shelving**
- **35,000 volumes** in the selective catalogue alone
- An estimated **16 million pages** of documents from Pope Pius XII's pontificate alone (1939-1958)
- Materials spanning from the **8th century to the present**
- The archive also contains state papers, correspondence, papal account books, and other documents accumulated over centuries

**Notable holdings:**
- Complete papal registers from **Pope Innocent III** (1198-1216) onward
- Some registers of earlier popes survive: **Pope John VIII** (872-882), **Pope Gregory VII** (1073-1085)
- Henry VIII's request for marriage annulment
- Handwritten transcript of the trial of **Galileo** for heresy
- Letters from **Michelangelo** complaining about not being paid for Sistine Chapel work
- The 1521 papal bull excommunicating **Martin Luther**
- Letter from **Mary, Queen of Scots**, written while awaiting execution
- Correspondence with figures including Mozart, Erasmus, Charlemagne, Voltaire, and Adolf Hitler

## 1.3 Physical Description and Location

- Located at **Cortile del Belvedere** in Vatican City (41°54′17″N 12°27′17″E)
- Entrance is through the **Porta di Santa Anna** in via di Porta Angelica (Borgo rione of Rome), adjacent to the Vatican Library
- In **1980**, new **underground storage space** was added with modern renovations — a concrete bunker, part of a wing behind St. Peter's Basilica
- Protected by **Swiss Guards** and officers from the Vatican City's own police force (Gendarmerie)
- The archive supports its own **photographic and conservation studios**
- As of 2018: **180 terabytes** of digital storage capacity, over **7 million images** digitized

## 1.4 The Reading Room — What It Looks Like

The reading room is a hushed, austere space — **long wooden tables** under warm lighting, with researchers working in near-silence. The atmosphere is one of intense scholarly concentration, broken only by the turning of pages and the soft scratch of pencils (no pens or ink are permitted). The walls are lined with reference volumes. Researchers work at individual carrels. The room has a distinctly ecclesiastical feel — high ceilings, marble elements, the institutional gravitas of centuries. It smells of old paper, leather bindings, and the faintly antiseptic air of climate-controlled archives.

For the novel: Imagine Father Kurien sitting at one of these tables, a pair of **white cotton gloves** on his hands, a crumbling vellum document before him. Around him, perhaps a dozen other scholars — Italians, Germans, Americans — each lost in their own century. The light is functional but not harsh. A Swiss Guard stands at the entrance. The silence is almost physical.

## 1.5 Access Rules

- Scholars need an **introductory letter** from a recognized research institution or qualified person
- Must provide personal data and purpose of research
- Only **paper, pencil, and laptop computers** permitted — no ink, pens, or digital cameras
- Only **five requested items** at a time
- Maximum **60 academicians per day**
- Materials dated after **1939** were unavailable until March 2, 2020 (when Pius XII materials opened)
- An entire section relating to personal affairs of Cardinals from **1922 onwards** remains inaccessible

## 1.6 How a Researcher Like Father Kurien Would Work

Father Kurien would:
1. Apply for an **entry card** (*tessera d'ingresso*) with a letter from his seminary, university, or a bishop
2. Arrive at the **Porta di Santa Anna** entrance, show identification to Swiss Guards
3. Proceed to the reading room, deposit bags and pens in lockers
4. Submit **request slips** for specific documents — he would need to know the archival reference numbers (fondi, serie, rubriche)
5. Wait for documents to be retrieved by archive staff from the underground bunkers
6. Work at a designated table, taking notes in **pencil only**
7. Documents arrive in archival boxes or wrapped in acid-free paper
8. He could request up to **5 items at a time**, with a daily limit

The key for the novel: Kurien would need to know exactly what he was looking for. The archive is **not browsable** — you cannot wander the stacks. You must request specific documents by reference number, which means either prior knowledge of the cataloguing system or months of preliminary research.

## 1.7 Famous/Sensitive Documents

- **The trial of the Knights Templar** (1307-1312) — including Chinon parchment
- **Galileo's trial transcript** (1633)
- **Henry VIII's annulment request** (Catherine of Aragon)
- **Luther's excommunication** bull *Decet Romanum Pontificem* (1521)
- **Letters from Abraham Lincoln** and **Jefferson Davis** to the Pope during the American Civil War
- **The Papal Bulls** concerning the division of the New World (Treaty of Tordesillas era)
- **Correspondence with the Japanese daimyōs** from the Christian Century (1549-1639)
- **Reports from Jesuit missions** across Asia — India, Japan, China
- Documents relating to the **Goa Inquisition**
- Records of the **Synod of Diamper** (1599)
- The **Pius XII wartime archives** (opened March 2020) — correspondence regarding the Holocaust

# 2. JESUIT MISSIONS TO INDIA

## 2.1 St. Francis Xavier in Goa (1542 onwards)

**Francis Xavier** (born Francisco de Jasso y Azpilicueta, 1506-1552) was one of the co-founders of the Society of Jesus. He arrived in **Goa on May 6, 1542**, at the age of 35, as Papal Nuncio to the East.

Key facts:
- Traveled throughout Portuguese India, Malabar, the Coromandel Coast, Maluku Islands, and Japan
- Learned enough of local languages to instruct, but never achieved fluency
- Initially used **Goa's Royal College of Santa Fe** as his base
- Baptized thousands along the **Fishery Coast** (Tamil Nadu) in 1542-1544
- In 1545, wrote to **King John III of Portugal** requesting the establishment of the Goan Inquisition
- Left Goa in 1545 for Malacca, then Japan (1549), and died on **Sancian Island** near China on December 3, 1552
- His **incorrupt body** is preserved in the **Basilica of Bom Jesus** in Old Goa — a major pilgrimage site
- Canonized in 1622

**For the novel:** Xavier's letters back to Rome are preserved in Jesuit archives and Vatican collections. They describe India, its peoples, customs, and religions in vivid detail. A researcher like Kurien would know these intimately.

## 2.2 Roberto de Nobili and the Madurai Mission

**Roberto de Nobili** (1577-1656), an Italian Jesuit, arrived in Goa in 1605 and moved to **Madurai in 1606**. He was nicknamed **"the White Brahmin"** for his revolutionary methods:

- Called himself a **"teacher of wisdom"** (தத்துவ போதகர்)
- Dressed as a **Sannyasi** (Hindu ascetic) — white dhoti, wooden sandals, shaved head with a tuft
- Wore a **three-stringed thread** across his chest representing the Holy Trinity
- Claimed noble parentage to approach high-caste people as an equal
- Mastered **Sanskrit, Telugu, and Tamil** — Max Müller called him "the First European Sanskrit scholar"
- Appropriated Hindu terms for Christian concepts: *kovil* for church, *arul* for grace, *guru* for priest, *Vedam* for the Bible
- Composed catechisms and philosophical discourses in Tamil
- His method was **controversial** — opposed by Archbishop Cristóvão de Sá of Goa
- **Pope Gregory XV** approved his methods in 1623 (apostolic constitution *Romanæ Sedis Antistes*)
- Died in Mylapore (Chennai) on January 16, 1656

**For the novel:** De Nobili's writings about Hindu philosophy, the Vedas, and Indian intellectual traditions would be preserved in Jesuit archives. His method of **accommodation** (*accomodatio*) meant he studied Indian texts intensively. Reports from his mission would document Indian mathematical concepts, astronomical knowledge, and philosophical systems.

## 2.3 Jesuit Documentation of Indian Mathematics, Astronomy, and Science

The Jesuits were **systematic documenters** of local knowledge:
- **Jesuit missionaries sent annual letters** (*Litterae Annuae*) from their missions to Rome, detailing everything they observed
- These reports included descriptions of Indian **astronomical systems** (Jyotisha), mathematical techniques, and medical knowledge
- **Garcia de Orta** (1501-1568), a Portuguese Sephardic Jewish physician in Goa, wrote *Colóquios dos simples e drogas da India* (1563) — a groundbreaking work on Indian pharmacology and medicine. He was later posthumously convicted by the Goa Inquisition.
- Jesuits documented the **Kerala School of Mathematics** — though the extent of their understanding of Madhava's work on calculus remains debated
- Reports on **Indian calendrical systems**, eclipse prediction methods, and astronomical instruments were sent to Rome
- Jesuit astronomers like **Jean Richaud** (arrived India 1689) studied Indian astronomical texts
- In the 18th century, Jesuit missionaries in India corresponded with European mathematicians about Indian computational methods

## 2.4 The Controversy of the Rites (Accommodation with Hindu Practices)

The **Malabar Rites controversy** paralleled the Chinese Rites controversy:
- De Nobili's accommodation approach — allowing converts to retain caste marks, thread, sandal paste — was challenged
- The debate centered on whether Hindu **social customs** (like caste distinctions) could be separated from **religious practice**
- **Pope Gregory XV** approved de Nobili's methods in 1623
- But later, under **Pope Benedict XIV** (1740s), accommodation was increasingly restricted
- The **Chinese Rites** were formally condemned in 1704 and 1715, and the same spirit eventually affected Indian missions
- By the late 18th century, much of the accommodation had been rolled back

## 2.5 What Specific Knowledge Did Jesuits Record About Indian Culture?

Jesuit records documented:
- **Religious practices**: Detailed descriptions of Hindu temple rituals, festival celebrations (including Onam), funeral customs, marriage ceremonies
- **Social structures**: Caste system, particularly Brahminical traditions
- **Languages**: Grammars and dictionaries of Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam, Konkani, Sanskrit
- **Philosophical systems**: Studies of Vedanta, Nyaya, Mimamsa schools
- **Scientific knowledge**: Astronomical tables, mathematical methods, medical practices (Ayurveda)
- **Agricultural practices**: Irrigation systems, crop varieties
- **Political structures**: Descriptions of various Indian kingdoms and their administrations
- **Architecture**: Temple architecture, city planning

## 2.6 The Goa Inquisition — What Was Documented

The **Goa Inquisition** (established 1560, abolished 1812):
- **Francis Xavier** requested its establishment in a 1545 letter to King John III
- The first inquisitor was **Aleixo Díaz Falcão**
- The Inquisition was housed in the **former palace of Sultan Adil Shah**
- Between 1561-1623 alone, approximately **3,800 cases** were brought
- Over 250 years: approximately **57 people** were executed by burning, **64** burned in effigy; **4,046** received various punishments
- The majority of those sentenced (~74%) were charged with **Crypto-Hinduism** — practicing Hindu rituals privately while nominally Christian
- **Sanskrit and Konkani books were burned**
- **Hindu temples were demolished** — a 1569 royal letter records all Hindu temples in Portuguese colonies had been destroyed (*desfeitos e queimados*)
- **Most records were destroyed** by Portuguese officials when the Inquisition ended in 1812 — making complete historical assessment difficult

**For the novel:** This is critical. The destruction of records means that what survives in the Vatican Archive represents only a **fraction** of what was once known. But the Vatican would have received **copies of key reports**, **annual letters from missionaries**, and **correspondence about the Inquisition's proceedings**. Father Kurien, as a researcher, might discover references to documents that no longer exist in Goa but whose **copies or summaries** survive in Rome.

## 2.7 What Happened to Jesuit Records from India?

- The Jesuit **annual letters** (*Litterae Annuae*) from Indian missions were systematically sent to Rome and are preserved in the **Jesuit Roman Archives** (ARSI — *Archivum Romanum Societatis Iesu*)
- When the **Society of Jesus was suppressed** (1773-1814), many local Jesuit archives in India were scattered or destroyed
- Some records passed to other religious orders (Carmelites, Franciscans) who took over Jesuit missions
- Portuguese colonial records relating to Jesuit activities were partially preserved in the **Torre do Tombo** archive in Lisbon
- The **Vatican Archive** contains papal correspondence with Indian missions, reports from bishops, and records of the Propaganda Fide (Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples)
- Many records from the **Madurai Mission** survive in the Roman archives of the Society of Jesus

# 3. JESUIT MISSIONS TO JAPAN

## 3.1 Francis Xavier in Japan (1549)

- Xavier arrived in **Kagoshima** on **July 27, 1549**, accompanied by **Anjirō** (a Japanese convert he met in Malacca), Father **Cosme de Torres**, and Brother **João Fernandes**
- Initially used the word **"Dainichi"** (Vairocana Buddha) for the Christian God — a confusion with Shingon Buddhism
- When he realized this was a Buddhist concept, he changed to **"Deusu"** from Latin/Portuguese *Deus*
- Stayed in Japan for **approximately 2 years and 3 months** (August 1549 to November 1551)
- Preached in Kagoshima, Yamaguchi, and briefly visited Kyoto (failed to meet the Emperor)
- Lacking Japanese fluency, he had to read from a Japanese translation of a catechism
- Left Japan in 1551, leaving behind an established community of converts
- By 1579, there were approximately **130,000 converts**
- By 1603: an estimated **200,000-300,000 converts**, 190 churches, and 122 Jesuits

## 3.2 The "Christian Century" in Japan (1549-1639)

This remarkable period saw:
- The conversion of several **daimyōs** (feudal lords), especially in **Kyushu**
- **Ōmura Sumitada** — first Japanese daimyō to convert (Don Bartolomeu)
- **Ōtomo Sōrin** — powerful daimyō of Bungo who converted
- The rise of **Nagasaki** as a Christian city (founded c. 1570, called "the Rome of Japan")
- By 1590: **70 native Jesuit brothers** — half of all Jesuits in Japan
- By 1614: Jesuits possessed **11 colleges, 64 residences, 2 novitiates, and 2 seminaries**
- **Alessandro Valignano** (1539-1606) supervised Far East missions and promoted deep cultural accommodation (*Accomodatio*)
- Valignano organized the **Tenshō Embassy** (1582-1590) — four Japanese Christian boys sent to Rome to meet the Pope
- The mission had its own printing press — the **Kirishitan press** — which produced religious texts in romanized Japanese

**Jesuit reports about Japanese knowledge:**
- Detailed descriptions of Japanese **writing systems** (kanji, kana)
- Reports on Japanese **metallurgy**, architecture, and military technology
- Descriptions of Japanese **social organization**, legal codes (Ritsuryō)
- Observations on Japanese **Buddhism** and Shinto practices
- Documentation of the **Japanese tea ceremony**, poetry, and arts
- Reports on Japanese **medical practices** and herbal knowledge

## 3.3 Suppression of Christianity in Japan (1614-1639)

- **1587**: Toyotomi Hideyoshi issued the **Bateren Edict** expelling Jesuits
- **1597**: **26 Christians crucified** in Nagasaki (the 26 Martyrs of Japan)
- **1614**: Tokugawa Ieyasu issued a **comprehensive ban** on Christianity
- **1622**: **55 Christians executed** in Nagasaki (the Great Martyrdom)
- **1637-1638**: **Shimabara Rebellion** — Christian peasants revolted, were crushed; approximately **37,000 killed**
- **1639**: Japan entered **sakoku** (closed country) — complete isolation from the West

## 3.4 Kakure Kirishitan (Hidden Christians)

- After 1639, Japanese Christians went **underground**
- Modified religious practices to resemble **Buddhist rituals** on the surface
- Created **"Maria Kannon"** — statues of the Virgin Mary disguised as the Buddhist deity Kannon (Avalokiteśvara)
- Prayers were adapted to sound like **Buddhist chants** while retaining untranslated Latin, Portuguese, and Spanish words
- The **Bible and liturgy were passed down orally** for over 200 years
- In **1865**, hidden Christians revealed themselves to Father **Bernard Petitjean** at **Ōura Church** in Nagasaki
- Approximately **30,000** secret Christians emerged when religious freedom was re-established in **1873**
- Some refused to rejoin the Catholic Church — becoming **Hanare Kirishitan** ("separated Christians")
- As of 2025: fewer than **100** Hidden Christians remain on Ikitsuki Island in Nagasaki
- Their faith is described as "barely recognizable as the creed imported in the mid-1500s"

**What records survive from the Japanese mission:**
- Jesuit **annual letters** from Japan (sent to Rome before the suppression)
- The **Kirishitan press** books (about 30 known surviving works)
- Documents preserved in **Lisbon, Madrid, Rome, and Macau**
- Vatican correspondence regarding Japanese missions
- Luís Fróis's **History of Japan** — a massive Jesuit history of the mission, partially published
- Valignano's **reports and instructions** for the Japanese mission
- Records of the **Tenshō Embassy** to Rome

# 4. THE VATICAN'S KNOWLEDGE OF ASIAN HERITAGE

## 4.1 Did the Vatican Know About Indian Mathematical/Scientific Traditions?

**Yes, but selectively and imperfectly.**
- Jesuit missionaries were the Vatican's primary intelligence network for Asian knowledge
- Reports on Indian astronomy and mathematics were sent to Rome throughout the 16th-18th centuries
- The **Kerala School of Mathematics** — which had developed infinite series, calculus-like concepts, and precise astronomical calculations centuries before Newton and Leibniz — was known to local scholars in Kerala, where the Jesuits were active
- Debate among historians: Did Jesuit missionaries transmit Kerala mathematical concepts to Europe? Some scholars (like George Gheverghese Joseph) argue this is plausible; others are skeptical
- What is certain: Jesuits in Kerala documented local astronomical methods and calendrical calculations
- The Vatican maintained the **Gregorian calendar reform** (1582) and was deeply interested in astronomical knowledge for calendrical purposes
- **Jesuit astronomers** studied at the **Collegio Romano** and were sent worldwide — they were the Vatican's scientific eyes

## 4.2 What Did the Vatican Know About Buddhism?

- **Francis Xavier** encountered Buddhism first in Japan (1549) and initially confused it with Christianity
- The Jesuit **Cosme de Torres** and later missionaries described Buddhist doctrines in detail in their reports
- **Roberto de Nobili** studied Hinduism intensively but also encountered Buddhist ideas in South Indian texts
- By the 17th century, the Vatican had **significant documentation** of Buddhist beliefs from Japanese and Southeast Asian missions
- However, this knowledge was filtered through **missionary and theological lenses** — Buddhism was studied to understand how to convert Buddhists, not as a subject of dispassionate scholarship
- The Vatican Library holds manuscripts related to Buddhism collected over centuries

## 4.3 Vatican-Sponsored Studies of Asian Manuscripts

- The **Propaganda Fide** (Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples, founded 1622) sponsored language study and manuscript collection
- The **Collegio Romano** trained missionaries in Asian languages
- Pope Nicholas V (1447-1455) had already envisioned a "public library" that would include works from all traditions
- The Vatican Library employed **Italian and Byzantine scholars** to translate Greek classics; similar efforts were later made for Syriac, Arabic, and some Asian texts
- The **Ezourvedam** — a purported Vedic text that was actually a French missionary forgery — shows the complex interplay between Vatican-affiliated scholars and Hindu scriptures

## 4.4 The Vatican Library's Asian Manuscript Collections

The Vatican Library holds:
- **75,000 codices** (manuscripts) from throughout history
- **1.1 million printed books**, including **8,500 incunabula** (pre-1501 printed books)
- Significant collections in **Hebrew, Arabic, Persian, Turkish, and Syriac**
- **Chinese manuscripts** — including materials from Jesuit missions to China (Matteo Ricci and successors)
- **Japanese manuscripts** — materials from the Christian Century
- **Indian manuscripts** — including works in Sanskrit, Tamil, Malayalam, and Syriac collected by missionaries
- The **Barberini Library** (acquired 1902) contains additional Oriental manuscripts
- The **Borgian Museum** collection includes materials from Asian missions

## 4.5 Did Vatican-Affiliated Scholars Study Brahmi or Other Indian Scripts?

- **Roberto de Nobili** was the first European known to have studied Sanskrit systematically (early 17th century)
- **Jean Calmette** (1692-1740), a French Jesuit, collected Sanskrit manuscripts and was likely the author of the Ezourvedam
- **Jean François Pons** (1688-1752), a French Jesuit in Bengal, studied Sanskrit and compiled a Sanskrit dictionary
- The **discovery of the relationship between Brahmi and other scripts** came later (primarily through British Orientalists in the late 18th and early 19th centuries)
- However, Jesuit missionaries in India would have encountered **Brahmi-derived scripts** (Tamil, Malayalam, Devanagari) and documented them in reports sent to Rome
- The **Vatican Codex** collection includes palm-leaf manuscripts from Kerala in various scripts

# 5. THE BENEDICTINE/CISTERCIAN CONNECTION TO CENTRAL ASIA

## 5.1 Catholic Missions to Central Asia (19th-20th Centuries)

- **Limited but real** Catholic presence in Central Asia during this period
- The **Russian Empire** controlled much of Central Asia by the late 19th century; Catholic (primarily Polish and German) communities existed within it
- The **Vatican established apostolic administrations** for Central Asian territories in the early 20th century
- **Benedictine monks** had a tradition of scholarship and mission that extended eastward
- The **Abbey of Monte Cassino** and other Benedictine institutions maintained scholarly networks
- In the 1920s, the Soviet Union's anti-religious campaigns made any Catholic mission to Central Asia extremely dangerous
- However, individual monks and priests did travel in the region — often under cover of scholarly or diplomatic purposes

## 5.2 Benedictine Scholars in the East

- **Benedictine monasticism** has a scholarly tradition stretching back to the 6th century
- The **Cassinese Congregation** and other Benedictine federations maintained libraries and scriptoria
- Individual Benedictines participated in **Oriental studies** throughout the 19th and early 20th centuries
- The **Benedictine mission to China** was established in the early 20th century
- There were Benedictine communities in **Mongolia** and regions adjacent to Central Asia
- The order's motto *Ora et Labora* (Pray and Work) encompassed intellectual labor

## 5.3 What Would a 1924 Report from a Benedictine Monk in Central Asia Look Like?

A plausible document for the novel:

- **Format**: Handwritten in **Latin or ecclesiastical Italian**, on monastery-issued paper
- **Structure**: Formal address to the Abbot or to the Vatican's Propaganda Fide, dated by both Gregorian calendar and liturgical year
- **Content**: Description of local conditions — geography, peoples, languages, religious practices (Buddhist, Islamic, shamanistic), any Christian communities discovered, ancient manuscripts or inscriptions encountered
- **Encoding**: If sensitive (given Soviet surveillance), might use coded language or ecclesiastical euphemisms
- **Signatures**: Would bear the monk's religious name, seal, and possibly the abbot's countersignature
- **Physical form**: Could be a **letter** (2-4 pages), a **relation** (longer report, 10-20 pages), or a **memorandum** to a specific Vatican dicastery

## 5.4 How Would Such a Report Be Catalogued in the Vatican Archive?

The Vatican Archive uses a complex cataloguing system:
- **Fondi** (collections/funds) — major groupings by originating office or collection
- Within each *fondo*: **serie** (series), **rubriche** (rubrics), **pezze** (individual items)
- A 1924 Benedictine report could be found in:
  - **Fondo Propaganda Fide** — if it was sent through the Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples
  - **Fondo SS (Secretariat of State)** — if it dealt with diplomatic or political matters
  - **Fondo Borgja** or **Fondo Barberini** — if connected to older collection streams
  - A special **Benedictine collection** if the order maintained its own archive deposited at the Vatican
- The document would be catalogued by **date, provenance, and subject**
- Since materials after 1922 relating to Cardinals' personal affairs are **restricted**, this could add narrative tension

# 6. FATHER KURIEN AND THE SYRO-MALABAR CONNECTION

## 6.1 Who Are the Syro-Malabar Christians?

The **Syro-Malabar Church** is:
- An **Eastern Catholic** church in **full communion** with Rome
- Based in **Kerala, India**, headquartered at **Mount Saint Thomas, Kakkanad, Kochi**
- The **second-largest *sui juris* (autonomous) church** in the Catholic communion after the Latin Church
- Approximately **4.5-5.5 million** members worldwide
- Claims origins from **St. Thomas the Apostle** (traditionally c. 52 AD)
- Uses the **East Syriac Rite** — a liturgy dating to 3rd-century Edessa
- Their liturgical language is **Syriac**; everyday language is **Malayalam**
- Described as **"Indian in culture, Christian in faith, and Syriac in liturgy"**
- Until 1887, they were under the hegemony of the Latin Church hierarchy
- The current head is **Major Archbishop Raphael Thattil** (since 2024)

## 6.2 Connection to the Vatican

- Originally connected to the **Church of the East** (Persian/Chaldean Christianity)
- After the **Schism of 1552**, a faction entered communion with Rome (Chaldean Catholic Church)
- The **Synod of Diamper** (1599) forcibly placed them under Latin Catholic Portuguese control
- After the **Coonan Cross Oath** (1653), the community split
- The majority faction reconciled with Rome through **Carmelite missionaries** by 1662
- In **1887**, Pope Leo XIII fully emancipated the Syro-Malabars from Latin Church control
- In **1923**, the Syro-Malabar hierarchy was formally organized
- Today the church has its own **synod of bishops**, canon law (CCEO), and self-governance

## 6.3 The Synod of Diamper (1599) — What Manuscripts Were Destroyed

The **Synod of Diamper** (Udayamperoor, June 20-26, 1599):
- Convened by **Archbishop Aleixo de Menezes** of Goa
- Attended by **153 local priests** and **660 lay representatives**
- Issued **200 decrees** in nine sessions
- **Condemned** the Patriarch of Babylon as heretic
- **Destroyed Syriac manuscripts**: Decree XVI ordered all Syriac manuscripts handed to the Archbishop
- **Prohibited books** including:
  - *The Infancy of our Saviour* (Syriac)
  - *Book of John Barialdan* (Syriac)
  - *Margarita Fidei* (The Jewel)
  - *Book of Synods* (Syriac)
  - *Maclamatas* (Syriac)
  - *Camiz* (Syriac)
  - *Menra* (Hebrew)
  - *An Exposition of the Gospels* (Syriac)
  - *Book of Lots* (Aramaic)
  - *Parisman* or Persian Medicine (Persian)
  - And at least 10 more titles
- The **Anaphorae of Nestorius, Theodore, and Diodore** were abolished and their manuscripts ordered destroyed
- **Hindu books in Sanskrit and Marathi** were also burned
- The synod banned Hindu practices: Onam celebrations, caste observances, transmigration beliefs, astrology
- After the synod, Menezes stayed in Kerala, visiting churches and **burning books deemed heretical**

**Critical for the novel:** The destruction was immense but **not total**. Hungarian scholar **István Perczel** found texts that survived the destruction. Some manuscripts were hidden by priests; copies of some documents had already been sent to Rome or the Middle East. The Vatican Archive would contain **reports about what was destroyed** — and possibly **copies or summaries of texts that no longer exist in India**.

## 6.4 How Would a Syro-Malabar Priest End Up Working in the Vatican Archive?

A plausible path for Father Kurien:
- Syro-Malabar priests are **highly educated** — the church runs numerous seminaries and pontifical institutions
- Many Syro-Malabar priests study in **Rome** at pontifical universities (Gregorian, Lateran, Angelicum)
- The Syro-Malabar Church has a **permanent presence in Rome** through its connection to the Holy See
- A priest with interests in **church history, Oriental studies, or Syriac manuscripts** would naturally be drawn to the Vatican Library and Archive
- The Vatican's **Pontifical Oriental Institute** specializes in Eastern Christian studies and would be a natural academic home
- Father Kurien could be:
  - A doctoral student at a pontifical university, with archive access for his dissertation
  - A priest sent by the Syro-Malabar Synod to research historical documents
  - A member of a Vatican commission studying Eastern Christian heritage
  - A scholar attached to the **Mar Thoma Pontifical Shrine** in Kerala with research ties to Rome

## 6.5 What Unique Knowledge Would a Kerala Christian Priest Have?

Father Kurien would possess:
- **Native fluency in Malayalam** — the language of Kerala
- Knowledge of **Syriac** (liturgical language of his church)
- Understanding of **East Syriac liturgical traditions** — the Anaphora of Addai and Mari
- Deep familiarity with **Saint Thomas Christian history** — including the trauma of the Synod of Diamper
- Awareness of the **Coonan Cross Oath** and the community's resistance to Latinization
- Understanding of **Kerala's unique cultural matrix** — Hindu, Christian, Muslim, and Jewish communities coexisting for millennia
- Knowledge of the **Kerala School of Mathematics** (Madhava, Nilakantha, Jyesthadeva) — mathematical traditions that flourished in the same cultural environment
- Understanding of **Sanskrit** scholarly traditions — Kerala had active Sanskrit learning
- Knowledge of the **Thomas of Cana** tradition and the **Jewish community** in Kochi (Cochin Jews)
- Awareness of what was **lost** — the manuscripts burned at Diamper, the liturgical traditions suppressed, the knowledge destroyed

**For the novel:** Father Kurien would carry an **inherited grief** about the lost manuscripts of his tradition. He would know that in the Vatican Archive lie references to texts his community once possessed — texts that were destroyed by the very Church he now serves. This creates powerful internal conflict: his loyalty to Rome versus his awareness of what Rome's agents destroyed.

# 7. FOR THE NOVEL — THE VATICAN SCENES

## 7.1 Physical Description of Vatican City

Vatican City is the **smallest sovereign state** in the world:
- **0.44 square kilometers** (110 acres) — about 1/8 the size of New York's Central Park
- Population of approximately **800 people**
- Bounded by **medieval walls** on three sides and by Bernini's colonnade on the fourth
- Contains: **St. Peter's Basilica**, the **Apostolic Palace** (papal residence), the **Vatican Museums**, the **Vatican Library**, the **Vatican Archive**, the **Vatican Gardens**, the **Swiss Guard barracks**, and administrative offices
- Entered through specific gates: **Porta di Santa Anna** (for archive/library access), **Arco delle Campane** (for St. Peter's), and others
- The **Borgo** district of Rome surrounds Vatican City — narrow medieval streets, religious shops, pilgrim hotels

## 7.2 The Experience of Walking Through the Archive

**Father Kurien's morning ritual:**
- He exits his residence (perhaps the **Pontifical Oriental Institute** on Via Nazionale, or a religious house near the Vatican)
- Walks through Rome's early morning streets — past the Tiber, through Borgo, to the **Porta di Santa Anna**
- Shows his *tessera d'ingresso* to the Swiss Guard — young Swiss men in Renaissance uniforms
- Passes through the gate into Vatican City — the noise of Rome falls away
- Walks through the **Cortile del Belvedere** — designed by Bramante, a long rectangular courtyard connecting the Vatican Palace to the Belvedere villa
- Enters the archive building — modern security, then the transition to older spaces
- Deposits his bag, takes his pencil and notepad
- Climbs stairs or takes an elevator to the **reading room** — the *Sala di Consultazione*

## 7.3 The Atmosphere

**For the novel, the archive should feel like:**
- A place where **time operates differently** — documents from the 9th century sit alongside those from the 20th
- The **silence is institutional** — the silence of an organization that has been keeping records for nearly two millennia
- The **air is climate-controlled** — cool, dry, designed to preserve parchment and ink
- Researchers speak in **whispers** if at all; the rustle of paper is the dominant sound
- The staff — priests, lay scholars, archivists — move with quiet purpose
- There is a **weight of knowledge** that is almost physical: here sits the documentation of the Crusades, the Inquisition, the conversion of continents, the correspondence of emperors and kings addressed to the Vicar of Christ
- The reading room is **functional but beautiful** — wood and marble, natural light supplemented by reading lamps
- Through the windows: glimpses of Vatican Gardens, the dome of St. Peter's, Roman rooftops

## 7.4 How Kurien Would Describe His Work

Possible internal monologue:

*"I sit here, in this marble hush, and I read the words of men who thought they were doing God's work. Sometimes they were. Sometimes they were doing the work of empires and calling it God's. The letters arrive from Goa, from Madurai, from Nagasaki — handwritten in Portuguese, Italian, Latin, sometimes in Syriac — and they describe my ancestors. They describe the churches my great-great-grandfathers built, the prayers they were forced to abandon, the books that were taken from them and burned. And I am supposed to study these documents objectively, as a scholar, while knowing that what was lost includes texts my community has been mourning for four hundred years."*

Or more succinctly:

*"I came here to study what the Vatican knew about India. What I discovered is what the Vatican chose not to know."*

## 7.5 The Emotional Weight of Institutional Silence

**Key themes for the novel:**

1. **What was preserved**: The Vatican kept records obsessively — which means there is documentation of decisions to destroy knowledge. The irony is that the archive may contain the only surviving references to texts that were ordered burned.

2. **What was suppressed**: The Syro-Malabar tradition was systematically Latinized. Manuscripts were destroyed. Liturgical traditions were altered. A Syro-Malabar priest working in the archive would be acutely aware that he is handling the records of the institution that tried to erase his tradition.

3. **What was known but ignored**: The Vatican received reports from Jesuits about Indian and Japanese knowledge systems — mathematics, astronomy, philosophy. What did Rome do with this knowledge? Often, nothing. Or it was filed away and forgotten. The institutional silence about Asian intellectual achievements is itself a form of suppression.

4. **The paradox of the archive**: It is both a monument to preservation and a monument to power. It preserves the records of the powerful. The voices of those whose books were burned, whose temples were demolished, whose languages were suppressed — those voices survive only as they were documented by the agents of their suppression.

5. **Father Kurien's unique position**: He is a bridge figure — an Indian Christian priest, formed in both the Syro-Malabar and the Roman traditions, working in the very archive that holds records of his community's subjugation. He speaks Malayalam, Syriac, perhaps Sanskrit, and Italian. He can read documents that others cannot — not just because of language, but because he understands the **cultural context** that the Vatican's Italian archivists miss.
