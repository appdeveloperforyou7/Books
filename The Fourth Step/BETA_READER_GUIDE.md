# THE FOURTH STEP — Beta Reader Guide

Thank you for reading THE FOURTH STEP. Your feedback is invaluable. Below are some guidelines to help you provide the most useful responses.

## What I Need From You

### 1. Reading Experience Tracker
As you read, please note:
- **Where you decided to keep reading** (page/chapter where you were hooked)
- **Where you considered stopping** (if any)
- **When you figured out the twist** (chapter number, what gave it away)

### 2. Character Reactions
Rate your trust level for each character on a 1-10 scale at these points: Chapter 8, Chapter 17, and Chapter 28:
- Neve
- Saskia
- Imogen
- Rowan
- Cate/Aisling
- Elaine

### 3. Key Questions
After finishing, please answer:
1. At what point did you suspect Elaine? What was the clue?
2. When did you realize Cate wasn't who she claimed to be?
3. Did the midpoint twist (Chapter 17) feel earned? Why or why not?
4. Were there any moments where you felt "this wouldn't happen"?
5. Which character's POV did you most look forward to? Why?
6. Which character's POV did you least enjoy? Why?
7. Did the ending satisfy you? Did it haunt you?
8. Would you recommend this book? To whom?
9. What was the single best line or moment in the book?
10. What was the weakest moment?

### 4. Pacing Feedback
Mark any chapters that felt:
- **Too slow** (chapter numbers)
- **Too fast** (where you wanted more detail)
- **Perfect** (chapters you couldn't put down)

### 5. Emotional Impact
- Did any scene make you cry or come close?
- Did any scene give you chills or make you hold your breath?
- Were there scenes that should have been more emotional but weren't?

### 6. Spoiler-Free Feedback
If you discuss the book with anyone before all readers have finished, please avoid revealing:
- The identity of the person who pushed Kiera
- Cate's real identity
- What Neve remembers in Chapter 17
- The meaning of "Both."

## What This Book Is Trying To Be
A literary psychological thriller in the tradition of Gone Girl and The Girl on the Train — multiple unreliable narrators, contradictory accounts of the same event, and a central question about whether you can trust your own memory. The target reader is someone who enjoys being surprised but wants the surprise to be fair.
