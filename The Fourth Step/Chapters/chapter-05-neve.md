# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 5: Neve

## The First Evening

Imogen makes pasta. She makes it the way she does everything — thoroughly, busily, with her hands in constant motion and a running commentary that fills the kitchen the way music fills a dentist's waiting room. Background sound. Comfort noise. The hiss of garlic hitting oil. The rolling bubble of the water. The soft tap of the wooden spoon against the pot rim. She moves around this kitchen as if she's known it for years, reaching for the colander without looking, finding the olive oil on the first try, opening the drawer with her hip while both hands are full. She has been in this kitchen for less than two hours.

"The renovation's lovely," she says. "Whoever did it had great taste. It's completely different, isn't it? Completely different."

She says it again. She has been saying it since she arrived. Completely different. As if repetition will make it true, or as if saying it aloud prevents anyone from disagreeing. The kitchen is different. New benchtops, new cabinets, modern fixtures where there were old ones. But the dimensions are the same. The window above the sink is in the same position. The stove is in the same place relative to the door. The bones are the same. I don't say this.

Saskia sits at the counter with a glass of water. She switched from wine an hour ago. Controlled as always. One glass with dinner, then water. She watches Imogen cook the way she watches everything. With precision and with purpose. Even at twelve she sat at the edge of the group, dark eyes tracking, cataloguing, filing. Now she's a criminal defence barrister and she watches people the way a radiographer watches an X-ray: looking for what shouldn't be there, the shadow on the lung, the fracture under the skin.

Rowan is in the armchair in the living room, camera bag at her feet, wine glass untouched on the table. She's looking at the wall. Not at the wall — at the light on the wall. The way the pendant lamp throws a gradient from warm yellow to deep shadow along the grey paint. Her eyes track the light the way most people track conversation. She's somewhere else — somewhere visual, somewhere sensory, somewhere that doesn't require words. She hasn't moved from that chair since she sat down after arriving.

The four of us arranged in separate positions. A configuration. A seating chart none of us are filling in.

Imogen calls us to the table. She's set four places — cloth napkins, wine glasses, a small vase with a sprig of rosemary she found in the garden. The pasta is penne with tomato and basil and it smells the way pasta smells — garlic and warmth and home. The table is a performance of normalcy. Four women having a normal dinner in a normal holiday house on a normal winter evening.

"Lovely," Saskia says. A single word. She takes her seat with the precision of someone choosing a position in a courtroom. Back straight. Chair at a specific distance from the table. Full view of the room.

We sit. Four women around a table. The same number as before. One on each side, the surface between us like a negotiation table, like a peace treaty being signed by parties who haven't yet agreed on the terms of the war.

"Greg," Saskia says. She's not asking. She's opening a line of inquiry. "Tell us about Greg."

Imogen brightens — which shouldn't be possible because she's already at maximum brightness, but somehow she finds another gear. "Greg's wonderful. He's an accountant — I know, boring, right? But he's not boring, he's actually very funny once you get to know him. He does impressions. His Paul Hollywood is uncanny. Sophie tells him to stop but she's laughing when she says it."

"Sophie's your daughter?" Saskia asks. She knows. She's asking for the record.

"Ten. She just got the lead in the school play — Dorothy, in the Wizard of Oz. She's so brave. Gets up on that stage and she's fearless." Imogen's hands move as she speaks. She gestures, she traces the rim of her glass, she smooths the fabric of her cardigan over her knee. "And Ethan's seven. Obsessed with Minecraft. I don't understand it but I try. Greg says I hover. I don't hover. I'm attentive. There's a difference."

She doesn't stop. She doesn't breathe between sentences. Every sentence is another layer of the containment she's constructing between herself and the silence — Greg, Sophie, Ethan, the school play, Minecraft — each detail compressing the unsayable further down, further away. I watch her hands. They haven't stopped since she arrived. Keep talking, keep busy, keep moving, and the thing you're avoiding stays contained where you put it. It's effective in the short term. It's exhausting in the long term. I've seen it in a hundred sessions.

I see it in Imogen.

"You're a wonderful mother, Imogen," Saskia says.

The words are a compliment. The delivery is not. There is something in the way Saskia places the sentence — precise, measured, a statement entered into evidence rather than a kindness offered. Imogen's hands pause. A half-second. The spatula suspended above her plate. Then the hands resume. The smile holds.

"Thank you, Saskia. That's — I try. I mean, we all try, don't we? We all do our best."

"We do," Saskia says. Her eyes are on Imogen's hands. "Some of us try harder than others."

The table holds the sentence. Imogen's smile doesn't waver but something behind it shifts — a door closing, a wall going up. She turns to me. "And you, Neve? Liam and — what's your daughter's name?"

"Lily."

"Lily! That's beautiful. How old is she?"

"Twelve."

Something shifts in my chest. A physical displacement. Twelve. Lily is twelve. I say the number flatly, the way you'd read it from a form, the way you'd state a statistic that has no personal meaning. Twelve. The same age I was when — the same age.

"Twelve! What a wonderful age. Sophie's ten but she acts twelve, she's so mature. Does Lily dance? Sophie's obsessed. Jazz and contemporary both. She's performing next month in the —"

"Imogen," Saskia says. Flat. A verbal full stop.

Imogen laughs. "Sorry! Sorry. I'm nervous. Can you tell I'm nervous? I'm not nervous. I'm just — it's been so long. Twenty-five years. That's a whole person. You could fit a whole person into twenty-five years."

She means a whole life. Kiera would be thirty-seven now. Kiera's life would fit into twenty-five years. She doesn't say it. The number sits on the table between us anyway, invisible and heavy.

I pour more wine. The red catches the overhead light. Garnet, almost black at the edges. I'm on my second glass. The edges are softening. Not the important edges. The ones I need to stay sharp — the ones that keep me upright and functional and fine. The other edges. The ones that noticed the floorboards creak and the smell underneath the new paint and the fact that the kitchen window is in the same position it was twenty-five years ago. Those edges can afford to soften. They're doing their work too well.

The smell. There it is again. Underneath the garlic and the basil and the detergent Imogen uses on the benchtop — underneath all of it, something else. Old wood. Salt air. The particular warmth of a house near the sea in summer, when the timber holds the day's heat and releases it slowly through the night. It's winter now. It's July. The house should smell like paint and cleaning products and nothing else. But underneath, underneath the renovation, underneath the new surfaces, the house smells the way it smelled. The way it smelled when I was twelve. The way it smelled when the air was warm and the windows were open and the salt came through the screens on the breeze and I was happy, I think, I was happy, I was —

The sensation dissolves. I take a breath. I pick up my fork. I eat.

I don't say anything about the smell.

"And what about you, Rowan?" Imogen directs the conversation like traffic, keeping it moving, preventing any one of us from sitting too long in the same place. "What's life like? Where are you living now?"

Rowan looks up from the light on the wall. "I was in Lisbon. Before that Reykjavik. Before that — I don't remember."

"You don't remember where you lived?"

"The order doesn't matter. The light matters. The light is different everywhere. Lisbon is gold. Reykjavik is blue-grey. Flat. Like steel."

"You're a photographer?" This from Saskia. Her voice carries a specific frequency — the one she uses when she's gathering information rather than making conversation.

"Freelance. I sell enough to live. I don't need much."

"You were always taking photos," Imogen says. "Even at twelve. Remember? You had that little disposable camera and you used the whole roll in one afternoon. Kiera was so —"

She stops. She doesn't finish the sentence.

The room holds. Four forks paused. Four breaths held.

And then Imogen does what Imogen does. She picks up the thread and ties it into something bright and safe and moves on. "You were always so creative, Rowan. Even then. We all were, in our way. Saskia was the logical one, Neve was the caretaker, I was the worrier. Isn't that funny? We had types. Like characters in a story."

"We weren't characters," Saskia says. "We were children."

"Same thing," Imogen says. "Children are characters. They haven't learned to be anything else yet."

The conversation turns to Cate. Saskia's phone buzzed during the main course and she glanced at the screen and said, "Cate's arriving tomorrow morning."

"Cate Morrow," Imogen says. "I've been trying and trying to place her. Was she in our year?"

"A year or two behind us," Saskia says.

"I don't remember her at all." Imogen shakes her head. "I feel terrible. Did we know her well?"

"Not well."

"I don't — I think I should remember her. I don't," I say. Cate Morrow. The name lands in a blank space. I try to place her face among the faces from that time and nothing comes; most of them dissolved years ago, except the ones that didn't. The ones I could still see if I closed my eyes. Which I don't.

"I'm sure she'll remind us who she was," Imogen says. She's stacking the empty plates, carrying them to the sink, running the water. "More the merrier, right? It'll be nice to have a new face. Freshen things up."

Imogen brings out the banana bread. She slices it with the serrated knife from the block — the right knife, the specific knife for the task, because Imogen would never use the wrong knife when the right one is available. She arranges the pieces on the plate and sets it in the centre of the table and the gesture is so perfectly domestic, so precisely Imogen, that for a moment — a single, held breath of a moment — we could be any four women at any weekend house, eating dessert, drinking wine, being normal.

Saskia mentions the property listing she tracked — how she found the renovation, how she arranged the rental. Imogen marvels at the changes. Between bites of banana bread, I say something I shouldn't.

"The light switch by the back door. It's on the right now." I pause. "Was it always on the right?"

Saskia's fork stops. Imogen looks at me. Rowan's eyes drift to the wall.

"I wouldn't know," I say quickly. "I can't remember."

But I shouldn't know. I was twelve. The position of a light switch should not survive twenty-five years and a renovation I never saw. The moment passes. Imogen talks about the banana bread recipe. Saskia watches me. The conversation moves on.

And then Imogen says it.

"Remember when Kiera used to make us laugh so hard we couldn't breathe?"

The name lands on the table and the air changes.

One.

Two.

Three seconds.

No one speaks. No one moves. The banana bread sits on the plate. The wine sits in the glasses. The house creaks; a low sound, timber settling, the sound houses make in winter when the temperature drops and the bones contract.

Three seconds is an eternity in a therapist's office. Three seconds at this table is long enough for the silence to acquire weight — to become a fifth presence, to fill the room with the thing no one is saying.

No one mentions how she died. No one says the word "window." No one says "she fell" or "it was an accident" or "she leaned out" or any of the other phrases that have been waiting in the air since we arrived. No one says anything at all.

Then Imogen picks up her wine. "She was so funny. God, she was funny. Remember her impressions? She could do anyone. Teachers. Parents. That woman at the corner shop who always gave us extra lollies. She had this way of tilting her head and narrowing her eyes and suddenly she was someone else entirely. I still think about her sometimes."

"We all do," Saskia says.

Her voice is level. Her face is level. Her eyes are not level. Something underneath has calcified — the kind of hard deposit that forms around an unhealed injury — and I see it because I am trained to see it and I file it and I don't examine what it means because examining what it means would mean examining other things and those other things are behind a locked door I built myself.

Rowan hasn't moved. Her hand is on the ring. The silver ring, the moonstone, the one a friend gave her. Her eyes are on the wall. The light on the wall. She hasn't looked at any of us during the silence. She has looked at the light.

"We should do this more often," Imogen says. Her hands find the cloth again, wipe a spot that doesn't need wiping. Four women. Normal dinner. Normal house. We are fine.

Saskia sets her fork down. She looks at me, then at Rowan, then at Imogen. Her eyes carry the particular weight of a question that has been waiting its turn.

"Do you ever think about that night?"

The room goes still. Imogen's hands stop. Rowan's fingers tighten on the ring. My own fork is in the air, suspended, and I don't remember lifting it.

"THE FOURTH STEP," Saskia says. "December. I mean — do you ever wonder if there's anything we're not remembering? Anything that doesn't fit the story we were told?"

Imogen's voice comes out too high. "It was an accident, Saskia. The police said —"

"I'm not asking about the police. I'm asking about us. About what we remember. Or don't."

The silence stretches. I can hear the wind against the window, the tick of the heating, the creak of a floorboard somewhere above. Rowan's eyes are on the wall — the light, not the faces.

"I was asleep," I say. "I was downstairs the whole time."

The words come out practised. Because they are practised. Because I have said them so many times they have the polish of stones worn smooth by water. Saskia watches me. Her expression doesn't change. But something in her jaw tightens; a muscle, a fraction of movement, the kind of tell a barrister files without commenting on.

"Of course you were," she says.

The sentence lands flat. It means the opposite of what it says.

Outside, the first rain hits the windows. Not the polite tap of a shower — the thick, sudden percussion of a front breaking. The wind presses against the east wall and the house groans, a long sound from deep in the timber, and the pendant lamp above the table flickers once. A half-second dim. Then it holds. Everyone looks up. The rain intensifies — sheets of it, horizontal, hammering the glass, the wind finding every gap in the eaves, every crack in the sealant.

"Power grid out here is old," Saskia says. "The peninsula always loses power in a storm." She says it the way she says everything. As a fact, not a warning. But the lamp flickers again and this time it stays dim for two full seconds before recovering, and in the dim the room looks different. Older. The grey walls darker. The faces around the table half-lit, stripped of warmth, like a photograph taken without enough light.

"I'll check the fuse box in the morning," Imogen says, too quickly. "There's probably a torch somewhere. Rental houses always have torches. Somewhere."

I help clear the table. I wash and Imogen dries and the warm water runs over my hands and I focus on the physical sensation — the temperature, the pressure, the slip of detergent between my fingers. A grounding technique. Be in the body. Name five things you can see, four you can touch, three you can hear. The water. The plate. Imogen humming something tuneless. The window above the sink, which I do not look at. The back garden beyond the window, which I do not look at.

I am fine. This is fine. We are four women washing dishes.

My phone lights up on the nightstand. A text. Mum.

*Sweetheart, I heard you were all at the old house. How are you feeling? You know what the doctors said about stress. I worry about you. Have you remembered anything new? Drink some tea. The chamomile blend — you remember the one.*

She's in Rosebud. In the hospice. Pancreatic cancer — diagnosed eight weeks ago, terminal, weeks to months. And she is still managing. Still steering. The text is five sentences and every one is a brick in the wall she built between me and whatever happened in this house. "How are you feeling" — not concern. Assessment. "You know what the doctors said" — invoking authority, clinical language, the way she always did when I asked questions she didn't want to answer. "I worry about you" — the weight she places on me, the obligation of a daughter to a dying mother, the debt that must be paid in compliance. "Have you remembered anything new" — the question that is not a question. The check-in. The wall inspection. Twenty-five years of asking me if I remember and the right answer has always been no. And the last: "Drink some tea." The chamomile blend. The one she made on the stove when I was small and frightened. The one that made everything soft and quiet and fine. The one that meant *let me handle this*. The one I drank every night for twenty years without asking what was in it.

I do not respond. I put the phone face-down on the nightstand.

Saskia says goodnight. She takes her water glass and her laptop and disappears into the front bedroom. The door closes. A lock turns. Of course she locks her door. Saskia would lock her door in a safe house.

Rowan drifts. She picks up her camera bag and stands in the hallway for a long moment, looking at the staircase. The first five steps visible through the doorway, rising into shadow. Then she climbs. The fourth step creaks — that low, resonant groan, that particular pitch — and she pauses and then continues. Her footsteps move along the upstairs hallway. A door opens. Closes. Silence.

I am alone with Imogen.

"Long day," she says. She wipes the benchtop for the third time. The cloth moves in small, precise circles. "We should sleep. Big day tomorrow. Cate's coming. I'll make pancakes. Do you think Cate likes pancakes?"

"She'll like what you make. I think. You always — people always like what you make."

"You're sweet." She stops wiping. Looks at me. For a moment the performance drops and what's underneath has the skin around her eyes pulled tight and lines at her mouth that weren't there at dinner and a stillness that has no place on her face. "It's good to see you, Neve. I mean that."

"It's good to see you. I think it is."

She hugs me. Vanilla and garlic and dish soap. Her arms tighten — too long, too hard — and then she lets go and walks to the stairs and climbs them and the fourth step creaks under her weight and she disappears into the dark at the top.

I stand in the kitchen after she's gone. The tap drips — once, twice — and the house settles around me and I think: I should go upstairs. I should see the upstairs. I am a grown woman and this is a renovated house and there is nothing up there that can hurt me.

Something taps the window above the sink. A branch, maybe. Or the wind finding the one gap in the new sealant. The tap drips again. The house breathes. And somewhere upstairs — maybe the hallway, maybe a bedroom — a floorboard groans with the particular weight of someone standing still.

I turn left. I walk to the downstairs bedroom. I close the door.

I don't think about why.

The room is small and white. A bed, a nightstand, a lamp, a window facing east with heavy curtains drawn. The carpet is new — grey, low pile, soft under my feet. The walls are Dulux Natural White, or near enough. Rental property. No personality. No trace of the people who lived here before. A blank room. A safe room. A room on the ground floor, at the back of the house, as far from the stairs as the floor plan allows.

I sit on the bed and I call Liam.

"Hey. How's it going?"

"Fine. The house is — fine. Renovated. Different."

"That's a lot of fines."

"I'm tired. Long drive."

"Is it weird? Seeing them after all this time?"

"I think so. Maybe. They're the same people but — I don't know. The surface is different."

"How's the house?"

"Different. You wouldn't recognise it."

I believe this when I say it. The house is different. New paint, new furniture, new carpet, new light. It is different. It is completely different.

"Is Lily asleep?"

"Not yet. Reading. She says to tell you the main character in her book reminds her of you."

"Of me?"

"Observant. Watches everyone. Doesn't miss anything."

Something contracts in my chest. "I'll call tomorrow. Give her a kiss for me."

"Will do. Get some sleep, Neve."

"Goodnight."

I hang up. I hold the phone in both hands and stare at the wall and think: I should have brought a book. Something to fill the space between now and sleep. The smell of old wood and salt air is still there underneath the paint, underneath everything new and clean and different. It won't leave.

I brush my teeth. I change into pyjamas. I get into bed. The sheets are stiff. Rental property sheets, washed in industrial detergent. They smell like nothing. I'm grateful for nothing.

I turn off the lamp.

The dark is complete. No streetlights on this road. A coastal street in winter, holiday houses shuttered, no traffic. The only light is the faint glow of the moon through the gap in the curtains, a pale grey line that falls across the ceiling like a scratch.

I lie still and I listen to the house.

I've explained hypnagogic auditory sensitivity to enough clients to recognise it. The mind in the dark amplifies sound. The auditory cortex increases its gain in the absence of visual input. Every creak and tick and groan is physics — timber expanding and contracting with temperature, pipes cooling, the house adjusting to the cold the way a body adjusts to a change in air pressure. Normal. Neurological. Nothing more than that.

Floorboards creak in the hallway. Once. Twice. The sound of the house settling its weight. The renovation replaced the surface boards but not the subfloor — the new blackbutt visible over the old bones the moment I walked in. The new blackbutt on top, the old bones underneath. The bones remember.

I close my eyes.

The smell is here too. In the dark, without the visual override of white walls and grey carpet and modern fixtures, the smell is stronger. Old timber. Salt. The particular warmth of a house that held a summer night twenty-five years ago and has never quite let it go. The smell of that December. The smell of that — I was twelve. I was happy. I was happy and then I was asleep and then it was morning and everything was fine.

My mother says I slept through the whole holiday.

I breathe. Four in, seven hold, eight out. The breath goes in and the breath goes out and the house settles and the smell fades and I am almost asleep when the sound comes.

From upstairs.

The fourth step.

That specific pitch. That resonant, weighted groan. The sound of a particular piece of timber being pressed by a particular amount of weight in a particular spot. The sound I've heard twice tonight — Rowan climbing, Imogen climbing — except Rowan and Imogen are in their rooms. Saskia is in the downstairs front bedroom with her door locked. The stairs should be empty.

I hold my breath. It doesn't happen again. I tell myself I imagined it. I close my eyes. I am fine.
