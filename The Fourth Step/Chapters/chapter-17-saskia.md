# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 17: Saskia

## Revelation

The timing is deliberate. Six PM. The light outside is fading — grey July dusk pressing against the windows, the sky the colour of wet concrete, the last of the afternoon draining from the coast like water from a basin. Cate left twenty minutes ago. Phone call, she said. Reception is better at the end of the street. She walked down the drive and turned toward the coastal path, handbag over her shoulder, step measured, unhurried. She did not look back.

I don't know where Cate goes when she leaves this house. I don't know who she calls. I have questions — the seventeenth through the twenty-third, updated hourly — but they will wait. What matters now is the room and the women in it.

I have arranged the living room. Not decorated. Arranged. The low wooden table in the centre has been cleared of Imogen's banana bread, the Tim Tams, the almonds in their precise row. In their place: the document sleeve. Rigid A4. Archival quality. Acid-free. The contents lie flat on the surface — the police incident report, two pages, photocopied from the original at the Sorrento police station. The autopsy summary, one page. The crime scene photograph, one print, glossy, twenty by twenty-five centimetres. And Rowan's photographs of the window frame, printed at the highest resolution the machine could manage, showing the scratches in the original timber beneath the aluminium.

The evidence array. twenty-five years of assembly. It fits on a coffee table. A girl's death reduced to documents you could slip into a manila envelope and mail for the price of a stamp. The disproportion is obscene.

The light in the room is overhead — a single pendant with a linen shade, casting diffused yellow light that softens edges and flattens surfaces. Not ideal. Courtrooms have better lighting. Fluorescent, harsh, unforgiving — the kind of light that shows you what is actually there. This light is kinder. I don't want kind. I want clear. But this is a living room, not a courtroom, and I will work with what I have.

Rowan sits in the chair with her back to the wall. Her camera is in her lap. She is watching the table the way she watches everything — as light and form and composition, as a frame within a frame. But her jaw is set and her fingers rest on the camera body with a stillness I have not seen in her before. She is waiting. Not in her usual way — adrift in sensation, present but elsewhere. She is present. Fully present. The fragments are assembling behind her eyes.

Neve is on the sofa. She arrived from upstairs ten minutes ago and she is different. The composure she has performed since she walked through the front door on Friday is gone. Not cracked. Gone. Her face is pale. Her hands rest in her lap, motionless. She is looking at the documents on the table and she is not looking away. She knows something. I don't know what. She came down white-faced and silent and when I asked if she was all right she said "Yes" in a voice that contained no information. A one-word answer designed to close rather than open.

But her eyes are different. The flat, guarded gaze she has maintained all weekend has depth now. Something moving behind it. Something sequestered for twenty-five years that has finally been called to testify.

Imogen is in the kitchen. She's in the kitchen — the clink of a mug, the rattle of a teaspoon, the small domestic percussion she uses to fill silence. She has been in the kitchen since I told them I wanted to talk. She heard "talk" and her hands started moving and they have not stopped.

"Imogen. In here."

She appears in the doorway. Her smile is small and uncertain. The wide, warm, everyone's-favourite-teacher smile is not available tonight. She has a mug in both hands — tea, not wine. Both palms wrapped around the ceramic as if it is the only solid thing in the room.

"Sit," I say.

She sits. Next to Neve. The mug between her palms. She is holding it the way you hold something that keeps your hands from doing something else.

The room settles. Four women. One table. Twenty-five years.

Outside, the wind has picked up — a low pressure sound against the glass, the kind that makes houses creak. Salt air. July cold. Inside, the heating hums. The documents sit in their precise row: report, summary, photograph, scratch images. Ordered. Sequential. Lead the witness from fact to fact until the conclusion becomes inescapable.

I stand. I don't sit for this. Standing gives me the room — the physical authority of someone presenting a case. The document is the witness. I am the voice.

"I've been waiting twenty-five years to have this conversation," I say. "Some of you know parts of what I'm about to show you. Rowan knows about the scratches on the window frame. Neve —" I pause. "Neve knows something happened in this house that was not what we were told."

I look at Neve. She is watching me. Her face is very still. She does not deny it.

"In 2004, I went to the Sorrento police station and asked to see the incident report from December 14, 1999. I was fourteen. They told me to let it go. Two years later, I obtained a copy through a police contact. I have carried it since."

I pick up the first page of the police report. The paper is soft with handling, the photocopy dark with age. The header reads: Victoria Police, Incident Report, Sorrento Station, 14 December 1999. Officer: Senior Constable D. Hargreaves, badge 4417.

"Senior Constable Hargreaves was first on scene. He arrived twelve minutes after the call. He was alone. No forensic team. No detective. No photographer — this image was taken by a constable with a personal camera. The initial classification was accidental death. A child fell from a window. Tragic but not suspicious. Small-town Victoria, December 1999, no resources, no reason to look harder. The report was filed in seventy-two hours. The case was closed before the family buried her."

I let that land. Then: "The injury notation." 'Minor bruising noted on the upper back and shoulders, bilaterally. Consistent with impact injuries from a fall.'"

I set the page on the table.

"'Bilaterally.' Both sides. Symmetrical. Not a fall pattern. A grip pattern. Hands on the shoulders from behind. A push."

I let the word sit. Bilateral. Clinical. Precise. A word that conceals what it describes.

"The body was found two metres from the wall. A child falling backward lands half a metre. Two metres requires horizontal force."

I pause. Let the number sit. Two metres. The distance from this table to the kitchen doorway. A child's body, launched.

"I obtained a biomechanical reconstruction. A forensic physicist ran the parameters from the incident report — Kiera's weight, forty-two kilos; her height, one hundred and forty-two centimetres; the window height, the sill position, the landing point. His finding: to produce a two-metre landing distance from that window, the horizontal force applied to the body must be roughly equivalent to a firm two-handed shove. Not a tap. Not a reflex. A deliberate, forceful push. The kind of force an adult uses when they want to move someone out of the way."

I pick up the crime scene photograph. The one that arrived under my door in the manila envelope. I hold it facing the room.

"This photograph was taken the night Kiera died. It shows the upstairs hallway as it was. Cream walls. Blue carpet. The original window — timber frame, top-hung casement, open, swung outward into the December night."

I turn the photograph so they can see the image.

"In the upper pane of the window, the flash has captured a reflection. A figure. Standing at the top of the stairs. Child-sized. Watching. Still."

Imogen is shaking. A fine tremor that moves through her hands and into the mug and makes the tea ripple against the ceramic. Her lips are parted. Her eyes are fixed on the photograph.

Rowan is still. Her gaze is on the reflection in the glass and her expression is one I have not seen before — not fragmented, not adrift in sensation, not lost in light and texture. Focused. The focus of someone who has found the missing piece and is watching it slot into place.

Neve is staring at the photograph. The moment she registers the reflection. Not recognition of what — recognition of who. The blood leaves her face in a second wave and her pupils dilate and her jaw tightens and for one brief instant her eyes close. Not a blink. A shutter closing. The deliberate suppression of an image she cannot afford to acknowledge.

She knows who the figure is. She is not saying.

I file the observation. Question twenty-four: Why is Neve protecting the identity of the child in the reflection?

I don't press it. Not yet. The evidence first. The questions later.

"The child in this reflection is not listed in any witness statement. All four surviving girls were recorded as being downstairs when police arrived. But this photograph shows a child in the upstairs hallway. At the top of the stairs. During the crime scene processing. Someone was there. Someone who was never accounted for."

I place the photograph on the table. Next to the police report. Next to the autopsy summary. Next to Rowan's scratch photographs. The evidence array is complete: the bruising that proves a push, the scratches that prove Kiera fought to stay inside, and the photograph that proves someone was in the hallway who wasn't supposed to be there.

I look at the array. twenty-five years. The numbers on the pages have not changed. The words have not changed. The only thing that has changed is the room and the women in it and the fact that I am finally, finally saying the words out loud to the people who need to hear them.

"The scratches Rowan found on the original window frame corroborate the bruising pattern. Five parallel lines. Downward. Gouges, not surface marks. She reached for the frame. She gripped it. She was trying to stay inside."

I set the photograph down.

"The scratches are the last thing Kiera touched. And they prove she did not go through that window willingly."

I look at each of them. Imogen. Rowan. Neve. Three faces, three silences. Imogen trembling. Rowan's gaze a fixed point on the table, still, unblinking. Neve white, jaw clenched, eyes two locked doors.

"The report says 'consistent with impact injuries from a fall.' Hargreaves noted the bruising, wrote 'consistent,' and filed it. No one questioned it. A twelve-year-old girl fell from a window. Tragic. Accidental. Case closed."

I pause. The room is holding its breath.

"Kiera did not fall."

The words land. Quiet and precise. No objection. No appeal.

"Someone pushed Kiera."

I look at Neve. I look at Imogen. I look at Rowan.

"And someone in this house knows who."

The silence is not the silence of an empty room. It is the silence of a room full of people who have stopped breathing. Imogen's mug trembles. Rowan's fingers curl around her camera. Neve's face is a mask — white, rigid, measured — and behind the mask, the architecture of something that has been holding for twenty-five years, and the architecture is failing.

I stand in the silence. I let it hold. I do not fill it. Silence is the room where witnesses break, and I have been waiting twenty-five years for this room and I will not rush it.

Sergeant Bill Tran, who owed my father a favour, handed me these pages across a desk in 2006 and said, "Are you sure about this, love?" The fourteen-year-old who walked into the Sorrento police station and was told to let it go. The forty-seven readings. The timeline, updated twenty-three times by hand. The fourteen-month FOI battle. The photograph of Kiera running at a school sports day — knees up, hair flying, mid-stride, alive. All of it compressed into this moment: four women, a coffee table, and the sound of nobody breathing.

From the hallway, a sound. The front door opening. Footsteps on the floorboards — light, even, unhurried. A pause.

Cate stands in the doorway of the living room. Her handbag is over her shoulder. Her coat is buttoned. Her face is calm and composed and she is looking at the documents spread across the table — the police report, the autopsy summary, the crime scene photograph with the reflected figure, the scratch photographs — and she heard it. The last sentence. I can see it in her eyes, which have moved from calm to alert in the space between one breath and the next, a shift so fast it is almost imperceptible.

She stands in the doorway. She does not enter. She does not leave. She heard me say it: someone in this house knows who.

And the room holds its breath around her.
