# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 29: Neve

## Morning

I come back to the house at seven.

The hospital stabilised her. That is the word the doctor used — stabilised. Not improved. Not recovering. Stabilised. The way you brace a structure whose load-bearing walls have failed — upright by intervention, not by right. Her vitals are within acceptable range. The pain is managed. She is resting. She asked me to go home and get some sleep and I said I would and I drove back to Sorrento instead because this house is where I need to be right now and I don't know why and I have stopped requiring myself to know why.

The drive from Mornington takes twenty minutes. I did it in the grey pre-dawn, the sea a darker shade of dark on my right. The radio was off. My hands were on the wheel at ten and two, the way she taught me. I drove past the war memorial and past the newsagent and through the roundabout and I know this road the way I know the sound of the fourth step.

The house is quiet when I pull in.

Saskia's car is gone — she left for the hospital an hour ago, she texted, to review Elaine's statement with the DPP liaison. Imogen is asleep in the downstairs bedroom — I can see the shape of her through the curtain, curled on her side, her hand under her cheek, her face slack with the kind of sleep that only comes after total emotional exhaustion. Rowan left sometime in the night. A note on the kitchen counter, in her angular handwriting: "Gone to walk. Need air. Back later." Aisling is gone too — no note, no text, just absence. She got what she came for. The truth. A name. The knowledge that her sister didn't fall.

I stand in the kitchen for a long time. The evidence is still on the table — the police report, the photographs, the printouts of Aisling's forensic analysis. Saskia organised them into neat stacks before she left. Saskia organises everything. It's how she copes. The same way Imogen cooks and Rowan photographs and I analyse. We all have our architectures. The scaffolding we erect to keep the structure standing when the foundation cracks.

I make coffee. I stand at the counter and drink it. The kitchen window faces east and the light is changing — grey to pink to gold, the slow colour wheel of a winter dawn. I can see the garden through the window. The stone wall. The path. The lawn, winter-pale, flat, still.

I go outside.

The air is cold. Sea air. Salt and grass and the dampness of a garden that has been dark all night. I stand on the back step and breathe it in and the cold fills my lungs and the smell fills my head and for a moment — just a moment — I am twelve and it is December and the air is warm and the garden is green and everything is about to happen and hasn't happened yet.

I shake it off. The flashback is gentler now than it was yesterday. Not a flooding response. A triggered ripple — contained, manageable, making contact with something that was always there. The memories are coming back in pieces — not the violent, immersive panorama of the hallway, but smaller fragments, quieter. The sound of the brick under my shoes. The smell of the pizza in the living room. The way Kiera laughed — with her whole body, her head thrown back, her dark hair swinging. She had a scar on her left knee from a bicycle accident. She showed it to me that night, pulling up her pyjama leg, and she said "battle wound" and I laughed and she laughed and we were twelve and it was a sleepover and nothing bad had happened yet.

I sit on the back step. The concrete is cold. I don't go back inside. I sit and I hold my coffee and I look up.

The window.

It's smaller than it was. The renovation made it smaller — a fixed pane, aluminium frame, sealed shut. You can't open it. The window that exists now is not the window that existed then. But the position is the same. Second floor. End of the hallway. The same rectangle of glass in the same wall of the same house.

It looks different now. Not smaller — I already knew it was smaller. Different in the way a thing looks different after you know what happened behind it. It was a window. A rectangle of glass. Now it is the place where a twelve-year-old girl's fingernails scraped paint while she held on. Now it is the threshold between the hallway and the dark and the grass below.

It looks harmless. Closed. Still. A window in a wall in a house in a coastal town on a Sunday morning in July. But I know what happened behind that glass. I know what those scratches mean. I know because my mother told me, in an ambulance, at two in the morning, while I held her hand and she held mine and the monitor beeped and the truth came out of her in pieces, like a suppressed memory surfacing through the breach — the thing the containment couldn't hold anymore.

The door behind me opens.

I turn. Aisling is standing in the doorway. She came back. She's wearing the same clothes — jeans, dark jumper — and her face is pale and tight and her eyes are red-rimmed and she is holding something in her hands.

Kiera's camera.

The disposable camera Rowan found under the floorboard in the upstairs room. White plastic, yellow trim, the kind of camera you bought at a chemist in the late nineties for five dollars and used once and dropped off at the photo counter and forgot about. The film is still inside. Twenty-five years of undeveloped film in a plastic shell. The last photographs Kiera ever took are on that roll. The last things she saw through a viewfinder.

Aisling sits beside me on the step. She holds the camera in both hands, resting on her knees. Her thumbs trace the edges — the plastic seam, the winder, the tiny viewfinder window. She touches it the way you touch a thing that was touched by someone you loved, as if warmth could pass from plastic to skin, as if the object carries a ghost of the hand that last held it.

"I found it on the kitchen counter," she says. "Rowan must have left it out."

"The film's still inside."

"I know."

She turns it over. The counter window on the top shows a number. Eight. Eight photographs. Eight frames of film that have been waiting in the dark for a quarter of a century.

"I don't know if I should develop it," she says.

Her voice is different this morning. Softer. The hard, flat, Australian vowels of last night's unmasking have settled into something quieter — the voice of a woman whose twenty-five-year investigation is over, the case file closed, the desk empty. She turns the camera in her hands. The evidence is not a document or a photograph or a forensic analysis. The evidence is her sister's camera. The last thing Kiera held. The last thing Kiera saw.

"I think you should develop it," I say.

She looks at me.

"You should," I say. "She took those photographs. They're the last things she saw. They're hers."

Aisling nods. She doesn't cry. She has cried enough this weekend. Or maybe she hasn't. Maybe there isn't enough crying in the world for what she's lost. A sister. A family. Twenty-five years of not knowing. But she nods and she holds the camera and we sit on the back step together — a woman whose mother killed a girl, and a woman whose sister was killed — and the morning comes up over the garden and the light touches the upstairs window and the window doesn't glow or change or transform. It's just a window. It's always been just a window. The meaning is what happened around it.

We sit for a long time. We don't talk. There is nothing left to say that hasn't been said in the last twelve hours. The evidence is on the kitchen table. The truth is known. The confession is given. The ambulance has come and gone and my mother is in a hospital bed in Mornington, and Aisling is holding her dead sister's camera, and the garden is the same garden it was twenty-five years ago, and the grass is the same grass, and the stone wall is the same stone wall. The window is smaller but the frame is the same.

Two women connected by a dead girl. That's what we are. Kiera Doyle is the space between us. She was twelve. She was brave. She told the best ghost stories. She went upstairs because she heard something that didn't sound right and she was the kind of person who went towards the thing that didn't sound right, and the thing was my mother on the phone with someone she shouldn't have been talking to, and Kiera was brave enough to say so, and my mother was frightened enough to push her, and the window was open, and everything changed in two seconds.

Aisling stands. She puts the camera in the pocket of her jumper. "I'm going to go," she says. "I need to sit with this. Properly. Alone."

"Okay."

She looks at me. Her face is still. What remains is something quieter — the face of a woman who has spent her entire adult life in pursuit of an answer and has finally received it.

"Thank you," she says.

I don't know what she's thanking me for. For the truth. For the confirmation. For holding my mother's hand in the ambulance while she said the things Aisling needed someone to hear. For being the one who was there, the one who saw, the one who was made to forget. I don't ask. I nod.

She goes. The front door opens and closes. A car engine starts.

I sit for a while longer. Then I go inside.

The house is quiet. The kitchen is empty. Imogen is still asleep. The hallway is dim — the morning light hasn't reached it yet. I stand in the hallway and I look at the staircase.

I go up.

Fourth step. The groan. Low and resonant, the same pitch, the same vibration through my feet. My body knows the sound. My body has always known the sound. I don't stop. I keep going. Step five. Step six. The turn. Step eleven. Step twelve. Step thirteen. Step fourteen. The top.

The hallway stretches ahead. Grey walls. Grey carpet. The doors to the bedrooms, all closed. The window at the end, small and sealed, the aluminium frame catching the morning light.

I walk.

Twelve steps. The same twelve steps Rowan counted, the same twelve steps my twelve-year-old feet counted on a warm December night. The dimensions are identical. The walls are different and the carpet is different and the light is different but the space is the same space and the space knows me and I know it.

I reach the window.

I stand in front of it. The glass is cold. The aluminium frame is smooth and new. Behind the aluminium, behind the rubber seal, behind the plaster and the paint — the original frame. The wood. The scratches. Five parallel lines. Downward. The marks of a twelve-year-old girl holding on.

I put my hand on the frame.

My palm flat against the aluminium. My fingers spread. The same spread as the scratches beneath — the echo of a hand that reached for this frame twenty-five years ago and held on and lost its grip. I press my hand against the new surface, the old surface beneath it. The bones of the house. The substrate. What remains when every surface has been replaced.

I stand there for a long time. I don't cry. I don't shake. I stand with my hand on the frame and I look through the glass at the garden below and I think about Kiera Doyle, who was twelve and brave and who told the best ghost stories. She stood where I am standing. She looked through this window at the dark and heard the brass hinge creak and felt the warm December air on her face. And then hands on her shoulders. And then the window took her.

I take my hand away.

I walk back down the hallway. Twelve steps. The staircase. Fourth step. The groan. The ground floor. The living room, empty. The armchair where my mother sat. The kitchen where I made coffee at five in the morning because making coffee is what you do when you don't know what else to do.

I pick up my phone from the kitchen counter. I scroll to Liam's name. I press call.

It rings twice.

"Neve? Are you okay? What time is it?"

"It's early. I'm okay."

A pause. He is sitting up in bed. I can hear the shift of the mattress, the click of the lamp. "Where are you?"

"I'm still at the house. I'm coming home. I'll be there in a couple of hours."

"Okay." Another pause. He is choosing his words the way he chooses them when he knows something is wrong but doesn't know what. "Do you want to talk about it?"

"Not yet. But I will. When I get home. I need to tell you something."

"Okay."

"I need to talk to you about my mother."

Silence. Liam has been married to me for fourteen years. He has met Elaine hundreds of times. He has watched me call her every day and arrange my life within twenty minutes of her and refuse to discuss my childhood before thirteen. He has accepted all of it with the steady, bewildered patience of a man who loves a woman he cannot fully know. He has never pushed. He has never asked why I can't talk about it. He has just waited.

"And I need to talk to you about what happened to me when I was twelve."

The silence is longer. I hear him breathe. In and out. The breathing of a man who has just been told that his wife — the woman he married, the mother of his child, the therapist who helps other people face their fears — has been carrying something for twenty-five years that she has never spoken about.

"Okay," he says. "I'll be here."

"I know."

"I love you, Neve."

"I know. I love you too."

I hang up.

I stand in the kitchen and I look through the window at the garden. The morning light is full now — gold, soft, the light of a winter day. The house is behind me. The staircase. The hallway. The window at the end. The scratches under the frame.

I walk to the car. I start the engine. I am not fine. I drive away.
