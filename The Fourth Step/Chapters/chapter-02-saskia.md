# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 2: Saskia

## First Arrival

I arrived at four-seventeen. The sun was lowering behind the pines, casting long shadows across the gravel, the winter light thin and amber at the edges. The rental agreement said check-in was four, but I don't like to be late. I don't like to be early, either. I like to be precise — the way a scalpel is precise, or a verdict, or the distance between what someone says and what their body does. The air was cold — July cold, carrying salt off the sea and something else underneath, something green and sharp that caught in the back of my throat and stayed. The barometer on my phone read 1003 and falling. A front was coming. I noted this the way I note everything.

The key was in a lockbox. Four-digit code. I entered it on the first attempt because I memorised it when the confirmation email arrived eleven days ago. The code was 7-2-0-4. I remember it because I chose the numbers myself — my mother's birthday, reversed, a system I use for all property access codes. Except the code I entered didn't match what I specified in the booking. The lockbox accepted 7-2-0-4, but my booking confirmation — I checked later — says 4-1-9-8. Someone changed the code after I made the reservation. The lockbox was metal, powder-coated black, mounted at forty-five degrees on the doorframe. Not the original lockbox. Not the original doorframe, either. The doorframe is new — pine, dressed, painted white. The original was hardwood. I know this because I have photographs.

The door opens into a hallway. Five metres long, one point three eight wide. I measured it this afternoon with a tape measure I brought from Sydney. The house is a simple rectangle: front door into the hallway, living room and kitchen at the far end, two bedrooms and a bathroom off the ground-floor hall. Upstairs — same layout, same dimensions — the hallway connects two bedrooms and a bathroom, with the window at the end. The renovation replaced surfaces but not bones. Load-bearing walls stayed. The staircase stayed. The window at the end of the upstairs hallway stayed, though someone made it smaller and sealed it shut. I measured the upstairs hallway too. Five metres. Exactly the same.

I set my bag in the front bedroom. Ground floor. I chose it deliberately. The room has one window, facing east, with a clear sightline to the driveway and the front gate. The door opens into the hallway. From the bed, I can see both the window and the door. This is not a preference. This is a requirement. I have always slept this way.

I unpack with efficiency. Toiletry bag on the sink. Clothes in the drawer. Laptop on the small desk by the window. Then I remove the document sleeve from the false bottom of my suitcase. It's a rigid A4 sleeve, archival quality, acid-free. Inside: the police incident report, two pages, photocopied from the original at the Sorrento police station in 2006 when I was nineteen and my father's colleague — Sergeant Bill Tran, now retired — owed my father a favour. The autopsy summary, one page, obtained through a freedom-of-information request that took fourteen months. A handwritten timeline I compiled in 2004 and have updated twenty-three times since. A photograph of Kiera Doyle, aged twelve, taken at a school sports day. I found it in the archives of the Sorrento Primary School newsletter, Volume 12, Issue 4, Term 4, 1999. She is running. Her hair is behind her. She is laughing.

I don't look at the photograph. I put the sleeve in the desk drawer and lock it. The key goes into my trouser pocket. I haven't decided if I'll use the contents this weekend. I haven't decided what this weekend is. A fact-finding exercise. An opportunity. Twenty-five years of waiting for these women to be in the same room as this house and now they're coming and I don't know what I'll do when they arrive.

Yes, I do. I'll watch. That's what I do. I watch and I note and I cross-reference. I've spent my career reading people for deception. Every twitch, every hesitation, every word chosen and word avoided. I know what a lie looks like the way a copy editor knows a fabrication — not because I was taught, but because I have read enough of them to recognise the joins where the story doesn't quite seal.

I walk through the house. Methodically. Room by room.

The living room is at the end of the hallway. The renovation opened it up — the original had a dividing wall separating it from what was a dining area. Now it's one space. New kitchen along the north wall. Stone benchtop — granite, not marble. Two stools, not three. The stove is gas, five burner, Smeg. The original kitchen was along the east wall. It had an electric stove, four burner, Westinghouse. I know this because I remember it. I remember the layout of this kitchen when I was twelve and the window above the sink looked out onto the back garden and there was a calendar on the wall with a picture of a lighthouse and the date was December and the square for the fourteenth was empty.

The calendar is gone. The window above the sink is the same — same dimensions, same position, same aspect — but the glass is new. Double-glazed. The frame is new. I note all of this.

There are two exits from the living room. The front hallway and a back door leading to a deck. The deck is new. The back door is new — sliding glass, aluminium frame. The original was a wooden door with a window panel, painted green. I remember the green. It was chipping.

The back garden is visible from the sliding door. Stone wall at the boundary. The stone wall is the same — local limestone, mortared, approximately one metre forty high. I can see it clearly. The lawn is new turf, neatly kept. A path of flagstones leads from the deck to a gate in the wall. The gate is the same. White wooden gate. I remember the gate.

I turn from the sliding door and walk back through the hallway. I don't go upstairs yet. I'm methodical. Ground floor first.

The ground floor has four rooms: the front bedroom I've claimed, a second bedroom at the rear, a bathroom, and the open-plan living space. The bathroom is new — it was a closet under the stairs in the original layout. The renovation kept the stairs in place — same wall, same rise — but replaced the treads and bannister. The stairs are against the west wall, where they've always been.

This matters. The stairs have been renovated in place and the upper hallway is in the same place. The upper hallway connects two bedrooms and a bathroom. The window at the end of the upper hallway is — I don't go upstairs yet. Ground floor. Methodical.

I make coffee in the new kitchen. The kettle is electric. It takes two minutes and fifteen seconds to boil. I stand at the counter and wait. The house is quiet. It's a Friday afternoon in Sorrento in July and the holiday houses on this street are mostly empty and the only sound is the wind off the sea and the tick of the kettle and the creak of timber as the house adjusts to the temperature drop.

My phone vibrates. A text. Neve's mother. Elaine Cassidy.

*Your mother told me you're all going to the house this weekend. I hope it's what you need. You don't have to do this. Call me if you want to talk. I'll make the tea — you know the one.*

The message is four sentences. I count the manipulations. First: "Your mother told me" — establishing that she has intelligence networks, that she monitors. Second: "I hope it's what you need" — the language of concern layered over the language of control, reframing a self-directed action as something granted. Third: "You don't have to do this" — permission to leave, which is not permission but a command dressed in kindness. Fourth: "I'll make the tea" — the chamomile-and-eucalyptus blend she brewed on the stove for all of us, the one that quieted us, the one that tasted like safety and worked like a sedative.

I do not respond.

I take my coffee to the front bedroom. I sit on the bed with my back to the wall and my eyes on the window and the door. I drink the coffee. It's acceptable. Not good. Pre-ground. Supermarket grade. Six weeks old, at least. I note this because I note things. I note the colour of the paint in this room — Dulux Natural White, or a near equivalent. I note the light fixture — single pendant, brass, with a linen shade. I note the absence of art on the walls. Rental property. No personality. No trace of the people who owned it before.

I note the text message. I note the timing — sent at four-fifty-three, eleven minutes before Neve's arrival. Elaine knew the schedule. Elaine knew who was coming. Elaine is in a hospice in Rosebud, dying of pancreatic cancer, and she is still monitoring. Still managing. Still using concern the way a locksmith uses tension wrenches — with precision, with patience, to turn something that should not turn.

Except.

The floorboards in the hallway creak. Not the new blackbutt — the blackbutt is too well-laid, too flat, too dead. The creak comes from underneath. From the original subfloor. The house was built in 1971, renovated in 2008, renovated again in 2019. They replaced the surfaces but they didn't replace the bones. The bones are still here.

At five-twenty-two, a car pulls into the driveway. Blue. Mid-size sedan. Victorian plates. I'm standing at my bedroom window, curtain held back two centimetres. The car stops. The engine turns off. The driver doesn't get out.

The house opposite has lights on. A woman in the front window, fiftyish, watching the street the way people in small towns watch — not with curiosity but with the proprietary surveillance of someone who has been observing this street for a long time and considers its visitors her business. Her eyes are on the blue sedan. Her eyes move to my window. I let the curtain fall. She saw nothing. She saw enough.

I watch.

The driver is Neve Cassidy. I haven't seen her in twenty-five years but I recognise her immediately. Brown hair, tied back. Steady posture. The posture is wrong for someone who's nervous — too rigid, too held. The kind of composure that takes effort. I've seen this performance in court a hundred times. The witness who has rehearsed composure so thoroughly that the mask itself becomes evidence of its opposite.

She sits in the car for five minutes. I time it. Five minutes and four seconds. She looks at the house. She doesn't get out. Her hands are on the wheel at ten and two and she is looking at the house and she is not getting out of the car.

She looks at her phone. I can see her right hand lift from the wheel, thumb moving across the screen. Texting. She puts the phone down. She sits for another two minutes. Then she opens the car door.

She stands in the driveway. The wind hits her. She takes a step towards the house. She stops. She starts again. She walks to the front door and knocks.

I open it.

She looks exactly the same. Taller than I remember — I was tall at twelve, but she's grown into her height. Same steady posture. Same watchful eyes. She's wearing a jumper and jeans and her hair is tied back and she looks like a woman arriving at a weekend house, nothing more, nothing unusual, nothing to see.

But she won't look at the stairs. She hasn't looked at them once. She came through the door and the stairs are directly to her right — west wall, impossible to miss — and she has not turned her head.

"Neve," I say.

"Saskia."

I step aside. She walks in. Her shoulders are rigid. She's breathing through her mouth. The floorboards creak under her feet — the subfloor, the original subfloor, the one that was here twenty-five years ago — and I see her flinch. A small flinch. Suppressed. Almost invisible. I see it.

"Five minutes in the driveway. You couldn't decide whether to come in?" I ask.

"Fine," she says. "Just tired from the drive."

I show her the house. I don't need to — she can see it herself — but I show her because I want to watch her move through it. The living room. The kitchen. She remarks on the changes. "It's completely different," she says. She says it twice. She says it the way people say things they're trying to believe.

She doesn't go upstairs. She doesn't even look at the stairs. When we pass the hallway, she angles her body away from the west wall, a quarter turn, just enough that the stairs are in her peripheral vision rather than her direct sightline. She doesn't know she's doing it. I know she's doing it.

I make more coffee. We sit in the living room and make conversation. She asks about my work. I tell her criminal defence, Sydney. She tells me she's a family therapist, Melbourne. We talk about the weather. The traffic. The drive. The coffee. We do not talk about why we are here. We do not talk about the house. We do not talk about the last time we were in a house that looked like this one.

At six-fifteen, she goes to her room. The downstairs bedroom. She doesn't say she chose it because it's downstairs. She says she likes the window. The window faces east. The morning light, she says. She likes the morning light.

I sit in the living room after she's gone. The house is quiet again. The wind is up. The sea is close — I can hear it when the wind is right, a low rhythmic sound, like breathing. December didn't sound like this. December was hot. Still. The air heavy with salt and eucalyptus, the sounds of cicadas and children and a boat bell somewhere in the harbour.

I was fourteen the second time I came to the Sorrento police station. The first time was the night Kiera died — all of us, the five girls, taken in a police van, sitting in the station with plastic cups of water and someone's mother's cardigan around our shoulders, the wool scratchy and smelling of lavender and someone else's washing powder. I don't remember much about that night. I remember the plastic cup sweating in my hand. I remember the fluorescent light, the kind of light that flattens everything and hides nothing. I remember a policewoman's hand on my shoulder, gentle, professional, the way you touch a child you don't know how to comfort because comfort requires understanding and no one understood what had just happened.

But the second time I came alone. Two years later. Fourteen. I'd taken the bus from Rosebud, forty minutes, and I walked into the station and I asked to speak to someone about Kiera Doyle. The desk sergeant was a heavy man with a grey moustache and he looked at me the way adults look at children who are asking about things they shouldn't know about.

"It was an accident, love," he said. "Let it go."

"I'd like to see the incident report. Fourteenth of December, 1999. Kiera Doyle."

"There's no report for you to see. It was an accident. You were there. You know what happened."

I did know what happened. I knew that what they told us happened didn't match what I remembered. I knew that Kiera didn't fall. I knew that the way they described it — "she leaned out the window," "she was looking at the stars," "it was a warm night and the window was open" — was a story. Stories have a shape. Real events have rough edges that catch.

I didn't let it go. I have never let it go.

I stand. I walk to the hallway. I climb the stairs.

The stairs are new. West wall. Fourteen steps, turned at the midpoint. The treads are the same blackbutt as the hallway floor. The bannister is steel and timber. New materials, but the original substructure remains beneath. The original stairs were narrower, steeper, with a wooden bannister worn smooth by hands. The fourth step creaked. I remember the fourth step. I remember stepping over it in the dark because I didn't want to wake anyone.

The upstairs hallway is five metres long. I don't need the tape measure. It's the same length. Same width. Same ceiling height. The walls are grey now — a pale, modern grey, Dulux, I'd guess Lonely Hour or similar. In 1999 they were cream. The floor is the same blackbutt. In 1999 it was carpet — blue, low pile, synthetic. The renovation stripped it back to the subfloor and laid the new boards on top. Underneath, the original boards are still there. The bones.

The hallway has two doors on the left, one on the right, and a window at the end. The window is what I came to see.

I stand in front of it.

The window is different. Smaller. Fixed-pane — no opening mechanism, no latch, no way to open it. The frame is white aluminium, modern, double-glazed. In 1999 the window was larger, timber-framed, with a top-hung casement that opened outward. It was open that night. December. Warm night. Salt air moving through the house.

I examine the frame. I run my fingers along the edge where the aluminium meets the wall. The new frame is recessed into the original opening, but the original opening is still there — behind the new frame, behind the plaster, the original timber lintel and sill are in place. The renovation buried the window, didn't replace it. They shrunk the glass and sealed it shut and called it done.

I stand in the hallway. I look at the window. The new window. The smaller, sealed, different window.

The window is different. But the frame is the same. And the frame is what matters — the frame holds the shape of what happened here, the way a scar holds the shape of the wound that made it.
