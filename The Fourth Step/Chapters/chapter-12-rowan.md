# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 12: Rowan

## The House Speaks

The morning light is different from the night light. It comes through the windows not as a blade but as a wash — soft, diffuse, the colour of skim milk, spreading across surfaces without direction or intent. It illuminates everything equally. It hides nothing. Morning light is the least forgiving light there is.

I take my camera out of its bag at seven. The house is awake — I can hear Imogen in the kitchen, the percussion of cooking, the rhythm of someone who needs her hands to move. Voices, low. The murmur of conversation I cannot make out and do not try to. I stand in my doorway and I listen to the house breathing — floorboards settling, pipes in the walls, the whisper of air through vents. Every house has a respiratory system. This one breathes shallow and cold.

I start in the hallway. Not the upstairs hallway — the downstairs one. I work from the bottom up, the way light does.

The entrance hall. Grey walls, blonde wood floor, white ceiling, pendant lamp. I frame it: the length of the hall, the front door at one end, the living room doorway at the other. I press the shutter. The moment is captured. The hallway is now a photograph — a flat rectangle of light and shadow, fixed, unmoving, true in the way photographs are true: precisely and nothing more.

I move to the living room. Leather sofas. Wooden table. Rug the colour of dried wheat. I photograph the rug — fibres catching morning light, throwing it back in bright points, each one a sun in miniature. The window next. Glass reflecting the room back on itself, so that in the photograph you see both garden and room, layered, transparent, inside and outside occupying the same flat space.

I move to the kitchen. The smell of eggs and butter and toast. Imogen is at the stove, spatula in hand, her face pink from the heat. Cate is sitting at the counter, hands around a coffee cup, watching Imogen the way you watch a performance. I lift the camera. Imogen turns.

"Oh! Rowan! Breakfast is almost ready — ten minutes! Are you hungry? Of course you're hungry, I'm sorry, that's a silly question. Sit, sit — okay, just — I'll make you a plate."

I lower the camera. Some spaces are not mine to take.

I go back to the hallway and I photograph the staircase from below — the rise of the steps, the turn, the bannister going up and curving out of sight. The bannister is new — pale wood, smooth, modern. But where the bannister meets the wall, where the newel post is bolted to the plaster, there is a scratch. Not a construction scratch. Not a wear mark. A scratch — thin, deliberate, the kind of mark a ring makes when a hand slides along wood.

My ring. Kiera's ring. On my right hand. Silver.

I photograph the scratch. I zoom in on the screen. The mark is consistent with metal on wood — pressure applied laterally, a single pass, the depth of a band being dragged across the surface. It could be from the renovation. It could be from a mover, a tool, a belt buckle. It could be from a hand gripping the bannister in the dark, a silver ring catching the wood as the body moved fast, fast towards the top of the stairs, fast towards the hallway, fast towards the window at the end.

I photograph it and move on.

The upstairs hallway. I climb the stairs — one, two, three, four. The fourth step groans beneath me — new timber, renovation-tight, but the sound persists, a frequency my body remembers, entering my chest and vibrating. I keep going. Five, six, seven, eight, nine, ten. Turn. Eleven, twelve, thirteen, fourteen. The hallway opens in front of me, grey and still and cold, the sealed window at the end a rectangle of morning grey, the blade of light on the carpet thinner now — morning light being less dramatic than moonlight.

I photograph the hallway. The empty hallway. The window at the end, the walls on either side, the doors to the bedrooms, the smoke detector, the fire extinguisher. I photograph the window — the aluminium frame, the fixed pane, the reflection of the hallway inside the glass, so that the window shows me the hallway I am standing in, flattened and reversed, a photograph within a photograph.

I photograph the wall beside the window. I photograph the place where the aluminium frame meets the original wood. The scratches are still there — five parallel lines, downward, the marks I found last night. In morning light they are less dramatic. Less like evidence. More like what they are: lines in wood. Old lines. Patient lines. Lines that have been waiting for someone to see them, the way light waits for a lens.

I move to the rooms.

The first door on the left. A bedroom, small, a single bed, a window facing the street. Frame. Click. The window, the ceiling — a faint brown stain near the corner, water damage from a roof since repaired, the shape of a continent on the plaster. Click. The house remembers what the renovation tried to forget.

The door on the right. A bathroom. White tile, white fixtures, a mirror above the sink. I photograph the mirror — my own face in it, framed by the camera, the camera reflected in the mirror, an infinite regress of seeing. My face is thin and angular and the morning light is not kind to it. My eyes are dark. My hair is dark. The silver ring on my right hand catches the flash and throws it back.

The second door on the left. The room at the back of the house. I open it.

It is not a bedroom anymore. It has been converted into a study — a desk under the window, a bookshelf, a desk lamp, an upright chair. The window faces the back garden. The walls are the same grey as the hallway. The floor is the same blonde wood. The ceiling is the same height.

But this room was a bedroom twenty-five years ago. This room was the bedroom where five sleeping bags were laid out in a row on the floor. This room was where the pizza boxes were, where the torch was, where Kiera told ghost stories with the light under her chin and her hair falling around her face and her voice making the walls disappear.

I know this because the room is the same size. The same proportions. The desk is where the sleeping bags were, along the wall under the window. The bookshelf is where the door is, to the left of the entrance. The proportions are precise — twelve feet by ten, ceiling eight feet, window occupying most of the external wall. The room has been reframed. The composition is the same.

I step inside. The floorboards are new — blonde, polished, modern. But they are wood, and wood is laid over wood in old houses, renovation over original, new over old, surface over bone.

I photograph the room. The desk. The chair. The bookshelf. The window. I photograph the walls — grey, clean, no marks, no scratches, no evidence that anything happened here, that five girls laughed here, that a torch lit a face from below here, that someone told stories about ghosts here. The walls are smooth and grey and they say nothing.

I photograph the ceiling — white, smooth, the same faint discolouration in the corner, the same continental stain. I photograph the light fitting — a single globe, modern, energy-efficient, the colour of weak sunlight.

Then — not a sound, not a sight. Something registers through the sole of my foot. The ball of my left foot, pressing down, and instead of the solid resistance of timber fixed to joists, there is give. Millimetres of it. A fractional softness, the way a print buckles when the paper underneath has shifted.

I look down.

The floorboard is the third from the window, second from the wall. It is the same colour as the others, the same width, the same finish. But the gap between this board and the one next to it is slightly wider — a millimetre, perhaps two. The edge is not factory-cut. The edge has been trimmed. Modified. By hand.

I kneel.

The floor is cold against my knees. The morning light falls on the board at a low angle, picking out the grain, and the edge is not uniform — one side is slightly bevelled, the work of a blade, careful, deliberate. I press my fingers into the gap. The board lifts. It comes up easily — too easily, as if it has been lifted before, many times, as if the fit has been worn smooth by repetition. The underside of the board is raw wood, unpainted, unpolished, the colour of bone.

I set the board aside.

Underneath: a cavity. The space between the floorboards and the ceiling below, a gap of about eight inches, dark and dusty and smelling of old wood and silence. The dust is thick and undisturbed — except in the centre, where something has been placed. The dust has been pressed down in the shape of an object. A rectangle. The size of a hand.

I reach in. My fingers close around it. I lift it out.

A disposable camera.

The casing is Kodak. Yellow and blue, the kind that was everywhere in the late nineties, the kind you bought at a chemist and took on school excursions and dropped off at the developers and forgot about. The plastic is scratched and dusty. The film advance wheel is stiff but functional. The exposure counter reads: 3.

Twenty-four taken. Three remaining. Twenty-seven exposures on a roll. Twenty-four moments she chose to hold still. Twenty-four.

I hold the camera in both hands. It is light — the weight of plastic and film and chemistry, of twenty-four exposures never processed, latent images still locked in silver halide like light sealed in a darkroom, the weight of twenty-four photographs that have been sitting in this cavity, in the dark, for twenty-five years. The film is still inside. The emulsion is still on the cellulose. The light that entered the lens is still there, latent, unprocessed, waiting for chemistry to make it visible.

The camera was placed here.

Not dropped — the cavity is too specific, the gap too narrow for a camera to fall into accidentally. Not lost — lost things are found in obvious places, under beds, behind furniture, in the bottom of bags. This was hidden. Lifted floorboard, cavity, camera placed inside, floorboard replaced. Deliberate. Careful. Someone did not want this camera found. Or someone wanted it found only by someone who would look — who would feel the give in a floorboard, who would kneel, who would press fingers into a gap, who would lift.

I turn the camera over. Plastic, cool and textured and old. Faded Kodak logo. Flash mechanism on the front, a small rectangle that would have popped up during use. No strap — just the attachment, empty. The lens is a circle of glass, smudged with fingerprints — old, the oils gone, just the pattern left in dust.

Kiera's fingerprints.

She held this. Her hands held this camera. Her eye looked through this viewfinder. Her finger pressed this shutter. Twenty-four times she pressed this shutter. Twenty-four moments she chose to capture. And the last photographs she ever took — the ones near the end of the roll, the last clicks of the shutter — those photographs are still inside. Undeveloped. Unseen. The last things her eye selected before — before she went through the window.

I sit back on my heels. The camera is in my lap. The floorboard is beside me. The cavity is open, dark, waiting to be closed again.

The room is quiet. Morning light fills it — soft, diffuse, merciless. The desk. The chair. The bookshelf. The walls, grey and clean. The floor, blonde and polished, with one board removed, a dark rectangle where the wood should be. The window, facing the back garden, the garden Imogen said was the same.

Kiera took photographs that night. I remember — not a story, not a sequence, but a sensation: the flash of a disposable camera, sudden and white, illuminating the sleeping bags and the pizza boxes and the faces of five girls in their pyjamas. Kiera laughing. Click. Click. The sound of moments being saved. Her finger on the button. Her eye at the viewfinder. Kiera who saw everything through a lens before I ever held a camera, Kiera who taught me to look before I knew what looking was.

But this camera was hidden under the floorboard. After the photographs were taken. Someone took the camera from Kiera's things — or Kiera hid it herself, knowing something, fearing something, pressing it into the gap and replacing the board — and placed it here. Before the end of the night, or after. The sequence is wrong but the object is real. The camera is real. It is here, in my hands, light in my hands and heavy with what it carries — twenty-four frames of undeveloped light, silver halide holding images the world has never seen.

I turn the camera over. The exposure counter: 3. I press the advance wheel. It clicks. The counter doesn't move — the film is advanced to the last frame. This is the last photograph. The last thing she framed. Whatever Kiera saw last, whatever she chose to capture in the final moments before — before the window, before the fall, before the air took her.

I think about the flash. A disposable camera flash is bright. Bright enough to illuminate a hallway. Bright enough to capture two shapes at the end of a corridor — one tall, one small. Bright enough to fix in silver halide the last thing she saw before the hand and the push and the window and the dark.

I hold the camera against my chest. The plastic is hard and light against my sternum. I can feel my heartbeat through it — or I can feel the camera's absence of heartbeat — twenty-five years of stillness, waiting in the dark for hands that would lift it into light.

The last photographs Kiera ever took are on this film. The last things she saw through a viewfinder. The last moments she chose to preserve. Twenty-four exposures of light that has never been developed, never been printed, never been looked at by anyone since Kiera's eye was behind the lens.

What did you see? I think. What did you photograph, at the end?

I think about the film inside — the undeveloped film, the latent images, the light trapped in silver crystals since December twenty-five years ago, waiting for someone to add the chemicals that would make the invisible visible. I think about what might be there. The bedroom, the sleeping bags, the girls laughing. The hallway, the window, the moonlight. The shapes at the end of the corridor — one tall, one small. The tall one's arm moving. The small one reaching for the frame. Five fingernails scraping paint.

I think: *If I develop this film, I'll see what she saw. The last things she saw.*

I haven't decided whether to tell the others.
