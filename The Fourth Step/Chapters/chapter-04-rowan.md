# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 4: Rowan

## Arrival

The bus from Melbourne pulls in at seven. I am the last one off. It exhales and pulls away and I am standing on the street with my camera bag over my left shoulder and my small backpack over my right and the evening light is turning the rooftops amber — the colour of honey held up to a window — warm, transparent, about to change. The light is already changing. Light always is.

The wind is up. Not the afternoon breeze — something stronger, coming off the Bass Strait, carrying the smell of rain that hasn't arrived yet. The pines along the street are swaying, their tops moving in long, slow arcs, the way things move when they've been moving for a long time and have settled into the rhythm of being pushed.

I see the house.

It is not white anymore. It is the colour of storm clouds — blue-grey, soft, weathered. The porch is wider. Or I was smaller. The roofline is the same and something in my chest shifts when I see it, a frame slipping on a wall you haven't looked at in years.

I lift the camera before I decide to lift it. My hands know. They always know. I frame the house — roofline against amber sky, the wide porch, the front door that was green and is now black — and I take the shot. The shutter clicks. The sound of a moment being removed from time and held still.

I take another. The window on the second floor. Smaller. Replaced. Aluminium frame where there was timber. But the wall around it is the same. The render catches the light. The frame is new but underneath it — there's always something underneath. The bones of the house don't disappear when you change the surface. The shutter clicks. The light is going. I want to see what's under that frame. I should go inside.

I walk up the path. The garden gate is new. Wrought iron, painted black. The path is concrete now. Smooth, modern, clean lines. It was brick once, uneven, moss growing between the cracks. My body adjusts for a dip near the third stone — muscle memory for a surface that isn't there.

I stop at the front door. My hand is raised to knock and I am looking at my own knuckles — thin, pale, the silver ring on my right hand catching the last of the light — and I think: I have been here before. Not the thought. The sensation. Like a tuning fork struck twenty-five years ago, still vibrating at a frequency just below hearing.

I knock.

Footsteps inside. The door opens and Saskia is standing there. Tall, sharp, short dark hair, eyes the kind of dark that absorbs light and gives nothing back. Pressed white shirt at a weekend house. Sleeves rolled to exactly the same point on each forearm.

"Rowan."

"Saskia."

She steps aside. I step in.

The hallway opens in front of me and the light is different — interior light, warm, yellow, a pendant lamp that wasn't there before — but the space is the same. The width. The ceiling height. The way sound behaves, slightly held, as if the walls are cupping noise like water. My gaze finds the staircase. The same angle. The same turn. New materials. Old geometry.

I do not go up.

"Only one light on. Where is everyone?" I ask.

"Living room." Saskia gestures left. Her eyes move to my camera bag, then my face, then back. She is cataloguing. She has always catalogued — filing people the way I file light, by angle and temperature and exposure.

I walk past her into the living room. Warm. New furniture. Leather sofas, a low wooden table, a rug the colour of dried wheat. Imogen stands by the window with a glass of wine. Neve sits on the edge of the sofa with her hands wrapped around a mug. They both look up and for a moment nobody moves.

A stillness like a long exposure — everything frozen, the world holding its breath while the shutter stays open. The four of us in the frame. Imogen by the window, light behind her, soft and rounded, hair lighter than it was. Neve on the sofa, shoulders slightly raised, her eyes — brown, steady, watchful — finding me and holding. Saskia behind me in the doorway, her shadow falling across the floorboards.

I see surfaces. I see light. The way the lamp catches the edge of Imogen's jaw and turns it gold. The way Neve's fingers press into the ceramic and the whites of her knuckles show. The way Saskia stands at an angle that lets her see every exit. I see all of this as light and form and texture. What they are feeling, I cannot say. I never could.

"Rowan." Imogen sets her wine down and wraps her arms around me. She smells like vanilla and fabric softener. Her cardigan is cream-coloured, a small stain on the left cuff. She touches people gently, as if the world is fragile and she is trying not to break it.

"You look wonderful," she says, pulling back. Her eyes move over my face. "A bit thin. Are you eating?"

"Enough."

"You were always thin. Remember how thin you —" She stops. She has remembered something. Her smile flickers. She picks up her wine.

Neve stands. She doesn't hug me. She puts her hand on my arm and squeezes, brief and firm. "It's good to see you, Rowan." Her voice is warm and even. The cost shows — the tension around her eyes, her body a pose held past the point of comfort, still and deliberate, waiting for a shutter that hasn't clicked.

"You too, Neve."

I sit down. I choose the chair with its back to the wall. From here I can see the living room, the doorway to the hall, and the staircase through it — the first five steps rising into shadow. The rest turn and disappear. I face them. I always face them. I do not know why.

Imogen pours me wine without asking. Red. It catches the light. Garnet, deep, almost black at the edges. I hold it up. I look at it the way I look at everything.

"We're all here," Imogen says. Her smile is wide and warm and lives in her whole face — the smile of someone who has decided everything is fine. "Well, almost. Cate's coming tomorrow morning. Isn't that exciting?"

"Cate," I say.

"Cate Morrow. Don't you remember? A year or two behind us. Moved to London." Imogen settles into the sofa and tucks her feet under her. "I don't remember her at all, honestly. But Saskia says —"

"I don't remember her either," Saskia says from the doorway. She is leaning against the frame, arms crossed. Not joining. Observing.

"I barely remember any of that time," Neve says. She says it lightly. Conversationally. Her fingers tighten on her mug.

I don't say anything. I remember all of it. Not as a story. Not as a sequence of events with a beginning and middle and end. I remember it as sensation. The cold of the floorboards. The quality of the moonlight — blue, not white. The blue of something seen through deep water. The sound of — 

The sound of —

Imogen is talking. Something about her daughter Sophie and the school play and how Greg was going to record it on his phone. Her hands move as she speaks. They never stop. She gestures, she traces the rim of her glass, she smooths the fabric of her cardigan over her knee. Her hands are small and pink-nailed and they perform a constant, restless choreography, as if she is knitting the air itself into something she can hold.

The conversation moves the way conversations move when people haven't been in the same room in twenty-five years. Carefully. In circles. Neve asks about Melbourne. Saskia asks about Sydney. Imogen asks about everywhere — Reykjavik, Lisbon, wherever I was last — and I say the names and they sound like postcards from someone else's life.

"Rowan." Imogen leans forward. Her eyes have found something. "That ring. That's a beautiful ring. Where did you get it?"

My hand moves to the silver ring on my right hand. The metal is cool. The band is thin. The stone is small. A moonstone, pale and opaque, the colour of condensed fog. It has sat against my skin for twenty-five years, so long the finger beneath it is thinner than the others.

"It was given to me," I say. "The stone holds light."

Imogen waits. Eyebrows raised, head tilted, the expectant architecture of someone holding space for an answer.

I take a sip of wine. The waiting dissolves.

"That's lovely," Imogen says. She settles back, her fingers intertwining in her lap, knuckles whitening.

The evening fills with small talk. The wine goes down. The light outside the windows shifts from amber to grey to the blue of early night. Neve returns from the kitchen with a tray of food Imogen must have brought — cheese, crackers, grapes in a precise spiral on a blue plate. Everything in its place. Everything just so.

I eat a cracker. I drink wine. I watch the room the way I watch everything. As a frame. The four of us arranged in our separate positions, each one a point of light in the composition. Saskia at the edge, angular, her sharp jawline catching the lamp. Imogen in the centre, warm and rounded, her hands never stopping. Neve beside her, contained, her stillness louder than movement.

And me. Back to the wall. Facing the door. Facing the staircase I can see through the open doorway, the first five steps rising into shadow, the new bannister pale and smooth, the wall beside it painted a clean grey.

This house was rented for us. Someone chose this house. Someone brought us back to this specific house, on this specific street, twenty-five years after a girl went through a window. Saskia says she found the listing. Saskia arranged the rental. But Saskia watches the others the way I watch light — with the cold, measured attention of someone who is not here for the weekend.

The question sits in the room like a camera on a tripod, pointed at all of us: who arranged this, and why?

The hallway was cold. The hallway was cold and the light was — the light was blue, December-moonlight blue, not this warm yellow lamp-light but blue, thin, the colour of — and there was a sound, somewhere between a gasp and a thud, a sound that came up through the soles of my feet and climbed the ladder of my spine and —

"Rowan? Do you want more wine?"

Imogen is holding the bottle. The wine inside it is dark and still. The surface of it is flat. I look at the surface of the wine and I am back in the room, the living room, the warm yellow light, the clean grey paint, the present.

"No," I say. "Thank you."

Neve is watching me. Her gaze is steady. Saskia is watching me too — her attention lands like a camera lens, a concentrated point of observation.

The evening turns. Imogen says "completely different" and Neve says "mm" and Saskia says nothing and I look at the staircase through the doorway — the five visible steps rising into shadow, patient, unchanged in their geometry.

I should not have come here. I should have stayed in whatever city I was in last — was it Lisbon? Reykjavik? The cities blur. The light in each is distinct — Lisbon gold, Reykjavik grey-blue, Melbourne flat white — but the cities themselves blur into one continuous motion. I live in a continuous present. The past exists only as sensation. The future exists only as light I have not yet photographed.

But this house. This house is not past or present. This house is a photograph taken twenty-five years ago, sitting in the developer all this time, slowly becoming visible. And I am standing in it.

The conversation has stopped. I have been silent too long. Three women waiting for me to say something and I don't know what because I cannot read what is behind their faces. Only the surfaces, the tension in Imogen's smile, the stillness of Neve's hands.

"I should get my things from the hall," I say.

"Your room is the one at the end of the upstairs corridor," Saskia says.

Upstairs.

I don't go upstairs. Not yet. I will. Later. When the house is quiet and the others are asleep and the light is — I will go upstairs later.

"I'll bring my bag up later," I say. "The light down here is enough."

The conversation resumes. Imogen talks about Sophie again. Neve nods, her eyes somewhere internal. Saskia pours herself water — she has switched from wine, controlled as always, every movement deliberate, like a photograph that knows it is being looked at.

I lean back in my chair. The wall is solid against my spine. The staircase through the doorway — beyond the turn, more steps, then the hallway, then the window, smaller now, fixed-pane, and the light was blue in December, blue as milk, blue as — 

From somewhere in the house a board creaks. The sound is specific. A pitch, a weight. Weight pressing timber that has been pressed in the same spot a thousand times. The sound is not new. The sound is twenty-five years old and it is happening now.

I am looking at the staircase.

"The fourth step still creaks," I say.

The room changes. I don't see it change but I feel it — a shift in pressure, the way a room feels when everyone stops breathing at once. I am looking at the staircase. The fourth step is just visible, pale wood in shadow.

Three women not speaking. Not moving. I turn and they are all looking at me. Imogen's wine glass frozen halfway to her mouth. Neve's hands still on her mug. Saskia's eyes wide.

The stillness holds. I look at their faces — the surfaces, the light — and I see surprise and something else I cannot name because I do not read the psychology of faces. I read the light on them. I do not understand why they are looking at me this way.

"Sorry?" Imogen says. Her voice is high. Too high. "What did you say?"

"The fourth step," I say. "It creaks. I heard it just now."

Saskia's eyes narrow. Not with suspicion — with something sharper. With attention.

"How would you know that?" Neve asks. Her voice is careful. Measured. The way you speak to someone who has said something that doesn't make sense.

I look at her. I look at the staircase. I look back at her.

I don't understand the question. I know the fourth step creaks the way I know the hallway upstairs is cold even though I have not been up there yet. The way I know light and texture without being told.

"I just know," I say.

The silence holds for another moment. Then Imogen laughs — a short, bright sound, too bright, a laugh trying hard to be casual. "Well, old houses, right? They all creak. Every old house has a creaky step."

Neve is not laughing. Saskia is not laughing. They are looking at me and then at each other and the looking is the kind that happens when the frame has moved and no one knows what the new picture is yet.

I pick up my wine. The room returns to its arrangement. Imogen talking, Neve nodding, Saskia watching. But underneath, there is something new in the air. Something I cannot photograph. Something I cannot see.

I sit with my back to the wall and I face the staircase and I do not think about what any of it means because meaning is not something I do. I do light. I do surface. I do sensation. The rest — the story, the sequence, the before and after — that is someone else's territory.

The staircase waits in the doorway. And somewhere in the back of my mind, a hallway is cold and the light is blue and a sound I cannot name is still happening. Has been happening for twenty-five years. A note that will not stop vibrating.

I do not go upstairs. Not yet.
