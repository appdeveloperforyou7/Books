# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 22: Neve

## Elaine

The headlights come first.

The wind has stopped. The gusts that rattled the windows all afternoon have died completely, as if the coast is holding its breath. The silence outside is not natural — it is the silence of a predator going still before it moves.

I'm standing in the living room with the others. Saskia has the police report spread across the coffee table. Aisling — not Cate, Aisling, Kiera Doyle's sister — has the autopsy photographs in her hands. Imogen's face is wet, hands pressed against her mouth. Rowan has her camera around her neck, her eyes fixed on some middle distance where past and present overlap. The air in the room is thick with what Aisling has told us. The photographs. The anonymous tips. The revelation that she has spent her entire adult life trying to find out who killed her sister.

And then the headlights.

Two bright beams sweep across the living room windows. They travel left to right, slow and deliberate, the way headlights move when a car is pulling into a driveway rather than passing on the road. The beams cross the ceiling, illuminate the far wall, and stop. Engine off. The lights die.

The road has been blocked since Friday night. The fallen pine at the end of the street — no one has come to clear it. But the hospice is in Rosebud, five minutes away. She drove around the back roads, the service tracks the locals know, the ones that don't show up on GPS. She found a way in. Of course she did. Elaine always finds a way in.

For a moment, no one moves.

Saskia is the first to speak. She's at the window in three steps, pulling the curtain aside. "Car in the driveway," she says. "Dark sedan. One occupant."

I'm already moving. I don't decide to move. My body is moving and my mind is following, the way it followed me up the stairs this afternoon, the way it followed me into the hallway, the way it has been leading me places all weekend that my conscious mind did not approve. I reach the window beside Saskia and I look out and the world goes very quiet.

It's my mother's car.

The blue Honda she's had for nine years. Parked at an angle in the driveway, not quite straight, the way she always parks when she's tired. The driver's door opens.

Elaine gets out.

She shouldn't be driving. She's in hospice. Pancreatic cancer, stage four, three weeks into palliative care. She drove herself — forty minutes from the hospice in Mornington to this house in Sorrento — in the dark — alone.

She stands behind the open door for a moment. Leaning on it. Her coat is too large — the camel coat I bought her last winter, before the diagnosis. It hangs from her shoulders the way a flag hangs in dead air — still shaped to the pole, but the wind has gone. Her hair is shorter than it was two days ago. Someone at the hospice must have cut it. She'd never let anyone cut her hair. She always said a woman's hair is the frame of her face and you don't compromise on the frame.

She closes the car door and stands for a moment, one hand on the roof of the car, steadying herself. Her posture — even now, even diminished, even leaning on a car for support — is straight. Her chin is up. She was a school principal for thirty years. She has commanded every room she has entered since before I was born. She is standing in a driveway at ten o'clock at night, gaunt and ill, and she is composing herself the way she composes herself before entering any room: shoulders back, chin level, expression neutral.

She walks toward the front door.

Her gait is slower than it was. She favors her left side — the cancer has spread to her spine, the pain is manageable, which is what she says about everything. Manageable. Contained. Under control. Three words that mean I will not admit what this is doing to me. She takes the path one step at a time. The concrete path that was brick. Her feet know it the way mine do. She walked this path every morning for two weeks, twenty-five years ago. This was her house. Her holiday house. Her jurisdiction.

She reaches the front step. The bluestone step. The step my foot knows without looking.

She knocks.

Three knocks. Even. Spaced. The knock of someone who expects the door to be opened.

I am standing in the hallway. Through the frosted glass, her silhouette — smaller than it should be, narrower, but the shape is unmistakable. The tall frame. The upright bearing. The way she holds her head. I would know her silhouette in a lineup of a hundred women. I have been watching her silhouette my whole life. Leaning over my cot. Standing in the kitchen doorway. Kneeling in a cream-coloured hallway with her hands on my face.

"Neve." Saskia's voice, low and precise, behind me. "Do you want me to answer it?"

No one else moves. Imogen is frozen on the arm of the sofa. Rowan is frozen in the corner. Aisling is watching me with an expression I can't read — she has been watching me differently since she revealed who she is. Since she learned whose mother was in the house that night.

I open the door.

The cold night air comes in first. Then the salt smell — the sea is close, I can hear it, the low rhythmic push and pull against the shore. Then the light from the hallway falls on my mother's face and I smell her — the hospice smell of antiseptic and lavender soap and underneath both the mineral tang of medication metabolising through skin — and I see her.

She is thinner than she was on Tuesday. I visit every day and she is thinner between visits. The cancer is consuming her from the inside. Her cheekbones are sharp. Her collarbones visible above the open collar of the coat I bought her. But her eyes are the same — dark brown, steady, intelligent. The cancer has not touched her eyes.

She looks at me and she smiles.

The smile is small. Warm. It reaches her eyes. The smile she has given me every day of my life — the smile that says I'm here, you're safe, everything is as it should be. The smile that held me together through three years of psychologists who asked the wrong questions because she was sitting in the room making sure they didn't ask the right ones.

"Hello, sweetheart," she says. "I think it's time we talked about that night."

Sweetheart. The word finds the fault line it was designed for. The word she used in the hallway. Kneeling. Her hands on my face. *You didn't see anything, sweetheart.* The same word. The same voice. The same warmth. She is standing on the threshold of this house — this house where she pushed a twelve-year-old girl through a window, this house where she knelt and rewrote my memory, this house where she held the story for twenty-five years — and she is smiling and calling me sweetheart.

I don't step aside. I don't speak. I stand in the doorway and she stands on the step and we look at each other. The cold air moves between us. The salt air. The air that was here that night, warm instead of cold, summer instead of winter, December instead of July. She was standing in this doorway twenty-five years ago when the ambulance came. She was standing here when the police came. She was standing here when they carried Kiera out and she held my hand and told the officers I was asleep, I was downstairs, I didn't see anything, my daughter was asleep on the couch the whole time.

"Neve, darling." Her voice is gentle. Patient. "Let me in."

She takes a step toward me. Not past me. Toward me. Her hand rises — slowly, deliberately — and finds my wrist. Her fingers close around it. Not tight. Controlled. The way she has always held me — firmly enough to steer, loosely enough to call it love.

"Pack your things," she says. Quiet. Just for me. "We're leaving. You don't need to be here. I'll take you home."

Home. The word she has used my entire life. Home is safe. Home is where I keep you. Home is nineteen minutes from my house, inside the invisible fence she maintained for twenty-five years by making me believe it was my choice.

I don't move.

"Neve." Harder now. The principal's voice. The voice that silenced classrooms, settled disputes, redirected conversations. The voice that told me I was asleep. "This isn't a discussion. Get your bag. We're going."

The room behind me is silent. Five women, not breathing. The evidence on the coffee table. Aisling on the sofa. Saskia by the mantelpiece. Twenty-five years of work, sitting in that room. And my mother is standing in the doorway, holding my wrist, trying to walk me out of it.

"No."

One word. The hardest word I have ever said to her.

Her fingers tighten on my wrist. A fraction. Then they release. She looks at me — not with hurt, not with anger. With calculation. The rapid assessment of a woman who has been managing situations for sixty-eight years and has just encountered resistance she didn't expect.

She drops her hand. She steps past me into the house.

She walks into the hallway. Her hand brushes my arm — lightly, almost incidental — and the touch sends a current through me. Not warmth. Not comfort. Recognition. My body knows her touch the way it knows the pitch of the fourth step. The hand that pushed Kiera is the hand that held my face and the hand that smoothed my hair and I cannot separate them because she never gave me a world where they were different hands.

She brings the cold in with her — and something else. A smell. Chamomile. The particular herbal sweetness of chamomile tea, clinging to her coat, rising off her skin. The same tea she brewed for all of us that night. The same tea she pressed into my hands in the kitchen after, when I was shaking and couldn't stop shaking and she wrapped my fingers around the mug and said "drink this, it'll help you sleep." I loved that tea. I asked for it every time I stayed at her house after. I didn't know it was a sedative. I didn't know she was already managing me.

Her feet find the floorboards and the floorboards creak and I know she hears the same pitch I hear. She is walking them now with the same measured step, a woman returning to a room that still holds the impression of her authority.

She looks at the staircase.

She doesn't look away. She looks at it the way you look at a piece of furniture you used to own — with recognition, with assessment, without sentiment. The staircase. The fourth step. The groan. She knows. She knows the sound it makes. She knew it before I did.

Then she turns left into the living room.

The room goes still.

Saskia has moved away from the window. She's standing by the mantelpiece, arms crossed, the police report still visible on the coffee table. Her face is unreadable — the studied neutrality of a courtroom professional watching a surprise witness enter the box. Aisling is seated on the sofa, the autopsy photographs in her lap, her jaw tight, her eyes on Elaine with the focus of a woman who has been waiting twenty-five years to see this face. Rowan is in the corner, camera raised — photographing, always photographing, recording everything, the instinct of someone who cannot trust memory alone. Imogen is on the arm of the sofa, tear-tracks on her cheeks, hands clasped in her lap, and she looks at Elaine the way a child looks at a headmaster who has entered the playground: frozen, waiting, hoping the attention passes elsewhere.

Elaine surveys the room.

Her eyes move left to right, cataloguing. The police report on the coffee table. She registers it — the slight narrowing of her eyes, the micro-pause before her gaze moves on. Aisling on the sofa with the autopsy photographs. Another micro-pause. Rowan in the corner with her camera. No pause. Imogen on the arm of the sofa, wet-faced. A pause here — not assessment. Something else. A woman assessing damage she may have caused. Then she looks at me. Standing in the doorway between the hall and the room. Neither in nor out.

She walks to the armchair by the fireplace.

Not any armchair. The best armchair. The one with the high back and the wide seat and the position facing the door. The power position. She sits down slowly, one hand on the armrest for support. She is frail. The coat settles around her in dark folds — too much fabric for what remains. Her wrist, where the sleeve rides back, is bruised at the IV site — a dark smear of purple-green beneath a bandage she hasn't changed. She sees me see it. She pulls the sleeve down without comment. But when she settles — when her back touches the chair and her hands rest on the arms and her chin comes up — the room shifts. The gravity bends toward her. She is sitting in the chair the way a judge sits on the bench. This was her house. I was her child. These were the girls who slept under her roof. This was the room where she controlled every narrative that passed through it. She is sitting in her chair and the chair remembers her.

"I see you've been busy," she says.

Her voice is even. Mild, almost. The voice she used when I came home late from a party in Year 10. The voice she used when she found out I'd missed a piano recital. The voice that says I am not angry. I am disappointed. And disappointment is a more effective weapon than anger because you cannot argue with it.

She looks at the police report on the table. "The original incident report. From Sorrento station. You've done your homework, Saskia."

Saskia doesn't respond. Her face is a mask of professional neutrality but her jaw is tight, her fingers pressing into her crossed arms. She is watching Elaine the way she watches a hostile witness — looking for the crack, the inconsistency, the opening.

Elaine's gaze moves to Aisling. "And you," she says. "You're not Cate Morrow."

It's not a question. She knew. Of course she knew. The anonymous tips Aisling described — the photographs, the autopsy details, the name Elaine Cassidy — they came from her. She fed the investigation. She fed Aisling. She has been orchestrating this entire weekend from a bed in a hospice with the same precision she used to build the cover-up.

"You're Aisling Doyle," Elaine says. "Kiera's sister."

Aisling doesn't confirm or deny. She stares at Elaine with a flat, hard intensity I've never seen on Cate's face. This is the real face. The face behind the performance. The face of a woman who was eight years old when her sister didn't come home.

"I knew your mother," Elaine says. "Margaret. She was a kind woman."

The room contracts. Aisling's hands tighten on the photographs. Imogen makes a small sound — not a word, a breath, a sharp intake that might be the beginning of something she can't finish. Rowan lowers her camera. She doesn't photograph this.

Elaine's gaze moves to Imogen. "Imogen. You look well." The way you'd greet a colleague at a conference. The way you'd greet someone whose name you remember but whose relevance you've outgrown. Then: "Rowan. Still taking photographs, I see."

The casualness of it. The way she names each of them, places each of them, assesses each of them in a single glance. She is conducting the room the way she conducted school assemblies — from the front, from the chair, from the position of authority. She is dying and she is gaunt and her coat hangs off her and she is still the most powerful person in this room.

Then she looks at me.

"Neve, sweetheart. Come sit down."

The word finds me again. The same word she used in the hallway, kneeling, her hands on my face. The same word that built the wall at the back of my mind. Hers. Always hers. Made to look like mine.

I don't sit.

I stand in the doorway and I look at my mother sitting in the armchair by the fireplace, in the house where she pushed a twelve-year-old girl through a window, and something cracks behind my sternum. Not one feeling but all of them, shoving through the same door, refusing to queue, and I cannot separate them because she never gave me a world where they were separable. She is the woman who held me when I cried. She is the woman who made me forget why I was crying. My mother sat in the waiting room at the psychologist's and controlled the narrative. My mother held my hand at Kiera's funeral. My mother packed our house in six weeks and moved me to a new school where no one had heard of Sorrento or sleepovers or windows. My mother called me every day for twenty-five years to check that the wall was holding. My mother loves me. My mother used me. Both. Always both.

She is looking at me the way she does when I'm ill — eyes soft, chin tilted, already reaching — and it is real. I have felt it my whole life. All of it love. And all of it built on top of — built to contain — built to hold together — a lie that reshaped my mind and stole my memory and made me doubt my own eyes for a quarter of a century.

"Sit down," she says again. "You're shaking. You need to rest."

I sit. On the sofa. Next to Aisling. The autopsy photographs are between us. I don't look at them. I look at my mother.

She looks around the room one more time. She sees everything — the police report, the photographs, Rowan's camera, Imogen's tears, Aisling's rigid jaw, Saskia's crossed arms, my face. Whatever my face is showing. Whatever is leaking through the cracks in the wall I can no longer maintain.

She folds her hands in her lap. The gesture is precise. Composed. A woman settling in for a long conversation she has been preparing for.

"I think," Elaine says, "it's time we all had an honest conversation. Don't you?"

Elaine looks at me, and for a moment — just a moment — I see her the way I saw her in the hallway. Standing over me. Her hands on my face. Telling me what I didn't see.
