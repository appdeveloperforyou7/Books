# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 24: Neve

## What I Remember

The room doesn't breathe. The air has gone solid — thick and close, pressing against my skin from every direction, like being inside a lung that has stopped mid-inhale.

My mother's hand is still extended. Still open. Still waiting. It hangs in the air between us, the fingers curved slightly inward, the palm upturned, and I am looking at that hand and I am seeing it in two places at once. Here, in the living room, reaching for me across the gap between the armchair and the sofa. And there. In the hallway. Twenty-five years ago. On Kiera's shoulder.

I pull my hand back further. Press it flat against my thigh. Both hands now. Both pressed down, fingers spread, holding me to the sofa the way anchor bolts hold a house to its foundation. Because what is rising inside me is not something I can name with the words I've been given. It is not sadness. It is not anger. It is not grief. It is all of those things and none of them. It is the thing that has been pressing against the wall at the back of my mind for twenty-five years, and the wall is gone, and the thing is loose, and it is enormous.

I open my mouth.

"I remember."

Two words. My voice doesn't sound like mine. It sounds like something dragged from deep water. Low. Broken at the edges. A voice that hasn't been used for the thing it's now doing.

Elaine's hand withdraws. Slowly. She places it in her lap. Her face doesn't change. But I see it — the thing behind her eyes. A sharpening. The way an animal goes still when it senses a threat. She is watching me the way she watches everything: assessing, measuring, deciding how to respond.

"I remember what happened," I say.

"Neve —"

"I was not asleep."

The words cut across hers. Sharp. Final. The first time in my life I have interrupted my mother. The first time I have spoken over her. The sound of my own voice doing it is foreign. Startling. Like hearing a door that has never been opened suddenly swing wide.

I stand up. I don't decide to stand. My body does it. The same way it climbed the stairs. The same way it walked the hallway. My body is done waiting for permission.

"I was not asleep," I say again. "I was not downstairs. I was not on the couch. I was not any of the places you told me I was."

Elaine's chin comes up. A millimetre. The movement of a woman bracing. Her left hand has moved to her abdomen — below the ribs, the heel of her palm pressing into flesh. She catches herself. The hand returns to the armrest. The gesture took less than a second. But I am my mother's daughter. I notice what people try to hide. "You were twelve, sweetheart. You were so little. You were —"

"I was standing at the top of the stairs."

The room goes silent. Not quiet. Silent. The kind of silence that has a sound. A high, thin ringing. The sound of air pressure dropping. The sound in the second after a bomb goes off and before anyone remembers how to scream.

Saskia's arms uncross. Aisling's hands tighten on the photographs. Imogen's breath stops — I hear it stop, a small hitch in her chest. Rowan's camera clicks. Once. The shutter sound very loud in the silence.

"I was standing at the top of the stairs," I say again, and now I am not speaking to my mother. I am speaking to the room. To all of them. To Imogen who was my friend before she was afraid and Rowan who saw things she couldn't name and Saskia who carried the truth like a stone in her pocket and Aisling who lost her sister before she was old enough to understand what losing meant. I am speaking to all of them and I am saying the thing I have never said. The thing that was taken from me and buried and paved over and I have been walking on top of it for twenty-five years without knowing it was there.

"I came upstairs. I heard something — a voice. Sharp. A woman's voice. Not one of us. Not one of the girls. A grown woman's voice from the top of the house. And I got up from the couch and I climbed the stairs. The fourth step groaned. I remember the sound. I have always remembered the sound."

I am shaking. My whole body. And I don't try to stop it. I don't press my hands flat. I don't breathe slowly. I don't do any of the things I have taught my clients to do. I let the shaking happen because the shaking is the truth and the truth has been locked up long enough and the lock has broken and the door is swinging open and there is no going back through it.

"The hallway was cream. The carpet was blue. The window at the end was open. December warm. Salt air. The moon was full. Everything."

I close my eyes. Not to hide. To see.

"There were two figures at the end of the hallway. By the window. One was tall. A woman. Her back was to me. Her hand was on Kiera's shoulder."

I open my eyes and look at my mother.

"Your hand. On her shoulder."

Elaine doesn't move. She is very still in the armchair. Her hands are folded in her lap. Her face is composed. But her eyes — her eyes are bright. Not with tears. With something else. Alertness. The look of a woman watching a structure she built begin to crack.

"I saw your arm move," I say. "One movement. A push. Your palm flat against her shoulder. I saw the force go through your arm into her body. I saw her stumble backward. One step. Two. I saw her arms come up and her hands reach for the window frame."

My voice breaks. Not the way Elaine's broke — controlled, measured, a performance of fracture. My voice breaks because it is breaking. Because the words have edges and they are tearing my throat on the way out.

"I saw her fingernails. She'd painted them. Blue polish. Chipped. She'd done all our nails earlier that evening. I saw her fingers spread. I saw her fingernails catch the paint on the frame. Five lines. Downward. She was holding on. She was trying to hold on."

I stop.

The room is waiting. Everyone is waiting. The air itself is waiting, suspended, strung tight, the way your lungs lock when you are standing at the edge of something and you know the fall is coming and you can't stop it.

"And then she was gone."

The words come out as a whisper. I don't choose the softness — the words are too heavy for volume. They fall out of me, weighted and final.

"She went through the window. One second she was there — her fingers on the frame, her nails in the paint — and the next she wasn't. The window was empty. The frame was empty. The air where she had been was empty. And there was a sound from outside. From the garden. A wet sound. A heavy sound. The sound of a body hitting the ground."

I am crying. I don't know when I started. The tears are running down my face and I am not wiping them away. I am not managing them. I am not performing restraint for the benefit of anyone in this room. I am standing in a living room in Sorrento and I am crying for Kiera Doyle, who was twelve years old, who was my friend, who fell from a window and died, and I am crying for myself, who saw it happen and was told it didn't. The tears are hot and fast and taste like salt and will not stop.

I look at Aisling. Aisling, who was eight when her sister didn't come home. Aisling, who spent her whole life looking for the person who took her sister. I look at her and I say: "I saw her fall. I saw your sister fall. And I am sorry. I am so sorry I didn't say anything. I am so sorry I didn't remember. I am so sorry I let her lie to me."

Aisling's face doesn't change. Her jaw is tight. Her eyes are hard. She doesn't nod. She doesn't forgive me. She watches me with the eyes of a woman who has waited twenty-five years for this and is going to hear every word before she decides what to do with them.

I turn back to my mother.

"And then you turned around."

My voice has changed. The crying is still happening — tears still falling, chest still tight — but there is something else underneath it now. Something harder. Something that has been growing in the space between the memory and the telling. It is fury. Clean and hot, and it is the first rage I have ever let myself feel toward my mother — because every other time the fury started to rise, the wall was there, the wall she built, the wall that whispered you don't feel this, you don't think this, you are confused, you have such an active imagination.

"You turned around and you saw me. Standing at the top of the stairs. And you crossed the hallway in three steps — you didn't run, you never run, you move fast but you never look panicked — and you knelt in front of me. You knelt down and you put your hands on my face. Your palms were warm. Your palms were steady. You held my face the way you hold it when I have a nightmare. The way you hold it when you want me to listen. The way you hold it when you are about to tell me something that you need me to believe."

I can see it. As clearly as the room around me. The cream walls. The blue carpet. The empty window. My mother's face, close to mine, her thumbs on my cheekbones, her eyes steady, her voice low and calm and warm.

"And you said: 'You didn't see anything.'"

I am looking at Elaine. She is looking at me. Her face is still. Composed. But the stillness has changed. It is not the stillness of control. It is the stillness of a woman holding very, very still because if she moves, something will crack.

"You said: 'You were asleep.' You said: 'You were downstairs.' You said those words. And you said them again. And again. And again. Until they replaced what I actually saw. Until the real memory was buried and your version was laid on top of it. Until I couldn't tell the difference between what I saw and what you told me I saw."

My hands are fists at my sides. My fingernails are digging into my palms. Four crescents on each hand. My own grip marks. My own evidence.

"You told me every day," I say. "You told me I was asleep. You took me to doctors and sat in the room and told them I was anxious and they believed you because you were the principal and the mother and mothers don't lie about things like that — that's the baseline assumption. That's what every clinician in the room would have assumed. You moved us. Cut me off from everyone who might have asked me what I remembered. And then you called me every day to check — I thought it was love. It was love. But it was also the wall. You were checking the wall was holding."

I stop. I am breathing hard. My chest heaves. My face is wet. My hands are shaking and they are fists and I cannot open them, the fingers locked in place as if the joints have rusted shut. The shaking is the truth and the truth has been in my body for twenty-five years and my body is done being quiet about it.

"Do you know what it's like," I say, and my voice cracks, splinters, "to be a therapist? To sit across from people every day and tell them to trust their perceptions? To tell them their memories are real? To tell them they know what they saw? And to go home every night unable to do any of it for myself? Because my mother told me I didn't see what I saw? Because she taught me my own eyes were not to be trusted?"

I look at her. She is sitting in the armchair and her face is grey. Not composed grey. Blood-drained grey. The colour of a woman who is hearing something she did not expect to hear. She prepared for this — she prepared for the confession, for the controlled narrative, for the accident story, for the tears and the hand-reach and the "I was trying to protect you." But she did not prepare for this. She did not prepare for me to remember. She thought the wall would hold. She thought the overwrite was permanent. She thought twenty-five years of daily reinforcement had buried the girl at the top of the stairs so deep she would never climb out.

But I climbed out. This afternoon. In the hallway. I put my hand on the frame and I climbed out.

"You were confused, sweetheart. You were overtired. You'd been dreaming," she says. The words come out slowly. Carefully. The same words. The same tone. The same technique. Sweetheart. The word hits me like a smell — chamomile and eucalyptus, steeping, the tea she pressed into my hands when I was shaking, the tea I drank every night for twenty years, the tea that meant let me handle this, the tea that quieted everything, the tea that was her love and her control steeped in the same cup. Sweetheart. The lock turning. The wall holding. The same word she used when she knelt in the hallway and began the overwrite.

Then her eyes move past me. To Rowan. In the corner. Something shifts in her face — not the performance, not the warmth, but something underneath. Calculation.

"The camera," Elaine says. Not to me. To Rowan. Her voice is flat now, the warmth gone. "You found it. Under the floorboard."

Rowan doesn't respond. Her camera is in her lap. Her face is still.

"Where is it?"

Two words. No sweetness. No "sweetheart." The principal's voice. The voice that directed, corrected, controlled.

"It's safe," Rowan says.

Elaine's jaw tightens. For a moment — one moment — the mask drops entirely. Not grief. Not confusion. Cold, rapid assessment. The camera contains twenty-four photographs. The last things Kiera saw through a viewfinder. Including, possibly, a flash photograph of a hallway with two figures, one tall and one small, taken moments before the push. The camera is evidence. The camera is a witness that doesn't forget. The camera is the one thing Elaine cannot gaslight.

"Give it to me."

"No."

The word comes from Rowan, but the movement comes from Saskia. She steps between Elaine's chair and the corner where Rowan sits. Not aggressive. Blocking. The way you block a door when someone is trying to leave.

Elaine looks at Saskia. Saskia looks back. Two women who speak the same language — evidence, control, strategy — having a conversation without words. The conversation says: you will not take that camera. You will not leave this room with anything. You will not manage this.

Elaine's hands press flat against the armrests. Her knuckles go white. The calculation runs behind her eyes — options, exits, angles — and finds none. She is trapped. Not by a locked door. Not by a storm. By five women and a coffee table full of evidence and a disposable camera that holds silver halide ghosts.

She turns back to me. And the mask goes up again — but it doesn't fit anymore. It sits crooked on her face. The warmth doesn't reach her eyes.

"Neve —"

"I'm not confused," I say. "I was confused. For twenty-five years, I was confused. Because you confused me. That's what confusion is when it's manufactured — it's someone else's certainty poured into a space where your own certainty should be. You made me confused and then you told me I was confused and then you took me to doctors who confirmed I was confused and the whole time the thing I was confused about was the thing you were hiding."

I step closer. Two feet between us now. I can see the veins on the backs of her hands. I can see the pulse in her throat, thin and fast. I can see the woman who held me when I cried and taught me to fold shirts and told me to be brave and be careful and be good. The woman who was the tallest person in every room. The woman who commanded school assemblies and parent-teacher nights and the narrative of my entire life. The woman who knelt in a cream-coloured hallway and placed her warm palms on my face and spoke a lie into me so deeply I mistook it for my own memory.

"I know what I saw," I say.

My voice is quiet now. The fury is still there — it hasn't gone anywhere, it's still burning — but beneath it, beneath the rage and the grief and the shaking, there is something else. Something clean and hard and immovable. The thing I have been circling all weekend. The thing the wall was built to contain. The thing that came back this afternoon in the hallway when the cream walls and the blue carpet and the warm December air returned and I was twelve and I was standing at the top of the stairs and I saw.

"I saw you push her."

Four words. Four syllables. The simplest sentence I have ever spoken and the hardest. I saw you push her. I. Saw. You. Push. Her. Each word a door closing. Each word a wall coming down — not my wall. Hers. The wall she built around the truth. The wall she maintained for twenty-five years with daily phone calls and clinical terms and the word "sweetheart" used as a lock.

Elaine doesn't speak. Her mouth opens and closes and no sound comes out. For the first time tonight — for the first time in my entire life — my mother has nothing to say. The woman who never once, in my whole life, was at a loss. The woman who narrated my childhood so thoroughly I couldn't find my own voice inside it. The woman who told me what I saw and what I didn't see and what I dreamed and what was real and what to fear and what to forget. She is sitting in the armchair and her mouth is open and there is nothing.

The room holds its breath.
