# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 19: Rowan

## Two Figures

The room is still ringing with Imogen's voice. The voice — her voice, the real one, not the warm layer she wears over it like a cardigan buttoned to the throat — is still in the air, an afterimage burning on the retina after the flash has fired — the object gone, the light remaining. I have never heard Imogen sound like that. I have never seen her face without its performance. The rawness of her — the stripped, unvarnished, twelve-year-old rawness — has changed the composition of the room. The frame has shifted. We are not who we were an hour ago.

The camera sits in my lap. I have not raised it. Some moments are not meant to be photographed — the lens would flatten them, reduce them to light and composition, and some moments are three-dimensional things that require a body present in the room to hold their weight.

Cate's question — "Can you remember the words?" — is hanging in the air between us. Imogen shakes her head. She doesn't know. Or she can't say. The voice was sharp, female, a command — but the words are lost. Twenty-five years of telling herself it was the TV have eroded the specific syllables. What remains is the tone. The register. The authority. The voice of a woman telling a child something that needed to be said immediately, with force, without room for misunderstanding.

I know what Imogen heard. Not because she has described it. Because I heard it too.

Not the voice. I heard something else. I heard the sound between the gasp and the thud, the sound that has lived in my spine since I was twelve, the sound that enters through the soles of my feet and climbs. But I saw something too. I saw — I have always seen — the two shapes. The tall one. The small one. The movement of the arm. The hand reaching for the frame.

For twenty-five years these have been fragments. Sensations without sequence. Impressions without narrative. The cold of the floorboards. The blue quality of December moonlight. A tall silhouette blocking the window. A small hand with fingers spread wide. The scrape of nails on wood. The empty frame.

The brain doesn't make a story from something like that. It breaks the story into pieces and hides them in different rooms. The fear in one room. The images in another. The cold in another. And it locks the doors.

But the shards are assembling. They have been assembling since I found the scratches on the frame. Since I saw Kiera's hand — I know it was Kiera's hand, I know those fingers, I held them, I pressed my palm against them in hand games and walking to school and in the dark of a sleeping bag when we whispered secrets — since I saw the fingers slide and the grip fail and the frame become empty.

And now Imogen's voice has given me another piece. A woman's voice, sharp, from upstairs. Before the fall. The timeline is resolving. Not a cloud of sensation. A contact sheet — frames in sequence, each one sharper than the last. Before, during, after. The word "before" has weight now. Before the fall, there was something. Before the window was empty, there were two figures. Before the sound that has lived in my spine for twenty-five years, there was a voice that Imogen heard and buried.

I look at the documents spread on the table — the police report, the autopsy summary, the crime scene photograph with its reflected figure, the scratch photographs I took with my own hands from a gap in the aluminium frame no wider than a fingernail. The evidence has been accumulating all weekend, each piece a fragment of a larger image. The image is almost complete. One more piece and the frame will hold. One more piece and the photograph will develop here, in the dark room of this living room, and we will all see what has been slowly emerging from the chemical bath of twenty-five years.

I set the camera on the chair beside me. I stand. The room adjusts — four women tracking my movement, Saskia from her position of authority near the table, Neve from the sofa, white-faced and still, Imogen beside her, face blotched and wet, eyes swollen to slits, and Cate in the corner, calm, watching, always watching.

"There's something I need to show you. Not with the camera. With words."

The words are formed before I speak them. This is new. Normally the fragments come in fragments — I say "the cold" and "the light" and "the sound" and the words trail into silence because the thread won't hold. But the thread is holding now. For the first time in twenty-five years, the thread is holding and I can pull it and the story comes, not in pieces, but in order.

"I was in the hallway," I say. "I don't know how I got there. I must have woken up. The house was — the house was breathing. You know how a house breathes at night? The air moves through the rooms. The floorboards shift. The sounds are different. Quieter. Deeper. The house was breathing and I was standing in the upstairs hallway and I didn't remember getting out of my sleeping bag or climbing the stairs. I was just there. Standing at the end of the hallway near the bathroom door. The carpet was cold under my feet."

I pause. The room is listening. Four women and the silence between them and the story I am telling — the first story I have ever told in sequence, start to finish, without losing the thread.

"The cold is what I remember most clearly," I say. "The cold of the floorboards coming through the carpet. It was summer, it was December, the window was open and the air was warm — but the floor was cold. The cold came up through my feet the way cold comes up through the ground when you stand on it barefoot. It was the cold of the underside — the cold the house keeps in its darkroom, beneath the exposure of its surfaces."

I look at my own hands. At the silver ring on my right hand. The moonstone is dull in the overhead light. In December moonlight it would have glowed.

I close my eyes. The darkness behind my eyelids is full of December.

"The hallway was warm," I say. "December warm. The window at the end was open — the old window, the one with the timber frame and the brass hinge. The moonlight was coming through and it was gold — thick, liquid gold, the way December moonlight is, the way it pools on surfaces. The carpet was blue. Deep blue. And the light lay on it like water."

I open my eyes. Saskia is watching me with the focused attention she brings to witnesses. Neve's hands are very still in her lap. Imogen is listening through the aftermath of her own collapse, her breath uneven, her eyes on my face.

"There were two figures at the end of the hallway," I say. "In front of the window. One tall. One small. The tall one was standing between me and the window, backlit — a silhouette against the moonlight. The small one was between the tall one and the window."

I pause. The next sentence is the one I have never been able to form — the frame I could never bring into focus, the exposure I could never get right. The sentence that has existed as a sensation — a movement registered by the body and stored without language. The arm. The push. The thing that happened between the tall figure being still and the small figure being gone.

"The tall one moved," I say. "An arm extended. A hand — large, adult — pressed against the small one's shoulder. And the small one moved backward. Towards the window. Not a stumble. A push. The force travelled from the tall one's arm through the hand into the small one's body and the small one — the small one was Kiera."

The name comes out clear. Kiera. Not "the small one." Kiera. The name attaches to the shape and the shape becomes a person and the person becomes my friend and my friend is being pushed towards an open window by a figure that is taller than any of us.

"She reached for the frame," I say. "Her hand — her right hand — came up and her fingers spread and she grabbed the wood. Her fingernails. Blue polish. Chipped. She'd painted them earlier that evening — she painted all our nails. Blue. The colour of the carpet. And her nails scraped the paint and the lines emerged — five lines, downward — and her knuckles were white and she was holding on."

I stop. The memory is vivid — not a flashback, not a fragment, but a narrative. I am inside it and outside it simultaneously. The twelve-year-old standing in the blue-gold light of the hallway. The thirty-seven-year-old standing in a living room in July telling the story for the first time.

"And then the grip failed," I say. "Her fingers slid. One by one. The pinky first. Then the ring finger. Then the middle. Slow. The way things fall in slow motion when your eyes are making a record and the record is too important to speed through. The hand came off the frame. The fingers opened. And she went through the window. Not fell. Went. As if the air behind her had reached up and taken her. One moment she was here — fingers white on the wood, body leaning into the opening, the moonlight catching the blue polish on her nails — and the next moment she was not. The window was empty. The frame held nothing. The space where she had been was just space, filling with moonlight."

I stop. The room is holding the image. The weight of four people seeing what I have carried for twenty-five years, the moment a private exposure becomes public evidence.

"Afterward — I don't know how long afterward, seconds, maybe — I heard a sound from below. From the garden. Not loud. The kind of sound grass makes when it receives something heavy. Something it was never meant to hold."

The room is silent. The heating clicks. Wind against the glass. The tick of the kitchen clock Imogen must have straightened at some point. But the silence beneath the sounds is enormous.

"The tall figure — the proportions, the stance, the weight of her — it was a woman," I say. "I could tell by the silhouette. She was backlit — moonlight behind her, no features, just shape. But she was tall. Taller than any of us. And she stood the way adults stand — planted, certain, the weight of her distributed with authority. Not the stance of a child. The stance of someone who expected the space around her to accommodate her."

Saskia speaks. Her voice is precise, measured, but there is something beneath it — a current, a frequency, the vibration of a question she has been waiting twenty-five years to ask.

"Tall like an adult?"

"Tall like an adult," I say.

The words land. Tall like an adult. An adult in the hallway. An adult who pushed a child. An adult whose face was hidden by the moonlight and whose voice I did not hear but Imogen did — sharp, female, authoritative. An adult who was in the house that night and who was not one of us.

I look at Neve. She is sitting on the sofa and she has not spoken since Saskia's presentation and her face is white and her hands are flat on her thighs and she is very, very still. The kind of still that is not peace. The kind of still that is a held breath before the shutter clicks — everything frozen, everything waiting, the image suspended in the instant before exposure.

She knows. I see it the way I see light on a surface — not through interpretation but through direct perception. She knows who the tall figure was. She has known since before this weekend. Perhaps she has always known. She is carrying it. The same way I carried the cold and the light and the sound. The same way Imogen carried the voice. The same way Saskia carried the word "bilaterally." We have all been carrying pieces of the same shattered night, each of us holding a fragment to the light, none of us with the whole frame. And the edges are sharp. Twenty-five years of carrying broken glass inside us, pressed against the soft tissue of our lives, and we learned to hold it still so it wouldn't cut. But stillness is not healing. Stillness is just the decision to stop bleeding.

But the fragments are on the table now. The scratches. The bruising pattern. The landing distance. The reflected figure. Imogen's voice. My two figures. The pieces are coming together and the picture they form is — someone tall, someone adult, someone who was in this house that night and pushed a twelve-year-old girl through a window.

Who was in the house? Whose house was it? Whose mother was upstairs? The questions form in the room without anyone speaking them. They hover above the documents on the table like heat above pavement.

Cate speaks from the corner. She has been quiet through all of it — through Saskia's presentation, through Imogen's collapse, through my account. Sitting in the armchair by the bookshelf, her face composed, her hands folded, her eyes moving between each of us with the careful attention of someone collecting information. She has not reacted with surprise to any of it. Not the police report. Not the photograph. Not the voice. Not the two figures.

She asks: "Was there — I mean, were there grown-ups around? That night?"

The question is simple. Five words. Syntax that a child could parse. But the question is not simple. The question is the centre of the maze. The question is the key that turns the lock on the room where the answer has been sitting for twenty-five years.

Everyone turns to Neve.
