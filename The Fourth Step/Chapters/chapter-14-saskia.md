# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 14: Saskia

## The Report

I leave Neve in the kitchen and climb the stairs.

The upstairs hallway is empty. Grey walls. Blonde floor. The sealed window at the end. I walk to the spot where I found her — four metres from the top of the stairs, one metre from the window. She was lying on her left side, knees drawn up, hands pressed flat against the floor, fingers splayed. Her eyes were open but unfocused.

Her skin was grey-white — not the pallor of a faint but the colour of someone whose blood supply has redistributed to where it's needed in a crisis. Core. Brain. Away from the face.

The floor shows no mark. The hallway maintains its alibi — blank, unrevealing, professionally renovated.

I look at the window. The sealed window that cannot be opened. Neve was standing here when it happened. Standing in front of the window. Looking at it.

What did you see, Neve?

I file the question. Fifteenth question, updated from fourteen. The fifteenth: what did Neve experience in front of this window that caused her to collapse?

The answer is not dizziness. I have seen witnesses faint. Fainting is specific — loss of consciousness, loss of muscle tone, the body going slack. Neve did not go slack. Neve went rigid. That is not a vasovagal response. That is a trauma response. The body re-experiencing something the mind has been protecting it from.

Something happened to Neve Cassidy in this hallway twenty-five years ago. Something more than being told a friend fell from a window. Something she has not remembered. Or has remembered and been made to forget — the two possibilities are different in every way except the one that matters: the result is the same. A woman who cannot trust her own mind.

I go back downstairs.

My room is the front bedroom. Ground floor. Door locked — I locked it this morning, key in my trouser pocket. I unlock it. Enter. Close the door.

The desk is by the window. I sit. I open the drawer. Small brass lock. I remove the document sleeve. Rigid A4. Archival quality. Acid-free.

I open it.

The police incident report is on top. Two pages, photocopied from the original at the Sorrento police station in 2006. I have read this document forty-seven times. I read it a forty-eighth.

The header: Victoria Police, Incident Report, Sorrento Station, 14 December 1999. Officer: Senior Constable D. Hargreaves, badge 4417. Retired 2008. Declined to speak to me when I contacted him in 2012. "It was a long time ago, Ms. Brennan. Let it go." The third person to tell me that.

I skip to the injury section. "'Minor bruising noted on the upper back and shoulders, bilaterally. Consistent with impact injuries from fall.'"

I have read this sentence forty-seven times. Bilateral bruising on the upper back and shoulders. Both sides. Symmetrical. This is not a fall pattern. This is a grip pattern — hands placed on the shoulders from behind, fingers wrapping around the upper arms, the force of a push compressing capillary beds under the skin. The report says "consistent." Hargreaves noted it and wrote "consistent" and filed it. Technically defensible. Fundamentally false.

The autopsy summary. "Cause of death: blunt force trauma to the head consistent with fall from height. Manner of death: accidental." The paramedic run sheet records the body's position: approximately two metres from the base of the wall. Two metres. A child falling backward from a window lands at most half a metre from the wall. Gravity pulls straight down. Two metres requires horizontal force. A push.

Until now.

Rowan's photographs — the fingernail scratches on the window frame. Five parallel lines. Downward. A child's hand gripping the frame, sliding, losing purchase. Someone held on. Someone was pulled away or pushed away or let go. The scratches are physical evidence. They corroborate the bruising pattern. They corroborate the landing distance. They corroborate what I have believed since I was fourteen years old: Kiera Doyle was pushed.

And Neve Cassidy just collapsed in the hallway, in front of the window, in the exact position where the scratches are, exhibiting a trauma response consistent with recovered memory.

I place the documents back in the sleeve. I close the sleeve. I place the sleeve on the desk. I look at it — twenty-five years of source material, indexed and cross-referenced, awaiting disclosure.

Tonight. I'll tell them tonight. At dinner, when they're all together — I'll put the report on the table and read the injury section aloud and explain what it means.

I'll show Rowan's photographs. The bruising. The landing distance. The scratches. The evidence that Kiera was pushed and the push was covered up and someone in this house knows who did it.

I haven't decided whether to accuse. Accusation requires a target. I have evidence. I have suspicion. I have twenty-five years of investigation that has led to this house and these women and this weekend. The answer is here. The tightness at the base of my skull that comes before a witness breaks, the shift in air pressure when a room is about to turn.

Tonight.

I stand. I walk to the door. I open it.

And I see it.

A manila envelope on the floor. Brown. A4. Sealed. Slid under my door — the hallway edge buckled from the pressure of pushing through a gap less than a centimetre wide. Whoever slid it pressed firmly.

I pick it up. The envelope is light. Not empty — something inside, something flat. No writing on the outside. No label. No postmark. No name.

Someone delivered this. Someone in this house, or someone who has access to it.

I close my door. I sit on the bed. I open the envelope.

Inside: a single photograph.

I slide it out. Glossy paper. Standard photographic print, twenty by twenty-five centimetres. The image is a crime scene photograph — I recognise the style immediately: the flat, unflattering flash, the wide angle, the forensic framing that captures everything and aestheticises nothing. This is not a photograph taken for beauty. This is a photograph taken for record.

The image shows the upstairs hallway. Not as it is now — grey walls, blonde floor, sealed window. As it was. Twenty-five years ago. Cream walls. Blue carpet. And at the end of the hallway, the window — the original window, the timber-framed casement, open, swung outward into the December night. The window that Kiera went through.

The photograph is labelled in the lower right corner with a date and a reference number: 14/12/1999. CR-4471-99. This is an official crime scene photograph from the night of Kiera's death. It is not in the file I obtained through freedom of information. The FOI release contained twelve photographs — exterior shots, the lawn, the position of the body. This is from inside the hallway. It was not released.

Someone has a copy of an unreleased crime scene photograph and they slid it under my door while I was in the kitchen with Neve.

The quality is high — not a photocopy of a photocopy but an original or first-generation print. The flash has illuminated the hallway evenly: cream walls, blue carpet, two doors on the left, one on the right. The window at the end, open, the night visible through the gap. Shot from the top of the stairs, looking toward the window.

The flash has created reflections in the window glass. The open casement has a pane of glass in the upper portion — the bottom pane swings open, the top pane is fixed — and in the glass, the flash has captured a reflection of the space behind the camera. The hallway. The staircase. And in the corner of the glass, at the top of the stairs, a figure.

I lean closer. The light in my room is not ideal — grey July afternoon, the window facing east, most of the morning light already gone. I angle the photograph toward the lamp on the desk.

The reflection is small — compressed by the angle and the curvature of the old glass. But it is clear enough. A figure. Standing at the top of the stairs. Behind the photographer, or to the side, in the position where someone would stand if they had just arrived at the top of the stairs and stopped.

The figure is small. Child-sized. The proportions are wrong for an adult — the head-to-body ratio, the height relative to the bannister. A child. A child standing at the top of the stairs, in the hallway, during the crime scene processing, watching. Very still. The stillness of someone who has been told not to move.

This child is not listed in any witness statement as being in the hallway. The witness statements — I've read them all — describe all four surviving girls as being downstairs, in the living room, when the police arrived. All four. Downstairs. Not one of them was reported as being in the upstairs hallway.

But the photograph shows a child in the hallway.

Someone was there. In the hallway. At the top of the stairs. While the crime scene was being processed. Watching. And that someone was not recorded in any official account.

I turn the photograph over. The back is blank. No annotation. No attribution. No indication of where this came from or who had it or how it arrived under my door.

I turn it back. I look at the reflection again. The child at the top of the stairs. The small figure, half-hidden by the reflection of the bannister, standing very still. Standing the way you stand when you're watching something you're not supposed to see. Standing the way Neve was standing when I found her on the floor of this hallway twenty minutes ago.

The photograph came from someone with access to unreleased crime scene materials. Someone who was in this house while I was in the kitchen with Neve. Someone who wanted me to see this specific image at this specific moment. Someone running an investigation parallel to mine, with access to evidence I've never seen and sources I haven't identified.

Cate. Cate Morrow, who asks questions like a journalist and slipped on Kiera's name and knew whose house this was before Imogen told her. Cate, who arrived this morning and has been watching all of us with the focus of someone gathering evidence rather than reuniting with friends.

Or someone else. Someone I haven't identified. Someone pulling strings from a distance.

I file the question — seventeenth — and I look at the photograph again.

The child at the top of the stairs. Watching. Not recorded. Not accounted for. Present in the hallway when every official account says she was downstairs.

I think about Neve. I think about her saying "I was downstairs" and "I felt dizzy" and the way her eyes dropped to the middle distance when she said it — fixed on something that wasn't in the room, the gaze of someone reading from a script she didn't write. I think about her collapsing in front of the window. Not fainting. Collapsing. Her body rigid, fingers splayed, jaw clenched. She was not in this July. She was somewhere else. In a hallway that was cream, not grey. With carpet that was blue, not blonde. With a window that was open.

I think about the reflected figure in the photograph.

I think about Neve.

I pick up my phone. I photograph the photograph — the crime scene image, the reflection, the figure. Then I slide the photograph back into the manila envelope and place it in the document sleeve with the police report and the autopsy summary. I lock the desk drawer.

I sit on the bed. The time is twelve-fourteen. The house is quiet. Neve is in the kitchen. The others are unaccounted for.

And someone in this house has access to evidence that was never released and is distributing it strategically.

Tonight. I will present what I have. The police report. The injury analysis. Rowan's photographs of the scratches. And now this — the crime scene photograph with the reflected figure, the child who was in the hallway when no one was supposed to be in the hallway, the witness who was never recorded. And the camera — Rowan showed me. Kiera's disposable camera, hidden under a floorboard, the film still inside. Twenty-four undeveloped frames. Evidence in silver halide, waiting for developer.

Tonight. All of it.

I look at the photograph. In the reflection: a figure at the top of the stairs. Someone was there. Someone watching. Someone who isn't in any official account.
