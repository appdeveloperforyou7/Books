# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 15: Imogen

## The Game

The wind has shifted. It came from the south this morning, steady, salt-laden, normal. Now it's from the west, gusting in irregular bursts that rattle the kitchen window and make the back door shudder in its frame. The house doesn't like this wind. The house fights it, creaking in the walls, the timber contracting, the whole structure tightening against something it cannot see.

Saturday afternoon and the house has that particular kind of quiet that happens when five women are all in different rooms and none of them are talking to each other.

We all needed a bit of space after the morning, after Neve felt unwell. It's okay. She's okay. She said she hasn't eaten enough, which is perfectly reasonable. Saskia got her water. Now Neve is in the garden and I am in the kitchen doing what I always do, keeping my hands busy.

I've made a pot of tea. I've sliced the remaining banana bread. I've arranged the Tim Tams on a plate, two rows, alternating chocolate and caramel, because presentation matters, especially when no one is watching, because the alternative is sitting still and sitting still in this house means the walls start to lean in and the quiet gets into your hands and makes them shake. So I arrange biscuits. That's okay. That's a normal thing to do.

Cate appears in the doorway. She's nice. Perfectly nice. A bit quiet. A bit watchful. The way she listens is the way I imagine a therapist listens, except Neve is the therapist, and Cate is just a nice woman who was a couple of years behind us at school and wanted to reconnect. That's all.

"Hey," she says. "How are we doing? You look like you could use another pair of hands — can I help with something?"

"Oh, I'm okay! Totally okay. Do you want tea? There's banana bread, though it's a bit stale, and Tim Tams, obviously." I laugh. The sound hits the kitchen tiles and bounces back wrong, too hard, too bright, like a cup set down with too much force. "Sorry. It's been a morning, hasn't it?"

Cate sits at the table and wraps her hands around a mug and looks at me with those steady grey-green eyes and smiles and the smile is kind and it's also searching. The way Saskia searches. Everyone in this house is hunting for something and I'm the one arranging plates.

"You know what we need?" I say, because the silence is starting to fill up the room. "A game. Something to break the tension. We used to play games all the time, didn't we? At THE FOURTH STEP. We played —"

I stop. The word is out. THE FOURTH STEP. I said it the way you say a name at a funeral — quietly, accidentally, and then immediately wishing you could take it back.

"I mean not that sleepover. I mean sleepovers in general. Birthday parties and —"

"What kind of game?" Cate asks. Her voice is gentle. She's giving me an exit from the sentence I was drowning in.

"I don't know. Cards?" I open drawers until I find them, a deck of cards in the third drawer.

"Cards!" I hold them up.

Cate takes the cards and turns them over in her hands. Her fingers are precise. Examining, not shuffling.

"We could play something more interesting," she says.

"Like what?"

"Truth or Dare."

The words land in the kitchen like a plate breaking on tile — the sharp crack, the silence after, the pieces that can't be unbroken. Truth or Dare. Kiera's game. Her favourite. She was the one who always suggested it — sitting cross-legged in her sleeping bag with the torch under her chin, grinning like she was about to set the world on fire. She always chose truth. She said dares were for people who were afraid of honesty.

"Oh, God, no," I say. I'm laughing again. The laugh is a door slamming. "That's — okay, no. We're thirty-seven. We're not twelve. We can't just — no."

"Why not?" Cate's smile doesn't change. "It's a reunion. We're getting to know each other again."

The back door opens. Neve comes in from the garden, pink-cheeked from the cold. Behind her, Saskia — where did Saskia come from? Was she in the garden too? I didn't see her go out. I don't always see Saskia. She moves through this house like the smell of something burning — everywhere before you notice, impossible to pin down.

"You two are plotting something," Neve says. Not a question. Her voice is steady. Therapist voice. She's recovered from this morning and she's okay and everything is okay.

"Cate wants to play Truth or Dare," I say. I say it the way you say something ridiculous, with a laugh, with an eye-roll, with the expectation that everyone will agree it's silly and we'll play cards instead.

"Oh," Neve says. Just that. Oh. The word is small and neutral and it contains about seventeen things she's not saying.

"Actually," Saskia says, and her voice is the voice she uses in courtrooms, measured, precise, the voice of someone building a case — "that's not a bad idea."

I look at her. She's leaning against the doorframe with her arms crossed and her eyes are doing the thing they do — the way she's been watching all of us since we were kids, like she's taking notes for a test none of us knew we were sitting.

"Saskia," I say. "You hate games."

"I hate wasted time. This isn't wasted time." She looks at the cards in Cate's hand. "Where's Rowan?"

"Upstairs," Neve says. "She's been upstairs most of the day."

"I'll get her." Saskia goes. The kitchen is quiet. I pour tea. Cate shuffles the cards — her fingers still precise, still examining — and Neve sits at the table and wraps her hands around a mug she hasn't drunk from and we are three women in a kitchen on a Saturday afternoon pretending that this is a normal thing that normal people do.

Rowan appears. Saskia behind her. Rowan is always somewhere else, even when she's here. Her eyes are soft, unfocused. Her hair is loose. The silver ring on her right hand. She sits in the chair nearest the wall and waits.

"Truth or Dare," Saskia announces. She sits at the head of the table. She has positioned herself with a clear line of sight to every person in the room. "Imogen's idea."

"My — no, it was Cate's —"

"It doesn't matter whose idea it was," Saskia says. "We're playing."

She takes the cards from Cate. She fans them face-down on the table. "Highest card goes first."

The cards fall. Cate turns over a jack, Neve a six, me a three — typical — Rowan a queen, and Saskia the ace of spades. Of course she does. Saskia always wins the setup.

"Truth or Dare," Saskia says to Cate. "Since you suggested it."

Cate smiles. "Truth."

"What's the one thing you've never told anyone?"

The question lands. Not the way a question lands in a game — light, jokey. The question lands the way a hand lands on a shoulder. Hard. Deliberate. Staying.

Cate doesn't blink. "I'm terrified of the ocean. Ironic, given I grew up near it. I had a nightmare as a child — I dreamt I was pulled under — and I've never been able to swim since." She says it. Rehearsed, almost. Like a speech she's prepared.

"I love the ocean," I say, because someone has to say something. "Sophie'd live in the water if I let her."

"My turn," Cate says. She looks at Rowan. "Rowan. Truth or Dare?"

"Truth."

"Where is home for you?"

Rowan blinks. The question seems to travel through her slowly, the way light travels through water. "Home," she says. "I don't have one. I have places. Lisbon was gold. Reykjavik was blue. But home — home is a smell. Eucalyptus and salt. I carry it."

"That's not an answer," Saskia says.

"It's the only one I have," Rowan says.

"My turn," Rowan says. She looks at me. "Imogen. Truth or Dare."

"Truth, obviously. I'm not doing a dare, I'm in a cardigan."

"What do you dream about?"

The question is simple. Four words. Rowan asks it the way she does everything — with the precision of someone who sees surfaces and doesn't understand what's beneath them.

"Dreams? Oh, you know. Normal stuff. Sophie, school, work. I dream about forgetting to mark homework. Very boring." I'm talking too fast. "Greg says I talk in my sleep. Says I say 'it's okay' over and over. Which, honestly, probably accurate."

No one laughs.

"My turn," I say. I grab the moment and I run with it. I look at Neve. "Neve. Truth or Dare."

"Truth." Neve's voice is careful. Measured. She's sitting with her spine straight and her hands flat on the table and she looks like she's bracing for something.

I should ask her something safe. Liam, Lily, her practice — anything that doesn't —

"How's Lily?" I ask. "Sophie's always asking about her. She thinks Lily sounds so cool."

Neve smiles. A real smile. A mum smile. "She's great. She's — she's so tall now. Twelve going on twenty. She asked me the other day if she could have a sleepover and I —"

She stops. Her hands press flat against the table. Her knuckles whiten.

"A sleepover," Saskia says. Not a question. An observation.

"She can have friends over," Neve says. "That's different. That's our house. That's — that's safe."

The word safe hangs in the air. Neve looks at it and her face does something, a flicker, a micro-movement, the kind of thing I wouldn't notice except I've been watching Neve since we were twelve because Neve is the one who keeps things together.

"My turn," Neve says. She turns to Saskia. "Truth or Dare."

"Truth."

Neve leans forward. "Why are you really here, Saskia?"

The question is a challenge. Even Cate leans forward, her body angled toward the answer.

"I'm here because someone sent me an invitation that appeared to come from me. I traced it. Dead end. But I was already going to come. I've been tracking this property for years." She says this flat, factual, like reading from a document. "I never believed it was an accident. Not since I was twelve."

The room goes very still.

"Saskia," I say. My voice is thin. "It was an accident. She fell. The window was open and she —"

"Do you think memories are reliable?" Saskia asks. She's looking at me but the question isn't for me. It's for the room.

"What?" Rowan asks.

"Memories. Do you think they're reliable? Do you think what you remember is what actually happened?"

"That's a therapist question," I say, trying to laugh. "Like when you're absolutely sure you locked the door but then you're not sure, and then you're not sure if you're not sure? Is that — is that what you mean?"

Neve doesn't answer. She's looking at Saskia with an expression I can't read — not anger, not fear, something between the two, something that looks like hands pressed flat against a table — holding something down, holding something in, knuckles bloodless with the effort of not letting go.

"My turn," Saskia says. Her eyes move from Cate to me to Rowan to Neve. She's not playing anymore. She's been building toward something and I can feel it rising in my chest, the underwater feeling, the pressure, the thing I hold down every day by keeping my hands busy and my voice bright and my thoughts on Sophie and Greg and lesson plans and anything except —

"Truth," Saskia says. "For all of you. One question. Same question."

The kitchen is quiet. The clock on the wall ticks — each tick a small hammer striking the same spot. Five women around a kitchen table on a Saturday afternoon and the air is thick with something I can feel pressing against my skin. Pressing against the inside of my throat. Pressing against the words I've spent twenty-five years not saying.

"What do each of you remember about the last time we were all in this house?"

The silence is not empty. It's full of the things five women are not saying, the things we've carried for twenty-five years in different architectures of denial and memory and survival. It fills the kitchen like dough rising in a bowl — slow, silent, pressing against the edges, finding every gap.

I break it. Of course I break it. That's what I do. I break silences the way I break bread, with both hands, with force, before they can break me.

"I don't — okay, I don't see the point of this." My voice is steady. Reasonable. The voice of a woman who has rehearsed deflection for twenty-five years. "We were kids. It was a long time ago. It was awful and we were kids and there's nothing to — I don't know what you want us to say."

Rowan speaks next. Her voice is the voice of someone describing a photograph, accurate, visual, stripped of narrative.

"I remember the cold."

Three words. They land on the table and they don't move.

"That's all?" Cate asks. Her voice is gentle. The kind of gentle that gets people to say things they didn't mean to.

"The cold," Rowan says. "And the light. Gold light. And the sound — between a gasp and a thud. And two shapes. One tall. One small. And then the window was empty."

"You saw two people?" Cate leans forward. "In the hallway?"

Her voice is gentle. The kind of gentle that gets people to say things they didn't mean to. But her hands — her fingers have stopped shuffling the cards. The cards are forgotten. Her body has oriented toward Rowan's description the way a compass orients toward north. Not surprise. Not curiosity. Recognition. The posture of someone hearing a description of something they already know.

"I saw shapes."

Neve stands. She pushes her chair back — not violently, not dramatically, just stands, the way you stand when your body has decided something before your mind has caught up. Her face is white. Not pale. White. The colour of paper. The colour of walls.

"I don't remember anything," she says.

She says it to the room. She says it to Saskia. She says it the way you say something you need to be true — not with conviction but with desperation, with the urgency of someone reaching for a life raft.

"That's convenient," Saskia says. Her voice hasn't changed. Still flat. Still precise. Still reading from a document. But her eyes — her eyes are sharp the way a blade is sharp, and they are fixed on Neve with twenty-five years of focus.

"It's not convenient, it's — I was asleep. I was downstairs. I was on the couch and I was asleep and I woke up when I heard — I woke up and everyone was —"

"That's not what happened." Saskia stands. She and Neve are facing each other across the kitchen table and the table is between them like a border, like a line that is about to be crossed. "I was in the bedroom at the end of the hall. The one by the stairs. I heard the sound — the gasp-thud — and I was the first one out. I was the first one into the hallway."

The room holds its breath. Cate is very still. Rowan is watching with her camera-eyes, recording. I am standing too — I don't remember standing. My palms are pressed flat against my thighs and I can feel my heart in my throat.

"You were in the hallway, Neve." Saskia's voice is low and precise and every word is a nail. "When I came out of my room, you were already standing in the hallway. Under the window. I was the first one out of the bedroom. But you were already there. You were standing under the window looking at me and —"

"No." Neve's voice is sharp. "No, I was downstairs —"

"You were upstairs. You were in the hallway. You were standing exactly where you stood this morning when you collapsed. Under the window. You were looking at me and I stopped and I looked at you and you looked — your face wasn't surprised, Neve. You weren't surprised. You were already terrified. You were terrified before I got there. Like you'd already seen something. Like you'd been standing there long enough to see it."

The kitchen is silent. The clock ticks. The sea moves. Five women and a table and twenty-five years of silence finally cracking.

"I was on the couch," Neve says. Her voice is controlled. Therapist voice. The voice she uses when she is holding herself together with language. "That's where I was. I was on the couch. I fell asleep during the movie and I woke up when I heard the — I woke up and everyone was running upstairs and I followed. That's what happened. That's what my mother told —"

She stops.

She said it. She said the thing she's not supposed to say. "That's what my mother told." Not "that's what I remember." Not "that's what happened." That's what my mother told. The sentence is a doorway and it's open and the air from the other side is cold.

"I was downstairs," Neve says again. Quieter now. Less controlled. "On the couch. That's where I was."

"I was the first one up the stairs after the sound," Saskia says. "You were already in the hallway, Neve. Under the window. Looking at me. And your face — your face wasn't surprised. It was already terrified."

The silence is long. It stretches across the table and fills the kitchen and pushes against every surface in this house that has been holding its breath for twenty-five years.

"I don't remember that," Neve says.

But her voice cracks on "don't."
