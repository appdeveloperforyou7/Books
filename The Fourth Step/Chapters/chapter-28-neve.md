# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 28: Neve

## The Last Conversation

The ambulance is white and bright and it smells of disinfectant and something underneath the disinfectant — the scent of a place where bodies are failing, faint but unmistakable, like the smell of rain before it arrives. My mother is on the stretcher. An oxygen mask covers her nose and mouth, fogging rhythmically with each breath. The paramedic — a woman with short blonde hair and steady hands — is taking her blood pressure on the other side. The siren is off. She's stable, they said. Non-urgent transport. My mother killed a twelve-year-old girl and covered it up for twenty-five years and the ambulance did not use its siren for her.

I am sitting on the bench beside the stretcher, holding her hand because there is nothing else to hold. The red glow of the equipment panel. The green pulse of the heart monitor. The white overhead light that makes my mother's skin look like paper.

She is awake. Her eyes are dark brown and steady and they are watching me the way they have always watched me — with attention, with precision, the calibrated focus of a woman who tracks the people in her orbit the way a clinician monitors a patient's affect.

I should not be here. The therapist in me knows this. The clinical term is enmeshment. The clinical term is trauma bond. The clinical term is a shelf of textbooks that describe exactly what I am doing, and none of them explain why.

I am doing it because she is my mother. And she is dying. And whatever she did, whatever she took from me — she was the first person to hold me when I was born and she will be the last person I watch die and the distance between those two facts is the distance of my entire life and it will never make sense.

She lifts her free hand. Slowly. The IV line tugs. She pulls the oxygen mask down to her chin.

"You don't have to stay, sweetheart. You should rest," she says.

Her voice is different. Not the guarded, measured voice of the armchair. Not the commanding voice of the school principal. Not even the sweetheart voice, the one that finds the crack in everything and widens it. This voice is thin. Tired. The voice of a woman whose instruments have all been switched off.

"I want to stay," I say.

I don't know if this is true. Maybe saying it makes it true. She would know about that. She constructed an entire truth for me out of repetition and authority and a mother's face telling a child what she did and didn't see.

"Neve."

"Mum. You should save your strength."

"I need to talk." She pulls the mask further down. It hangs around her neck. Her breathing is labored without it — each breath a small effort, a slight hitch at the top, the sound of a body working hard to do something it used to do without thinking. "I need to say this while I can."

The paramedic glances over. She hears us. She hears the intensity in my mother's voice — the urgency of a woman who is running out of time and knows it. She doesn't intervene. She monitors the equipment and lets us talk.

"I loved you."

Past tense. I have heard these words thousands of times. I love you. Present tense. Past tense is different. Past tense suggests a thing that is ending, or a thing that needs to be stated in its complete form before the opportunity passes.

"I loved you," she says again. "Everything I did — every lie, every phone call, every time I told you to forget — I thought I was keeping you safe."

The ambulance hits a bump. The stretcher jolts. Her hand tightens in mine. The grip is weaker than it was. Six months ago, her grip was firm. Three months ago, it was fading. Now it is the grip of someone who is holding on because holding on is the only thing left.

"You kept me blind," I say.

The words come out before I can stop them. Not clinical. Not measured. The kind of raw that has edges. The words of a thirty-seven-year-old woman who just discovered that her mother stole her memory and made her doubt her own eyes for a quarter of a century.

She doesn't flinch. She is beyond flinching. She is lying on a stretcher with her organs shutting down and her daughter's hand in hers and she has no performances left.

"Is that worse?" she asks.

"Yes."

The word is small and absolute and it sits between us in the ambulance the way the truth has sat between us for twenty-five years — present, unavoidable, shaping everything around it without being acknowledged.

She closes her eyes. For a moment I think she's going to stop. I think we will ride the rest of the way in silence, two women in an ambulance, one dying and one holding her hand.

She opens her eyes.

"I'm sorry."

Two words. Not the rehearsed apology from the armchair. Not the calibrated tears. This is not performed. This is a woman with nothing left to lose, saying the thing she should have said twenty-five years ago, in a voice that cracks on the first syllable and doesn't recover.

"I'm sorry I took that night from you."

The words land. Not in my chest — in my spine. In the place where the neck pain has lived for two decades. In the place where the migraines start. In the place where the wall was built, brick by brick, by a woman kneeling in a cream-coloured hallway with her hands on her twelve-year-old daughter's face, saying you didn't see anything, you were asleep, you were fine. Everything is fine. You're fine, sweetheart. Fine.

"I'm sorry I made you doubt your own eyes."

The ambulance moves through the dark. The monitor beeps. The road hums. And my mother is crying.

Not the managed tears of the armchair. These tears are messy. They run down the sides of her face, into her ears, into her hair. Her chin trembles. Her mouth opens and no sound comes out for several seconds and then the sound that comes is a thing I have never heard from her — a raw, broken, animal sound, the sound of a woman who is no longer managing anything.

I have never seen my mother cry like this. I have seen her cry at the right moments with the right amount of emotion. I have never seen her cry like someone who has lost control of the crying. The woman who controlled every room she entered is lying on a stretcher and she has lost control of her tears and the loss of control is the most honest thing I have seen from her in my entire life.

"You were right," she says. "You saw it. You were standing at the top of the stairs and you saw it. And I told you that you didn't. I told you every day. I told the doctors. I told the police. I told you so many times that you stopped believing yourself and started believing me and I knew what I was doing. I knew I was replacing your memory. I told myself it was love. But it wasn't love. Or it was love and something else at the same time. It was love and fear and control and it was the only thing I knew how to do."

I don't speak. I hold her hand and I listen. The ambulance moves through the dark and the monitor beeps and the road hums. And my mother tells me the truth for the first time in twenty-five years.

"I was on the phone," she says. "Upstairs. In the hallway. I was talking to — it doesn't matter who. Someone I shouldn't have been talking to. Kiera came up the stairs. She heard me. She was twelve years old and she was bold and she was brave and she looked at me with those eyes — those dark, certain eyes — and she said she was going to tell. She said it was wrong and she was going to tell the others and I grabbed her arm. I grabbed her arm to stop her from going downstairs. I wasn't thinking. I was panicked. I grabbed her and she pulled away and the window was open and I pushed — I pushed her away from the stairs, away from the door, toward the window. I didn't mean — I didn't intend — the window was — I didn't know it was that close. I didn't know she would — it was two seconds. Two seconds. My hand on her arm and then she was — she was gone."

Her voice breaks on "gone." Not a controlled break. A fracture. A fault line through sixty-eight years of composure.

"And then you were there. Standing at the top of the stairs. In your pyjamas. The blue ones with the stars. And you looked at me and I could see that you'd seen. You were so small. You were looking at me like the world had changed shape and you didn't recognise it anymore."

I remember the pyjamas. Blue with silver stars. I haven't thought about them in twenty-five years. But she's right. I was wearing them when I followed Kiera upstairs. I was wearing them when I stood at the top of the stairs and watched my mother's hand move and a girl went through a window.

"And I told you that you didn't see it," she says. "I knelt down and I held you and I told you that you were asleep. And I kept telling you. Every nightmare. Every time you looked at me with that face — the face that knew something was wrong — I told you it was a dream. I told you your mind was playing tricks. And I took you to doctors who treated you for anxiety because I told them you were anxious. And I moved us away so you wouldn't be near anyone who might contradict what I'd told you. And I called you every day for twenty-five years to make sure the wall was holding."

Her hand is shaking in mine. Or mine in hers. I can't tell anymore. The tremor passes between us like a current, like two conductors touching the same live wire.

"I thought if I controlled it — if I controlled the story, if I controlled what you remembered — I could keep you. I could keep you from knowing that your mother killed someone. I could keep you from carrying that. I could keep you."

"You kept me from carrying it. You carried it instead."

"I carried it and it crushed me. And I let it crush you anyway."

The ambulance slows. A red light, maybe. Or a turn. She closes her eyes. Her breathing is labored. I reach over and pull the mask back up over her nose and mouth and she lets me. She doesn't resist. She lets her daughter put the mask on her face the way she must have let me do things when I was a baby, before the controlling started, before the lie, before the hallway.

"I don't forgive you," I say.

The words come out and they are true and I didn't know they were true until I said them. Every version I'd composed was careful, measured, therapeutic. The words came out raw. The truth was: I don't forgive you. Not yet. Maybe not ever. Because twenty-five years of making me doubt my own mind is not a mistake. It is an architecture. It is a wall she built inside me and forgiveness is a word that belongs to the person on the other side of the wall, not the person who built it.

She nods. Behind the mask, her eyes are open and wet and she nods.

"I'm not asking you to," she says. The mask muffles the words. I hear them anyway.

I hold her hand. I hold it the way I've been holding it since the ambulance left the house — loosely enough that she could pull away if she wanted to, tightly enough that she knows I'm here. I don't say I forgive you. I don't say I don't. I hold her hand. The monitor beeps. The road hums. My mother breathes through a mask that fogs and clears and fogs and clears.

She turns her head toward me. The mask is on her face and her eyes are above it and she looks at me with an expression I have never seen on her face before. Not composed. Not controlling. Not managing. The expression of a woman who has run out of things to manage — who is looking at her daughter and seeing, not the child she controlled, not the adult she gaslit, not the therapist she trained by example to manage other people's pain while ignoring her own, but a person. A person she wronged. A person she loves. A person she cannot fix.

"I'm sorry," she says again. The mask swallows the words. I hear them through the fogging and the clearing and the beeping and the hum of the road.

I hold her hand.

The ambulance slows. The driver says something into a radio. The paramedic beside me straightens, checks the monitor, makes a note. We are here. The hospital. The lights outside the window change — streetlights, then the bright white fluorescence of a hospital loading bay, then the overhead doors of the emergency entrance rolling open.

The ambulance stops.

The back doors open.

Light floods in — harsh, white, institutional, the light of a place where people go when their bodies are failing and the people who love them follow because there is nowhere else to go. The light fills the ambulance and I squint and my mother's hand tightens in mine and the paramedic reaches for the stretcher and I am still holding Elaine Cassidy's hand.

I am still holding my mother's hand.
