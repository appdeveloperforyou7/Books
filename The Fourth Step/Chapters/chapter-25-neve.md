# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 25: Neve

## The Truth

The wind has picked up.

I can hear it pressing against the walls, finding every gap in the sealant, every place where the renovated house doesn't quite meet the frame of the old one. The windows rattle in their fittings, chattering like something alive. The kitchen door, which doesn't latch properly, taps against the jamb — once, twice, a rhythm like a finger tapping a table, a rhythm that says I'm still here. The house groans. A long, low sound from somewhere deep in the walls, the sound of timber contracting in the cold, the sound my rational mind assigns to every noise this house makes because the alternative is that the house is responding to what is happening inside it and that alternative is a door I will not open.

Elaine is sitting in the armchair.

I have started thinking of her as Elaine. Not Mum. Not Mother. The name surfaced sometime during Saskia's evidence presentation — somewhere between "bilateral grip-pattern bruising" and "horizontal force" — and it stuck. Elaine. A name at a distance. A name that creates space. The space I need to look at the woman in the chair and see what is actually there instead of what I've been trained to see.

She is gripping the arms of the chair.

Both hands. Her knuckles are pale against the dark fabric, the blood pulled away from the surface as if her body is rationing. The grip is tight — tendons standing out on the backs of her hands, the thin skin stretched over bone like fabric over a wire frame. She is holding the chair the way a person holds a rail in rough seas — not for comfort but because letting go means going under. The poise she walked in with — the principal's posture, the measured voice, the measured tears from her version of events — is still there, but it is thinner now. A glaze over something less controlled underneath. Her composure cracked when I said "That's not what happened." Cracked further when I told the room what I remembered — the hallway, her back, her hand on Kiera's shoulder, the push. Saskia laid out the physics and the bruising and the two-metre landing distance. Elaine's voice lost its steadiness.

But she hasn't broken. Not fully. Not yet.

She is still Elaine. Still composed. Still sitting in the best chair in the room with her chin up and her hands on the armrests and her eyes steady. Diminished, yes. Frail, yes. Gaunt in her too-large coat, the cancer eating through her the way termites eat through a beam — invisibly, structurally, from the inside out. The hospice forty minutes behind her. But the authority is still there, the way a building's bones remain even after the façade falls away. She commands this room. She commanded every room she entered since before I was born. She is sitting in a room full of evidence that dismantles everything she has built and she has not left. She has not broken.

I am standing in front of her.

I didn't plan this. I was sitting on the sofa, next to Aisling, the autopsy photographs between us. Then I was standing. The way I stood in the hallway this afternoon. The way my body leads and my mind follows and my feet know the way even when I don't. I am standing in front of Elaine Cassidy and the room is behind me and the wind is outside and the house is groaning and I have a question. My jaw is clenched so tight my molars ache. I unclench them. I breathe. The therapist trained into my bones: breathe before you speak, regulate before you engage, the calm voice is the strong voice. I am not calm. But my voice is. The voice is a tool. The voice is the one thing I control in a room where I have never controlled anything.

"What were you doing upstairs?"

The words come out steady. Clearer than I expected. The therapist's voice — the calm, measured, controlled tone I use with clients who are avoiding something. The tone that says: I am not angry. I am asking. And I will wait as long as it takes.

Elaine's eyes meet mine.

For a moment — one beat, two — she doesn't answer. The silence has weight. The room behind me is utterly still. No one breathes loudly. No one shifts. I can feel five pairs of eyes on the back of my head, on my shoulders, on my mother's face. The air is thick with it — the feeling in a room that is about to break open.

"I had a headache," Elaine says. "I went to lie down."

The same answer she gave before. The rehearsed answer. The practised version. The narrative she built twenty-five years ago and has been maintaining ever since.

"You were on the phone," I say. "Who were you talking to?"

The room contracts. A tightening, a drawing-in. The controlled confession didn't include a phone call. The headache version didn't include a phone call. I am asking about something she didn't account for, something she left out of the script, and the script is the only thing holding her together.

Elaine's grip on the chair arms tightens. Her knuckles go from pale to white.

"I wasn't on the phone."

"You were."

"I was in bed. I had a headache, Neve. I told you —"

"I heard you."

The words are out before I've examined them. They come from somewhere below language. From the twelve-year-old who stood at the top of the stairs and heard her mother's voice before she saw her mother's face. I heard you. The sentence doesn't come from the memory I recovered this afternoon — the visual memory, the panoramic memory of the push. It comes from something older. Something deeper. A sensory fragment I didn't know I carried. The sound of her voice, low and intimate and not meant for anyone else, the tone she used when she was talking to someone she cared about in a way she didn't want the world to know about.

"You were talking to someone," I say. "A man. Upstairs. In a house full of twelve-year-old girls at eleven o'clock at night. Who was he?"

Elaine's composure cracks.

It doesn't shatter. It cracks. A single fracture line running from the surface to the core. I see it in her eyes first — the steadiness wavers, not for long, a fraction of a second, but enough. Then in her jaw — a small tightening, the muscles bunching beneath thin skin. Then in her hands — the grip on the chair arms loosens, just slightly, as if the effort of holding on has become too much.

She closes her eyes. The first time she has broken eye contact with me since she sat down. She closes her eyes and her chin drops — just slightly — and for a moment she looks what she is: a dying woman who is tired.

"A colleague," she says. The performance is gone. What's left is lower. Rougher. A woman choosing to speak instead of performing.

"His name was David. David Harmon. He was married. A teacher at a neighbouring school. We met at a conference two years before. It wasn't planned. It wasn't something I went looking for. It happened."

She pauses. Her eyes are on her hands.

"Do you know what it took to become a school principal in 1997? As a woman?" Her voice is flat now. Not defensive. Exhausted. "Fifteen years of being the most competent person in every room and the last one promoted. Every decision scrutinised. Every mistake magnified. A male teacher could be mediocre and forgettable. I had to be flawless. I had to be beyond reproach. And I was. I built that career from nothing. I built that reputation. I built that standing in a community where every parent knew my name and trusted me with their children."

She looks at me. Her eyes are wet but the tears aren't falling.

"If anyone found out about David — his wife, the school council, the department — it wouldn't have been a scandal. It would have been annihilation. Everything I'd built. Everything I'd earned. Gone. Not because of the affair. Because I was a woman who had an affair. That was the standard. That was the world."

The room is silent. Not performance silence. Real silence. Five women hearing something they did not expect. Aisling, behind me, is very still. Saskia hasn't moved from the mantelpiece. Imogen's breath has gone shallow. Rowan's camera hangs at her side, forgotten.

"You were having an affair," I say.

"Yes."

The word hits me somewhere below the sternum. Not the chest — the stomach. Acid rises at the back of my throat, sharp and physical, the body's involuntary verdict on what it has heard. My mother had an affair. In this house. While five twelve-year-old girls slept downstairs. The taste of it is metallic and warm and I swallow it down.

The word is simple. Unadorned. No justification. No framing. Just: yes.

---

The wind outside surges — a gust that rattles every window, that makes the kitchen door bang against its frame, that fills the walls with the sound of air under pressure. And in the silence after the gust, I ask the question.

"What was so important that a twelve-year-old girl had to die?"

The word hangs in the air. Die. The word I have not said out loud until this moment. The word that turns the managed confession into something else entirely.

Elaine's eyes open. She looks at me. The crack in her façade has widened. Behind her eyes, the structure buckles — twenty-five years of denial and control, splitting under a single word.

"I didn't — that's not what happened. I didn't kill anyone."

Her voice wavers.

Not controlled-uncertain. Not performed. Genuinely uncertain. The uncertainty of a woman who is no longer sure which version of the story she is telling.

"You didn't kill anyone," I say. Not a question. A scaffold. A place for her to stand while I pull the ground out from under her.

"I didn't. She startled me. She came upstairs and I was on the phone and she heard — she heard me talking. She was a child. Twelve years old and she heard something she shouldn't have heard and she was upset. She was angry. She said —"

Elaine stops. As if she's hit a wall. Her hands are shaking now. Visible tremors. She presses them flat against the chair arms the way I press mine flat against my thighs. The gesture is mine. I learned it from her.

From behind me, Aisling moves.

She reaches into her bag and pulls out the autopsy photographs. She crosses the room and sets them on the arm of Elaine's chair. One by one. The shoulders. The back. The grip marks — ten points of contact, bilateral, even, the width of fingers compressed into bruised skin. The landing site, measured from the building. Two metres.

"Look at them," Aisling says.

Her voice is flat. Steady. The voice of a woman who has spent twenty-five years wanting to put these photographs in front of the person responsible.

Elaine looks.

Her eyes go to the first photograph. The bruising on Kiera's back. Purple-black, twenty-five years frozen in emulsion. Her jaw tightens. She looks at the second. The shoulder marks — ten points of contact, the width of fingers, bilateral, even, grip-pattern bruising that no amount of narrative management can explain away. Her lips press together. The third. The measurement. The distance from the wall. Two metres. The physics of a push, not a fall. The evidence that turns "she stumbled" into a lie and "it was an accident" into something else entirely.

I watch her face as she looks. The composure — what's left of it — drains away. Not gradually. Like saturation sliding to zero in a photograph. The skin goes slack around the jaw. The careful architecture of control — the raised chin, the steady gaze, the measured breaths — collapses in on itself like a tent in a windstorm. She is looking at the photographs of the damage her hands did. The bruises her fingers left. The evidence that has been sitting in a file in a forensic pathologist's office for twenty-five years, waiting for the right person to find it.

She stares at the photographs for a long time. Five seconds. Six. The room waits. The wind presses against the glass. The house groans. And Elaine Cassidy sits in her armchair and looks at what she did, and the looking is the thing that breaks her.

"What did she say?" I ask.

Elaine's voice, when it comes, is barely above a whisper.

"She said she was going to tell."

Four words. The words that changed everything. A twelve-year-old girl standing in a hallway at eleven o'clock at night, hearing something she shouldn't have heard, and doing what brave, funny twelve-year-old girls do — refusing to keep a secret for an adult who was doing something wrong. Kiera Doyle was going to tell.

"She was going to tell everyone," Elaine says, her voice the slow-motion description of a car accident — the way witnesses describe it, the detached clinical narration of a mind that has replayed the event so many times the emotion has worn grooves into the words. "I panicked. I reached for her. I grabbed her arm. She pulled away. She was heading for the stairs and I grabbed her again. Her shoulders. I was trying to stop her. I needed a minute. I needed to think. Your father couldn't know. His wife. My career. A school principal having an affair — it would have destroyed everything. Everything I'd spent twenty years building. And she looked at me like I was — like I'd done something terrible. And she was right. I had. But I couldn't let her —"

"What happened?"

The words are quiet. Not demanding. Not angry. The quiet of a woman who already knows the answer and is giving her mother one last chance to say it.

"I pushed her."

Two words. The words she has never said. Not once. Not in twenty-five years. Not "she stumbled." Not "she fell." Not "there was a struggle." I pushed her. Active voice. The agent. The truth.

"I was holding her shoulders. She was pulling away. She was strong — stronger than she looked. She was fighting me. Trying to get to the stairs. And I pushed. Not toward the window. I wasn't trying to — I pushed her away from the door. Away from the stairs. I needed her to stop moving. I needed her to stay still so I could think. And the window was right there. It was open. It was summer and it was always open at night in summer. I pushed and she went backward and her hands came up and —"

Elaine's voice breaks.

Not the controlled break from before. Not the performed fracture designed to elicit sympathy. A real break. A collapse. Her face crumples — the word is exact, it crumples, the way a structure gives way when the load exceeds capacity. Her mouth opens and closes and no sound comes out. Her eyes overflow. The tears she has been managing all night — managing for twenty-five years — are no longer manageable.

"Her hands reached for the frame," she says, her voice a wreck of itself. "I saw her fingers. I saw her fingernails catch the paint and I thought she had it. I thought she'd hold on. I thought there was time. I thought I could grab her."

"There wasn't time."

"No."

Nearly inaudible. A breath with a consonant attached.

"It was two seconds. I pushed. She went back. Her hands caught the frame. Her hands slipped. She was gone. Two seconds and everything changed. And I couldn't take it back."

She pauses. Her eyes are on the photographs still sitting on the arm of her chair. When she speaks again, her voice is different — quieter, directed not at me but at the room.

"The emails. The anonymous tips. That was me. I was dying. I wanted the truth out. On my terms." A pause. "I ran the school's database system for eighteen years. I know how to use a computer. I know how to send an email that can't be traced back to a hospice bed."

Her hands have left the chair arms. They are in her lap. Shaking. Not the controlled tremor from before. This is different. The shaking starts in her fingers and spreads to her wrists and her hands won't stay still and she presses them flat and they won't stay flat. A thin sheen of sweat has appeared on her upper lip. Her breathing has changed — shorter, shallower, the kind of breathing that costs something. She is managing the pain the way she has managed everything: silently, with discipline, refusing to let the room see what it costs her to be here.

The wind outside drops. For one moment, the house is quiet. No rattling. No tapping. No groaning. Just the sound of breathing. Six women in a room that has been waiting for this truth.

"And then you made me forget."

The words come out flat. Not angry. Not sad. The flatness of a statement so fundamental it doesn't need inflection. My mother killed a girl and then made me forget I saw it.

Elaine looks at me. Her face is wet. Her eyes are red. The tears run freely down the hollows of her cheeks, into the corners of her mouth. She doesn't wipe them.

"I was protecting you, sweetheart. I was always protecting you."

The words are so familiar they could be an echo. I have heard them my whole life. In the psychologists' offices. In the car on the way to the new school. In the kitchen of every house we lived in after Sorrento. The phrase that turned control into love and cover-up into care and twenty-five years of gaslighting into motherly devotion.

"You were protecting yourself."

The words land between us. No cruelty. No venom. A statement of fact. You were protecting yourself. Your reputation. Your career. Your marriage. Your freedom.

Elaine doesn't flinch. She holds my gaze. Even now. Even diminished and broken and exposed. She holds my gaze because Elaine Cassidy has always held my gaze, has never been the first to look away.

"Both."

One word. The truest word she has spoken tonight.

Both. She was protecting herself and she was protecting me. The love and the self-interest. The woman who held my face in the hallway and whispered the lie into me loved me. She loved me. And she lied to me. And the lying was done in the name of love and in the name of fear and in the name of a life she was not willing to lose. Both. She is not a monster who pretended to be a mother. She is not a mother who became a monster. She is both. She has always been both.

Silence.

The room is utterly still. The wind outside has stopped. The kitchen door has stopped tapping. The house is holding its breath. The only sound is breathing — six women in a room where the air has been rearranged by a single word.

Both.
