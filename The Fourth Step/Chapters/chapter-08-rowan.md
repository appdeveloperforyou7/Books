# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 8: Rowan

## Night Vision

The hallway is cold.

I don't remember coming upstairs. The storm woke me — or the power cut did, the sudden absence of the heating's hum, the house dropping into a silence that wasn't sleep but shock. I was asleep — or something like sleep, the grey space between sleeping and waking where the body moves without the mind's permission. Legs carrying. Hands opening doors. Feet finding the floor by texture, by temperature, by the particular cold of timber that has been cold for a long time. I was asleep and now I am standing in the upstairs hallway and my feet are bare and the floorboards are cold against my soles and the cold is the same cold.

Exactly the same cold.

The storm is at its peak outside — wind driving rain against the east wall, the groan of the roof under the pressure, the rattle of the sealed window at the end of the hallway holding against something that wants to come through. The power is out. The whole house is dark. Not the grey dark of moonlight — the black dark of a coastal winter storm at three in the morning when the clouds have swallowed everything and the only light is the kind your eyes invent when they're desperate.

Twenty-five years of paint and carpet and double-glazed windows and the cold is the same. It rises through the floorboards into the arches of my feet, up through my shins, settles in my knees like shadow pooling where light can't reach. Not a temperature. A presence. The cold lives here. It has always lived here — in this hallway, in this specific length of space between the top of the stairs and the window at the end — and it knows me and I know it and we are standing face to face in the dark.

My eyes adjust. Not black — the darkness here has texture, weight, the way dark has in rooms that have seen something. The storm clouds have swallowed the moon but my eyes find the edges of things anyway — the walls, the carpet, the shape of the window at the end of the hall, a rectangle of slightly less dark against the dark. The hallway divides into layers of shadow, each one a shade deeper than the last.

I am standing in exactly the right place.

The space is measured in my body. Twelve steps long. Eight feet high. Four feet wide — enough for two to pass, narrow enough to touch both walls with arms extended. Not memories. Facts stored in the muscles, in the bones, in the way the body holds the architecture of a room it hasn't entered in twenty-five years.

The window is smaller now. At the end of the hallway — the rectangle of grey light, the aluminium frame, the fixed pane. You can't open it. The window that is here now is not the window that was here then. Then it was tall and wide, opening outward on a brass hinge that creaked in the wind. December wind. Warm, carrying salt and eucalyptus. The window was open that night. The air moved through the house like breathing.

This window does not breathe. It is sealed. Aluminium and double glass and rubber seals and the air stays where it is. But the frame — behind the new frame, where the aluminium meets the wall — the original frame is still there. The bones of the house. You can renovate surfaces. The bones remain.

The moonlight through the new window is winter light. July light. It is blue-grey and thin and it doesn't reach the walls. December light was different. December light was gold. December light filled this hallway the way water fills a glass — from the bottom up, pooling against the carpet, rising until even the ceiling was gold, until the air itself seemed to glow, warm and dense and alive with the colour of summer. I remember the light. I remember it the way I remember temperature and texture — as a physical fact, not an emotion. The light was gold. The air was warm. The window was open. These are facts. They do not require interpretation.

My camera is in my hands. I don't remember picking it up. It is there, weight familiar against my palms, the strap around my neck, the cold metal of the body against my fingers. Twenty-five years of framing, focusing, pressing the shutter, taking moments out of time and holding them still. My hands lifted the camera. My hands brought it upstairs. My hands are already composing the shot.

I lift the camera to my eye. The viewfinder frames the hallway: the window at the end, the grey blade of moonlight on the carpet, the walls on either side, the ceiling above. The composition is symmetrical. The window is the vanishing point. The hallway is a corridor of shadow leading to a rectangle of grey. I press the shutter.

The flash fires.

The hallway erupts in white light — the harsh, clinical overexposure that blows out shadow and depth and renders everything in the flat equal brightness of a photograph taken without care. For one fraction of a second I see it all: the grey carpet, the white walls, the aluminium window frame, the fire extinguisher in its case on the left wall, the smoke detector on the ceiling, two doors on the left, one on the right, leading to bedrooms. For one fraction of a second the hallway is not a hallway but a photograph — a surface, a two-dimensional plane, a moment extracted from time.

Then the flash dies. The white collapses. The shadows return. The grey blade of moonlight falls on the carpet and the hallway is dark again and my eyes are full of afterimages, bright purple shapes swimming in the black.

I look at the screen on the back of the camera. The image glows: the hallway, frozen, lit by flash. Flat and bright and detailed. The window at the end. The walls on either side. The carpet. The doors. Everything visible. Nothing hidden.

I zoom in on the window. The aluminium frame is white and clean and modern. The glass is dark — it reflects the flash back at the camera, a bright white square in the centre of the frame. But around the edges, where the aluminium meets the wall, where the new frame is screwed into the old structure — the original wood. Dark, stained, old. The timber that was here before the aluminium, before the renovation, before everything changed.

And on the wood — something. Lines, maybe. Dark marks against the old timber. I zoom closer. The screen fills with grain and pixel and the image breaks apart. Too low resolution. The details won't resolve. Something is there, scratched into the old timber, but the camera can't reach it. I would need to see it properly — on a larger screen, with more light.

I lower the camera.

The hallway is dark. The cold is in my feet. The moonlight is grey and thin. I am standing in exactly the right place and I have been standing here for twenty-five years.

You are standing in the hallway. You are twelve. The carpet is blue and scratchy under your bare feet and the air is warm — December warm, summer warm, the kind of warm that makes you want to lie on the floor and feel the house breathing around you. The window at the end of the hallway is open. The brass hinge is creaking. The air comes through in slow pulses, salt and eucalyptus and something else, something metallic, something you don't have a word for because you are twelve and your vocabulary has not yet grown large enough for the taste of fear.

You hear a sound from below. From the stairs. No — from above. From the hallway. You are already in the hallway. You were always in the hallway. The warmth, the light, the creak of the hinge, the taste of metal on your tongue — these are right even when the order is wrong.

You look up.

The hallway is twelve steps long. At the end, in front of the window, there are two shapes. One is tall. Tall the way an adult is tall, tall the way your mother is tall, tall the way the world is tall when you are twelve and the ceiling is eight feet high and everything above you is larger than you. The tall shape is between you and the window. Its back is to you. It is blocking the light.

The other shape is small. Small the way you are small. Small the way Kiera is small — was small, is small, has always been small. The small shape is closer to the window. The moonlight touches its edges. The small shape is not moving.

The tall shape moves.

The arm of the tall shape lifts and extends and presses against the small shape and the small shape moves — backward, toward the window, toward the open window, toward the dark and the warm air and the brass hinge that is creaking — and the small shape reaches out. A hand. Small fingers splayed wide. Nails catching the wooden frame. Five parallel lines of pressure against old paint. The hand grips. The fingers curl. The knuckles whiten.

The tall shape moves again.

Arm. Hand. Push. Back. Window. Hand. Frame. Grip. Grip — gone.

And the small shape — the small shape goes through the window.

Not fell. Not jumped. Not slipped or stumbled or lost her balance. Went. The way light goes through glass. The way sound goes through walls. The way a thing goes from being here to being not-here without any of the expected steps between. One moment the small shape is at the window, gripping the frame, fingers white, and the next moment the window is empty. The frame is empty. The hand is gone. The air where the shape was is just air. The space where the fingers gripped is just wood with scratches — five parallel lines, downward, the marks of someone holding on.

As if the air took her. As if the night reached in and pulled her through. As if the window was not a window but a door that opened in one direction only and the small shape went through it and did not come back.

I am back in the hallway. The cold is in my feet. The moonlight is grey. The window is sealed. I am thirty-seven and I am standing in the same spot and the cold is the same.

I don't look at the window.

I go back to my room. My laptop is on the bed where I left it. I sit on the edge of the mattress and I open it and the screen fills my face with blue-white light. I insert the memory card. I open the photograph. The hallway, frozen. The flash, flat and bright. The window at the end. The walls on either side.

I zoom in.

Past the aluminium frame. Past the rubber seal. Past the new paint and the new plaster and the careful renovation that was supposed to erase everything that was here before. To the wood. The old wood. The bones of the house.

The scratches are there.

I zoom closer. Five parallel lines. The spacing is even — the width of a child's hand. The depth is consistent — the depth of fingernails pressing into soft timber. The direction is downward, top right to bottom left, the angle of a hand gripping from below, a body being pulled away from the frame, fingers holding on and losing their grip, nail by nail, the wood taking the last of the person's weight and then letting go.

I zoom until the image is nothing but grain and pixel and five lines in the wood. I zoom until the scratches are the only thing on the screen. I trace the lines with my thumbnail through the glass of the display. Physical. Present. Here, in this house, under the new frame, waiting. Not memory. Not sensation. Not the ghost of a feeling. They exist whether I look at them or not.

Someone held on. Someone's fingernails pressed into this wood with enough force to leave marks. Someone gripped this frame the way you grip something when letting go means falling. And someone lost their grip. The scratches go down. The motion is downward. The fingers slid. The hand opened. The body went through the window.

Went. The word again. The only word I have for what I saw. Not fell. Not jumped. Went. As if the thing that occurred exists outside the frame of language — visible to the eye, absent from the negative, a shape the film refused to capture.

I close the laptop.

The room is dark. The house is quiet. Somewhere below me, three women are sleeping or not sleeping, each one carrying her own version of this hallway, her own fragments of this cold.

I open the laptop again. I zoom in on the photograph. The scratches are real. Five parallel lines. Downward motion. Still here after twenty-five years of paint and renovation and denial.

Someone held on. Someone lost their grip.
