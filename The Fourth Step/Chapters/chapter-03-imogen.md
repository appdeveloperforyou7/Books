# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 3: Imogen

## Okay

I packed three bags, which is okay, it's a whole weekend, you need options, and one of them is just food anyway. The cooler bag is the big one, the insulated one from Costco that Greg says is "a bit much for a weekend" but Greg is not here and Greg does not understand that you cannot arrive at a weekend away with other women and not have food. That's not how it works. You bring food. You bring options. So I have: a container of my banana bread (the one with the chocolate chips, which is Sophie's favourite, though I'm not sure why I made Sophie's favourite when Sophie isn't coming), a box of Tim Tams, a bag of honey-soy almonds, and a wheel of Brie that I'll need to refrigerate within the hour. The Esky has an ice pack and it's July, not December, and the Brie should be okay, it should be okay, everything is okay.

The GPS says I'm eight minutes away. I turn off the podcast — something about decluttering — because I want to arrive calm and happy and normal. Normal. What a word. I'm a normal woman driving to a normal weekend with old friends in a holiday house and that is a completely normal thing to do.

The road opens up and there's the sea on my left, grey and flat, and the air changes even through the closed windows. Salt. You can always taste the salt in Sorrento — it coats your lips, it finds the gaps between your teeth, it gets into the fabric of everything. Sophie loves the beach. Sophie is ten and she's safe at home with Greg and Ethan and she is not here and she is okay. Sophie is okay. Everyone is okay.

The GPS says turn right. I turn right. The street is familiar in the way that places from childhood are familiar — not a memory exactly, more like a feeling your body has before your mind catches up. The houses are holiday houses, close together, weatherboard and brick, the kind of street that's busy in January and empty in July. A few cars in driveways. A dog barking somewhere. The quiet of a coastal town in winter. The light is going — that flat, pale, late-afternoon grey that slides into dark without asking permission.

And then I see the house.

It's blue-grey. It was white. The porch is wider. The garden gate is new. There's a blue sedan in the driveway — Neve's car — and I pull in next to it and turn off the engine and sit for a moment.

"Right," I say out loud, to nobody. "Here we go."

I get out. The wind is cold — July cold, the kind that finds the gaps in your cardigan the way a draft finds the space under a door you forgot to close. I open the boot and take out my three bags and the cooler bag, which is heavier than I remember. I should have brought the wheeled one. Never mind.

I walk to the front door. The path is new — concrete, modern, clean lines. The original path was brick, old brick, with moss between the joints. I remember — no. I don't need to remember the path. The path is a path. It's a new path to a new door on a renovated house and that's all it is.

I knock. The door opens.

Saskia.

She looks exactly the same. Tall, sharp, short dark hair, those eyes that see everything and give nothing back. She's wearing a pressed shirt at a weekend away in a holiday house in July. A pressed shirt. Of course she is. I have a sudden flash of her at twelve, the same sharp eyes, the same precise posture, standing at the edge of every group, watching.

"Imogen." She says my name the way she says everything — like she's filing it.

"Saskia!" I say, and I mean it to be warm and it is warm, I am warm, I am a warm person. "Oh my goodness, look at you! You look exactly the same! How are you? How's Sydney? How's work? It's been too long, hasn't it? Way too long."

I'm talking too much. I know I'm talking too much. I can hear myself doing it — the words tumbling out, one after another, filling the space between us. But the alternative is silence, and silence in this hallway would be — would be not okay. I don't want silence in this hallway.

I step inside. The hallway is different. Wider. Lighter. The floor is new — some kind of blonde wood, modern, flat. The walls are white. The ceiling is white. It's bright and clean and renovated and it is completely different from the hallway I remember, which was darker and narrower and had blue carpet that was scratchy under bare feet and a coatrack by the door with too many jackets on it and a mirror that made everyone look shorter than they were.

"Look at this place!" I say. "Completely different. You wouldn't even recognise it, would you?"

I set the cooler bag down in the hallway. I keep the tote on my shoulder. I look around and I say "completely different" and I want that to be true so much that I can feel it in my chest — a pressure like the weight of water when you've dived too deep and the surface is suddenly far above you.

"Where's Neve?" I ask.

"Her room. Downstairs."

"Of course! I'll take whatever's left. I don't mind. Whatever's available is okay." I pick up the cooler bag. "Should I put this in the kitchen? I brought food — banana bread, almonds, Tim Tams, some Brie that needs refrigerating. It's nice to have options."

Saskia watches me. She's always watched me. Even at twelve, those dark eyes tracking everything.

I follow her to the kitchen. It's new — stone benchtop, white cabinets, modern fixtures. A lovely, modern, renovated kitchen and there is nothing about it that reminds me of the old kitchen at all. Nothing. The old kitchen had yellow tiles and a fridge that hummed and a calendar on the wall with a picture of a lighthouse.

There's a frame on the wall near the back door. A generic print — a watercolour of the coastline, all blue and white, the kind of thing rental companies put up to make a house feel like it belongs to the place. But the frame is old. Older than the renovation. The wood is dark and the edges are worn and the hook it hangs from has been painted around, not removed and rehung. This frame was here before the new walls. This frame was here when the kitchen was a different kitchen.

There's a window above the sink. It's the same window. Same position, same size. The glass is new but the window is in the same place.

I set the cooler bag on the benchtop and start unpacking. The Brie goes in the fridge. The Tim Tams on the counter. The almonds next to them, lined up straight, the bag facing forward. My hands are busy and that's good because when my hands are busy my mind is busy and when my mind is busy it's not — it's not going somewhere I can't follow.

---

"I'm making tea, okay? Tea for everyone? I don't know, I just need to — tea." I fill the kettle. Neve appears in the doorway — brown hair tied back, steady posture, watchful eyes.

"Imogen." She smiles. It's a real smile and a careful smile, both at once. "You look wonderful."

"So do you! Both of you! It's like no time's passed, isn't it? Except it has. Twenty-five years. I don't know where it all went." I laugh. The laugh is real. The laugh is too high. "Speaking of time passing — Sophie just got the lead in her school play! She's playing Dorothy. She's so brave. She gets up on that stage and she's fearless. She's ten and she's fearless."

I'm doing it again. Talking too much. Talking about Sophie because Sophie is safe and Sophie is alive and Sophie is ten and Sophie is not twelve. Sophie is two years younger than Kiera was. Two years. That's nothing. That's the length of a held breath.

"The banana bread's still warm," I say. "Sophie helped make it — she cracked the eggs. She's getting so good." I pause. "She's so sensitive. I love that about her, but I worry about sensitive girls. The world isn't always kind to sensitive girls."

The kettle boils. I pour three cups. I stand in this renovated house with these two women I haven't seen in twenty-five years and I hold my tea and I think: this is okay. This is normal.

"I should call Greg," I say. "He's got Sophie and Ethan tonight. Sophie has dance class on Friday evenings — jazz, she's been doing it since she was six — and Greg always forgets to pack her dance bag. I texted him three times on the drive down. I know that's excessive. I just don't want her to be uncomfortable."

I look around the kitchen. "Like someone took everything out and put a fresh tablecloth over the whole thing."

Except the window.

The window above the sink is in the same place. The back garden through it. The garden.

I turn away from the window. I pick up my tea and say, "So! Should I make dinner? I brought pasta. I thought it would be nice to cook together, the way we — well, we didn't cook, did we? We ordered pizza and ate it on the floor in the living room and —"

The memory comes back, fast and warm. The living room. Five sleeping bags in a row. Pizza boxes open on the carpet. Kiera telling a ghost story with a torch under her chin. Rowan screaming. All of us screaming. Laughing so hard we couldn't breathe. Kiera was the best storyteller. She could make the room disappear and all that was left was her voice and the dark and the story.

We played Truth or Dare. Kiera's idea — it was always Kiera's idea. She dared Saskia to eat a spoonful of Vegemite, straight, and Saskia did it without flinching, and Kiera laughed, and Kiera's laugh was — Kiera laughs with her whole body. Throws her head back. Hair everywhere. Like the joke is the most wonderful thing that has ever happened to her.

Laughs. Present tense. I said laughs.

I correct myself, inside, where no one can hear. Laughed. Kiera laughed with her whole body.

We ate pizza and we played games and it was a good night. One of the best. That's what I remember. The laughing. The ghost stories. The sleeping bags. The warm, safe feeling of being surrounded by friends. That's all that happened. A good night. A normal sleepover.

And then — and then it was morning and we were all okay.

That's where the memory stops. That's where I always let it stop. The good parts. Kiera laughing. The torch under her chin. The pizza. The good night. That's all I need to remember. The rest is — it was an accident. She fell. The window was open. It was summer. She leaned out. That's what everyone said happened. That's what happened.

"Imogen?" Neve is looking at me.

"Sorry! I don't know where I went just then." I laugh. Too high again.

I busy myself with the tea things. I rearrange the mugs. I wipe a spot of water from the benchtop. I straighten the tea towel. My hands need to move. When my hands move, the rest of me stays in place.

"Rowan's not here yet?" I ask. "And who else is coming — Cate, was it? I don't remember a Cate."

Saskia says Cate is coming in the morning. Rowan by seven. I nod and drink my tea and feel the pressure in my chest, the underwater feeling, my skin too tight around my ribs like a sweater shrunk in the wash.

I don't go upstairs. The thought doesn't occur to me. When Saskia offers to show me the upstairs, I say "Oh, I'll explore later, I'm sure it's just as lovely as down here," and I turn back to the counter and start slicing the banana bread. I don't think about why I said no. I don't think about the fact that the upstairs is somewhere I haven't been in twenty-five years. The upstairs is upstairs. I'll see it later. Or I won't. What matters is the banana bread, which I am slicing into even pieces, because running out of things is not okay.

I arrange the slices on a plate and carry them to the living room. Saskia and Neve are on the couch. I sit in the armchair — the one facing the hallway, not the window — and tuck my feet under me and smile.

"It's so good to see you both," I say. "A weekend away with old friends."

The room is quiet for a moment. Wind outside. The creak of the house.

"Completely normal," I say, softer, trying to convince myself.

I pick up a slice of banana bread. I don't eat it. I hold it in both hands the way Sophie holds her toast, like it's something precious, and I think: Sophie is safe at home and Kiera is — Kiera fell. It was an accident. The window was open. She leaned out. She fell.

It was an accident.

I take a bite of the banana bread. I chew. I swallow. I smile.

Later, after Rowan arrives — thin, angular, long dark hair, a silver ring on her right hand I don't recognise — and after we've eaten dinner and drunk wine and made conversation that circles around everything important like plates set around a table nobody's sitting at, I find myself in the kitchen alone. The others have gone to bed, or are pretending to. I'm putting the leftover Brie in the fridge and rinsing the wine glasses and wiping down the benchtop because I can't leave a kitchen messy, I physically cannot do it, my body won't let me, the same way my body won't let me look at the upstairs hallway or think about December or say Kiera's name in past tense.

I stand at the sink. I run the water. I look out the window.

The back garden is dark. Winter dark. The moon is behind clouds. But I can see the shape of it — the stone wall, the lawn, the flagstone path, the white gate. The stone wall is limestone, mortared, about chest-height on an adult. The flagstones are uneven — I remember them being uneven. The white gate is the same.

I look at the garden and the garden looks back at me and for a moment, just a moment, it's not winter, it's summer, and the grass is green and the gate is open and ambulance lights strobing against the stone wall — red, blue, red, blue — painting the limestone in colours that don't belong to gardens, and someone is screaming and —

"The garden's the same," I say.

The words come out before I can stop them. I hear my own voice in the empty kitchen and it sounds wrong. It sounds like a confession.

I turn away from the window. I dry my hands on the tea towel. I turn off the light. I go to bed.

I do not look at the window again.
