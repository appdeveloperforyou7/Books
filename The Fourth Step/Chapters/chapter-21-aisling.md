# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 21: Aisling

## Unmasked

The silence lasts four seconds. I count them. I've been counting things all weekend — steps, seconds, breaths, lies. Four categories. Four women. The counting is Aisling's habit, not Cate's. Cate doesn't count. Cate flows. Cate smiles and asks questions and moves through rooms like water finding the easiest path downhill, leaving no trace. Aisling counts everything because Aisling doesn't trust anything to be what it seems.

Four seconds. Then I stop.

I stop being Cate.

It's not a decision. It's a release. Like setting down a rucksack I've been carrying for two years, or twenty-five years, or my whole life. The muscles in my face unlock one by one. My shoulders drop two inches. My hands, which have been performing stillness the way a statue performs calm, grip the arms of the chair, and the grip is real, the first real thing my hands have done since I walked through the front door of this house.

"My name," I say, "is not Cate Morrow."

Saskia doesn't move. She's sitting across from me with her back straight and her hands flat on her knees and her eyes on my face and she is not surprised. She's known — not everything, but enough. She's been building her case against Cate Morrow all weekend the way I've been building my case against all of them for twenty-five years. She's a lawyer. I should have expected this. I did expect this. I just didn't expect it now.

"My name," I say, "is Aisling Doyle."

The name sounds foreign in this room. It sounds like a match struck in the dark — sudden, illuminating, irreversible. Something locked away for twenty-five years, finally turning in its frame.

"Aisling Doyle," Saskia repeats. Not a question. A confirmation.

Doyle. The surname lands in the air and I hear it land and I hear what it means. Doyle. Kiera Doyle. The name that has lived inside me like a second heartbeat for twenty-five years, the name I've written on the byline of every investigation I've ever published, the name I've whispered into encrypted email servers at three in the morning when I couldn't sleep because the case was going nowhere and Kiera was still dead and no one cared. Doyle. The name I couldn't say all weekend because saying it would break the mask and the mask was the only thing keeping me in this house.

Footsteps in the hallway. Imogen, drawn by the voices, standing in the doorway with a tea towel in her hands, her face open and confused. Behind her, the back door slides open and Rowan steps inside, camera hanging at her side, her dark eyes finding me across the room. On the stairs — I hear them, the fourth step groaning — Neve. Coming down. Coming to see what's happening.

They gather. Four women in a living room in the house where my sister died on a Saturday night in July, and I am about to tell them who I am, and my hands are shaking, and I let them shake.

"I'm Kiera's sister."

The word hangs in the air. Sister. The most ordinary word in the language and the heaviest. Sister. The word I haven't said out loud to another human being in ten years, because every time I said it before their faces changed — pity, discomfort, the quick pivot to another subject. The sister of the dead girl. The girl whose sister fell out of a window. The family that was destroyed.

"She had a — wait. Kiera had a sister?" Imogen says. Her voice is small. The tea towel is twisted in her hands, wound tight, a tourniquet of fabric.

"She did. She does. I'm sitting in front of you."

I stand. I've been sitting for too long. Sitting is a passive position. Sitting is Cate's position. Cate sat and smiled and asked her careful questions and let other people talk. Aisling stands. Aisling has been standing for twenty-five years.

"I was eight when Kiera died. Eight years old. She was twelve. She was my big sister and she was the centre of my universe and she went to a sleepover at a friend's house and she never came home."

My voice is different. I can hear it. So can they. The Cate voice — smooth, measured, London-touched, grammatically perfect — is gone. What's left is raw. Australian vowels, sharp and flat. Short sentences when I'm angry. Longer ones when I'm not, when I'm grieving, when the words come from the place where Kiera still lives.

I am angry. I am grieving. I have been both for twenty-five years and the mask didn't suppress them — it compressed them. Compression produces pressure. Pressure produces rupture. The rupture is happening now, in this living room, in front of these women. I've earned it. The right to stand here without a disguise and speak in my own voice and feel what I actually feel instead of performing a careful, measured, moderate version of someone else's grief.

"I didn't know what happened," I say. "I was eight. My parents sat me down and told me Kiera had had an accident. A fall. That she'd gone to sleep and didn't wake up. That's what they told an eight-year-old. 'Kiera had an accident.' 'Kiera fell.' 'Kiera's in heaven now.' They used the word 'accident' so many times it lost its meaning. It became a sound, not a word. Accident. Accident. Accident."

Neve has stopped at the foot of the stairs. She hasn't come closer. She's standing very still with her hand on the bannister and her face — her face is the colour of paper. White. Flat. The blood gone from it as if someone turned a valve.

"After she died, everything broke," I say. "My parents divorced within two years. My mother started drinking. Not wine-with-dinner drinking. Vodka-in-the-morning drinking. Fall-down-in-the-kitchen drinking. She couldn't say Kiera's name so she poured it into glasses instead. My father tried to hold it together. He worked. He cooked. He took me to school and picked me up. He did it all with this face — grey, empty, scooped out. He died when I was twenty-five. Heart attack. Fifty-two years old. The doctor said cardiovascular disease. I said grief. The death certificate says one thing. I carry another."

I stop. Breathe. The next part is harder.

"My mother is alive. She lives in a unit in Geelong. She drinks. She doesn't talk about Kiera. She has never once, in twenty-five years, said Kiera's name to me. Not once. The name is forbidden. The topic is forbidden. My sister's entire existence has been erased from my family because my mother cannot bear to say her dead daughter's name and my father took the grief with him when he died and I am the only one left who remembers her."

My voice cracks on the last word. Not Cate's controlled, measured vocal break. A real crack. A fault line running through twenty-five years of compressed grief, the kind that reshapes terrain. I let it crack. I don't fix it. Aisling doesn't fix things. Aisling holds the broken pieces and keeps going.

---

"I became a journalist because of Kiera. I was twelve — the same age she was when she died — when I decided. I wanted to find things out. I wanted to know what happened to my sister. The official story didn't make sense. A girl falls from a window at a sleepover. An accident. A tragic, terrible accident. That's what everyone said. The police said it. The newspapers said it. The families said it. My parents said it. But accidents leave traces and the traces in Kiera's case didn't match the story."

I reach into the back pocket of my jeans. I pull out my phone. I open the encrypted folder — the folder that has lived on every phone I've owned for the past six years, the folder with two-factor authentication and a twelve-character password that is Kiera's birthday, backwards. I swipe to the photographs.

"These are autopsy photographs."

Imogen makes a sound. A small, sharp intake of breath. Rowan doesn't move. Saskia leans forward.

"I obtained them through journalism contacts. A pathologist. A friend of a friend of someone who owed someone a favour. It took me four years to find someone willing to share them. They're not in any public record. They're not in the police file. They were filed and forgotten and I found them."

I hold the phone out. Saskia takes it. She looks at the screen. Her face doesn't change but her jaw tightens, a small contraction, barely visible. She's reading the photographs the way she reads evidence — clinically, precisely, without flinching.

"The bruising," I say. "Upper back and shoulders. Bilateral. Even spacing. Grip marks, not impact marks — I won't repeat the forensic detail Saskia has already laid out. But the pathologist I consulted described it as 'manual compression consistent with hands gripping from behind.' Someone held my sister by the shoulders. Someone held her hard enough to bruise. And she landed two metres from the wall — a distance that requires force, not a fall."

Rowan is crying. Not loudly. Not dramatically. Tears running silently down her angular face like rain on unwiped glass, her jaw set, her eyes on the phone in my hand. She doesn't wipe them. She lets them fall and each one is a small, quiet verdict.

"I've been receiving anonymous tips for two years," I say. "Emails. No return address. Routed through servers I can't trace. The first one came with a photograph of the original police incident report. The second came with a name: Elaine Cassidy. The third came with a question: 'Who was the adult in the house that night?'"

Neve's hand tightens on the bannister. Her knuckles go white. She's gripping the wood the way Imogen gripped her fork this morning — without knowing, without feeling, the body responding to something the mind hasn't caught up with.

"I don't know who's been feeding them to me," I say. "I've tried to trace the source. I've tried to reply. The emails come from rotating anonymous addresses — they stop as suddenly as they start, then resurface weeks or months later with another piece. Someone knows what happened to my sister. Someone has known for twenty-five years. And someone has been feeding me information, piece by piece — like they want me to find the truth but won't come forward and say it themselves. I have my suspicions. A dying woman in a hospice. But I can't prove it."

I put the phone away. I look at each of them.

Imogen is standing in the doorway with the twisted tea towel and her mouth is open and her eyes are wide and she has stepped backward half a pace without seeming to realize it, as if the words physically pushed her.

Rowan is sitting on the arm of the sofa, tears on her cheeks, her camera in her lap, and she is looking at me — at my face — with an expression I can't immediately read. Then I understand. She sees it. The resemblance. I'm thirty-three, not twelve, and I've changed beyond recognition, but the bone structure is the same. The eyes. Kiera's eyes, looking out of a different face.

Neve is still at the foot of the stairs. Still gripping the bannister. Still white. She hasn't moved since I said my name.

And Saskia. Saskia is sitting in the chair across from where I was sitting, her hands flat on her knees, her face composed, and she is recalculating. I can see it happening in real time — the rapid reassessment of everything she thought she knew. She corresponded with A.M. Doyle for a year. She shared information with A.M. Doyle. She trusted A.M. Doyle with details about the case, about her suspicions, about the police report she obtained when she was nineteen. And A.M. Doyle was sitting across the breakfast table from her yesterday morning, asking about the house, smiling with calibrated warmth, performing the role of Cate Morrow while Aisling Doyle took notes.

"You manipulated me," Saskia says. Not angry. Not yet. Stating a fact.

"Yes."

"You manipulated all of this. The reunion. The emails. You made me think it was my idea."

"Yes."

"Why?"

The question is simple. The answer is not.

"Because I needed you all in this house. In the place where it happened. Surrounded by the same walls, the same light, the same air. Memory is contextual — you know this. Witnesses remember more in the environment where the event occurred. I needed you here so the walls would do their work. So the house would press against your denial the way it's been pressing against Neve's since she arrived."

Neve flinches at her name. A full-body flinch, visible from across the room.

"I've spent ten years investigating," I say. "I built Cate Morrow from a deceased person's records. I reached out to you, Saskia, because you never let it go. Your public record, your career, the way you'd pursued the case through official channels — you were the one who would get the others here. The one they'd trust."

"You lied to me for a year."

"I told you the truth about everything that mattered. The bruising. The landing distance. The inconsistencies in the report. I just didn't tell you who I was."

Saskia holds my gaze. Ten seconds. Fifteen. The longest silence in the room since I said my name.

The silence stretches. The wind presses. The house breathes. The weight of what I've done settles over her like snow.

"I trusted you with twenty-five years of my work," Saskia says. Not angry. Not yet. The voice of a woman measuring the distance between trust and betrayal and finding it longer than she expected.

She nods. Once. A single, precise acknowledgment. Not forgiveness. Not acceptance. Recognition. She understands why I did it. She would have done the same.

I turn to face all of them. The room is small and the night is dark and the wind is pressing against the windows and I am standing in a house where my sister died and I am not pretending anymore and the relief is so sharp it cuts.

"I didn't come here to make anyone suffer," I say. "I'm not here to punish anyone. I'm here because my sister was twelve years old and she was happy and she was funny and she told the best ghost stories and she used to close her bedroom door in my face and say 'go away, Aisling, we're doing big girl things.' I used to sit outside her door and listen to her laugh with her friends. I used to press my ear against the wood and wish I was on the other side of it."

My voice is not steady. I let it shake. Aisling's voice shakes. Cate's voice never shook. Cate's voice was a wall. Aisling's voice is a window — broken, open, letting everything through.

"She was my sister. She was my big sister. And she went to a sleepover in this house on the fourteenth of December and she never came home and my family fell apart and I have spent every day since trying to find out why."

I look at Neve. She is looking at me like I am a ghost. Like Kiera has walked into the room wearing a different face.

I look at Imogen. Her hand is over her mouth. The tea towel has fallen to the floor.

I look at Rowan. Rowan, who is still crying, who has been crying since I mentioned the bruising, who is looking at me with recognition that goes beyond the resemblance. Rowan, who has been holding Kiera's ring on her finger for twenty-five years.

I look at Saskia. Saskia, who corresponded with me for a year and never knew she was talking to Kiera's sister. Saskia, who has the police report and the autopsy summary and the photographs and the same obsessive need to know that I have.

"I am Aisling Doyle," I say. "I am Kiera Doyle's younger sister. I am a journalist. I have spent twenty-five years investigating my sister's death, and I know — I know — she did not fall from that window. She did not lean too far. She did not slip. She was pushed."

I pause. The room is silent. The wind is silent. Even the house is silent — no creaking, no settling, no breath. As if the building itself has gone still — the way a room goes still when the last card is placed on the table and everyone sees the hand.

"Someone pushed my sister. I know who was in this house that night. And I think one of you saw what happened."
