# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 11: Imogen

## Breakfast

I wake at six, which is normal, which is okay. Back home I wake at five forty-five because Sophie needs to be at school by eight and there are lunches to make and uniforms to find and Ethan's asthma pump to check and breakfast to cook, and the mornings are my favourite part of the day because the mornings are when everything is under control, when the house hums with toast and running water and Sophie's voice asking for the pink cup, not the blue one, Mum, the pink one. I love the pink cup. I love the blue cup. I love all the cups because the cups mean Sophie is here and Sophie is alive and Sophie is ten and Sophie is okay.

I don't have the pink cup here. I don't have Sophie here. I have a holiday house kitchen that has been renovated within an inch of its life and a stocked pantry — someone arranged this, the rental company, a welcome basket with muesli and instant coffee and a bottle of milk in the fridge — and four other women asleep in their rooms, or not asleep, and a morning ahead of me that needs eggs and toast and the sound of forks on plates and all the small noises that make a kitchen sound like a kitchen and not like a place where something happened. The power came back sometime before dawn but the road is still blocked — Saskia checked — and the phone signal is patchy at best, and the fallen tree at the end of the street means no one is going anywhere today. We are here. All of us. The storm made sure of that.

I need to cook.

I open the pantry. I open the fridge. I take stock. Eggs — a dozen. Butter. Milk. Bread. There's a punnet of cherry tomatoes, some spinach, a block of cheddar. In the pantry: flour, sugar, a tin of baking powder, a jar of honey. There's enough here. There's enough for a proper breakfast, a real breakfast, the kind of breakfast that says this is a normal morning in a normal house with normal people and everything is okay.

I start with the eggs. I crack six into a bowl — no, eight. There are five of us now, with Cate. Eight eggs. I whisk them. The whisking is good — the motion, the rhythm, the sound of the fork against the bowl. My hands are busy. When my hands are busy the rest of me is okay. I add a splash of milk. A pinch of salt. A grind of pepper. I whisk more. The eggs become pale yellow foam, uniform, smooth. That's good. That's how eggs should be.

I put butter in the pan. It melts. The sound it makes — the sizzle, the foam, the way butter sounds when it hits hot metal — is the sound of a kitchen being a kitchen. I pour in the eggs and I stir, low and slow, the way I do at home, the way I've done every morning for fifteen years, pulling the curds from the edge, letting the liquid run back, folding, folding.

I toast bread. I slice the tomatoes in half and put them in a bowl. I lay the table — five places, five plates, five knives, five forks. I adjust the spacing between the plates. I adjust it again. I go back to the eggs and stir. I go back to the table and adjust the forks. I straighten the napkins. I reorganise the cherry tomatoes into a neat circle. I wipe a smudge from the water glass. My hands are moving. They have not stopped since I entered the kitchen. They will not stop.

"There you are."

I turn. Cate is standing in the doorway.

She's dressed — jeans, a cream jumper, flat shoes. Her hair is pulled back. Her face is — she looks the way everyone looks first thing in the morning, which is okay, which is normal, except her eyes. Her eyes are awake. Fully awake. The way Saskia's eyes are awake. Like they've been awake for hours, watching.

"Good morning!" I say, and my voice is bright and warm and I mean it. "I'm making breakfast. Scrambled eggs, toast, tomatoes. Sit down, sit down. Coffee?"

"I'd love some." She crosses to the counter and pulls out a stool. She sits. She rests her chin on her hand. She looks at me the way Sophie looks at me when I'm cooking — attentive, interested, like what I'm doing matters.

I pour her coffee. I hand it to her. I go back to the eggs.

"Can I help?"

"No, no, I've got it. I like cooking. Greg says I use cooking as a — well, he says I channel my energy into it. He's probably right. I made three different kinds of pancakes for Sophie's birthday last year. Three. Chocolate chip, blueberry, and plain. She only ate the chocolate chip ones, but the point is I made them. Options. Options are important."

I'm talking too much. I know I'm talking too much. The words are coming the way the eggs are coming — steady, continuous, not stopping. I stir. I talk. I talk. I stir.

"So this is the house," Cate says. She wraps her hands around the coffee cup. "It's not how I pictured it. Was it always this — open? The layout, I mean?"

"You should have seen it before. Well — you probably don't remember. You were a couple of years behind us, right?"

"Right. I don't remember much."

"It was different. The kitchen had yellow tiles. Can you imagine? Yellow tiles. And there was a calendar on the wall with a picture of a lighthouse. Very holiday-house. Very—"

I stop. The spatula is in my hand. The eggs are nearly done, soft and glossy, the way they should be. I shouldn't remember the calendar. I shouldn't remember the yellow tiles. Those details are from twenty-five years ago and I haven't thought about them in — I haven't thought about them.

"It was cosy," I say, recovering. "Old-fashioned, you know? Like your nan's house. Everything a bit worn but in a good way, like — okay, I don't know, it was just warm. That's the word. Warm."

"I don't, really," Cate says. "I was so young. Tell me about the old days. What was it like? You all being here together?"

She's looking at me with those awake eyes, chin on her hand, and she is asking me a simple question about old times and I should be able to answer it because I've been thinking about the old times since I got in the car yesterday, rehearsing them, organising them, keeping the good ones at the front and the other ones somewhere else, somewhere I don't have to look.

"It was lovely," I say. "It really was. We were twelve — twelve and silly and — we played Truth or Dare, do you remember that game? Everyone played it. Kiera was the one who always suggested it. She was — she was the one with all the ideas. The games, the ghost stories, the — she was the one who made everything fun."

The eggs need stirring. I stir them. My hand moves in circles, folding, folding.

"We had pizza," I say. "We ordered pizza and ate it on the floor in the living room, on our sleeping bags, five in a row, all lined up. There were five of us. Five sleeping bags. Five girls. Me, Neve, Saskia, Rowan, and Kiera."

I say the names in order, the way I've always said them, a list, a roster, five names that go together, a set, a complete set, except the set is not complete anymore, has not been complete for twenty-five years.

"We stayed up late," I say. "We told ghost stories. Kiera told the best ghost stories. She'd hold a torch under her chin and — she was the best. She was the best of us."

Cate is watching me. Her hands are around her coffee cup. The steam rises between us.

"What was she like?"

The question is simple. Four words. What was she like. A question anyone might ask about someone they've heard about, someone from the past, someone they never met. A normal question at a normal breakfast in a normal house.

I stop stirring.

The spatula is in the air. My hand is frozen mid-motion, the spatula held above the eggs, and the eggs are starting to stick to the bottom of the pan because I'm not stirring them, but I can't move, not yet, because she's asked me about Kiera and I have an answer. I've always had an answer. I've been practising this answer for twenty-five years, in the shower and in the car and in the dark when Sophie is asleep and the house is quiet and the question rises from somewhere I don't like to go and I answer it. I answer it the way I'm about to answer it now, every word in the right order, every word in the right place, rehearsed, because some things need to be rehearsed, because some things need to be said correctly, because if you say a thing enough times the right way with the right words in the right order the saying becomes indistinguishable from what happened.

"She was the best of us," I say. "She was brave and funny and she — she fell."

A breath. The eggs are sticking. The butter is browning. The kitchen smells like toast and coffee and something hot, something I don't have a word for.

"It was an accident. She leaned out the window and fell. It was summer and the window was open and it was just — just an accident."

The words come out the way they always come out. Flat. Level. The same cadence, the same rhythm, the same arrangement of syllables I've been arranging since I was twelve years old. It was an accident. She leaned out. The window was open. Summer. Just an accident. The words have worn a groove in my mind the way a spoon wears a groove in a saucepan — smooth and deep and unplanned, the path the metal has taken every morning for years, the place the words go when they leave my mouth because there is nowhere else for them to go.

Cate nods. Her face is composed. She takes a breath and says, "You were her best friend, weren't you? The way you talk about her — you two must have been inseparable."

The sentence is smooth. Too smooth. A compliment that also tells me something: she already knew Kiera and I were close. She didn't ask *if* we were close. She confirmed it. The way you confirm something you already know and want the other person to elaborate on without realizing they're being led.

Spatula down. Heat off. I plate the eggs — five plates, five portions, the soft yellow curds sliding onto the toast, cherry tomatoes in neat circles, a sprig of spinach on each plate because presentation matters, because when food looks right it is right, because when things are arranged correctly they are correct.

I am not looking at Cate.

But I glance at her — a flicker, the way your eyes catch something at the edge of vision — and I see it.

Her face.

For one second — less than a second, a fraction, a flash — her face is not the face of a woman asking a casual question at breakfast. It is something else. Something raw. Something that looks like a shelf giving way. Like a jar slipping from fingers that have held it too long. Everything she's been carrying crashing down in the half-second before she catches it. Her eyes, for one fraction of a second, are not awake but destroyed. Her mouth is open, not speaking, not breathing, just open, as if the air has been knocked out of her.

Then it's gone.

Her face smooths. Her hands tighten around her cup. She looks at me and she says, "God. I'm so sorry. We were so young — all of us. I can't imagine what that was like for you."

Her voice is steady. Completely steady. The voice of someone who heard a sad story and is responding appropriately. Measured. Level. The way you'd say it to a stranger. The way you'd say it about someone you never knew.

"Yeah," I say. "It was — I mean, yes. Obviously. It was terrible. We were twelve, you know? You don't — you don't expect that at twelve."

I don't register what I just saw on Cate's face because by the time I turn back to the counter it has already been replaced by something else — a neutral expression, a polite nod, the face of a woman who is okay. Everything is okay. The eggs are plated. The toast is done. The table is set. Five places. Five plates. Five forks.

"I'll get the others," I say, and I walk out of the kitchen and down the hallway and I call up the stairs, "Breakfast! Eggs are ready!" and my voice is bright and warm and okay.

They come. Neve first, hair tied back, the careful posture, the watchful eyes. Saskia next, already dressed, always dressed, the pressed shirt, the precise movements. Rowan last, thin and angular and moving the way she moves everything — like light, like she's not entirely in contact with the ground.

We sit. Five women at a table. Five plates of eggs and toast and tomatoes. Five cups of coffee. A normal breakfast. A normal house. A normal morning. Everything is okay. I am okay. We are all okay.

I eat. I lift my fork and I put eggs in my mouth and I chew and I swallow and I say, "Does everyone like them? I can make more if we need more, there are plenty of eggs," and I smile and I eat and I talk and I fill the silence with words the way I fill the kitchen with cooking, the way I fill every space I enter with warmth and noise and the appearance of everything being okay.

Cate eats quietly. Neve picks at her food. Saskia eats with mechanical efficiency. Rowan holds her fork in her long fingers and looks at her plate as if she's photographing it, as if the eggs and the toast are a composition to be studied.

I'm talking about Sophie. I don't mean to be talking about Sophie but I am. "She's just started Year Five and her teacher says she's very advanced in reading, which doesn't surprise me because she's always got a book in her hand, always, and I think that's wonderful, don't you? A child who reads? Greg says I worry too much but I don't think you can worry too much about children, I think worrying is — it's what mothers do, it's —"

I stop talking because I'm talking too much. Because the silence I'm trying to fill is not the kind of silence that can be filled with words. It's the kind of silence that collects under the refrigerator hum and the kettle's click and the sound of a house with people in it — patient and permanent, like the cold spot in an oven that never quite heats through. And no amount of noise will move it.

I eat my eggs. The eggs are good. Soft and creamy, the way I like them. I am aware of Cate across the table, eating steadily, her face composed, her hands calm around her fork. I am aware of Neve beside me, staring at her plate as if she's trying to read something written on it. I am aware of Saskia at the end of the table, watching everyone, always watching, her dark eyes moving from face to face the way a camera moves across a room.

I am aware of Rowan, who has stopped eating and is looking toward the hallway, toward the stairs, her fork suspended in the air, her gaze fixed on something the rest of us can't see.

"Rowan? Are you okay?" I ask.

She doesn't answer. She sets her fork down. She stands.

"I need to — excuse me," she says, and she leaves the table and I hear her footsteps on the stairs — one, two, three, four, and the fourth step groans — and then she is gone, upstairs, and the table has four women at it now, and the silence is back, and it's bigger than before.

I pick up my fork. I take another bite. I chew. I swallow. I say, "She's probably not a breakfast person. Not everyone is. Greg isn't. I make him eggs every morning and he has toast instead. Men are funny like that."

I'm talking. I'm filling. I'm okay.

I look down at my hand.

My knuckles are white.

I've been gripping my fork so hard the tendons in the back of my hand are raised, sharp lines under the skin, and my fingers are locked around the handle, the metal pressing into my palm, and I didn't know I was doing this. I didn't feel it. I don't remember gripping.

I let go.

Four red marks on my palm. Four indentations where the metal pressed into the flesh. Four marks in a line, evenly spaced, the width of fork tines pressed hard enough to leave a pattern in the skin.

I don't remember gripping.
