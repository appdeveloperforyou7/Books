# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 7: Imogen

## The Dream

I can't sleep.

I took something to help me sleep at half ten — just a herbal thing, something from the health food shop, Greg says it's placebo but it usually works — and it's now midnight and I'm still awake. Which is fine. Sometimes it takes a while. The room isn't strange. It isn't strange at all.

I'm lying on my back with my eyes open and the room is dark and the ceiling is the same height.

I know because I'm measuring it with my eyes. I can't stop measuring it. The ceiling in our bedroom at home is higher. This ceiling is lower. This ceiling is the exact height of the ceiling in the room I slept in when I was twelve. The room with the yellow lamp and the floral bedspread and the window that faced east, towards the morning. My body knows the distance between my face and this plaster the way my hands know the weight of a mixing bowl.

I close my eyes.

Greg. I should think about Greg. Greg is at home with Sophie and Ethan and he texted at nine to say goodnight and Sophie had already brushed her teeth and Ethan wanted a second story. Greg is okay. Greg handles everything when I'm away, he's good at it, and I should stop worrying about whether Sophie's dance bag was packed because Sophie is ten, not two, and she can pack her own dance bag. Sophie being independent. Sophie being capable and brave and wonderful.

Sophie is ten and she is wonderful and she is safe and she is asleep in her bed at home and she is not here and she is not twelve.

I open my eyes.

The window faces east. I can see the shape of it against the dim grey of whatever light is getting through the curtains — not much, it's July, it's winter, the mornings are dark. But the window is in the same position. Same wall, same size, same orientation. The glass is new — double-glazed, modern, the kind of window you put in a renovated holiday rental to keep the heating bill down. But the rectangle of it is the same. The way the dim reaches the wall is the same.

Under the paint — there's new paint, you can smell it, a thin chemical sharpness that catches the back of your throat when you breathe — but under the paint there's something else. Old wood. Salt air. A house that's close to the sea, that has been close to the sea for a long time, that has absorbed salt into its bones. It's the same smell. Twenty-five years of paint and renovation and new carpet and it's the same smell and my body knows it and my mind doesn't want to.

Lesson plans. I should think about lesson plans. Next term is busy — the Gold Rush unit, the diorama project, the Sovereign Hill excursion. Sophie got the lead in the school play. She's playing Dorothy. She's so brave. I don't know where she gets it because I was never brave, I was the quiet one, the one who sat at the back and coloured neatly. Kiera was the brave one.

Kiera is brave. Brave and funny and — she fell. It was an accident. She leaned out the window and fell. That's what happened. Everyone said so. The police, my mum, the counselor at school. It was an accident. The window was open because it was summer and December and she leaned out too far. That happens. It's terrible and it's sad but it was an accident and accidents happen.

I should take another dose. I'm allowed to — the bottle says a half dose is okay if the first doesn't work within an hour. But I don't want to be foggy in the morning. I want to be bright and warm and normal, the way I was today. The banana bread was good. The dinner was nice. The wine — I shouldn't have had three glasses. The wine is why I can't sleep, interacting with the pill, that's all. A perfectly normal explanation for not sleeping in a holiday house in a room that —

Greg doesn't know about the pills. The prescription is in my maiden name, filled at a pharmacy across town. He thinks I take vitamins. That's what the bottle says on the label. Greg doesn't know about a lot of things. He doesn't know I called a counselling hotline three times last year and hung up before speaking. He doesn't know I Googled "how to stop having nightmares" at two in the morning in March. He doesn't know I cried in the school car park for twenty minutes after drop-off on the anniversary. These are not things you tell your husband. These are things you hold in your hands like a mug that's too hot to put down and too precious to drop.

The room I slept in when I was twelve had a single bed against the right wall. The bedspread was white with yellow flowers — daisies, I think, or maybe sunflowers. There was a bedside table with a lamp shaped like a mushroom. I chose that room because it was the smallest and the furthest from the stairs and because the window faced east and the morning light would wake me and I wanted to be awake early because —

Because why?

I don't remember why. I was twelve. I wanted to be awake early because I was twelve and it was a sleepover and sleepovers are exciting and I wanted to make the most of every minute. That's why. That's a perfectly normal reason.

But I'm lying here now and the ceiling is the same height and the window faces the same direction and the air smells the same under the new paint and my fingers have found the edge of the sheet and are gripping it, and the thing I'm not thinking about is the living room.

Not this living room. The old one. The one that was here before the renovation. The one with the cream walls and the blue carpet and the brown couch that was too soft and swallowed you when you sat down and the coffee table with the ring stain where someone had put a mug down without a coaster and the TV in the corner, a big boxy thing with a CRT screen and a remote that only worked if you pointed it exactly right.

I was standing in that living room.

I was standing in that living room at — I don't know what time. Late. After the sleeping bags. After the ghost stories. After we turned out the lights and the room went dark and someone said "goodnight" and someone else giggled and then the giggling stopped and the breathing became slow and even and one by one they fell asleep. But I didn't fall asleep. I never fell asleep at sleepovers. I was always the last one awake, lying in my sleeping bag, staring at the ceiling, listening to the others breathe. So I got up. I went to the kitchen to make hot chocolate because I couldn't sleep and hot chocolate helps, it always helps, warm milk and sugar and the ritual of it — filling the mug, putting it in the microwave, watching it turn, listening to the hum.

I was standing in the living room with the mug in both hands. The red mug — the house mug, the one already in the cupboard when we arrived, chunky, with a chip on the rim. The hot chocolate was too hot. I was blowing on it. Steam rising. The room dark except for the kitchen light behind me. The TV was — was the TV on? I think it was. A movie. A woman's face, close-up, the volume low, almost muted, the way you keep a TV when people are sleeping.

And then: something. From upstairs. A voice. A woman's voice. Sharp. Brief. The way you speak when you don't want to be heard but something has startled you and the words come out before you can catch them. One syllable, maybe two. A command or a warning or a name. I don't remember the words. I remember the sound of them — the edge, the sharpness, cutting through the quiet of the house like a crack through ice — sudden, clean, and after it, nothing held.

Then the sound.

The sound that came after.

Not a voice. Not a word. A sound that was — it was between sounds. Between a gasp and a thud. Between something falling and something landing. Between a cry and a silence. A sound that started and stopped and started — a sound that couldn't finish itself.

The mug.

The mug slipped. Or I dropped it. Or it leapt out of my hands — I don't know how to describe it because I wasn't there anymore, I was somewhere else, the sound had taken me out of my body and the mug was on the floor and the hot chocolate was everywhere, all over the carpet, all over my bare feet, and the mug was in pieces, the red mug, the handle in one corner and the rim in another and the pieces were still settling, still skittering across the carpet, tiny ceramic fragments finding their resting places, and I was standing there with empty hands and hot feet and the sound was still inside me, not in my ears but in my chest, a vibration that wouldn't stop.

And then — 

I don't remember what happened after that.

I don't. I don't remember. Someone came downstairs — one of the girls, the adults, the ambulance — I don't know. The next thing I remember is morning. Daylight. My mother's hand on my shoulder. "Time to go home, sweetheart." And the car, and the drive, and my mother's voice on the phone in the kitchen that night, low and serious, and me in my bed in my own room with the ceiling at the right height and the window facing the right direction and everything okay, everything okay, everything back to normal.

Because it was the TV.

It was the TV. That's what the voice was. The TV was on and there was a movie and the woman in the movie said something sharp and that's what I heard. The movie. I don't remember what movie. An action movie, maybe. Or a drama. Something with a woman's voice. The volume was low but I heard it because the house was quiet and everything sounds louder in a quiet house. That's what I heard. The TV. That's all it was.

And the sound after — the mug. The mug fell because I dropped it. I was tired and the mug was hot and I dropped it and it shattered and that's the sound I heard, my own mug on the carpet, not anything else. Of course that's what it was. I was standing in a dark living room holding a mug I was too tired to hold and I dropped it. People drop things. Mugs shatter. Hot chocolate spills. Little accidents. That's all it was. An accident.

The word sits in my chest. An accident. It was an accident.

I sit up in bed. My pulse beats in my throat, my wrists, behind my eyes. The sheet is damp at the small of my back. The room is dark. The ceiling is the same height. The window faces east. The air smells like old wood and salt and something underneath both that I can't name and don't want to.

I need water. I always bring a glass to bed at home — Greg teases me about it, "You and your water glass," and I say "Hydration is important, Gregory," and he laughs and that's our life, that's our normal life.

I get out of bed. The carpet is new — soft, deep pile. The carpet that was here when I was twelve was blue and flat and scratchy. I know this because my feet remember.

I open the bedroom door. The hallway is dark and the rain is hammering the roof so hard it sounds like something trying to get in. The floorboards creak under my feet — the same pitch, the same groan, the house speaking in its sleep, or maybe the house is not sleeping, maybe none of us are sleeping, maybe the storm is keeping the house awake the way it's keeping me awake. The kitchen is to the left. The stairs are straight ahead. I turn left towards the kitchen. I find the light switch. The fluorescent tube above the benchtop flickers twice and holds, throwing the kitchen into that flat white light that bleaches the colour from everything the way flour dusts a work surface. Then it flickers again and goes out. Two seconds of total dark. My hands find the counter edge. Then the light surges back, weaker now, buzzing, the tube struggling. The storm is winning.

I fill a glass from the tap. The water is cold. I drink half of it and the cold is grounding, real. I set the glass on the benchtop. I should go back to bed. I should turn off the light and think about lesson plans and Sophie's dance bag and Greg's sandwiches and all the normal things waiting for me at home in Perth where the ceilings are high and the air smells like our house, not this house, not this salt-and-wood smell that keeps finding me.

But instead of turning left towards my room, I walk to the foot of the stairs.

I don't decide to do this. My feet carry me to the bottom of the staircase, and I stand there and I look up. The staircase rises into darkness. The new bannister is pale, catching a sliver of light from somewhere. The steps are in shadow. The fourth step — Rowan said it still creaks. The stairs are new, they shouldn't creak, but something groaned when Neve came downstairs for tea. That particular groan. That particular weight.

The hallway at the top of the stairs is invisible from here. Dark. The kind of dark that looks solid, with edges and a surface you could touch. The air at the top is colder, stiller, the air in a place where nothing moves.

I stand at the bottom of the stairs and I look up into the dark and I think about the voice I heard twenty-five years ago. Sharp. Brief. A woman's voice. From upstairs. Not a TV. Not a movie. Not a dream.

And I think, for the first time in twenty-five years, a thought I've never let myself think: *What if it wasn't an accident?*

My hands clench so hard the nails bite crescents into my palms and my jaw aches from the pressure and the glass of water on the kitchen benchtop is waiting for me and I should not be standing at the bottom of these stairs thinking thoughts that are not okay and not okay and not anything I have permission to think.

I go back to bed. I drink the rest of the water. I lie down. I close my eyes. The ceiling is the same height. The window faces east. The air smells like salt and old wood and underneath it all, underneath the paint and the carpet and the years, something that was here before us and has been waiting under every surface for us to come back.

I don't sleep.
