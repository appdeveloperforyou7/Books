# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 26: Neve

## Aftermath

The kitchen is the only room that doesn't carry the weight of what just happened.

I don't know why I came in here. The pull of habit, maybe. The therapist's instinct to create a neutral space. Or the simpler truth: I needed to move. I needed to put my body in motion and let the motion be purposeful and the kitchen is a room where movement has purpose. Boil water. Find mugs. Open cupboards.

The rituals of hosting that I learned from Imogen, who learned them from her mother, who learned them from the same cultural script that tells women to make tea when something unbearable happens. The script is a lie — tea doesn't help. But the hands need a task while the mind catches up to the wreckage.

I fill the kettle. Set it on the base. Press the switch. The blue light comes on. The water begins to heat. Mechanical actions. They require no thought. I am grateful for them.

Through the doorway, I can see the living room. The scene is frozen. No one has moved. Elaine is in the armchair and her eyes are closed and her hands are in her lap and she is very still — the stillness of a woman who has said everything she is going to say and now has nothing left.

Aisling is on the sofa, sitting exactly where she was during Saskia's evidence presentation, her face unreadable, her hands flat on her knees. The autopsy photographs are still on the arm of Elaine's chair. Saskia is by the mantelpiece, arms crossed, her jaw working — not tension, calculation. She is thinking. Saskia is always thinking. Imogen is on the far end of the sofa, her face still buried in her hands, her shoulders shaking with silent crying that has moved beyond the point of sound. Rowan is in the corner, camera in her lap, staring at the sealed window with an expression I would call emptiness if I had to name it — the diagnostic blank of a system that has exceeded its threshold and shut down.

The kettle clicks off.

I pour water into six mugs. I don't check whose mug is whose. It doesn't matter. Tea is tea. The act of carrying it matters more than the drinking. I set the mugs on the counter. I don't carry them in yet. I'm not ready to go back into that room.

My phone is on the counter. Face up. The screen dark. I pick it up and stand there, holding it the way you hold something you're about to use, not using it. The screen reflects the overhead light. My face looks back at me, ghostly, transparent. I don't look like myself. I look like someone who has been through something. Which I suppose I have.

I press the contact. Liam.

It rings twice. Three times. I picture him in our kitchen in Melbourne, Lily upstairs in bed, the house quiet, his phone on the counter where he always leaves it. He picks up on the fourth ring.

"Neve? It's late. Is everything okay?"

His voice. The steady, slightly bewildered voice I married. The voice of a man who loves me and knows there are rooms in my mind he has never been allowed to enter and has stopped asking about. He stopped asking about my childhood before age thirteen years ago. He accepted that there were things I didn't discuss. He thought it was shyness. He thought it was privacy. He didn't know it was a locked room built by someone else inside my mind.

"Liam."

My voice sounds wrong. Thin. Stretched. He hears it immediately — he always hears it, the small shifts, the way I say his name when something is different.

"What's wrong? Is it your mum?"

Is it your mum. My ribs tighten. The question expands inside them. I almost laugh. Almost. Is it my mum. Yes. It is entirely my mum. My mum who pushed a twelve-year-old girl through a window and then spent twenty-five years making me forget I saw it. My mum who drove forty minutes from a hospice to sit in an armchair and try to control the narrative one last time. My mum who just admitted everything. My mum who is crying in the next room and whose tears are real.

"I need you to — can you tell Lily that I love her?"

A pause. The pause of a man hearing something in his wife's voice that he has never heard before. Not fear. Not sadness. Something before both. The silence of someone standing at the edge of a thing he cannot see the bottom of.

"Neve, what's going on?"

"I need you to tell her that I love her. I need you to tell her that her grandmother —"

I stop.

The sentence won't finish. It sits on my tongue, half-formed. Her grandmother. The woman who will hold Lily and kiss Lily's cheek and buy her birthday presents and tell her she's smart and beautiful and brave. The woman who will do all of those things genuinely and with love because that is who Elaine is — who she also is. The woman who will love my daughter the way she loved me. The woman who held my face in a hallway and whispered a lie into me and then held me when I had nightmares about the lie she whispered.

I cannot tell Liam about his mother-in-law tonight. I cannot form the sentence. The words exist but the passage between knowing and saying is blocked.

"I need to tell her I'll be home soon," I say. "That's all. I'll be home soon."

"Do you want me to come? I can get a sitter. I can drive down tonight."

"No."

"Neve —"

"I'll explain — when I'm home. I just need you to tell Lily I love her. Can you do that?"

"Of course."

"Thank you."

"Neve?"

"Yeah?"

"I love you."

The words are so simple. Three words. No architecture behind them. No manipulation. No strategy spanning decades. Just a man in a kitchen in Melbourne telling his wife he loves her because it's late and she sounds wrong and he doesn't know what else to do.

"I love you too," I say. I mean it the way I have never meant anything. I hang up before my voice breaks — if it breaks now it won't stop, and there are things that need to happen first.

I set the phone down. Pick up two mugs. Carry them into the living room.

Aisling takes one without looking up. Her hands close around it automatically. She has spent twenty-five years investigating her sister's death, ten years as a journalist, two years posing as someone else, and now she has the confirmation she came for. The truth is out. The woman in the chair did it. The evidence aligns. The confession is complete. I don't know what Aisling is feeling. I'm not sure Aisling knows. She structured her entire identity around the investigation. The investigation is over. What's left when the thing that defined you is finished? The grief underneath. The grief she never processed because she turned it into a case file instead.

I set a mug beside Saskia. She nods — a small, precise acknowledgment — and wraps her hands around it. Her eyes are moving. I can see the calculation: evidence, witnesses, confession, admissibility, statute of limitations, coronial reinvestigation. She is building a case. The structure is how she processes. She became a lawyer for the same reason Aisling became a journalist. The same origin. Different paths. Both arriving here.

I bring a mug to Imogen. She takes it and her hands are shaking so badly the tea sloshes over the rim and burns her fingers and she doesn't react. She is somewhere else. Not dissociating — I would know dissociation. She is too close. The minimization has collapsed and underneath it is twenty-five years of grief she was never allowed to feel.

"I'm sorry," she whispers.

I don't know who she's talking to. Me. Aisling. Kiera. Herself.

"I'm sorry I didn't say anything. I knew. I think I always knew. I heard the voice and I knew it wasn't the TV and I was twelve and scared and I told myself it was the TV because that was easier. And then it was too late to unsay it."

"That wasn't yours to carry."

"I should have —"

"You were twelve. The adults told you what to think and you thought it. That's being twelve."

She nods. The nod is automatic. The grief is still there — undischarged, building pressure. She will need to feel it later. When the shock wears off and the guilt rises and the twenty-five years of "it was an accident" come crashing down. But not now. Now the rule is: stay upright. Keep breathing. Hold the mug.

Rowan hasn't moved.

She is in the corner, camera in her lap, staring at the sealed window. Her face is expressionless. Not composed — empty. The way a face looks when the thing behind it has been used up. Her right hand — the one with the silver ring — rests on the camera body, fingers curled around it, the knuckles bloodless, gripping a weight that isn't there. She has been crying on and off all evening. Now the tears have stopped and what's left is a stillness that is worse than tears. I set a mug beside her. She doesn't reach for it. She stares at the window and I think: she is seeing what she saw. The hand. Kiera's hand. The fingernails scraping paint. The fingers splaying, grasping, losing grip. The fragments she's carried for twenty-five years — cold, light, sound, silhouette — now assembled into a complete picture by the truth Elaine just spoke.

Elaine is still in the armchair.

Her eyes are closed. Her breathing is slow and audible — a slight rattle in the chest, the kind cancer makes when it reaches the lungs. She has been sitting there for — I don't know how long. Time has become unreliable. What matters is: she hasn't left. She hasn't asked to leave. The truth is in the room with all of us now, shared, known, and it cannot be taken back.

I stand apart from the group.

My body has taken me to a place by the wall, near the doorway, between the living room and the kitchen. A liminal space. I lean against the wall. The plaster is cool through my shirt. I press my shoulder blades into it.

Two truths.

My mother is a killer.

My mother is my mother.

I hold them simultaneously. The way a clinician holds a dual diagnosis — both present, both real, neither reducible to the other. Not choosing between them. Not dropping either. Just holding. The weight of each. The weight of both. The woman who pushed a child through a window is the woman who taught me to ride a bicycle. The woman who gaslit me for twenty-five years is the woman who sat up with me all night when I had my first migraine at thirteen, pressing a cold cloth to my forehead, stroking my hair, saying "it's all right, sweetheart, I've got you." The woman who just admitted to killing Kiera Doyle is the woman who drove forty minutes from a hospice because she knew the truth was coming and wanted to be here when it arrived. She wanted to control it. She wanted to manage it. She wanted to hold my face one more time and tell me what I saw.

And she couldn't.

The wall held for twenty-five years. Through a police investigation, a coronial finding, a funeral, a house sale, a relocation, three psychologists, two decades of my life. She held it together from a hospice bed, sending anonymous emails, orchestrating a reunion, arranging the evidence so it would surface in this room on this night. The architecture was magnificent in its way. A masterwork of maternal manipulation built on the simple insight that a child will believe whatever a loving parent insists is true. She held it together when she walked through the front door. She held it together through the managed confession — the headache, the startled reaction, the accident. She held it together until I asked about the phone call.

Until the script failed.

Until both.

The room is quiet. The wind has not returned. The house is still. The clock on the mantelpiece ticks. The kitchen tap drips.

And then, from the next room, a sound.

A crash.

Not the wind. Not the house settling. Not a door or a window or a pipe. A different sound. The sound of something heavy hitting the floor. The sound of wood striking wood — a chair tipping, an arm hitting a side table. The sound of a body falling.

Elaine.

I am moving before I know I'm moving. Through the doorway. Into the living room. The armchair is empty. The coat is draped over the arm like a shed skin. And on the floor, between the armchair and the coffee table, Elaine is lying on her side. Her eyes are closed. Her face is grey — not the grey of composure, the grey of blood leaving the surface. Her lips are slightly parted. Her left arm is bent underneath her at an angle that doesn't look right.

"Elaine."

I am on my knees. I don't remember kneeling. My hand is on her shoulder — the thin shoulder, the bone sharp under the fabric. Her skin is cold. Not cool. Cold.

"Mum."

The word comes from somewhere deeper than language. From the place where the twelve-year-old still lives, the girl who watched her mother push her friend through a window and then believed her mother when she said it didn't happen. From the place where the monster and the mother are the same person and that person is on the floor and she is not moving.

"Mum."

She doesn't respond.

The room erupts behind me. Saskia is already on her phone — 000, ambulance, Sorrento, the address spoken with the precision of a woman who gives addresses for a living. Imogen standing with her hands over her mouth. Rowan risen from her corner, camera forgotten, dark eyes fixed on Elaine's still form. Aisling beside me — when did she get there? — her hand reaching for Elaine's wrist, two fingers pressing below the thumb, checking for a pulse.

"She's breathing," Aisling says. "Faint. She's breathing."

I hold my mother's shoulder. The bone under my palm. The cold skin. The too-large coat pooled on the chair behind her, empty, the shape of her still pressed into the fabric.

The woman who held my face in a hallway. The woman who told me I was asleep. The woman who loved me and controlled me and lied to me and protected me and pushed a twelve-year-old girl through a window and couldn't take it back.

The monster and the mother. The same person. On the floor. Not moving.

And I am on my knees beside her, holding on.
