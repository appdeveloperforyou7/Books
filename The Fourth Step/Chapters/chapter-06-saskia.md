# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 6: Saskia

## Night Watch

The house is quiet by ten forty-three. I know this because I am sitting at the kitchen table with my laptop open and the clock on the screen reads 22:43. The last sound from upstairs was a door closing forty minutes ago. Since then: wind. Rain. The particular silence of a house full of people pretending to sleep — not silence at all but a collective held breath, four women in four beds listening to the same sounds, telling themselves different stories about what they mean. The storm came in hard around nine. The BOM forecast was accurate for once — 1003 millibars at four, 998 by nine. The wind is gusting at sixty kilometres an hour and the rain is hitting the south wall in bursts that sound like handfuls of gravel. The road into Sorrento from the highway is coastal and narrow and will flood in this. By morning, we will be cut off. I note this without concern. We are already cut off in every way that matters.

I didn't come here to sleep. I came here to work.

The laptop screen is the only light in the kitchen. I've closed the blinds. The battery is at eighty-one percent. The file is labelled "KDOYLE_INCIDENT" and it is password-protected and it lives on a thumb drive in my toiletry bag and I have read it four hundred times. I read it again.

I open a second file. "RENTAL_ANOMALIES." I added it this afternoon. The lockbox code was changed after my booking. I used my standard code — 7-2-0-4, my mother's birthday reversed. The confirmation email from the rental agency specified 4-1-9-8. But the lockbox opened to 7-2-0-4. Someone accessed the booking after I made it and changed the code to one I would recognize. Someone who knows my systems. Someone who wanted to make sure I got in without difficulty. I have contacted the agency. They have no record of a code change. The property manager says the code was "set by the guest who made the reservation." The guest who made the reservation was me. I did not change the code.

I note this. I underline it. I do not yet understand it.

I close the file. I open the incident report.

The report is two pages. Two pages for a girl's life. "The deceased, Kiera Anne Doyle, aged 12 years, was found at approximately 2120 hours on 14 December lying on the rear lawn of the premises, having apparently fallen from a second-floor window." Apparently. The word is doing a lot of work in that sentence. It's the kind of word police use when they haven't verified something but don't want to say they haven't verified it. Apparently. Reportedly. Allegedly. I use these words in court. I know what they cost.

"Minor bruising noted on the upper back and shoulders, bilaterally. Consistent with impact injuries from a fall."

I was nineteen when I first understood that sentence. I was in the law library at Sydney Uni, third floor, the section on forensic pathology that no undergraduate ever visits. I'd been there for six hours, cross-referencing bruising patterns. Bilateral bruising on the upper back and shoulders. Two sides. Left and right. Symmetrical. The report says "consistent with impact injuries from fall" and that's technically possible — a body hitting the ground can produce bruising on the back. But bilateral bruising on the shoulders specifically? The shoulders are not primary impact points in a fall from height. The back, the pelvis, the head — these are primary. The shoulders are secondary at best. And bilateral means both sides, evenly distributed, which suggests force applied from behind, not force applied by the ground. Two hands. Gripping. Pressing into flesh. Ten fingers leaving marks that the attending officer noted and dismissed in the same breath.

I've spent twenty-five years trying to get someone to take that detail seriously.

I typed a timeline in 2004. I was fourteen. Two years after the night in question. I typed it on my mother's desktop computer, in her study, while she was at work. I printed it and put it in a manila folder and hid the folder under my mattress. I have updated that timeline twenty-three times since. The current version is on the screen in front of me.

The timeline begins at 1800 hours, 14 December. Arrival at the Sorrento house. Five girls: Neve Cassidy, Saskia Brennan, Imogen Hart, Rowan Thorne, Kiera Doyle. One adult: Elaine Cassidy, mother of Neve. Owner of the property.

1830. Pizza delivery. Ordered by Elaine Cassidy. Five girls ate in the living room. Elaine in the kitchen on the phone. I noted this at the time because I thought it was strange — an adult ordering pizza for children and then not eating with them. She said she had a headache. She said she was going to lie down.

1900–2100. Sleepover activities. Truth or Dare. Ghost stories. Music. The details here are fragmentary. My own memory provides some. Imogen's — if she were honest — would provide others. Rowan's would provide images but not sequences. Neve's would provide nothing. Neve has always claimed she doesn't remember the latter part of the evening. She was asleep, she says. She fell asleep on the couch. Her mother confirmed this.

2100. The approximate time the girls settled into sleeping bags. I remember this because I was the last one awake. I always was. Even at twelve I lay in the dark, ears tuned to the house. The creak of timber. The tick of pipes. The wind through a window that was open somewhere upstairs. Salt and eucalyptus in the air.

2120. Approximate time of the fall. The report says Kiera was found at 2120. The fall would have occurred minutes before. The report does not specify who found her or in what order the girls came upstairs. It notes that Elaine Cassidy "was alerted by a noise and descended from her bedroom." Her bedroom was on the ground floor, according to her statement. She said she had been in bed with a migraine.

But I was the first one up the stairs. I know this because I remember the staircase. I remember the fourth step creaking under my weight. I remember the hallway at the top — the carpet blue and thin, the walls cream, the window at the end open and warm air coming through. I remember standing in that hallway and looking toward the window and seeing —

I close the laptop.

I don't need to read my own timeline to know what I saw. I've carried it for twenty-five years. Two shapes at the end of the hallway. One tall. One small. The tall one moved. The small one — the small one was gone. And Neve was already standing in the hallway. Under the window. Looking at me. Her face wasn't surprised. The mouth was already open. The eyes were already wide, the whites visible all the way around. She'd been wearing that expression before I arrived — the terror was established, not fresh.

I told the police this. I was twelve and I told the police that Neve was already upstairs and the policewoman wrote something down and no one ever followed up. No one ever asked me again.

I open the laptop.

There are files on each of the women. I compiled them over the past two years, after the reunion email arrived. Professional discipline. You don't walk into a situation without knowing the players.

Neve Cassidy. Family therapist. Private practice, Melbourne. Married to Liam Cassidy, 40, high school teacher. One daughter, Lily, 12. Lives in Northcote, nineteen minutes from her mother's current address (Elaine Cassidy, hospice care, Caulfield). Daily phone contact with mother, based on my observation of her phone activity today. Neve has never discussed her childhood before age thirteen in any public forum — no interviews, no publications, no commentary. She chose a career that requires her to help others access their memories while maintaining a perfect void in her own. This is either irony or architecture. I note both possibilities.

Imogen Hart. Primary school teacher. Year 6 — eleven- and twelve-year-olds. Perth. Married to Gregory Hart, 39, accountant. Two children: Sophie, 10, and Ethan, 7. Prescribed medication for anxiety — I don't know the specific drug, but I've observed the pill bottle in her toiletry bag. The label is turned inward. She doesn't want anyone to see it. She teaches twelve-year-olds. She has built her life around the supervision of children the age Kiera was when she died. This is not coincidence. This is compensation behaviour. The minimiser constructs a world where twelve-year-olds are safe because she is the one keeping them safe.

Rowan Thorne. Freelance photographer. No fixed address. The last two years: Lisbon, Reykjavik, Melbourne, Perth, back to Melbourne. No permanent home. No partner. No children. Wears a silver ring on her right hand — moonstone, simple setting, child-sized. Not her ring. She didn't buy it. Someone gave it to her. A friend, she said. Rowan's narration is sensory, not narrative. She remembers what things looked like and felt like and sounded like but she cannot tell you what happened. This is not evasion. This is the way trauma stores itself when the hippocampus fails to consolidate the experience into a story. She has images, not events. She has light, not sequence.

And Cate Morrow. Arriving tomorrow. A year or two behind us. Moved to London. Lost touch. No one remembers her. I've tried to verify her. The digital footprint is thin — too thin for someone who supposedly lived in London for twenty years. A LinkedIn profile created three months ago. A Facebook account with twelve friends and no posts before January. No Instagram. No Twitter. No professional history that can be independently confirmed.

I've noted this. I've underlined it. I've put a question mark next to her name.

I close the file. I open the autopsy summary.

One page. Photocopied, grainy, the toner slightly uneven across the right margin. I obtained it through a freedom-of-information request that took fourteen months. The request was denied twice and granted on the third application, after I provided a legal justification that was technically sound and personally motivated and that no one at the coroner's office had the energy to dispute.

"Minor bruising on the upper back and shoulders, bilaterally. No fractures. Cause of death: blunt force trauma to the head consistent with fall from height. Manner of death: accidental."

Accidental. There it is again. The word that closes doors, stops investigations, turns a dead twelve-year-old into a statistic instead of a case.

I've shown this page to three forensic pathologists. Two said the bruising was "not inconsistent with a fall." One — Dr. Amira Hassan, University of Sydney, retired — said: "This is grip bruising. Five-point contact, bilateral. Someone held this child from behind. The question isn't whether she fell. The question is what happened before she fell."

I keep Dr. Hassan's email in the file. I've read it a hundred times. It's the only professional confirmation I've ever received and it lives in a folder on a thumb drive in my toiletry bag because the world does not want to hear that a twelve-year-old girl was pushed out of a window in a holiday house in Sorrento twenty-five years ago. The world wants accidents. The world wants closure. The world wants two pages of report and the word "apparently" and the word "accidental" and everyone moving on.

I have never moved on.

I take out the photograph. Kiera at the school sports day. Running. Hair behind her. Laughing. She is twelve and alive and the photograph is the only proof I have that she existed in motion, in joy, in the specific way that twelve-year-old girls exist when they haven't yet learned that the world can hurt them. She ran with her whole body. She laughed with every inch of herself. She entered rooms the way light enters — you noticed her before you noticed anything else.

My hand is shaking. The photograph trembles between my fingers, a micro-tremor, the kind I mock in witnesses. I press my thumb flat against the edge of the photograph to stop it. The shaking doesn't stop. I press harder. The photograph bends. I let go and press both palms flat against the table and hold them there and breathe and the shaking stops and I pick up the photograph again and put it in the sleeve and close the file.

I do not look at it again. I put the autopsy summary back. I lock the file. I close the laptop.

The kitchen is dark now. The only light is the glow of the laptop's sleep indicator, a slow white pulse marking time no one is using. I sit in the dark and I think about the funeral.

It was December. A church in Sorrento — St. John's, weatherboard, white, the kind of church you see in small coastal towns and forget immediately. The pews were full. Kiera's parents in the front row — I remember her mother's face, not her father's. Her mother's face was the face of someone whose world has ended. Not crying. Beyond crying. A dry, hollow devastation — the face of a crime scene that has been contaminated, the evidence destroyed before anyone thought to preserve it.

Aisling was there. Kiera's younger sister. She was eight. She sat between her parents and she didn't understand what was happening and she kept looking around the church with these wide, confused eyes and no one explained anything to her because no one could. How do you explain to an eight-year-old that her sister fell out of a window? You don't. You say it was an accident. You say it was quick. You say she didn't suffer. You say the things adults say when the truth is too large for the room.

And Elaine Cassidy. Front row. Arm around Neve. Composed.

That's the word I've carried for twenty-five years. Composed. Not grieving. Not devastated. Not dry-hollowed like Kiera's mother. Composed. The way you compose yourself before a meeting. The way you compose your face when you need it to say nothing. She sat in that pew with her arm around her daughter and her face was smooth and her eyes were dry and her posture was perfect and she looked like a woman attending a function she was required to attend. A principal at a school assembly. A duty. An obligation.

She doesn't look sad, I thought. I was fourteen and I was standing at the back of the church with my mother and I looked at Elaine Cassidy and I thought: She doesn't look sad. She looks tired.

Tired the way you look after a night you didn't sleep — the skin slack, the eyes unfocused, the body running on nothing but momentum. Tired the way you look when the adrenaline has washed out and the performance is over and only the exhaustion is left.

I was fourteen and it registered and I filed it and I have never unfiled it.

I stand. I pour a glass of water from the tap. I don't drink it. I stand at the sink and hold the glass and look at the dark window. Same position as the original. Double-glazed now, aluminium frame. But the garden beyond it is the same garden. Stone wall. Flagstone path. White gate. Winter-dormant grass, pale in the moonlight. In December it was green and Kiera's body was on it and the ambulance lights turned the stone wall red and blue and red and blue.

I put the glass down. I turn from the window.

And I hear it.

Footsteps. Slow. Deliberate. From the upstairs hallway. The sound of weight on floorboards — not the stairs, the hallway. The movement of someone pacing, or walking, or standing and shifting from one foot to the other.

I go to the foot of the stairs.

The staircase rises in front of me. Fourteen steps, turned at the midpoint. The bottom half is visible — pale wood, steel bannister, the wall beside it painted grey. The upper half turns and disappears into darkness. The hallway at the top is not visible from where I stand.

I look up.

Dark.

The footsteps stop.

The house settles. A board groans somewhere to the left — the living room, perhaps, or the wall between the kitchen and the hall. The wind moves around the eaves. The sea, faint, rhythmic, far away.

I wait. Thirty seconds. Sixty. Nothing.

I could go up. I could climb these stairs and confront whoever is awake in the hallway where Kiera fell, in the dark, in the cold, and ask the questions I've been waiting twenty-five years to ask.

I don't go up. Not because I'm afraid. Because tonight is not the night. Tonight is observation. Tonight is data. Tonight is the gathering of information that will be used later, precisely, in a room where the lights are on and the questions are direct and the answers cannot be avoided.

I turn from the stairs. I go back to the kitchen. I sit at the table. I open the laptop. I add a note to the file.

22:47. Footsteps in upstairs hallway. Origin unclear. Duration: approximately twelve seconds. Stopped when I reached the foot of the stairs.

I save the file. I close the laptop.

The kitchen is dark. The house is dark. Somewhere above me, in the hallway where a twelve-year-old girl went through a window, someone is awake and listening to the same evidence I am.

I sit in the dark and I think about Kiera. Not the report. Not the bruising. Not the timeline. Kiera. Running at the sports day. Laughing with her whole body. Twelve years old and the most alive person I've ever known.

Someone else is awake in this house. Someone else knows.
