# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 18: Imogen

## The Voice

The mug is empty.

I look down at it and it is empty and I don't remember drinking the tea. The tea I made to keep my hands busy, the tea I wrapped both palms around because the ceramic was warm and solid and real — and everything else in this room is not real, nothing about this room is real, nothing about this weekend is — I set the mug on the table. Carefully. Both hands. The way you set down something you're afraid of dropping.

The documents are still there. Saskia's documents. The police report. The autopsy summary. The photograph with the reflection — the small figure at the top of the stairs, child-sized, watching, and I don't know who it is but the shape of it is familiar in a way that makes my stomach fold. Rowan's photographs of the scratches. Five lines. Fingernails. Someone holding on.

I keep looking at the mug because the mug is empty and safe and I understand empty mugs. I understand mugs and tea and the way a kitchen smells when you're making something warm for people you care about. I understand the domestic machinery of caring — the stirring, the pouring, the "here you go, love, it's nice and hot." I understand these things because they are the stitching that holds my life together — the small physical rituals that bind the fabric, reinforce the seams, keep everything from unravelling.

Everything is not okay.

Saskia said "bilateral" and I didn't know what it meant and then she explained it and I understood and the understanding was like a hand reaching inside my chest and closing around something I had been protecting for twenty-five years. Grip marks. Not fall injuries. Grip marks. Hands on shoulders. Push.

Someone pushed Kiera.

The words are in the room now. Saskia said them and they are in the air and they are not leaving. They have moved in. They are permanent. Someone pushed Kiera and the mug in my hands is empty and I am thirty-seven years old in a renovated holiday house in Sorrento and the world I built — the warm, well-lit world where I teach Year Six and bake banana bread and pack Sophie's dance bag and say "it was an accident" with the practised ease of someone who has said it ten thousand times — that world is a lie.

The mug is on the table. Empty. I don't remember setting it down.

Neve is beside me on the sofa. She hasn't spoken since Saskia finished. Her face is white. Not the paleness of someone who is unwell — the whiteness of someone who has seen something they cannot un-see. She is staring at the documents and her hands are in her lap and they are very still. Neve's hands are never still. She grips pen clips and chair arms and her own wrists. But right now her hands are flat on her thighs and they are not moving and the stillness is wrong, it is the stillness of a person who is holding themselves together with so much force that nothing can move, not a finger, not an eyelash, nothing.

Rowan is in the armchair. She is holding her camera but not photographing. Her eyes are on the table — on the evidence — and they are clear. Present. For the first time all weekend she looks like someone who is not lost in fragments. She looks like someone who has found the thread and is pulling.

Cate is in the doorway. She came in from outside and she heard Saskia's last sentence and she sat in the other armchair — the one near the bookshelf, away from the table — and she has not spoken. She is watching. Calm, still, her face arranged in an expression of concern that is perfectly calibrated. It is a good expression. It is the right expression for the moment. And I would believe it except I am a primary school teacher and I have spent sixteen years reading the faces of children who are performing innocence, and Cate's face is a performance — the right muscles moving at the right time, concern arriving precisely when it should, not a beat early or late.

But that is not my concern right now. My concern is inside me. My concern is the thing I have been carrying for twenty-five years — lodged behind my sternum the way a pill gets stuck when you swallow without water — and I have told myself it was nothing, just a tiny thing, not a stone at all, just a grain of sand, nothing to worry about, it was the TV, it was a dream, it was —

It was not the TV.

The thought arrives with the force of a door kicked open. The frame splinters and the door swings wide and behind it is the room I have kept locked for twenty-five years and the room is not empty. The room has been waiting. The room has a sound in it — muffled, patient, pressing against the wood.

I hear it now. Not as a memory. As a presence. As something that has been in the room with me all along, sitting quietly behind the locked door, patient, waiting for me to stop pretending it wasn't there.

A voice.

A woman's voice. Sharp. From upstairs. Brief — one second, maybe two. Not a scream. Not a cry. A command. The kind of tone an adult uses when a child has done something wrong and the adult needs the behaviour to stop immediately. Authoritative. Commanding. Female.

Then the sound of the fall.

Then the mug shattering in my hands.

I was in the kitchen. The old kitchen, the one with the yellow tiles and the calendar with the lighthouse. I was making hot chocolate. Five mugs lined up on the counter. The pink one with the chipped handle was mine. I was stirring — the chocolate powder into the milk, the spoon going round and round, the way I always stirred, the way I still stir, round and round, the small circular motion that makes powder dissolve into liquid, that makes separate things become one thing, that makes chaos into something warm and drinkable.

And then the voice. From upstairs. Sharp. Female.

And then the sound. Not a voice. A sound. The sound a body makes when it stops being vertical and starts being horizontal and the distance between vertical and horizontal is two storeys.

The mug in my hand shattered.

That's what I remember. The shattering. The hot chocolate on the floor — on my feet, warm, spreading, the brown liquid running between the tiles, the handle still in my hand, the ceramic in pieces around me — a thing that had been whole and was now not whole and could never be whole again, no matter how carefully you gathered the pieces.

I have told myself for twenty-five years that the voice was the television. A movie. Someone left the television on. Or it was a dream — half-asleep in the kitchen, stirring. I have told myself this so many times that the telling has become the memory. The memory has become the telling. I cannot separate them anymore. The TV. A dream. That's what it was. That's what it has always been.

Except it wasn't.

I knew it wasn't. At twelve years old, standing in the kitchen with the broken mug and the hot chocolate on my feet and the sound still echoing through the house — a sound that was not the TV, that was not a movie, that was not a dream — I knew. I knew the way you know the sound of your own mother's voice in a crowd. Not by thinking. By knowing. The voice was real. The voice was upstairs. The voice was female and it was not one of us.

"It wasn't the TV."

The words come out of my mouth and I don't decide to say them. They escape. The way steam escapes from a sealed pot — the sudden, uncontrolled release of something kept under a lid for too long.

Saskia turns. Rowan turns. Neve turns. Three faces and I am looking at them and they are looking at me and I cannot perform anymore. The performance is gone. The warm smile, the busy hands, the banana bread and the Tim Tams and the "everything is okay" — it is gone. What is left is a twelve-year-old girl standing in a kitchen with broken ceramic at her feet and hot chocolate cooling on the floor and a sound in her ears that she will never unhear.

"I heard — I don't know, I heard something," I say. "That night. I was in the kitchen making hot chocolate and I heard —"

My voice cracks. Not a small crack. A break. The kind of break that happens when something held under tension finally gives way. I press my palm against my mouth. Hold it there. Breathe.

"A voice," I say. My hand drops. "I don't — a voice. I heard a voice."

The room is very still. Saskia is watching me with those dark, absorbing eyes. Rowan's fingers have tightened on her camera. Neve — Neve is looking at me and her lips have parted and her eyes have gone wide and glassy, and I cannot tell if she is seeing me or through me.

"From upstairs," I say. "Before — before the fall. Before the sound. There was a voice. A woman's voice. Sharp. Like she was — like she was telling someone to do something. Or stop doing something."

I am crying. I don't know when it started. The tears are not gentle. Not the tidy crying of a woman in a movie who looks beautiful doing it. They are hot and messy and my nose is running and my face is contorted and I am making sounds — ugly sounds, gasping sounds, the sounds of a person whose scaffolding has collapsed and who is standing in the rubble.

"I told myself it was the TV," I say. "For twenty-five years. The TV. A movie. A dream. I told myself — I told myself that because if it wasn't the TV, if it was real, if it was a real voice, a real woman's voice from upstairs before Kiera fell —"

I can't finish the sentence. The implications of the sentence are too large. They undo everything. Every lesson plan, every batch of banana bread, every night I lay in bed and told myself the world was safe and children were protected and accidents were random and Kiera fell and it was nobody's fault. Every time I said "it was an accident" to Greg, to my friends, to the mothers at the school gate, to myself — every time was a coat of paint over the crack and the paint is peeling now and I am standing in the open and the wind is cold and there is nothing between me and the truth.

"The voice," I say. "I heard a voice. A woman's voice. And I knew — even then, I knew it wasn't the TV. I knew."

The words hang in the room. They are out. After twenty-five years of being the TV, a dream, nothing, they are out. The voice was real. The voice was a woman's. The voice was upstairs before Kiera fell. The voice is the thing I have been carrying inside the stone in my chest, and now the stone is cracked open and the voice is in the room with all of us and I cannot put it back.

I am sobbing. Full-body. The kind of crying that comes from the diaphragm, that shakes the shoulders, that makes you gasp for air between each wave. I am not performing grief. I am not the warm, nurturing Imogen who makes tea and straightens tea towels and arranges everything just so. I am twelve years old, standing in a kitchen with hot chocolate burning my bare feet and broken ceramic everywhere. I heard a woman's voice. A girl went through a window. And I have been pretending I didn't hear it for my entire adult life — because if I heard it then it was real, and if it was real then it wasn't an accident, and if it wasn't an accident then everything I built to survive it is a house built on a sinkhole, and the sinkhole is opening now. Under my feet. Under my chair. Under my whole life.

Sophie. Sophie is ten. Safe at home with Greg. And in two years Sophie will be twelve and twelve is the age when girls have sleepovers and lean out of windows and fall, they fall, they fall and — no. Sophie is safe. I am a good mother. I protect my children. I protect other people's children. I teach Year Six and I watch them and I make sure they are safe because I couldn't keep Kiera safe. I was twelve and I heard a voice and I didn't say anything and I have never said anything and if I had — if I had said "I heard a woman's voice, it wasn't the TV" — would it have mattered? Would someone have looked? Would someone have seen the bruising and the landing distance and the scratches and said "this was not an accident"?

But I didn't. I said it was the TV. I said it was a dream. I built my entire life around not saying it and now Saskia has put a police report on a coffee table and read the word "bilaterally" and the wall is down and I am standing in the rubble of myself and I cannot breathe.

Neve's hand touches my arm. Light. Tentative. The touch of someone who is barely holding themselves together but is reaching out anyway. I take her hand. I hold it. We sit on the sofa and we hold hands and the documents are on the table and the photograph with the reflected figure is on the table and I am crying and crying and the crying won't stop because the voice won't stop, it has been playing inside me for twenty-five years and now it is in the room.

The room is silent. My sobbing has faded to breathing — uneven, ragged breathing, but breathing. The quiet after the collapse. The dust settling. I wipe my face with my sleeve. My eyes are swollen. My nose is running. I am not okay. For the first time in twenty-five years I am not saying I am okay.

"I think — I don't know, but I think it was a command," I say. The words come out small. "I think she told Kiera to do something. Go back downstairs. Be quiet. Something. And Kiera wouldn't. Not her. She was the kind of kid who'd look an adult in the eye and say 'that's not fair' and mean it."

The room is silent.

And then Cate, from the corner, says: "Can you remember — the words? Any words at all?"
