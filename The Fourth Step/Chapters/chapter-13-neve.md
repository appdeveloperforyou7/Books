# THE FOURTH STEP � Chapter 13: Neve

## The Hallway

The bathroom door is closed.

I stand in the hallway and look at it � the narrow white door with the chrome handle, the gap underneath where light shows, the hum of the extractor fan. Someone is in there. Imogen, maybe. She went that direction ten minutes ago with a toiletry bag and a cheerful "back in a mo" and the door closed and the fan started and it hasn't stopped.

I could wait. I've been waiting all morning � through breakfast, through Cate's questions, through Saskia's watchful silence. Waiting for the house to become a house again, not a container for something I can't name, something pressing outward the way gas fills whatever space it's given.

The pressure in my bladder is real. A simple need with a simple solution: go upstairs, use the bathroom, come back down. An adult woman goes upstairs in a house and uses a bathroom and comes back down. This is not remarkable. This is what bodies do.

I look at the staircase.

It's on the west wall. The same wall. The renovation kept them in place: same position, new materials. They lead to the same place.

The same place.

I have not been upstairs since I arrived. I took the downstairs bedroom. I angle my body away from the staircase when I pass it. I keep my eyes on the floor and the walls and everything except the thing at the top of the stairs that I don't look at. Liam would call this avoidance. I call it practical. The bathroom downstairs was adequate. I didn't need to go up.

The bathroom downstairs is closed. I need to go up.

My hands are at my sides. My fingers are tingling. The blood moving inward, towards my core. Fight or flight. My body preparing for a threat that doesn't exist � except my fingers are numb and my palms are damp and my heart is loud in my ears, which means my body disagrees with me about whether the threat exists.

I take a breath. Four in, seven hold, eight out. The technique is reliable. I've taught it to a hundred clients.

The extractor fan hums behind the bathroom door.

I turn right.

The first step is easy. A rise, a tread, a foot placed with intention. The wood is solid beneath my shoe. New timber. New finish. Nothing to recognise. The second step is the same � unfamiliar, modern, neutral. The third. My hand finds the bannister: pale wood, smooth, steel fittings, nothing like the bannister that was here before, which was dark and worn and warm to the touch. Which was � I don't know what it was. I don't remember the bannister. I don't remember the stairs.

The fourth step groans.

The new stairs don't creak � I know this, the renovation replaced them � but the sound is there, rising through my feet and into my shins and into my femurs and into my pelvis and into my spine and into the base of my skull and something at the base of my skull � something deep, something pre-verbal, something older than language and older than memory � lights up like a switch being thrown.

Not remembers. Recognises.

The way a conditioned response recognises its trigger. The way a wound recognises the shape of the instrument that made it. A particular frequency. A particular resonance. A sound my body has catalogued and filed under a heading my mind cannot read, kept in a drawer my mind cannot open.

I freeze.

My foot is on the step. Half my weight on it. My hand grips the bannister. My knuckles are white. The sound is still moving through me � reverberating through bones and joints and the fluid in my inner ear � and I know, with my tissue, not with my cognition, that I have heard this sound before. I have stood on this step before.

The knowledge is not cognitive. It is cellular.

The sound fades. The house settles. I am standing on the fourth step with elevated heart rate and damp palms � the physiological markers of acute stress triggered by a sound my body has encoded as significant.

I should go back down.

I keep going.

Step five. Step six. Step seven, eight, nine. The turn � I grip the newel post and swing around it and the angle is the same, the turn is in the same place, but the materials are wrong: pale wood where there was dark, steel where there was iron � and my body knows the geometry even as my eyes see the new surface. Step ten, eleven, twelve, thirteen, fourteen. The stairs open onto the hallway.

I stop at the top.

The upstairs hallway stretches ahead of me. I know it the way I know the reach of my own arms: in steps and breaths and the time it takes to walk from here to there. The time is too long. And also not long enough.

Grey walls. Pale grey, flat finish, modern and inoffensive. The walls were not grey before. They were cream. Warm cream that held the December light and made it golden. The grey absorbs light. The grey tells me nothing. But underneath � I can feel it. Not see it. Feel it. The cream is under the grey. The cream is under everything.

The floor. New blackbutt � blonde timber, laid in straight lines. The original was carpet. Blue carpet, low pile, synthetic. I walked on that carpet. My feet pressed into that carpet. My feet are pressing into the new blackbutt and feeling the old carpet underneath, the way ears hear an overtone, the way a string vibrates at a frequency no one plucked.

I walk.

The hallway is quiet. The others are downstairs. I am alone up here, walking towards a window I have not looked at since I was twelve years old. My body has not once in twenty-five years allowed me to stand in front of an upstairs window without turning away.

The dimensions are precise. The width � I could extend both arms and touch both walls. The distance to the window � twelve steps. I know because I am counting. Grounding technique. Be in the body. Track the data.

One. Two. Three.

The grey walls are smooth. No photographs. No art. But I can feel the cream underneath.

Four. Five. Six.

Doors on either side. Bedroom. Bedroom. The bathroom � the one I came to use. I should stop here. I should turn in and close the door and go back downstairs.

Seven. Eight. Nine.

My body is responding. Elevated heart rate. Constricted throat. The pressure behind my eyes � building, always building, something my brain has been trying to show me since my hands began to shake on the drive down.

Ten. Eleven.

I stop.

Twelve steps. I'm standing in front of the window. The rectangular weight of it pulls at my attention the way a symptom pulls at awareness: insistent, involuntary, demanding to be observed. My breath is shallow, my hands trembling, my jaw locked tight.

I look at the window.

## The Window

Smaller than it was. Fixed-pane. Sealed shut. White aluminium frame, double-glazed. It can't open. Someone replaced the original with this smaller, sealed, immovable thing. The glass reflects the hallway behind me � grey walls, blonde floor, a woman standing very still.

I look at the window and something shifts.

It starts at the edges. My vision narrows � a softening, a dissolving, the world beyond this point losing its coherence. The grey walls lose their edge. The blonde floor loses its colour. There is only the window. Only the glass. Only a rectangle of light that is changing � brightening, warming, shifting from the flat grey of a July morning to the gold of a December night.

The grey walls shimmer.

They are cream. The grey falls away like a curtain dropping and the cream is there � warm, soft, the colour of the hallway as it was, underneath the paint. The cream catches a light that is not in this room. Gold light. Amber light. The light of a summer evening when the sun is low.

Then it goes.

The cream, the gold, the warmth � it snaps back to grey, and I am standing in front of a sealed aluminium window in July, and my hands are shaking, and I almost saw something and it will not come.

I close my eyes. I reach for it the way you reach for a word on the tip of your tongue � the harder you reach, the further it retreats.

A fragment surfaces. Not an image. A sensation. A hand on a shoulder. Not my shoulder. Small. The grip hard enough to press fabric into skin. Then nothing. The fragment sinks.

Another. The smell. Metallic. Copper. The taste of blood on the tongue. Then nothing.

Another. A sound. Between a gasp and a thud. The sound that came after. Then nothing.

I am trying to assemble a room from its dust. Each piece I recover meets resistance � a force pushing it back down, a voice underneath my own that says *you were asleep, you were downstairs, you saw nothing.* The voice is calm. It is female. It has been saying this for twenty-five years and it says it now, at the exact moment the pieces try to meet, and the pieces scatter.

I open my eyes.

The hallway is grey. The window is sealed. I am thirty-seven. I am fine.

I'm on the floor.

The hallway is grey. The carpet is gone, the blue carpet, the cream walls, the warm air, the open window � gone. The hallway is grey and the floor is hard and I am on it. I am on my left side, knees drawn up. My hands are pressed flat against the boards, fingers splayed. My body is trembling � a deep vibration that starts in my core and radiates outward, teeth chattering, jaw clenched, muscles firing in waves I cannot control.

The grey walls are grey. The sealed window is sealed. The December air is July air and it is cold and it smells of new paint and cleaning products and nothing else.

My body knows something my mind has been taught to forget. The body has no agenda. The body has never been gaslit. It registers what happened and stores it in muscle and bone and nerve, uncorrupted by the story my mother wrote over it. The body tells the truth. The mind tells the story Elaine told it. Right now they are both speaking at once and the noise is unbearable.

"Neve. Neve, can you hear me?"

Saskia's voice. Above me. Saskia is kneeling beside me � I can see her knees, dark trousers pressed into the blonde floor, her hands on my shoulders. Her grip is firm. Anchoring. Her face is above mine, dark eyes wide, and underneath the concern there is something else � something sharp, something forensic, something that is cataloguing this event even as it happens.

"Neve. You're on the floor. You're in the hallway. You're safe. Can you hear me?"

I can hear her. The words arrive through a long tunnel. My ears are ringing. My vision is blurred. I blink. Saskia's face sharpens. She's watching me with the intensity I've seen in her eyes since we were twelve: the intensity that misses nothing, records everything.

"You were on the floor," she says. "Unresponsive for thirty seconds. What happened?"

I sit up. The room tilts. Saskia's hand stays on my shoulder. I press my palm flat against the floor. The blackbutt is cool and solid and real. Not carpet. Not blue carpet. Timber. Now. July. I am thirty-seven. I am fine.

"I felt dizzy." My voice sounds far away. "I haven't eaten enough. I skipped � I didn't eat much at breakfast."

Saskia watches me. Her hand doesn't move from my shoulder. Her eyes don't move from my face. She is reading me the way she reads witnesses, the way she reads briefs, the way she reads the distance between what people say and what their bodies tell.

"That was more than dizziness," she says.

"It was dizziness. I stood up too fast. I've been dehydrated � I didn't drink enough water this morning. It's simple. Orthostatic hypotension. Blood pressure drops, brain doesn't get enough oxygen, you faint. It's common. It happens."

I am speaking the way I speak to clients in crisis � providing a framework, a label, a box. This is what I do. I name things. Naming gives control. Dizziness. Dehydration. Good, clean words that keep the thing behind the locked door where it belongs.

Saskia doesn't respond. Her hand lifts from my shoulder. She stands. She holds out her hand.

"Can you walk?"

"Yes."

I take her hand. She pulls me up. My legs are steady � or steady enough. The trembling has moved inward, underground, like a symptom driven deep � managed, not resolved, still shaping the architecture of the body from below.

Saskia walks me downstairs. Her hand on my elbow. The fourth step groans under our combined weight. I don't freeze this time. I keep moving. Down. Down. Into the kitchen.

She sits me in a chair. Puts a glass of water in front of me. I drink. The water is cold and tasteless. It fills my mouth with something real. Something present. Something that belongs to this moment and not to any other.

"I'll be fine," I say. "I think I just need � a minute. Maybe some water. I'll be fine."

Saskia nods. She says, "Sit. Finish the water." Her voice is level. Her face is level. Her eyes are not level. Under the composure her jaw is tighter than it should be and her pulse is visible in the hollow of her throat, and I see it because I am trained to see it and because Saskia has never been able to fully hide what she sees.

She walks out of the kitchen. I hear her footsteps on the stairs � measured, precise, ascending. She is going to the hallway. She is going to look at the place where I fell. She is going to read the floor and the walls and the window the way she reads everything � for evidence, for pattern, for the thing that doesn't fit.

I sit in the kitchen. The glass of water is in my hand. Both hands. Knuckles white. The grip of someone holding on to the only fixed point in a room that won't stop moving.

The kettle clicks on in the other room. Someone is making tea. The house creaks, and through the kitchen window I can see the fallen pine still lying across the road end, the storm debris still scattered across the lawn � branches, leaves, the green waste of a winter front that passed through in the night and left us here, cut off, the five of us alone in this house with no way out and no one coming.

I should call Mum. I should call her and hear her voice and let her tell me what she always tells me � that I was asleep, that I was downstairs, that I didn't see anything. The words would settle me. The words have always settled me. A lullaby of negation, twenty-five years of it, enough to fill a childhood, enough to pave over whatever is cracking underneath.

I don't call her.

I sit in the kitchen. I think: *I saw something. In the hallway. A hand on a shoulder. A sound. I was � no. No, I was downstairs. I was always downstairs. Mum said I was asleep. I was asleep.* I repeat it like a mantra. But for the first time, the mantra doesn't work.
