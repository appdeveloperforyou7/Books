# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 23: Saskia

## The Confession

Elaine Cassidy is the most composed person in this room.

I note this the way I note the posture of a witness in the box. The set of the shoulders. The angle of the chin. The placement of the hands — folded in her lap, fingers interlocked, left thumb over right. A grounding gesture. Self-soothing. Also deliberate. The hands are visible. They say: I have nothing to hide.

She is dying. This is evident. The coat, the thinness, the careful way she lowered herself into the chair, the slight tremor in her left hand that she is controlling by pressing her thumb against her index finger. She has driven forty minutes from a hospice bed to sit in a room with five women who have been circling the truth for hours.

Most dying people want comfort. Elaine Cassidy wants control. She is running the last meeting of her life and she intends to chair it.

The room is arranged around her. Imogen on the sofa, tearful, hugging herself. Rowan in the corner, camera lowered, watching with the unblinking attention of someone who registers light and shadow before meaning. Aisling — not Cate, Aisling, Kiera Doyle's sister, the revelation still settling into the room like snow on a branch — weight accumulating, something about to snap — seated with the autopsy photographs in her lap, her face hard and still. Neve on the other end of the sofa, her body angled away from her mother, her hands pressed flat against her thighs. Me, by the mantelpiece, arms crossed, the police report on the coffee table between us like an exhibit entered into evidence.

And Elaine in the armchair. The best armchair. The one facing the door.

She chose that chair. She walked in, assessed the room in one sweep, and selected the position with the best sightline, the highest back, the widest view of every face and every exit. Four seconds. She has been doing this for sixty-eight years.

But first — before the chair, before the performance — she moved to the coffee table. Casually. The way someone adjusts a vase or straightens a magazine. Her hand reached for the manila envelope containing the police report. Her fingers touched the edge.

I was faster.

My hand closed over hers. Not hard. Not aggressive. Controlled. The way you stop a witness who is about to touch an exhibit.

"Leave it."

Her eyes met mine. For one second — exactly one, I timed it — the mask slipped. Not grief. Not confusion. Something colder. The rapid recalculation of a woman whose gambit has been read. Then her hand withdrew. She smiled — small, bemused, as if I had overreacted to an innocent gesture. She turned from the table and walked to the armchair and sat down.

The documents stayed where they were.

"I think it's time we were all honest about what happened that night," she says.

Aisling speaks first. Of course she does. She has been waiting for this moment longer than any of us.

"You know what happened that night."

Her voice is harder than Cate's voice. The accent has shifted — less London overlay, more Australian. The vowels are flatter. The journalist's measured cadence has been replaced by something rawer. Grief compressed into fury over two and a half decades, now directed at the woman who was in the room when her sister died.

"I know some of what happened," Elaine says. "I was there."

Three words. I was there. An admission dressed as a statement of fact. She was the adult in the house. We all knew she was there. But hearing her say it, in this room, in this house, is different from inferring it. An admission spoken aloud cannot be unspoken. She has just placed herself at the scene.

She is speaking to the room. Not to any one of us. To all of us. Her voice is clear and measured and unhurried. The voice of a woman who has had time to prepare. The voice of a woman who has rehearsed.

"I had a headache," she says. "I went upstairs to lie down. The girls were downstairs — watching a film, I think. Or playing games. Their laughter rose from the living room."

She pauses. The pause is calculated. One point five seconds. A beat for the image to land. The happy girls. The laughing. The innocence. She is setting a scene, and the scene is peace. The scene says: everything was fine until it wasn't. Everything was normal.

"Kiera came upstairs."

Another pause. This one longer. Her gaze moves to Aisling — held for two seconds, no more — then returns to the centre of the room.

"She came into the bedroom. She startled me. I'd been lying down and I got up quickly and — there was a struggle."

Not a fight. A struggle. The word choice is precise. A fight implies two willing participants with intent. A struggle implies bodies in proximity, contact without agency. A struggle is something that happens to you. Something you didn't choose. The passive construction of violence.

"Not a fight," she says, confirming my analysis in real time. "A startled reaction. She came up behind me and I turned and — she stumbled backward. The window was open. It was summer. December. The window was always open at night in summer. She stumbled and she — fell. The window was right there and she — it was an accident. A terrible, terrible accident."

Her voice breaks on "terrible."

The break is slight. A catch in the throat. Contained. Measured. Just enough fracture to signal emotion without losing composure. I have watched defendants manufacture this exact vocal break in the witness box. I have watched lawyers coach it. The technique is straightforward: think of something genuinely sad, let the emotion touch the vocal cords, then recover before it becomes uncontrolled. Elaine breaks and recovers in the space of a single breath. The precision is remarkable.

"It was an accident," she says again.

Repetition. A closing technique. Say it once to introduce. Say it twice to establish. The room hears "accident" the second time and it sounds less like a claim and more like a fact. She is not arguing. She is establishing.

She pauses. She looks at her hands. She looks at them for a long time — four seconds, five — and when she looks up, her eyes are wet.

Not overflowing. Not streaming. Wet. Glistening. The eyes of a woman holding back tears because holding back tears is what strong women do. The performance of restraint. Withholding emotion as a display of emotion. I have seen this in court more times than I can count. The jury always responds to it. The suppressed tear is more persuasive than the flowing one. It withholds, and the withholding reads as depth. It suggests a woman who would cry if she could but is choosing not to, for your sake, for the room's sake.

"I have lived with this every single day," she says. Her voice is lower now. Rougher. The voice of a woman who has been alone with something terrible. "I should have said something sooner. I should have told someone. I was afraid."

She pauses. Her gaze finds Neve.

"I was afraid of what it would do to you."

Neve doesn't move. She is sitting on the sofa and her face is still and her hands are flat on her thighs and she is watching her mother with an expression I cannot categorise. Not anger. Not grief. Not confusion. Something harder to name. The expression of a person watching a performance and recognising it as a performance but unable to look away because the performer is her mother.

"I told myself you were better off not knowing," Elaine continues. "I told myself you were young and you'd forget and that by the time you were old enough to remember, it wouldn't matter anymore. The police ruled it an accident. The case was closed. I thought — I thought if I said something, it would only hurt you. It would only reopen something that was already healing."

Healing. The word sits in the room like a stone on a table. Healing. As if Neve was healing. As if the panic attacks and the migraines and the chronic neck pain and the daily phone calls and the life arranged within a twenty-minute radius of her mother were healing. As if a gaslit mind can heal from a wound it has been prevented from identifying.

Elaine reaches for Neve's hand.

The movement is slow. Deliberate. A mother reaching for her daughter across the space between the armchair and the sofa. Her fingers extend. Her palm opens. She is asking for contact. Offering comfort. The gesture says: I am here. I am yours. We are together in this. Whatever happened, we face it together.

Neve's hand twitches. Her fingers move — towards her mother, then stop. Suspended in the air. The reach uncompleted. The hand hangs between them like a wire pulled taut.

"I was trying to protect you, sweetheart," Elaine says.

Sweetheart. The word again. The word she uses the way a surgeon uses a scalpel — with precision, with purpose, in exactly the right place. The diminutive that closes the distance, then weaponises it. She is not just speaking to Neve. She is speaking through Neve. She is using Neve's presence, Neve's hand, Neve's response, to make the room see a mother and a daughter, not a killer and a witness.

The room is quiet.

I watch her face. The tears are present but contained — two bright crescents along her lower lashes, not falling. Her hand is still extended towards Neve. Her voice has recovered its steadiness. She has delivered a coherent, emotionally modulated account of a traumatic event. The precision of a woman reading from a script she has been writing and rewriting for a quarter of a century.

"Stumbled," she says. Not "was pushed." I note the verb. "Startled reaction," not "I grabbed her." I note the frame. "The window was open." Circumstance, not agency. "Accident," said twice. Framing, not fact. Every word is chosen to minimise. Every word is technically defensible.

This is not a confession. This is a performance.

And a skilled one. If she were in the box, I would dismantle it piece by piece. The startled reaction: how does a startled reaction produce bilateral grip-pattern bruising on the shoulders? The stumble: how does a stumble send a body two metres from the building? The open window: why was a window in an upstairs hallway open wide enough for a child to fall through? The accident: how does an accident produce fingernail scratches on the frame consistent with a child gripping for purchase?

I would take each word apart in cross-examination. I would make the jury see the distance between "stumbled" and "was pushed." I would make them see that "accident" is a conclusion, not evidence. I would hold up the autopsy photographs and point to the bruising and say: show me how a fall produces this pattern. Show me how a child stumbling backward generates enough horizontal force to land two metres from the wall. Show me how an accident leaves fingernail marks in wood.

But I am not in court. I am in a living room in Sorrento at half past ten on a winter Saturday. And the witness is a dying woman in a too-large coat, reaching for her daughter's hand, her eyes bright with tears that may or may not be genuine.

I note my own response with the detachment I bring to everything. Something in my chest shifts — not sympathy, closer to the feeling you get watching a structure survive an earthquake. She is formidable. Even dying. Even diminished. The mind behind those steady brown eyes built a cover-up that held through police, coroners, and a family's grief for twenty-five years. She is sitting in a room full of evidence — the police report on the table, the autopsy photographs on Aisling's lap, Rowan's scratch photographs on someone's phone, five women who remember or have reason to suspect — and she is not flinching. She is controlling the room. She is controlling the narrative. She is, in her way, controlling us.

Neve's hand is still suspended between them.

Elaine's fingers wait. Patient. Open. The hand of a woman who has been preparing for this moment — not the confession, but the reaching. The gesture that transforms the narrative from "woman responsible for a child's death" to "mother who made a tragic mistake and spent her life shielding her daughter from it."

The transformation depends entirely on Neve's hand.

If Neve takes it, the narrative holds. Mother and daughter, united in grief, the accident behind them, the love between them intact, the truth managed and contained and rendered bearable by the bond between them. The room would see love, not manipulation. Protection, not control.

If Neve doesn't take it — the narrative fails. The extended hand becomes what it is: a strategic gesture designed to cement a performance.

I watch Neve's face. Her eyes are on her mother. Her jaw is set. Her chest rises and falls with slow, deliberate breaths. She is fighting something. The wall she built. The wall her mother helped her build. The wall that has held for twenty-five years and cracked this afternoon in the upstairs hallway when the memory came back — the full, panoramic memory of what she saw. Her face when she came downstairs after that — the colour hadn't returned. Her hands were shaking, pressing flat against her thighs the way she's pressing them now. Her eyes had the look of a person who has seen something they cannot unsee.

Her hand moves again. Towards her mother. The fingers extend.

Elaine's face softens. Genuine, I think. Or the best performance of genuine I have witnessed, and I have watched professional actors on the stand sell stories with less conviction. The softening says: I'm here, take my hand, we'll get through this the way we've gotten through everything.

Neve's fingertips touch her mother's.

Then she pulls her hand away.

The movement is sudden. Sharp. Her hand returns to her lap and presses flat against her thigh. Her fingers splay — the same splay I saw in the hallway when she collapsed. Her body is remembering. Her body is refusing.

Elaine's hand stays extended for one second. Two. Then it withdraws. Slowly. She places it back in her lap, interlocked with the other. Her face does not change. But something behind her eyes shifts — a door closing, a calculation recalculating — a strategy built on a single gesture, just denied.

The room holds its breath.

And something shifts in me too. Not analysis. Not observation. Something older. I was the first one into the hallway. I was twelve and I saw the window empty and I heard the sound from below and I have been carrying that sound for twenty-five years in a file I labelled "evidence" because calling it grief would have meant feeling it, and feeling it would have meant stopping, and stopping would have meant letting her get away with it. I built my entire life around that file. Law school. The bar. The cases. The forensic precision with which I disassemble every witness who sits across from me. All of it — all of it — was built on top of a twelve-year-old standing in a hallway looking at an empty window.

My jaw is tight. My jaw is always tight. But right now the tightness is not strategy. It is the only thing keeping my face together.

Neve looks at her mother and says: "That's not what happened."
