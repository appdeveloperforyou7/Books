# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 16: Neve

## What I Saw

The house is louder when it's empty. The silence has a texture — not absence of sound but the sound of a place listening to itself.

Saskia and Rowan left twenty minutes ago. From the window: two figures walking down the drive, turning left towards the coastal path, heads close, voices too low to hear, moving with the purpose of two people who have agreed — without saying so — that whatever they're discussing cannot happen inside these walls. Imogen is in her room. Another anxiety pill, she said, her voice thin, her hands trembling. "I just need to rest my brain for a bit, okay?" I said of course. Cate drove to the village. Supplies. She took her jacket and her handbag and her calm, measured smile and the house exhaled when the engine faded.

Four PM on a Saturday in July. Grey light through the windows, the colour of dishwater. The particular stillness of a house that has been full of noise and is full of noise no longer. The heating clicks. The pipes settle. The floorboards groan in places that have nothing to do with weight — an empty house settling into its own silence. Physics. Timber expanding and contracting. The sounds are not haunted. They are thermodynamic. I have explained this to clients a hundred times.

I'm on the sofa with a book. A literary thriller about a woman who discovers her husband is not who he says he is. Safe. Escapist. Unrelated to anything real.

I can't read.

The same paragraph four times. The words enter my eyes and slide through without sticking. The book could be anything. It could be blank pages. I am not reading. I am listening to Saskia's voice on repeat, a recording I can't turn off, playing the same seven words over and over:

*You were already in the hallway, Neve. You were looking at me. And your face wasn't surprised. It was already terrified.*

Already terrified. Not becoming terrified. Not discovering something terrible. Already. The fear was established. Already means I had seen something before Saskia arrived. Already means I was standing in that hallway for a reason and the reason was already inside me, already known, already processed, already — I was downstairs. I was on the couch. I was asleep. My mother says I slept through the whole holiday. I was twelve. I was a child. Children sleep. Children fall asleep in cars and on couches and I was asleep, I was asleep, I was —

I close the book.

I stand up.

The movement is sudden. Unconsidered. The book slides to the floor. I don't pick it up. I'm standing in the living room looking at the doorway that leads to the hallway that leads to the staircase.

I have been in this house nearly twenty-four hours and I have been upstairs once. Yesterday. The hallway. The window. The flash — not a memory, I told Saskia, dizziness, low blood sugar — and then I was on the floor and she was beside me and I said I was fine because I am always fine because fine is the wall I built between myself and everything I cannot feel.

I am not fine.

The thought arrives without permission. Clear and simple and terrible. I am not fine. I have not been fine since I was twelve years old.

The wall is cracking. I can feel the hairline fractures running through it — the same kind of fracture that split my coffee mug in the garden, invisible until the pressure became too much and the ceramic broke in my hands.

I walk towards the hallway.

My feet know the way. They knew the brick under the new concrete. They knew the bluestone step. They knew the pitch of the floorboards. My body has been navigating this house by a map my mind refuses to read, and now my feet are moving and my mind is not stopping them and the staircase is rising ahead of me and I can feel it — the pull, the gravity, the weight of the upper floor drawing me upward.

I put my hand on the newel post. The wood is smooth — renovated, sanded, sealed. But the height is the same. The angle is the same. The way my hand fits around it is the same. My palm knows the curve of this post the way my palm knows the shape of my daughter's hand.

I climb.

First step. Second. Third.

The fourth step groans.

That sound. That specific, weighted, resonant groan. My pulse kicks hard — throat, wrists, behind my eyes. The somatic response is immediate and disproportionate. The body keeps its own records.

I do not stop.

Fifth step. Sixth. Half-landing. Three more. The upper hallway opens ahead.

Grey walls. New carpet. Overhead light off. The only illumination comes from the window at the end — fixed-pane, sealed shut, white aluminium frame. The window that used to be large and open, letting in salt air and moonlight and the warm breath of a December night.

I stand at the top of the stairs.

The hallway stretches ahead. Five metres. The grey walls are not the colour they were. They were cream. Cream with a dark wood dado rail. The carpet was not grey low-pile. It was blue. Deep blue, the blue of summer twilight, the blue of — I know this. I know the colour of the carpet. I know the colour of the walls. I know this hallway and I have known it since I was twelve years old and I have been pretending not to know for twenty-five years.

I walk.

The floorboards groan softly. The ceiling is the same height. The doors are in the same positions — bathroom on the left, two bedrooms on the right. My feet know the exact number of steps to the window. Twelve. I am counting without deciding to count. I reach the window on twelve.

Here.

The window.

Smaller now. Sealed. Fixed-pane. The aluminium frame reflects grey afternoon light. Someone made it smaller. Made it fixed. Made it so it cannot open. As if the window itself was a danger that needed to be contained.

I put my hand on the frame.

The new frame sits on the old woodwork. Aluminium edge, and underneath, the original timber. The wood is still here. Under the paint, under the renovation, the original frame is still here. My fingers trace the edge. And on the wood, under the overhang of the aluminium — scratches. Thin lines. Parallel. I don't need to see them. Rowan showed the photographs. Five lines. Downward. Fingernails.

I press my palm flat against the old wood.

The cold arrives first.

Not the July cold. Not the hallway cold. A specific cold — the cold of a December night felt through the soles of bare feet on timber. It starts in my fingertips where they press the frame and moves inward, up through my palms, into my wrists, spreading along the bones of my forearms like frost on glass. My fingers go white against the wood. The cold reaches my shoulders and my chest contracts and the air I breathe in is suddenly, impossibly, warm.

The smell arrives second.

Copper. Sharp and metallic and close, as if a coin has been pressed to my tongue. Underneath it: chamomile and eucalyptus, steeping somewhere below, the tea that meant safety, the tea that meant sleep, the tea that made the edges soft. And salt — salt air moving through an open window, through an open hallway, through the gap where the glass should be but isn't because the casement is wide open and the night is pouring through.

My vision narrows.

The periphery dims. Grey walls darken. The carpet deepens — grey giving way to blue, the low pile growing thick and soft and the blue of summer twilight. The sealed fixed-pane dissolves and the window is large, wide, open, and the air coming through is December-warm and it carries salt and eucalyptus and beneath both, something metallic — something my body knows the way my body knows the pitch of the fourth step.

The hallway is warm.

The hallway is warm and I am twelve.

I came up. I was on the couch. I was supposed to be asleep. But I wasn't asleep and something — a voice, sharp, female — and I climbed the stairs and the fourth step groaned and I am standing at the top.

Cream walls. Blue carpet. The window open. Warm air. Moonlight. Silver-blue. Not gold, not amber — this time it is silver-blue, and the last time it was gold, and I register the difference the way I register everything now: without flinching. Memory reconstructs. Each recall builds the scene anew. The colour of the light shifts because the mind is not a camera. This does not mean the memory is false. It means the memory is alive.

And I see what I saw before — what I saw this morning, what I have been carrying behind the wall for twenty-five years. The two figures. The arm. The push. The small hands reaching for the frame. The grip failing. The window emptying. I see it again and this time I don't collapse, this time I don't close my eyes, this time I watch because the wall is down and the wall is not coming back and I am standing in the hallway and I am twelve and I am thirty-seven and I am watching.

The tall figure turns.

Her face —

I know this face. The first face I ever knew. The face that leaned over my cot. The face that taught me to hold a knife and fold a shirt and answer a question without answering it.

Mum.

She crosses the hallway. She kneels. Her hands are on my face. Her palms are warm. Her voice is low and calm.

She says: "You didn't see anything."

She says: "You were asleep."

She says: "You were downstairs."

She says it and says it and the words press into me, overwrite the memory as it forms, replace it in real time. Her thumbs stroke my cheekbones. She holds my face. She holds the story. She holds both.

I open my eyes.

I am on the floor of the hallway.

Not trembling. Still. Very still. My back is against the wall under the window. My legs are extended. My palms are flat on the carpet — the grey carpet, the carpet that was blue. The floor is solid. The wall is solid. The window is above me and I can feel the frame — the old wood, the original wood, the wood her fingers touched, the wood my mother's hand touched when she pushed —

My palms are flat on the carpet. Steady. Not trembling.

The stillness of a body after the diagnosis lands. Not crying yet. Not moving. The moment before the response pattern forms. The world has rearranged itself and nothing is where it was and the shift is permanent.

I know what I saw.

The thought is the quietest thought I have ever had. Not loud. Not emotional. It sits in the centre of my mind with the weight of a diagnosis confirmed — irreversible, clarifying, the kind of certainty that ends all speculation. Not "I think." Not "maybe." Not "I'm confused." I know. The way I know my own name. The way I know my daughter's face. The way I know the sound of the fourth step.

My mother pushed Kiera.

My mother's hand on Kiera's shoulder. My mother's arm extending. My mother turning and seeing me and crossing the hallway in three steps and kneeling and holding my face and telling me I didn't see anything. The woman who called the ambulance. The woman who held me while I cried. The woman who told the police and the doctors and me, over and over, for twenty-five years, that I was asleep.

She didn't erase the memory. She overwrote it. She held my face and spoke the new version into me — every nightmare, every flash, every time I said *I think I saw—* and she said *You couldn't have. You were on the couch.* Another layer of suppression laid over the original. A wall that was never mine, that she built and made me think was mine. She moved us. New school. Cut contact with anyone who might have contradicted her. And then the daily calls — *I'm worried about you, Neve. You seem confused* — checking that the wall was holding. The warmth that made it impossible to identify. Because how could someone who loves you this much be lying? The love is real. The gaslighting is real. Both are true. That is what makes it unforgivable.

My body has known for twenty-five years. The neck that wouldn't unclench. The migraines that arrived every time the forgetting threatened to crack. The way I couldn't look at open windows. The way I called her every day and said *I'm fine* and she said *Good* and the call was never about how I was — it was about her checking the wall.

The question isn't what happened anymore. The question is: my mother killed Kiera, and she made me forget.

My phone rings.

Sharp. Sudden. A blade through the quiet of the hallway. The signal is back — intermittent, one bar, enough for a call but not enough for certainty. I pull it from my pocket and the screen is bright in the dim and my hand knows the shape of this intrusion before my eyes read the name.

Mum.

I look at the name. The phone rings and rings. Seven seconds. Eight. Nine. I don't answer. I watch her name pulse on the screen. Mum. Elaine. The woman who knelt in front of me and rewrote my mind. The woman who told me not to come here. *You don't have to do this, sweetheart. Sometimes it's better to leave things where they are.* Reverse psychology, or a genuine attempt to protect me, or both. She loves me and she did this to me and both of those things are true.

The phone stops.

The screen goes dark.

Then it lights again. A text.

*Call me, sweetheart. We need to talk about this weekend.*

I stare at the text.

The words are ordinary. Maternal. Concerned. But I am reading them with new eyes. With the eyes of the girl who stood at the top of the stairs and watched her mother push her friend through a window. *We need to talk about this weekend.* Not "How is it going?" Not "Are you having a nice time?" We need to manage it. We need to control what happens next, the way we have controlled everything.

She knew about this weekend. The invitation came from Saskia's address but who sent it, really? Who knew about this house, these women, this weekend? Who has been feeding information — a crime scene photograph under a door, an anonymous tip — who has been pulling strings from a bed in a palliative care unit with the same precision she used to build the cover-up?

She knew I would come back. She knew the hallway was the same. She knew the frame was underneath, the scratches still there. She knew what would happen when I stood in this spot and put my hand on this wood. She knew the memory would come back. She knew I would remember. And she let it happen — or she needed it to happen, or she couldn't stop it anymore, or she wanted to be here when it did, to hold my face one more time and tell me what I saw. The text glows on the screen. *Call me, sweetheart.* Love and control. The same word. It has always been the same word.

I stare at the text. *You knew. You knew about this weekend. You knew I'd remember.* I put the phone face-down on the floor and the screen light leaks around the edges and the dark swallows it and I sit in the dark with the weight of everything she did and everything she made me not see pressing against my ribs from the inside.

I was never fine.
