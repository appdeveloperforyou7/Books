# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 20: Saskia

## The Confrontation

The house is quiet after Rowan's revelation. Imogen is in the kitchen, running water, filling silence. Neve is upstairs — alone, I note, which is either courage or compulsion. Rowan is sitting on the back deck with her camera, not photographing, just holding it. And Cate is in the living room with a book she hasn't turned a page of in twenty minutes.

It's nine PM. We have been in this house for twenty-seven hours. In twenty-seven hours I have accumulated more inconsistencies in Cate Morrow's story than I have seen in sworn affidavits from pathological liars. The inconsistencies are small. Microscopic. The kind of detail that wouldn't register in normal conversation, in normal life, between normal people. But I am not normal. I have never been normal. I am a criminal defence barrister who has spent her adult life reading deception, and Cate Morrow is the most rehearsed witness I have ever encountered.

I've been watching her all day. Since she walked through the door yesterday morning with her calibrated smile and rehearsed questions and eyes that see too much and show too little. I build files in my head the way other people breathe — continuously, without deciding to — and the file on Cate Morrow has been growing since the moment she said hello.

The file reads like this:

Cate Morrow arrived at nine-fifteen Saturday morning. Claims to have been two years behind us at Sorrento Primary. Claims to have moved to London at fifteen. Claims to have lost touch. She asks the questions a journalist asks — open-ended, apparently casual, designed to elicit disclosure without the subject noticing. She performs warmth. Performs interest. Performs the role of a woman reconnecting with old acquaintances.

The performance is good. It is very good. I have seen worse performances from witnesses who were coached by senior counsel.

But performances have seams. And I am very good at seams.

I close my laptop. I stand. I walk to the living room doorway.

Cate looks up from her book. The smile arrives before the eye contact — a fractional error. Real smiles synchronize with attention. Performed smiles precede it.

"Saskia. Everything okay?"

"We're going to have a conversation."

"Of course." She closes the book. Sets it on the arm of the chair. Her movements are deliberate, unhurried, the movements of someone who has trained themselves not to flinch. "What about?"

I sit in the chair across from her. I don't take the sofa — the sofa is too soft, too casual, it puts you at a physical disadvantage. The chair is upright, hard-seated, at the same height as hers. Level ground.

"You," I say.

"Me?" The smile again. Warm, open, curious. The right amount of surprise. "What about me?"

"Your backstory. I've been auditing it."

"I'm an open book." She gestures at the closed novel on the chair arm. A small joke. A deflection. I note it.

"Your mother's name," I say.

The question lands. I watch her face. Not her eyes — her eyes will be controlled. I watch the muscles around her mouth, the micro-contractions of the orbicularis oris, the tell-tale tension that precedes a performed answer.

"Margaret," she says. "Margaret Morrow. She passed away four years ago."

A dead mother. Convenient. Dead people don't corroborate. In my experience, witnesses who claim deceased family members as corroboration for their identity are either telling the truth or have constructed their backstory with care. The latter is more common than the former.

"What was her maiden name?"

A pause. Two-tenths of a second. Within normal range but at the upper limit — the cognitive load of retrieving a fabricated detail takes fractionally longer than a real one. I've timed hundreds of these pauses in cross-examination. Two-tenths is the edge. She's at the edge.

"Winters. Margaret Winters. She was from Adelaide."

"Which school did you attend?"

"Sorrento Primary. Years Three through Six. Then we moved, and I went to a school in Rosebud before — before London."

"Which school in Rosebud?"

"Rosebud Secondary College."

"Rosebud Secondary College closed in 2003. It merged with Rosebud High to form Rosebud Secondary College — the current school — in 2004. If you left for London at fifteen, you would have been enrolled there in 2002 or 2003. It was still Rosebud High."

She doesn't blink. But her left hand — the one resting on the arm of the chair — shifts. A small adjustment. A grounding movement. I've seen witnesses do the same thing when they realise opposing counsel has a document they weren't expecting.

"You're right. Rosebud High. I always forget — they changed the name, didn't they? It's confusing."

It's also the kind of error a real former student wouldn't make — the name of your school is embedded in autobiographical memory, not subject to this kind of confusion. But she's recovering well. The self-deprecating laugh, the acknowledgment of error, the redirect. She's been trained for this, or she's trained herself. Either way, the performance is sophisticated.

"It is," I say. "Why did your family move to London?"

"My father's work. He was — he was in finance. Transferred to the London office."

"What firm?"

Another pause. This one is longer — four-tenths of a second. I'm counting. I will always be counting.

"A small firm. It doesn't exist anymore. Merged, actually — there was a lot of consolidation in the early 2000s."

A dead mother. A defunct firm. A maiden name from Adelaide — three hours' flight away, difficult to verify. Every detail in Cate Morrow's backstory is structured like a coached witness statement: specific enough to sound real, unverifiable enough to withstand scrutiny. The construction is thorough. But thoroughness has a weakness. Real biographies have gaps. Cate's has none. Every detail precisely positioned to resist challenge. That precision is itself the evidence.

I lean forward. Not much. Two inches. Enough to change the spatial dynamic, to move from conversational distance to something closer to interrogation.

"You said yesterday you were a couple of years behind us at school. You would have been nine or ten when we were twelve. When THE FOURTH STEP happened."

"I was nine. I remember hearing about it. It was all over the — everyone was talking about it."

"What do you remember hearing?"

"That a girl fell. From a window. It was — it was terrible. It was one of those things that — that marks a community."

The sentence is too complete. Too well-constructed. A real memory of a real overheard conversation would be fragmentary — "I remember people talking about it," "something about a girl," "I didn't really understand." Cate's version has narrative shape. It has been composed.

"Who told you about it?"

"I don't remember specifically. It was just — everywhere. You know how small towns are."

"I do. But you were nine. You were at a different school — you said you were at Sorrento Primary, but two years behind us. You would have been in Year Three or Year Four. THE FOURTH STEP happened in December. End of the school year. Would a nine-year-old in Year Three have been talking about a twelve-year-old in Year Six?"

"Kids talk."

"They do. But you said you remembered the five of us being like a little gang. Everyone knew the five girls from Sorrento Primary. Except you said five. There were six of us. I corrected you. You moved past it very quickly."

Something shifts in her face. Not the expression — the expression is still controlled, still open, still warm. But underneath, the eyes change. A flicker — there and gone — the look of a witness recalculating mid-testimony.

"I was nine, Saskia. I'm sure I got some details wrong."

"You did. You've been getting details wrong all weekend. You asked about the Doyles yesterday — specifically, where they went after Sorrento, what Kiera's parents do now, whether anyone has seen the grave. Those are not the questions of someone who overheard a childhood tragedy and vaguely remembers it. Those are the questions of someone conducting an investigation."

The smile doesn't waver. But her breathing has changed. Shallow. Measured. The breathing of a rehearsed witness who knows the next question is coming.

"I ask questions. It's habit. I'm sorry if it feels like —"

"You knew whose house this was before Imogen told you. You said 'oh, of course' when she mentioned it belonged to Neve's family. But the micro-expression before the 'oh, of course' was grief. Not surprise. Grief. You already knew."

Silence.

The house is quiet. Wind around the eaves. The distant sound of the sea. Imogen's water still running in the kitchen. Rowan's footsteps on the deck outside, slow, aimless, the pacing of someone who is somewhere else.

I let the catalogue sit. The pattern is the argument — no single discrepancy proves deception, but the accumulation does.

"And there's the knowledge," I say. "You know things you shouldn't. Yesterday, when Imogen described the kitchen — yellow tiles, calendar with the lighthouse — you reacted. Not with surprise. With recognition. A woman who was two years behind us, who never visited this house, should not have recognised that kitchen. But you did. Your pupils dilated. Recognition, not novelty."

"Saskia —"

"Your accent slips. Under stress. You've been in London, supposedly, since you were fifteen. Twenty-two years. Your accent should be fully anglicised, or at minimum a genuine hybrid. Instead it's a performance — a London overlay on an Australian foundation. And the foundation comes through. Your vowel sounds sharpen when you're pressed. 'Grave' — you said 'grave' yesterday, and the vowel was Australian. Not London. Not even close."

I am watching her. I have been watching her for two days. I have catalogued every pause, every micro-expression, every question, every over-specific answer. I have built a file and the file says: this woman is not who she says she is. This woman is performing an identity. This woman knows things about Kiera Doyle and this house and this group that a casual acquaintance from two years behind us at school should not know.

"I don't believe you are who you say you are," I say.

The words land between us. Clean. Precise. The way words land in a courtroom when the prosecution says "no further questions" and the witness realises the cross-examination is over and they have not survived it.

Cate's face does not change. A wrongly accused person would react — shock, indignation, hurt. The absence of reaction is the reaction. She is managing her face instead of feeling through it.

"I'm not sure I follow," she says.

"I want a name. A real one."

"I've been telling you the truth."

"You've been telling me a story. The truth and a story are different things."

"You don't know what I've been telling you."

"I know you weren't nine when this happened. I know you never lived in London. I know you walked into this house and recognised a kitchen you've never been in."

"You know nothing about what I recognise."

"I know your name."

She looks at me. Really looks — not the performed eye contact of the last two days, but something rawer. An assessment behind the mask. Not "is she a threat" but "how much does she know."

I know more than she thinks.

I've spent twenty-five years investigating Kiera's death. I obtained the police report when I was nineteen. I filed freedom-of-information requests for the autopsy summary. I tracked the Doyles — the surviving Doyles — through public records, electoral rolls, social media. I know Kiera had a younger sister. I know the sister's name. Aisling. It means dream, or vision, in Irish. I've seen the name on bylines — A.M. Doyle, investigative journalist, Melbourne. I've read her articles. She writes about institutional failures, cover-ups, cold cases. She writes like someone with a personal stake in truth.

And I know something else. Something I haven't told anyone. Something I've been carrying for twelve months, building in my inbox one encrypted message at a time. A correspondence that started with a single email — brief, professional, citing the bruising pattern from Kiera's autopsy, asking if I had additional information. A correspondence I entered because I am a fool for information, because I have never met a lead I didn't follow, because this person knew things about the case that weren't in any public record and I needed to know how.

Twelve months of emails. Twelve months of sharing what I knew. Twelve months of building a relationship with someone who used the name A.M. Doyle, who never once told me their real name, who I trusted because they had information and I am constitutionally incapable of trusting no one when information is on the table.

I handed twenty-five years of evidence to a stranger. And now I'm sitting across from her.

Twelve months of trust I do not give easily, handed to a woman who invented a name to earn it. The anger is there — under the analysis, under the courtroom calm, a hot thread I have not yet pulled. I will pull it later. Right now the truth matters more than the betrayal.

"I've been corresponding with a journalist," I say.

Cate's face does not move. But her body does. A stillness — not relaxation but rigidity. The stillness of a suspect who has just been told the interview is being recorded.

"For the past year," I continue. "Someone who's been investigating Kiera's death. Someone who contacted me through an encrypted email service with information about the case. The bruising pattern. The landing distance. Details that aren't in the publicly available records. Details that required access to autopsy files, police reports, forensic analysis."

I pause. I let the silence do what silence does in a courtroom — fill the space, press against the witness, give the truth room to surface.

"This person used the name A.M. Doyle."

I watch her face. I watch every muscle, every micro-movement, every flicker of the eyelids and twitch of the jaw and contraction of the pupils. I watch the way you watch a witness who is about to break.

"A.M. Doyle," I repeat. "Would you like to tell me who that is?"

Cate's face goes completely still. Not the stillness of composure, not the stillness of performance. A deeper stillness. The stillness of something that has been running for twenty-five years and has just heard the starting pistol it has been dreading.
