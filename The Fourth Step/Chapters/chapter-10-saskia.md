# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 10: Saskia

## Observation

I am awake at six. I don't set an alarm. I don't need one. My body wakes at six the way a court convenes at ten — precisely, without enthusiasm, because the schedule requires it.

The house is quiet. I lie in bed for four minutes and listen. No footsteps. No voices. The power came back sometime after four — the heating clicked on and woke me, the sudden hum of the system restarting after the storm. The wind has died since last night. The sea is audible, faint, rhythmic, approximately once every four seconds. Normal wave interval for this section of the coast in winter. I checked the road on my phone at five. No signal. The cell tower is down. The fallen tree at the end of the street was confirmed by visual inspection from the front window. We are isolated — no road access, no phone signal, no internet. The house has landline capability but the line is dead. I checked.

I dress. Dark trousers, white shirt, grey cardigan. I don't own weekend clothes. I don't see the purpose of signalling relaxation through fabric.

The kitchen is empty. I make coffee — the capsule machine produces an acceptable approximation — thin crema, temperature three degrees below optimal. I note this without irritation. Facts are not complaints. Facts are data.

I sit at the kitchen table with my laptop. The screen displays my notes from last night: a timeline of the evening, observations of each woman's behaviour, a list of questions I intend to ask. I have fourteen questions. I've ranked them by priority. The first three are about the night Kiera died. The last eleven are about what each woman has done with the twenty-five years since.

Neve is the first downstairs. Eight-seventeen. She's wearing trackpants and a jumper and thick socks and she's carrying a white mug from the cupboard. She looks like she hasn't slept. The skin under her eyes is discoloured — bilateral, purplish, consistent with venous pooling from poor sleep. Her shoulders are held at an angle that suggests chronic tension in the trapezius. She fills the mug from the capsule machine, wraps both hands around it, and walks to the back door without making eye contact.

"Morning," I say.

"Morning." She doesn't turn. "I'm going to sit in the garden for a bit."

She slides the door open and steps outside. I watch her walk to the centre of the lawn and stop. She stands there, holding her mug, looking at the house. Her face is turned up toward the upper storey. Toward the window at the end of the upstairs hallway. She's looking at it the way you look at something you can't stop looking at — not with curiosity, but with the fixed attention of a juror who has noticed something the prosecution didn't intend to show.

Then she looks away. Quickly. Deliberately. The way you look away from something that hurts.

Neve performs calm. She performs it the way a witness performs credibility — with effort, with control, with the minute attention to detail that reveals the performance itself. Her hands shake. Her breathing is shallow. She won't go upstairs. She hasn't been upstairs once since she arrived. She angles her body away from the staircase when she passes it. She took the ground-floor bedroom and justified it with "I like the morning light," which is a reason that explains the choice without explaining it.

Her avoidance is specific. It's not the house she avoids — she moves through the ground floor with ease. It's not the past she avoids — she references it cautiously, testing the edges. It's the upstairs. Specifically the upstairs hallway. Specifically the window. She knows something is up there the way a body knows a wound — not with the mind, but with the tissue.

Imogen appears at eight-forty-two. She's wearing a floral dressing gown and she's already talking.

"Good morning! Did anyone sleep? I slept terribly, the mattress is so firm, isn't it? Not that I'm complaining — it's a lovely house, such a lovely house. I'll make breakfast. Does everyone want breakfast? I make a very good eggs Benedict if there's hollandaise — there probably isn't hollandaise, is there? Rental properties never have hollandaise. I'll check."

She moves through the kitchen with efficiency, opening cupboards, assessing supplies, her hands in constant motion. The behaviour is consistent with what the literature calls undoing: ritualised action aimed at symbolically reversing an event.

Imogen performs cheer. She performs it the way Neve performs calm — thoroughly, with the exhausting commitment of someone who has been rehearsing for twenty-five years. But there's a gap. Imogen doesn't look at the back of the house. Her gaze slides across the sliding door, the deck, the garden, and she turns away. Every time.

She'll face the front. She'll face the side. She'll face the kitchen and the hallway and the living room. But the back of the house, where the view includes the window above the door — no. She keeps her back to it. She positions herself at the counter with her shoulders to the sliding door, and she moves through the space like someone navigating a room with a missing floor — carefully, around the gap, never looking down.

I note these observations in my file. Neve: upstairs avoidance, somatic distress at upper window, performative calm, tremor, restricted breathing. Imogen: back-of-house avoidance, performative cheer, constant manual activity, possible undoing. One avoids height. One avoids sightline. Both avoid the same thing.

Rowan is not downstairs.

I notice this the way I notice everything — as a data point. Four women in the house. Three visible. One absent. Rowan's shoes — canvas sneakers, mud-caked, size eight — are still by the front door. She's here. She's not in the kitchen.

I wait until Imogen is absorbed in breakfast and Neve is in the garden. Then I go upstairs.

The stairs are new. Fourteen steps, west wall. I don't count them. I counted them yesterday. The renovation replaced the stair structure — new timber, new risers, new treads. But the fourth step still creaks. A low, resonant groan. The new timber has already settled into the old pitch. The house remembers. But the upstairs hallway has the same dimensions. Five metres. One metre thirty-eight wide. Two metres sixty-four high. The grey carpet is the same grey carpet. The window at the end is the same sealed, fixed-pane, aluminium-framed window.

Rowan is standing in front of it.

She's holding her camera to her eye. Not a phone — a proper camera, black body, large lens, the tool of someone who sees the world in frames and light values. She's photographing the window frame — not the glass, not the view, but the edge where the aluminium meets the wall, the junction of new and old.

She doesn't hear me. Or she does and doesn't acknowledge it. With Rowan, it's impossible to tell. She exists in a different register — sensory, not social. The camera is her aperture on the world — everything passes through it, adjusted, metered, framed before it reaches her.

"Rowan."

She lowers the camera. Turns. Her face is the same face it was at twelve — angular, pale, framed by dark hair that's longer now but still Kiera's style. The silver ring is on her right hand. Kiera's ring. She took it at the funeral. I saw her. I didn't say anything. I've never said anything.

"Saskia."

"What are you photographing?"

She looks at the camera, then at the window, then back at me. The sequence takes three seconds. She's processing the question through the lens of her own experience — translating my words into her sensory vocabulary before answering.

"The frame," she says. "I found something last night."

She sits on the floor — the hallway carpet, cross-legged, the camera in her lap. She scrolls through the images on the screen. Then she turns the camera toward me.

The photograph is well-composed. Flash-lit, flat, clinical — the kind of light that eliminates shadow and reveals surface. The image shows the window frame: the white aluminium edge, the rubber seal, and behind it, visible in the gap between new frame and old wall, the original timber. Dark, rough, old.

And on the timber: scratches.

Five parallel lines. Evenly spaced. Running downward, top right to bottom left. The width of a child's hand. The depth of fingernails in soft wood.

My pulse accelerates. Sixty-five to ninety in under two seconds. Adrenaline, sharp and autonomic — the body's response to evidence it recognises before the mind does. My breathing remains even. My face remains still. My expression does not change.

Twenty-four years of waiting. Police reports. Autopsy summaries. Freedom-of-information requests. Dead-end conversations with retired officers who told me to let it go. Twenty-four years of knowing Kiera was pushed and being unable to prove it. And here, in a photograph on a camera screen, held by a woman sitting on the carpet of a renovated hallway, is the proof. Fingernail scratches. Grip marks. Someone held on. Someone was pulled.

I do not reach for the camera. I do not lean forward. I do not change my posture. I lower myself to the carpet, cross-legged, facing her, maintaining eye contact. My pulse is at ninety beats per minute. My face is an unreadable document.

"When did you take this?" I ask.

"Last night. Two, maybe three in the morning. I came upstairs. I couldn't sleep. I photographed the hallway and I saw them."

"Show me the original image."

She scrolls. Shows me. The full hallway, flash-lit. The window at the end. The walls. The carpet. And in the corner of the frame, where the aluminium meets the wall, the scratches. Undeniable. Physical. Present.

"Have you shown anyone else?"

"No."

I reach into my pocket and take out my phone. I open the camera app. "Hold it up again. I want to photograph it myself."

She holds the camera screen toward me. I photograph the screen — her photograph of the scratches. Then I stand and walk to the window frame. I crouch. I angle my phone into the gap between the aluminium and the wall. The light is poor. I use the flash. Three photographs from different angles. The images are adequate. Not high resolution — phone camera, LED flash, suboptimal conditions — but legible. The scratches are visible. They can be verified against Rowan's higher-quality images.

I stand. I pocket the phone.

"Rowan. Fourteen December, 1999. What do you remember?"

She's sitting on the carpet with her camera in her lap and her knees drawn up and she looks twelve. The way she looked at twelve. The way all of us looked at twelve — small, young, unprepared — evidence before examination, a scene before the first question was asked.

"I remember the cold," she says.

Her voice is different here. Not the dreamy, sensory voice she uses in company. Quieter. More precise. As if the hallway itself demands a different register.

"I remember the light. Gold light. December light. It filled the hallway like water."

She looks at the window.

"I remember two shapes. At the end of the hallway. One was small. One was tall. The tall one moved. The small one —"

She stops.

"The small one what?" I ask.

"The small one went through the window."

The word she uses is went. Not fell. Not jumped. Went. The same word she used at twelve, when the police asked her what she saw and she said "she went through the window" and they wrote it down as "she fell from the window" because went was not a word that fit their narrative. I read the transcript. I know what they changed.

"Went," I say.

"Went. Like — like the window was a door. Like she went through it on purpose. But she didn't. She didn't go. She was — the tall one moved and then she went. The tall one moved first."

"The tall one moved," I repeat. I'm recording this. In my mind, with the precision of a court transcript, word for word. "Rowan. Was the tall one an adult or a child?"

She closes her eyes. I watch her face. The processing is visible — her eyelids flickering, her jaw tightening, her fingers curling against the camera body. She's in the hallway. Not this hallway. The other one. The one from twenty-five years ago.

"Tall," she says. "Tall like an adult. I couldn't see — it was dark. The shapes were dark. But the tall one was tall the way a grown-up is tall. The small one was small the way we were small."

"Did you see a face?"

"No. Just shapes. Just the shapes and the movement and then —"

"Then?"

"Then the window was empty."

I have enough. Scratches. Testimony. A witness who saw two figures and one of them was adult-sized and the adult moved and the child went through the window. Twenty-four years of waiting and now I have this.

I stand. I put my phone in my pocket. Three photographs of the frame, one of Rowan's camera screen. Evidence. Admissible? No. A coronial investigation doesn't require the same evidentiary threshold as a criminal trial. Relevant? Yes.

"I need to go downstairs," I say. "Don't show those photographs to anyone else. Not yet. Can you do that?"

Rowan nods. She lifts the camera back to her eye. She photographs the window again. She photographs the light. She photographs the space where the shapes stood. She is doing what she has always done — recording, preserving, holding moments still. The camera is her memory. The camera holds what her brain cannot sequence.

I go downstairs.

The kitchen is occupied. Imogen is at the stove — eggs now, scrambling, the spatula moving in quick circles. And at the table, with a cup of coffee and a smile, is a woman I don't recognise.

Brown-haired, average height, pleasant-faced. Jeans, navy jumper. Seated in Neve's chair from last night — angled toward the room, back to the wall, eyes on the doorway. She chose the position I would choose. I note this.

"Saskia," Imogen says from the stove. "This is Cate. Cate Morrow. She arrived this morning — she was a couple of years behind us at school."

"Cate." I sit across from her. I don't smile. I don't extend my hand. I look at her face the way I look at a witness who hasn't been prepped.

"Saskia. It's wonderful to finally meet you." Her smile is full and warm and precisely timed. "Saskia Brennan, right? The barrister? I've heard such things about you."

"Have you."

"I feel like I know you all already. From — well, from reputation, I suppose. It's a small community, Sorrento, even from London. You hear things."

"What kind of things?"

"Oh, just — how close you all were. The five of you. It was like a little gang, wasn't it? Everyone knew the five girls from Sorrento Primary."

"There were six of us," I say.

Her smile doesn't falter. A micro-pause — three-tenths of a second, possibly less — and she nods and says "Of course. I meant — yes. The group. I was too young to be part of it, of course. I was in Year Three when you were in Year Six. But I remember watching you. You all seemed so — grown up. So confident."

The correction lands. The pause is a fracture line in the performance — three-tenths of a second, visible only to someone trained to look for the seam. A person who genuinely didn't know the group wouldn't have known there were six. A person who did know wouldn't have said five. The error reveals familiarity. The speed of the correction reveals practice.

She asks questions. Not about the present. About the house, the group, the history. Subtle questions, wrapped in nostalgia, delivered with a warmth that makes them feel like conversation rather than interrogation. She asks when the renovation happened. She asks who owned the house after the — after the holidays. She asks whether anyone kept in touch with the Doyles.

When she reaches for the tea towel hanging beside the stove, her hand goes to where the hook was in the original kitchen — not where the new rail sits, four inches to the left. Her fingers brush air. She recovers without comment. Drapes the towel on the new rail. Smiles.

I note this. The hand that reaches for a ghost. Muscle memory from a kitchen she should never have seen.

The Doyles.

Kiera's family.

She asks about the Doyles the way you ask about a distant relative — with appropriate concern, with the right amount of curiosity, with the follow-up question ready before the first answer is finished. She asks where they went after — after Sorrento. She asks if anyone knows what Kiera's parents do now. She asks if anyone has seen the grave.

These are not the questions of someone who was two years behind us at school. These are the questions of someone investigating. Someone building a picture. Someone who needs information and has learned how to ask for it without asking.

I watch her. I answer minimally — yes, no, I don't know. I let Imogen fill the gaps. Imogen talks about the area, the changes, the new cafes, and Cate listens and asks follow-ups and every follow-up is a thread being pulled, gently, persistently, to see how far it unravels.

She asks about the holiday house specifically. "Was it a family friend's place? The one you all stayed at?" And Imogen says "It belonged to Neve's family, didn't it?" and Cate says "Oh, of course" and looks at me and I see it — a flash, a micro-expression, the contraction of the muscles around the eyes that Ekman would classify as grief or pain, gone in under half a second, replaced by interest, replaced by the listening face, replaced by nothing.

She knew whose house it was. She didn't need Imogen to tell her. The "oh, of course" was cover. The micro-expression was real.

I stand. "I need some air."

"Of course," Cate says. "We should talk more later — just the two of us, I mean. If that's all right?"

I walk to the hallway. I stop at the doorway. I turn. Cate is watching me. She was watching me leave — her head was turned, her eyes were following me, and when I turned, she looked away. The break was smooth. Practised. The kind of look-away that comes from knowing you're being observed.

I watch from the doorway. She is a source I haven't vetted, a witness whose testimony doesn't match the record. *Who are you?*
