# THE FOURTH STEP — Chapter 27: Saskia

## The Decision

The ambulance is on its way. Imogen and I moved her to the dining room, half-carrying, half-dragging. Elaine's legs gave out in the living room after the confession — the body finally surrendering what the mind had been holding together. Neve is in the dining room with her mother now, who is conscious but grey, her head against the back of the chair, her breathing shallow and wet. Imogen is in the kitchen, running water for no reason, filling and emptying the same glass. Rowan is sitting on the stairs with her camera in her lap. Aisling is standing at the window, watching for headlights.

I am at the kitchen table with the evidence spread in front of me.

The police report. The autopsy photographs. Rowan's photographs of the scratches on the window frame. Aisling's forensic analysis — the biomechanical report, the landing-distance calculations, the bruising-pattern comparison. Four witness statements, given tonight, in this house. Imogen's account of the voice. Rowan's account of the two figures. Neve's account of what she saw. And Elaine's confession — the controlled version and the uncontrolled one.

I arrange it in my mind the way I arrange a brief.

The standard for a coronial reinvestigation is low. The coroner needs only to be satisfied that there is new evidence not available at the time of the original finding. The autopsy photographs were never in evidence. The forensic biomechanical analysis did not exist twenty-five years ago. The witness statements were never properly taken. The scratches on the window frame were never examined.

Any one of these would be enough. All of them together constitute a compelling case. The crime scene photograph with the reflected figure at the top of the stairs — Neve, standing in the hallway, watching — was never followed up. The original attending officer noted "minor bruising" and wrote it off as impact injury from the fall. No forensic pathologist was consulted. No biomechanical analysis was performed. The investigation was perfunctory, the finding was swift, and the case was closed within seventy-two hours of a twelve-year-old girl hitting the ground.

I have spent twenty-five years trying to get someone to reopen it.

And now, at one in the morning on a Sunday in July, sitting at a kitchen table in the house where it happened, I am laying out the options to a room full of women who have earned the right to hear them.

If the coroner finds homicide — and I believe the coroner will — the matter is referred to the Director of Public Prosecutions. The standard for prosecution is reasonable prospect of conviction. The evidence is strong. Elaine's own admission, given in a room full of witnesses, would be admissible. The physical evidence supports a finding of force, not accident. The witness accounts corroborate each other on the central point: Kiera was pushed.

But Elaine is dying.

This is the fact that sits beneath every legal analysis I construct. A committal hearing. A Supreme Court trial. Appeals. The fastest path from charge to verdict in a homicide matter in Victoria is eighteen months. Elaine will not be alive in eighteen months. She may not be alive in eighteen weeks.

The DPP knows this. Charging a dying woman with a twenty-five-year-old homicide — even with strong evidence — would be extraordinary. The defence would argue abuse of process. The trial would be a media spectacle. The victim's family would be dragged through cross-examination on memories a quarter of a century old. And the defendant might die before the jury returned, rendering the entire exercise moot.

Neve comes into the kitchen. Her face is drawn. She sits across from me and looks at the papers and doesn't touch them.

"Status?"

"Conscious. Asking for water. The ambulance is eight minutes out."

"Is she going to be alright?"

She means: is she going to die tonight? She means something she cannot say because saying it would make it real and she has spent her whole life managing reality by not saying the thing that would make it real. I recognise the pattern. I have seen it in witnesses. The careful avoidance of the sentence that transforms possibility into fact.

"She's stable for now," I say. "The collapse is consistent with the progression. Pain, exhaustion, dehydration. She drove forty minutes from a hospice bed to confront five women with evidence of a homicide she committed. Her body has limits."

Neve flinches at the bluntness. I don't soften it.

"What are — what are the options? I need to understand what happens now," she says.

"Option one. We take everything to the coroner. The photographs, the report, the biomechanical analysis, the witness statements, Elaine's admission. The coroner opens a reinvestigation. If the evidence supports it — and it does — the cause of death is changed from accidental to homicide. The matter is referred to the DPP."

"And then?"

"The DPP decides whether to prosecute. Given Elaine's condition, charges are unlikely. A dying defendant. A twenty-five-year-old case. Memories that defence counsel would tear apart on cross-examination. The DPP may conclude there is no reasonable prospect of conviction. But the record changes. Kiera Doyle's death certificate would read homicide, not accident. The official history is corrected. The truth is established in law, even if no one goes to prison."

"Option two."

"We get a full, recorded statement from Elaine. Not the controlled version. The real one. The phone call, the affair, the push, the gaslighting. Recorded, witnessed, preserved. If the DPP ever needs it, it exists. If Aisling publishes, it backs the reporting."

"Option three."

"We do nothing. Elaine dies. The evidence exists but is never formally presented. Aisling publishes what she has. The story is told. But the coronial finding stands. Kiera Doyle's death stays classified as accident."

The kitchen is quiet. The tap runs. Imogen turns it off. She has been listening from the doorway and I let her listen because this decision does not belong to Neve alone.

Aisling appears behind Imogen. She moves differently now — not the calibrated glide of Cate Morrow but the direct walk of a woman who no longer needs to perform ease.

"What do you want, Aisling?" I ask.

"I want the truth on the record. I want the coroner to change the finding. I want my sister's death certificate to say what actually happened."

"Even if no one is charged?"

Aisling's jaw tightens. "That's not my call."

"It's the DPP's call. And the DPP's most likely decision is not to charge."

"Then the truth is on the record. And the person who did it dies without facing a jury."

"Is that enough?"

"No. But it's more than I had yesterday."

Imogen speaks from the doorway. Her voice is rough, scraped raw by hours of crying. "But what about — I mean, what about Neve? What does this do to her?"

"This isn't my decision," Neve says.

"It's partly your decision," I say. "You're the primary witness. Your testimony would be central. And Elaine is your mother. The law doesn't require your consent, but practically, ethically — you're in the room."

Neve nods. Her fingers press against the table. Her knuckles whiten.

"The evidence is sufficient," I say, summarising because that is what I do when the room becomes too heavy for analysis. "And the timeline now supports it. Kiera went upstairs at approximately eleven pm — we know this because Imogen was awake in the living room and the movie was still playing. Kiera encountered Elaine in the upstairs hallway — Elaine was on the phone, which is confirmed by Neve's auditory memory and Elaine's own admission. The argument lasted between thirty and sixty seconds. The push occurred. Kiera fell. Neve arrived at the top of the stairs within seconds — the crime scene photograph places her there. Rowan woke and entered the hallway within thirty seconds of the sound. Imogen heard the voice from the living room. I was the first out of the bedroom — I entered the hallway to find Neve already standing under the window. From voice to fall: under two minutes. From fall to first responder: four minutes. The entire event — from whatever prompted Kiera to go upstairs to the moment I found Neve in the hallway — lasted less than five minutes. The investigation that followed lasted seventy-two hours and concluded accidental death."

I pause. "The disproportion is the argument. Five minutes of reality. Twenty-five years of concealment."

"But it's not any other case," Imogen says.

"No."

The headlights sweep across the kitchen window. The ambulance. Aisling steps back. Imogen presses herself against the doorframe. Neve stands.

I stay at the table with the evidence. Someone needs to hold the brief while the room comes apart.

The front door opens. Two paramedics, young, efficient, with kits and a stretcher and the calm professional neutrality of people who arrive at crises and leave with bodies. They move past us into the dining room. I hear Elaine's voice — thin, directional, still commanding — giving them information. She is managing her own emergency. Of course she is.

Neve goes to the doorway. She stands watching, her back to me, her shoulders rigid, her spine a steel rod, her mother's posture exactly — the stillness of a woman who holds herself together by refusing to move.

She comes back. "They want to take her to Mornington. She's stable. She's asking for me."

"Go," I say.

She hesitates. The hesitation is the whole evening compressed into two seconds — a choice I can see her making in the set of her shoulders, the tilt of her chin. Toward the mother or away.

"Go," I say. "We'll be here when you get back."

She nods. She goes.

The ambulance leaves. Aisling sits in the chair Neve vacated. Imogen sits nearest the door. Rowan appears in the hallway and leans against the frame.

Four women in a kitchen at one in the morning.

"The coronial inquest," Aisling says. "That's the path."

"It's the most likely path to a corrected finding."

"And charges?"

"Unlikely."

Aisling nods. Her hands move on the table — opening and closing, the fingers of a woman who has been building a case for twenty-five years and is about to discover whether the case produces the thing she was chasing.

I have seen this before. The moment when the pursuit ends and the thing pursued is either delivered or it isn't. The discovery, in case after case, is that the verdict does not fill the space the prosecution occupied.

But this is not a case. This is not a brief. This is a twelve-year-old girl named Kiera who went through a window and I was the first one into the hallway and I was twelve and I have been running this investigation for twenty-five years and tonight it ended and Kiera is still dead and the file is still here and I am sitting in a kitchen at one in the morning and my hands are shaking.

I look down. My hands are shaking. A fine tremor, bilateral, the kind I catalogue in others. I press my palms flat against my thighs. The shaking doesn't stop. Twenty-five years of forensic distance and the distance just ran out.

"Justice and truth overlap," I say. "They are not the same jurisdiction."

The room is quiet. Imogen is looking at her hands. Rowan is looking at the ceiling. Aisling is looking at me.

"What do you mean?" Aisling asks.

"I mean that a coronial finding of homicide is truth. It is the official, legal, documented truth that your sister was killed. It is the correction of a record that has been wrong for twenty-five years. It matters. It matters enormously. But it is not justice in the way most people mean justice. Justice means consequences. Justice means a courtroom and a verdict and a sentence. Justice means the person who did it faces the weight of what they did. You may not get that. Not because the evidence isn't strong enough, but because time and illness have moved faster than the legal system can follow."

Aisling looks at the evidence on the table. The photographs. The report. The scratch images. The biomechanical analysis. Twenty-five years of her life, reduced to papers on a kitchen surface in a rented house in Sorrento at one in the morning.

She places her hands flat on the table — palms down, fingers spread, the mirror of Neve's posture. A woman anchoring herself to the physical evidence of the present: the table, the photographs, the surface beneath her palms. Because the alternative is being pulled under.

"I spent twenty-five years wanting to know who killed my sister," Aisling says. "Now I know. And I don't feel anything. I thought I'd feel something — a release, a surge, a gate opening. I don't feel anything at all. Just the echo of all that wanting, reverberating in an empty space where the wanting used to live."
