I wake at five forty-three. The clock on the bedside table says so � white numerals on a black face, the kind of clock that doesn't apologise for the hour. The room is grey. Not the grey of morning but the grey of not-quite-morning, the pre-dawn stillness where the world hasn't decided what colour to be yet. The storm passed sometime after three. I know because the silence woke me � the sudden absence of rain after hours of it, the way your ears reach for a sound that's been there so long it became the room. The power is still out. The heating hasn't restarted. The house is cold � genuine cold, not the cold of the hallway, but the cold of a building with no functioning systems, the cold of a shell. My phone is on the nightstand. I reach for it. One bar of signal. I try Liam's number. The call fails. I try again. Failed. The cell tower on the peninsula must have taken a hit. I text instead � the message sits in the queue, not delivered, spinning, waiting for a signal that doesn't exist.

Houses have rhythms. The heating clicking on and off. Pipes expanding and contracting. Timber adjusting to temperature. I know this because I have spent fifteen years sitting in rooms listening to the sounds people don't know they're making. The leg that won't stop bouncing under the table. The pen clicking during a difficult question. The silence that says more than the words. The house is quiet but not silent; it's making sounds, settling sounds, breathing sounds, the way all houses breathe. And underneath those sounds, underneath the heating and the pipes and the timber, there is something else. A frequency. A hum. A pressure in the air that weights my sternum like a diagnostic lead � the kind that tests whether a reflex still fires.

I should assess this objectively. I'm a therapist. I know that trauma sensitises the nervous system to threat cues, that returning to a trauma site produces physiological arousal without conscious recall. Basic conditioning. The body holds what the mind refuses to carry. I've explained this to a hundred clients. Understanding the mechanism does not stop it from working.

I get out of bed. The floor is cold. My feet find the rug and then the floorboards and then the rug again and the texture change registers somewhere in my brain as data, not memory. I dress in the dark: trackpants, jumper, thick socks. My hands know where the bag is, where the clothes are, where the door is. My hands are capable. My hands do most of my living.

The hallway is empty. The house is empty, or the others are still asleep. I step outside.

At nine-fifteen, a car pulls into the driveway. I hear it from the garden � tyres on the drive, engine cutting, a door opening and closing. Footsteps on the front path. Then the front door opens and a voice says "Hello? Is anyone here?" and the voice is warm and bright and confident in the particular way of someone who has practised being welcome.

I go inside.

She's standing in the hallway. Brown hair, shoulder length, average height, a pleasant face � the kind designed to blend, not striking, not forgettable. Jeans, navy jumper, flat shoes. A small overnight bag over one shoulder. And she is smiling. It's the first thing that registers, because it arrives fully formed, without the hesitation that distinguishes a real expression from a rehearsed one. It is a good smile � full, genuine-seeming, crinkling the corners of her eyes. It is the smile of someone who is happy to be here, who is delighted to see us, who has been looking forward to this reunion with the uncomplicated pleasure of an old friend returning.

Something in her face snagged for a second — the set of the eyes, or the line of the jaw — a resemblance to someone I could not name. I let it go. Twenty-five years is a long time; everyone reminds you of someone.

"Hi," she says. "I'm Cate. Cate Morrow. Saskia invited me � I think? I was a couple of years behind you all at school. You probably don't remember me."

She extends her hand. I take it. Her grip is warm and firm and exactly the right duration: not too brief, not too long, not too tight, not too loose. A calibrated handshake. A handshake designed to convey friendliness and trustworthiness and exactly nothing else.

"Neve," I say.

"Neve Cassidy. Of course." Her smile widens. "It's so good to finally put a face to the name. Saskia's told me quite a bit � do you all still see each other? It's been so long."

I look at her the way I look at clients in the first session: watching for the seams. The smile is perfect. That's what bothers me. It's perfect in the way things are perfect when they have been practised � no hesitation, no micro-adjustments, no moment of genuine uncertainty before the expression settles. Real smiles are messy. They arrive too early or too late, they're asymmetrical, they engage the wrong muscles. This smile engages all the right muscles in all the right order with all the right timing. This smile is a performance.

I know this because I do the same thing. Every day. In my office, with my clients, with my family, with Liam, with Lily, with my mother. I perform calm. I perform competence. I perform fine. I know what a performance looks like because I give one every day of my life.

And here is the trained response: I see something wrong and I tell myself I'm seeing things. Before anyone else can dismiss me, I dismiss myself. The smile is too perfect and the handshake is too calibrated and she looked at the upstairs hallway like it meant something � and I am already constructing the counter-argument. She's nervous. She's trying too hard. I'm being paranoid. I'm reading threat into ordinary social behaviour. This is what my mother taught me to do: not to trust what I see, but to trust the explanation that makes what I saw less frightening. The gaslighting was so thorough that I do it to myself now. I am my own gatekeeper.

This doesn't mean anything. I was twelve. My therapist brain is running. Cate's questions are well-constructed. Open-ended. They invite disclosure without demanding it. They're the questions a therapist asks. The questions a journalist asks. The questions of someone trained to draw information without people noticing.

Or I'm projecting. I don't remember this woman from school, my jaw has been tight since she walked in, and my gaze keeps tracking her across the room without my permission. That's a nervous system in overdrive, not evidence. That's the more rational explanation. That's the one I should go with.
