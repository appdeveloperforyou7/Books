# THE FOURTH STEP — Sample Passages

Each passage demonstrates the target voice, prose style, and unreliable narration pattern for one of the novel's POV characters. Written in present tense, first person, as the novel will be.

---

## Passage 1: NEVE (Chapter 1 Opening — "The Drive")

The GPS says eleven minutes. I know the way without it.

I should turn it off. I don't. The voice — calm, female, Australian — tells me to turn left in four hundred meters, and I follow her instructions even though I turned left at this intersection hundreds of times before I was thirteen. My hands are on the wheel at ten and two, the way my mother taught me. My mother taught me everything. How to hold a knife. How to fold a shirt. How to answer a question without answering it.

My phone buzzes. Mum. I answer on the second ring.

"How far are you?"

"Ten minutes."

"You don't have to do this, sweetheart."

"I know."

"Sometimes it's better to leave things where they are."

The GPS says turn left. I turn left. The road opens and the sea appears on my right, grey and flat under a winter sky. The last time I drove this road I was twelve and the sea was blue and my mother was driving and I was in the back seat with my best — I was in the back seat. I don't remember who was in the back seat with me. I don't remember the drive at all, actually. I was probably asleep. I was always falling asleep in cars. My mother says I slept through the whole holiday.

"Neve? Are you still there?"

"I'm here, Mum."

"You'll call me when you get there?"

"Of course."

"And if it gets to be too much —"

"Mum. I'm fine."

I say it the way I always say it. The way she taught me to say it. Fine. Such a useful word. It means nothing and it means everything and it means stop asking.

The GPS says seven minutes. I can smell the sea now — salt and something else, something underneath, something I can't name. My chest tightens. I tell myself it's the heating. The car is too warm. I turn the heating off.

I see the house before the GPS tells me to turn. Different color — it was white, now it's a blue-grey. The porch is wider. The garden gate is new. But the roofline is the same. The shape of it against the sky is the same. My hands tighten on the wheel. I pull into the driveway and stop the car.

I sit for a long time.

My phone buzzes again. A text from Mum: "You don't have to do this, sweetheart."

I text back: "I know. I'm already here."

I don't get out of the car. I sit and I look at the house and I wait for something — a feeling, a memory, a reason to turn around. Nothing comes. The house is just a house. It's painted a different color. The porch is wider. The garden gate is new.

I get out of the car.

My legs are shaking. I lock the door and stand in the driveway and the sea air hits me and I smell — I smell something I can't — the house is just a house. It's just a house.

I walk toward the front door. My knees want to buckle. I keep walking. I have helped hundreds of people walk toward the things they're afraid of. I know how this works. One foot. Then the other. The fear is information, not instruction.

I knock.

Saskia opens the door. She looks exactly the same — taller, sharper, older, but the same. Same dark eyes that miss nothing. Same short hair, same precise posture. She's wearing a pressed shirt at a weekend house. Of course she is.

"Neve." She says my name like she's verifying it.

"Saskia."

She steps aside. I walk in. The hallway is different — wider, lighter, renovated. But the floorboards. The floorboards creak the same way under my feet. The same pitch, the same groan. I know this sound. I know it in my feet.

"You okay?" Saskia is watching me.

"Fine," I say. "Just tired from the drive."

I don't look at the stairs. I don't look at the stairs. I don't look at the stairs.

---

**Craft Notes:**
- Clinical language emerges naturally ("the fear is information, not instruction") — therapist voice as armor
- "Fine" used deliberately — the gaslit word
- "My mother taught me everything" — casual line that becomes devastating on reread
- Passive voice for past actions: "I was always falling asleep" — not "I fell asleep"
- "I don't look at the stairs" repeated three times — incantation, denial, avoidance
- Sensory detail precedes conscious understanding: she smells something before she knows what it is

## Passage 2: SASKIA (Chapter 6 — "Night Watch")

I sit in the kitchen after the others have gone to bed. My laptop is open. The file is labeled "KDOYLE_INCIDENT" and it is password-protected and it lives on a thumb drive I keep in my toiletry bag and I have read it four hundred times.

I read it again.

The report is two pages. Two pages for a girl's life. "The deceased, Kiera Anne Doyle, aged 12 years, was found at approximately 2120 hours on 14 December lying on the rear lawn of the premises, having apparently fallen from a second-floor window." Apparently. The word is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

"Minor bruising noted on the upper back and shoulders, bilaterally. Consistent with impact injuries from fall."

I was nineteen when I first understood that sentence. I was in the law library at Sydney Uni, surrounded by textbooks on forensic pathology, and I read about bruising patterns and I understood that bilateral bruising on the shoulders does not come from a fall. It comes from hands. Two hands, gripping from behind. Ten fingers pressing into flesh. The kind of bruise you get when someone holds you and you try to pull away.

I close the laptop.

The house is quiet. Wind around the eaves. The creak of timber settling. I hear a sound from upstairs — footsteps, slow, in the hallway. I go to the foot of the stairs and look up. Dark. The sound stops.

Rowan, probably. She can't sleep either. None of us can. We're all lying in our beds thinking about the same thing and pretending we're not.

I go back to the kitchen. I pour a glass of water I don't drink. I stand at the window and look at the garden and I think about Kiera. Not Kiera dead. Kiera alive. The way she laughed — she laughed with her whole body, thrown back, hair flying, like the joke was the best thing that had ever happened to her. She was twelve and she was the most alive person I've ever known and she is dead because someone pushed her out of a window and two pages of report was all her life was worth.

I will find out who did it. I've spent twenty-four years. I can spend twenty-four more.

I hear the footsteps again. I don't go to the stairs this time. I stand at the window and I wait. The footsteps stop. The house settles. The wind blows.

I am very tired. I have been very tired for twenty-four years.

---

**Craft Notes:**
- Short, precise sentences. Minimal adjectives. Legal mind on display.
- "Apparently" — one word carrying forensic skepticism
- The bruising explanation is both exposition and character revelation — Saskia has spent 24 years on this detail
- Emotional breakthrough is a single sentence: "I am very tired. I have been very tired for twenty-four years."
- No self-pity. Just fact. That's the character.

## Passage 3: IMOGEN (Chapter 7 — "The Dream")

I can't sleep.

I took one of my pills — just one, the regular dose, I'm allowed one — and I'm lying in bed and the ceiling is the same height. I know because I'm measuring it with my eyes. I can't stop measuring it. The ceiling in our bedroom at home is higher. This ceiling is lower. This ceiling is the exact height of the ceiling in the room I slept in when I was twelve and I know that because my body knows the distance between my face and the plaster.

Greg would say I'm overthinking. Greg always says I'm overthinking. Greg is not here. Greg is at home with Sophie and Ethan and he texted to say Sophie got the lead in the school play and I said "That's wonderful!" with an exclamation mark and I meant it, I did, Sophie is wonderful, Sophie is ten and she is wonderful and she is safe and she is asleep in her bed at home and she is not here and she is not twelve.

I close my eyes.

The smell of this house. Under the paint — there's new paint, you can smell it, a thin chemical sharpness — but under the paint there's something else. Old wood. Salt air. The particular smell of a house that's close to the sea. It's the same smell. Twenty-five years of paint and renovation and new carpet and it's the same smell.

I open my eyes.

It was an accident. She leaned out the window. It was summer. The window was open. She was looking at the stars — she loved stars, Kiera loved stars, she was always looking up — and she leaned too far. That's what happened. That's what I've always known happened. My mother told me. The police told me. Everyone agreed. It was an accident.

I hear something. From upstairs. A sound — no. Not a sound. A voice. Low, female, sharp — like a command — and then —

I sit up in bed. My heart is pounding. The room is dark. The ceiling is the same height. The air smells like old wood and salt.

It was the TV. We had the TV on. I remember the TV was on. There was a movie — I don't remember what movie. An action movie, maybe. There was a woman's voice in the movie and she said something sharp and then there was a crash and I dropped my mug. The hot chocolate mug. It shattered on the floor.

That's what happened. The TV was on. The mug slipped.

I get up to get water. I pass the stairs. I look up. The hallway is dark.

*What if it wasn't an accident?*

The thought comes from somewhere deep and cold and I push it down so hard my hands shake.

I go back to bed. I don't sleep.

---

**Craft Notes:**
- Warm, chatty voice that fractures under pressure
- "Fine" equivalent: "It was an accident" — the rehearsed phrase
- Sentence length increases as anxiety increases — she talks to fill silence, even internally
- The voice memory intrudes in present tense — it's happening NOW, not then
- Sophie mentioned compulsively — displacement of Kiera
- The mug: "it slipped" — passive voice for a detail that was significant (shattering = the sound of the fall)
- "I push it down so hard my hands shake" — physical manifestation of repression

## Passage 4: ROWAN (Chapter 8 — "Night Vision")

The hallway is cold.

I don't remember coming upstairs. I was asleep — or something like sleep, the grey space between sleep and waking where the body moves without the mind's permission — and now I am standing in the hallway and my feet are bare and the floorboards are cold against my soles and the cold is the same cold. Exactly the same cold. Twenty-five years of different paint and different carpet and the cold is the same.

The window is smaller now. Fixed-pane. You can't open it. The moonlight through it is grey and thin — winter light, July light, nothing like December. December light was gold. December light filled this hallway like water. I remember — I remember the way it turned the walls to cream and the carpet to blue and the air was warm and the window was open and the air moved through the house like breathing.

I lift my camera. I don't decide to lift it. My hands lift it. My hands know what to do even when my mind doesn't. I frame the shot: the window, the wall, the length of the hallway. The flash fires. White light, gone. I look at the screen.

The hallway is empty. Of course it's empty. What did I expect to see?

I zoom in on the window. The new frame is white aluminum, clean, modern. But behind it — where the new frame meets the old wall — I can see the original wood. Dark, stained, old. And on the wood: scratches. Thin lines. Five of them. Parallel. Downward.

Someone held onto this frame. Someone dragged their fingernails down the wood. Someone was holding on and someone let go.

I know whose nails those were.

I was standing here. I was standing exactly here. I was twelve and the hallway was warm and the window was open and I looked up and I saw — shapes. Two shapes. One tall and one small. The tall one was between me and the window. The small one was closer to the glass. The tall one's arm moved. Not fast. Deliberate. A push. Not a shove. A push. And the small one — the small one went through the window. Not fell. Not jumped. Went. Like the window took her. Like the air swallowed her.

I don't know who they were. I have never known. I have been standing in this hallway for twenty-five years, standing right here, looking at this window, and I have never been able to name the shapes.

But I know the hand. The hand that reached for the frame. Small fingers, splayed wide, nails catching paint. I know those hands. I held them. We held hands all the time, the way twelve-year-old girls do, walking to school, running across the lawn, linking fingers under the table. I know the shape of those fingers. I know the knuckles. I know the way they gripped.

That was Kiera's hand. And it let go.

---

**Craft Notes:**
- Poetic, sensory, fragmented style
- Long descriptions of light and temperature — Rowan perceives the world sensually
- Shift to second person in flashbacks is avoided here; instead, past tense intrudes on present
- "Went through the window" — not fell, not jumped. The word "went" is Rowan's evasion — her brain can't categorize what she saw
- The hand description is intimate, specific, personal — she's describing a friend's hands from memory, not evidence
- Camera as proxy perception — she processes through the lens more than through her eyes

## Passage 5: "CATE" / AISLING (Chapter 11 excerpt — Kitchen scene from her POV)

The kitchen is warm. Imogen is making breakfast and I am sitting at the counter and I am performing the role of Cate Morrow, a woman who grew up nearby and moved to London and lost touch and wants to reconnect. I have rehearsed this role for two years. I know every line.

Imogen cracks eggs into a pan. She moves around the kitchen like she's choreographed it — reaching for the butter without looking, flipping the toast at the exact right moment, humming something tuneless. She is performing too. We are all performing. But Imogen's performance is different from mine. Imogen is performing normalcy to convince herself. I'm performing normalcy to survive.

"Cate, do you want pepper on yours?"

"Please."

I watch her hands. They're busy — always busy, always moving. Stirring, smoothing, straightening. She cannot let them rest. I've noticed this. In my notes — the notes I keep in a hidden file on my phone, the notes that are not Cate's notes but Aisling's — I have written: *I. Hart: constant manual activity. Undoing behavior. The minimizer cannot stop repairing.*

"Remember when we used to have sleepovers and make pancakes at midnight?" Imogen says. She's smiling. The smile is real and it's a mask and it's both at once.

"I remember," I say. I don't remember. I was eight. I wasn't invited to THE FOURTH STEPs. I was Kiera's annoying little sister, too young, too uncool, too much in the way. Kiera used to close the door in my face. "Aisling, go away, we're doing big girl things." I used to sit outside her door and listen to them laughing.

"Do you remember Kiera?" Imogen says it casually, the way you'd ask if someone remembers the weather.

The name hits me in the sternum. Every time. Every single time. I have been bracing for this name for two years and it still hits me.

"What was she like?" I ask. My voice is steady. I've practiced this conversation in the mirror. I've practiced the right amount of interest, the right amount of sadness, the right amount of distance. Not too much interest (that's suspicious). Not too much sadness (that's suspicious). Just enough of both to read as a casual acquaintance remembering a childhood tragedy.

Imogen's spatula stops in mid-air. "Kiera was — she was the best of us." Her voice catches. "She was brave and funny and she — she fell. It was an accident. She leaned out the window and fell. It was summer and the window was open and it was just — just an accident."

She's said this before. I can hear the grooves in the words, like a record played too many times. This is a speech she's given — to herself, to others, to the mirror. "It was an accident." She's not telling me. She's telling herself.

And something happens to my face. I don't decide it. I don't plan it. The mask slips — just for a second, just a fraction — and what's underneath is not Cate's grief but Aisling's. Twenty-five years of grief. Twenty-five years of a sister-shaped hole in my life. Twenty-five years of *Kiera used to close the door in my face and I would have done anything to be in that room with her and she died in that room without me.*

I pull it back. Fast. The mask reassembles. But for one second — one fraction of a second — my face told the truth.

"That must have been terrible," I say. My voice is steady.

---

**Craft Notes:**
- Over-controlled prose — grammatically perfect, rhythmically even, emotionally measured
- When describing others: precise, observational, journalistic
- When describing herself: generic, constructed — "I am performing the role of Cate Morrow" (meta-awareness of the performance)
- The mask slip: raw grief breaking through the controlled surface. Not described in flowery language — stated directly
- "She's not telling me. She's telling herself." — journalistic observation, not empathy
- The detail about Kiera closing the door in her face — intimate, real, the kind of detail a stranger wouldn't have
- "I've practiced this conversation in the mirror" — admission of deception hidden in plain sight
